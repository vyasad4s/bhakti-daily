>>> COSMIC INTERPRETATION <<<

Ekādaśī is the day of sense control. The number eleven represents the ten senses (five knowledge-gathering, five active) plus the mind — all eleven must be turned toward Viṣṇu. Kamada Ekādaśī specifically grants the kāma (desire) for liberation to those who fast and worship. What Vedas call deliberate restraint, Patañjali calls pratyāhāra; what the devotee practices as fasting, the yogi recognizes as the withdrawal of consciousness from its objects.

Āśleṣā nakṣatra embodies the serpent's embrace — binding the soul to matter, yet also the power to release through awareness. Mercury rules this asterism, and Mercury (Budha) is the planet of discrimination, analysis, the intellect that can untangle what it has woven. The serpent Nāgas guard treasures beneath the earth; similarly, the senses guard the treasure of self-knowledge beneath their surface activity. The binding and the release are the same energy viewed from different stages of practice.

The Dhṛti yoga demands not fleeting enthusiasm but steadiness — exactly what BG 2.58 promises to one who masters the tortoise-method of withdrawal. Dhṛti means perseverance, resolution, the capacity to hold a line. On Śanivār (Saturday), Śani's day of karma and limitation, this steadiness is tested against the weight of accumulated saṁskāras. The fast becomes karma-yoga: action (refraining from food) offered with detachment from result.

Viṣṭi karana is "transformative work" — labor that builds what endures. Sense control is not suppression. It is retractability: the ability to extend and withdraw at will, like the tortoise's limbs, used for service rather than compulsive engagement. The viṣṭi karana makes the day's work effective — not just effort, but effort that transforms.

The moon enters Simha (Leo) tomorrow morning, but today it remains in Karka (Cancer), its own sign, strong with soma-flow. Emotional capacity is heightened, which means emotional entanglement is also heightened. The Ekādaśī fast cuts the fuel supply to emotional reactions, revealing what stands when the waves settle.

BG 2.58 — The Tortoise of Withdrawal

This verse speaks directly to the Āśleṣā-Ekādaśī convergence. The serpent (Āśleṣā) and the tortoise both retract — one coils, one withdraws limbs — but the yogi learns from the tortoise: deliberate, total, reversible. The serpent strikes suddenly; the tortoise moves slowly, never surprised. On Kamada Ekādaśī, the "sudden strike" of sense-objects loses its target because the limbs are already within.

Why this verse today? Because Dhṛti yoga demands pratiṣṭhitā prajñā — wisdom firmly established. Not the flickering insight of retreat, but the steady flame of one who can move through the world with senses extended, yet never lose the center. The fast proves: the self remains when the body's demands are not met. This is the Kamada (desire-fulfilling) quality — the desire for liberation is granted by demonstrating that the self is independent of sensory gratification.

Śanivār adds the karmic dimension: what you retract from today, you do not bind yourself with tomorrow. The saṁskāras not planted are the future liberations not required.
