>>> COSMIC INTERPRETATION <<<

Śukla Dwādaśī approaches Pūrṇimā — the moon at the threshold of fullness. This is a tithi of completion pressure: the vortex builds toward maximum expression, yet the ancients knew what physics now confirms — fullness precedes only the wane. The form (vyakta) reaches peak visibility precisely when the return to avyakta (unmanifest) becomes inevitable.

Maghā nakṣatra, the Royal Throne, contains the seeds of dissolution in its very exaltation. Its deity is the Pitṛs — those who have traversed this cycle before us. They are not "ancestors" as sentiment; they are the proof that jātasya hi dhruvo mṛtyuḥ: for one who has taken birth, death is certain. The Pitṛs sat on thrones; now they watch. The throne remains; the occupant changes. This is Sāṅkhya 101: the form (throne) is prakṛtic, the witness (soul) is puruṣic. When Ketu (mokṣa-kāraka) rules this asterism through its destructive side, the message sharpens: even royal power dissolves.

The ⚠️ Gandamool Warning period (Mar 29 05:07 – Mar 30 05:17) marks a pitfall zone where the bridge between inhalation and exhalation becomes unstable. The tithi transitions in this window (Dwādaśī → Trayodaśī), the nakṣatra holds Gandamool's edge. This is not superstition; it is recognition that certain configurations bring pressure, demand heightened discernment (viveka). As YS I.15 teaches: dṛṣṭānuśravika-viṣaya-vitṛṣṇasya vaśīkāra-saṁjñā vairāgyam — dispassion is controlled consciousness of one who is free from craving for sense objects, seen or heard. On Gandamool, the craving itself becomes the pitfall; the yogi must recognize the transition-zone before acting.

Combined with Soola yoga — meaning "thorn" or "spike" — the day's energy cuts. The thorn does not negotiate; it pierces. Face what pricks rather than retreat, but face it with vairāgya: not attachment to outcome, not aversion to sensation, but observation. The Sūtra says vitṛṣṇasya — one who has no thirst, not one who has no feeling.

Simha rāśi placement dignifies the encounter — we cannot avoid destiny, but we can meet it with sovereignty. The lion does not lament its prey; it acts efficiently. Simha is the Sun's sign, and the Sun is the ātmā-kāraka — indicator of self. The cosmic data points: the self (Sun/Simha) witnesses the cycle (Maghā/Pitṛs) with the inevitability of law (Soola) during the threshold of fullness (Dwādaśī). The only question is whether you watch with lamentation or with prajñā.

BG 2.27 — Inevitable Cycles

"One who has taken his birth is sure to die, and after death one is sure to take birth again. Therefore, in the unavoidable discharge of your duty, you should not lament."

The verse was chosen because dhruva (certain/inevitable) appears twice — anchoring the teaching in unshakeable law. Maghā's ancestral connection makes this verse particularly resonant: the Pitṛs walked this cycle and now witness it from the other side of the equation. They're not gone; their witnessing continues in the form of the nakṣatra's energy.

The Soola's cutting energy demands we not soften this truth with sentiment (the śocitum — lamentation — that BG 2.27 explicitly rejects). The Gandamool warning period amplifies the message: some things have no workaround (aparihārya). The verse is about accepting what the panchanga itself demonstrates — birth and death are the fundamental oscillation of material existence between vyakta (manifest) and avyakta (unmanifest).

But here is the Garga Muni insight: the panchāṅga does not merely describe death-birth alternation; it encodes liberation in the same data. When the Pitṛ-nakṣatra appears and the verse says "do not lament," the teaching is: witness the cycle without identifying as the cycler. This is viveka — the discernment between puruṣa (certainty) and prakṛti (flux).

>>> YOGA SŪTRA CONNECTION <<<

YS I.15: दृष्टानुश्रविक-विषय-वितृष्णस्य वशीकार-संज्ञा वैराग्यम्

"Dispassion is controlled consciousness of one who is free from craving for sense objects, perceived or heard."

Vyāsa comments: "This applies to objects seen (perceived directly) and objects known through śruti (scripture)." The Gandamool Pitṛs are "seen" only as felt-presence, as the weight of ancestral pattern. The BG 2.27 cycle is "heard" through the Gītā's testimony. Both must be approached with vitṛṣṇasya — freedom from thirst, not freedom from engagement.

Today's cosmic configuration tests this: when Maghā energy brings ancestral expectation, when Soola demands facing thorns, when Dwādaśī fullness pressures completion — can you practice vairāgyam? Not withdrawal from duty, but duty without the fever of expectation. The Pitṛs did not escape the cycle with attachment; witness them now in the stars, still threaded. Learn the lesson: śocitum (lamentation/attachment to past/future forms) is optional; prajñā (discriminative wisdom) is not.
