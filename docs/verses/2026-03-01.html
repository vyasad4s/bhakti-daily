🏹 Bhagavad-gītā 3.6 | March 01, 2026

 

कर्मेन्द्रियाणि संयम्य य आस्ते मनसा स्मरन्

इन्द्रियार्थान्विमूढात्मा मिथ्याचारः स उच्यते ॥ ३.६ ॥

 

karmendriyāṇi saṁyamya
ya āste manasā smaran
indriyārthān vimūḍhātmā
mithyācāraḥ sa ucyate

 

"One who restrains the senses of action but whose mind dwells on sense objects certainly deludes himself and is called a pretender."

 

The pretender paradox: external restraint without internal transformation. The body sits in lotus posture while the mind runs to sense objects. This is mithyācāraḥ — the greatest cheater, as Prabhupāda warns. Not the action binds us, but the saṅga (attachment) and the smaraṇa (dwelling thought).

 

Kṛṣṇa exposes the fundamental illusion: you cannot NOT act. Yesterday verse (3.5) established that the guṇas of prakṛti compel action. Today Kṛṣṇa goes deeper — even the attempt to restrain action while the mind clings to pleasure is itself a form of bondage, and worse: it is deceptive.

 

The karmendriyāṇi (five working senses: hands, legs, speech, genitals, anus) can be physically controlled. But manasā smaran — thinking with the mind of sense objects — this is where the theft occurs. The body is restrained; the consciousness is chained.

 

This verse is the bridge between external renunciation and internal yoga. Arjuna fear was of sinful action; Kṛṣṇa answer is that the false yogī is more dangerous than the sincere householder. A sweeper who sweeps with devotion is better than a "meditator" who meditates only for reputation.

 

The test: where does your mind go when you close your eyes? That is your true worship.

 

Sankhya Concepts: The tri-guṇas operate through both buddhi and indriyas. When one restrains the karmendriyas without purifying the antaḥkaraṇa, the rajas finds new channels. The puruṣa remains bound not by action but by saṅkalpa.

TERM DEFINITIONS:

Karmendriyāṇi — the five organs of action (hands, legs, speech, genitals, anus)

Saṁyamya — having controlled, restraining

Āste — sits, remains

Manasā — by the mind

Smaran — thinking of, dwelling upon

Indriya-arthān — objects of the senses

Vimūḍha — deluded, bewildered

Ātmā — self, soul

Mithyācāraḥ — pretender, hypocrite

Ucyate — is called
