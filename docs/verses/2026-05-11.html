🏹 BG 5.2 | May 11, 2026

 

सन्न्यास: कर्मयोगश्च नि:श्रेयसकरावुभौ ।
तयोस्तु कर्मसन्न्यासात्कर्मयोगो विशिष्यते ॥ ५.२ ॥

 

sannyāsaḥ karma-yogaś ca
niḥśreyasa-karāv ubhau
tayo stu karma-sannyāsāt
karma-yogo viśiṣyate

 

Translation: The Personality of Godhead replied: The renunciation of work and work in devotion are both good for liberation. But, of the two, work in devotional service is better than renunciation of work.

 

The struggle for liberation is often misread as a choice between two extremes: the hermit who flees the world to find peace, and the householder who is crushed by the machinery of duty. Here, Kṛṣṇa debugs this binary. He acknowledges that both sannyāsa (the formal abandonment of activity) and karma-yoga (action performed as service) can lead to niḥśreyasa—the ultimate liberation. However, the "patch" provided by Karma Yoga is far more stable. Artificial renunciation is like trying to delete a system process without understanding the dependencies; it often leads to a crash in the form of repressed desires and spiritual pride. True renunciation is not the absence of action, but the absence of agency. When we shift our identity from the "Doer" (the ego) to the "Instrument" (the servant), the burden of the result vanishes. The action remains, but the bondage is stripped away. In this state, work becomes a form of meditation. We no longer fight the world; we use the world as the gymnasium for our devotion. By offering every result back to the Source, we transform the heavy "compiled code" of our karma into a light, transparent flow of grace. This is the secret of the dāsānudāsa: the servant of the servant finds the highest freedom not in the forest, but in the heart of service.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

Sannyāsa: The act of renunciation. In its lower form, it is the physical abandonment of duties; in its higher form, it is the mental abandonment of the sense of proprietorship.

Karma-Yoga: The yoga of action. The process of transforming mundane work into spiritual service by offering the results to the Divine.

Niḥśreyasa: The ultimate, unsurpassed good; liberation from the cycle of birth and death (saṁsāra).
