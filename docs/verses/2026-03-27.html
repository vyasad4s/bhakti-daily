आपूर्यमाणमचलप्रतिष्ठं

समुद्रमापः प्रविशन्ति यद्वत् ।

तद्वत्कामा यं प्रविशन्ति सर्वे

स शान्तिमाप्‍नोति न कामकामी ॥ ७० ॥

 

āpūryamāṇam acala-pratiṣṭhaṁ
samudram āpaḥ praviśanti yadvat
tadvat kāmā yaṁ praviśanti sarve
sa śāntim āpnoti na kāma-kāmī

 

A person who is not disturbed by the incessant flow of desires — that enter like rivers into the ocean, which is ever being filled but is always still — can alone achieve peace, and not the man who strives to satisfy such desires.

 

The analogy is precise: rivers flow ceaselessly into the ocean during monsoon, yet the ocean remains acala-pratiṣṭham — steadfast, unwavering in its depths. It does not flood. It does not overflow its banks. The surface may churn with storms, but the abyssal waters are still.

 

So too with the mind established in consciousness of the Absolute. Desires continue to appear — this is the nature of prakṛti, the material matrix. The senses perceive objects, the mind processes sensations, attachment and aversion arise. But these are surface phenomena. They do not reach the depths.

 

The devotee is already full. Not through sensory gratification, but through bhakti — the fullness that comes from connection to source. Therefore desires arrive like rivers to an already-full ocean: they merge without disturbance.

 

Kṛṣṇa draws the contrast sharply: the one who strives to fulfill desires — kāma-kāmī — never attains peace. Each satisfaction breeds new longing. But the devotee who has found fullness in service? Desires enter and dissolve. They do not find purchase.

 

🔥 Yoga Sūtra II.54: svaviṣayāsaṃprayoge cittasya svarūpānukāra ivendriyāṇāṃ pratyāhāraḥ — "Pratyāhāra occurs when the senses do not come into contact with their respective sense objects." Patañjali describes withdrawal as natural when the mind is fixed elsewhere. When citta is absorbed in its object of meditation, senses cease their functioning automatically. This is exactly what Kṛṣṇa describes: the devotee whose mind is fixed in fullness does not need to restrain senses separately — they withdraw naturally, like rivers flowing into a self-satisfied ocean.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

āpūryamāṇam (present passive participle) — always being filled, constantly replenished. Like a server receiving requests but requiring no processing cycles — already at capacity.

acala-pratiṣṭham (compound) — immovably situated, permanently established. acala = unshaken, pratiṣṭham = foundation. The depths remain steady regardless of surface conditions.

kāma-kāmī (compound) — one who desires desires, the desirer of fulfillment. Pleonastic construction emphasizing compulsive pursuit — trapped in recursive wanting.

pratyāhāra (masculine noun) — withdrawal of senses from objects; literally "drawing back toward oneself." The fifth limb of yoga where senses cease outward projection.

śānti (feminine noun) — peace, tranquility, unperturbed consciousness. Not absence of disturbance but positive fullness, completeness.
