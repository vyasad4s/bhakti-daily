🏹 Bhagavad Gita 2.27 | 2026-03-29

 

जातस्य हि ध्रुवो मृत्युर्ध्रुवं जन्म मृतस्य च ।

तस्मादपरिहार्येऽर्थे न त्वं शोचितुमर्हसि ॥ २७ ॥

 

jātasya hi dhruvo mṛtyur dhruvaṁ janma mṛtasya ca
tasmād aparihārye 'rthe na tvaṁ śocitum arhasi

 

"One who has taken his birth is sure to die, and after death one is sure to take birth again. Therefore, in the unavoidable discharge of your duty, you should not lament."

 

The Inevitable Code

This is not consolation. This is law.

 

The Sanskrit dhruvaḥ means fixed, certain, unshakeable — not merely "sure." In Sāṅkhya, the birth-death cycle is the default state of the conditioned soul, the prakṛtic loop running until debugged. This is your operating system. Until you install the yoga-patch, you execute the same birth-death binary.

 

Krishna doesn't comfort Arjuna. He clarifies: these relatives were born, so they will die. The body is temporary. What remains is duty (dharma) without attachment to outcome. This is karma-yoga as exploitation, not sentiment.

 

Aparihārye 'rthe — "in the unavoidable matter." Some things cannot be opted out of. Lamentation adds only noise to a process already executing. The yogi recognizes the inevitable and moves efficiently.

 

🔥 Yoga Sutra I.15: dṛṣṭānuśravika-viṣaya-vitṛṣṇasya vaśīkāra-saṁjñā vairāgyam — "Dispassion is the controlled consciousness of one who is without craving for sense objects, whether these are actually perceived, or described [in scripture]."

 

The commentary notes: real detachment is indifference to sense objects whether absent or present. Extend this: the yogi cultivates the same indifference to birth-death cycles that they apply to sense objects. BG 2.27 demands we recognize birth-death as prakṛtic phenomena, subject to the same vairāgya.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

jātasya (adj.) — of one who has taken birth; from jan to be born, produced, arise. In Sāṅkhya: manifestation of effect from its subtle cause. Hacker note: Like a process that's spawned — once running, it has lifecycle: init, execute, terminate.

dhruva (adj.) — fixed, certain, constant, unshakeable. The pole star, Dhruva, is literally "the fixed one." Hacker note: Immutable constants. Death is const mortality = true; it doesn't take parameters.

mṛtyu (m.) — death, cessation. From mṛ to die, decay. The dissolution of form. In Sāṅkhya: return of effect into cause — manifestation contracts to latency. Not annihilation of soul; retirement of garment.

aparihārya (adj.) — unavoidable, inalienable. From a (not) + pari-hṛ (to leave, abandon). That which cannot be opted out of, cannot be bypassed. Hacker note: You can't rm -rf the cycle. Learn to execute without friction.

śocitum (inf.) — to lament, grieve. From śuc to beam with heat, burn, suffer anguish. Wasted processing power on output that cannot be changed.
