🏹 BG 5.21 | May 13, 2026

 

बाह्यस्पर्शेष्वसक्तात्मा विन्दत्यात्मनि यत्सुखम् । स ब्रह्मयोगयुक्तात्मा सुखमक्षयमश्नुते ॥ २१ ॥

 

bāhya-sparśeṣv asaktātmā
vindaty ātmani yat sukham
sa brahma-yoga-yuktātmā
sukham akṣayam aśnute

 

Translation: Such a liberated person is not attracted to material sense pleasure but is always in trance, enjoying the pleasure within. In this way the self-realized person enjoys unlimited happiness, for he concentrates on the Supreme.

 

The material senses are doors that open outward, seeking contact with external objects. Each contact produces a fleeting sensation—what we call pleasure—but because the object is material and temporary, the sensation inevitably fades. This fading creates a sense of lack, which the mind interprets as a need for more stimulation, thus beginning the cycle of craving. The intelligent person (buddhimān) sees this mechanism clearly. They understand that lasting happiness cannot be found in a process that inherently contains its own end. Therefore, they redirect the senses' natural tendency to seek outward—not by suppressing the senses, but by engaging them in a higher purpose: the service of the Supreme. When the senses are engaged in brahma-yoga—yoga of the Absolute—each action becomes an offering. The hand that would grasp for sense pleasure now offers flowers to the Deity. The tongue that would crave sweet flavors now chants the Holy Name. The ears that would seek mundane music now listen to kīrtan. In this way, the senses are not denied, but purified. As the senses become purified, the mind naturally turns inward. The constant outflow of consciousness toward external objects diminishes, and the awareness of the self (ātmā) becomes prominent. This is not a void or a negation, but the discovery of an inner wellspring of joy that is independent of external circumstances—a happiness that has no beginning and no end (akṣayam). This internal happiness is not mystical or vague; it is the natural state of the soul when it is situated in its constitutional position as an eternal servant of Kṛṣṇa. The soul's intrinsic nature is ānanda (bliss), but this bliss is covered by the designations of the material body and the false identification with the senses. When those coverings are removed through brahma-yoga, the soul's innate joy shines forth. Thus, the verse describes not a state of austere denial, but a state of rich fulfillment. The self-realized person enjoys a happiness that is superior to any material pleasure because it is eternal, unreserved, and ever-increasing as devotion deepens. This is the promise of Ekādaśī: by voluntarily reducing the stimulation of the lower senses today, we create the space to perceive this higher taste.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

asakta (असक्त) — Not attached, unattached. In BG 5.21, asakta-ātmā refers to one whose mind is not attached to external sense objects.

brahma-yoga (ब्रह्मयोग) — Union with the Absolute through concentration. The state where the mind is fixed on Brahman, leading to inner satisfaction.

akṣaya (अक्षय) — Imperishable, unlimited, undiminishing. The happiness of the self-realized person never decreases.

pratyāhāra (प्रत्याहार) — Withdrawal of the senses. The fifth limb of yoga where consciousness stops flowing out through the senses and turns inward.
