🏹 BG 5.15 | May 15, 2026

 

अधिष्ठात्रि परमे भर्ति कर्तारि सर्वलोकस्मात् । न कर्तारि न कर्माणि लोके सॄजति नियन्ता ॥ १५ ॥

 

adhiṣṭhātari parame bhartā kartāri sarvalokasmāt
na kartāri na karmāṇi loke sṛjati niyantā

 

Translation: The embodied living being, however, does not create his own impetus for action, nor does he create the agent who causes action, nor does he create the fruits of action. All this is effected by the modes of material nature.

 

This verse is a profound declaration of non-doership (akartṛtva). It teaches that the living being (jīva) is not the ultimate source of action. The impetus to act (pravṛtti), the agent who performs the action (kartā), and the results (phalam) are all orchestrated by prakṛti—the material nature governed by the three guṇas (sattva, rajas, tamas). The jīva, conditioned by prakṛti, mistakenly identifies itself as the doer due to ahankāra (false ego). When this misidentification is removed through knowledge and devotion, one sees that action happens through the instrumentality of the body and mind, but the true self remains a neutral witness. Consider the analogy of a robot on an assembly line. The robot performs tasks, but it does not originate the design, the power supply, or the purpose of the line—it is merely an instrument. Similarly, the body-mind complex is an instrument powered by prakṛti. The soul, when situated in its true nature as ātmā, is like the electricity that powers the robot: it enables action but does not dictate the specifics of the task. When we realize this, we can engage in work without attachment to outcomes, because we understand that we are not the proprietor of the action or its fruits. This understanding is the foundation of karma-yoga: acting as an instrument of the Divine. When we offer our actions to Kṛṣṇa, we transfer the sense of proprietorship from the false ego to the Supreme. The action remains, but the burden of doership and the anxiety of results fall away. We become like a lotus leaf in water—unaffected by the moisture because our essence is not of the water, though we reside within it. On the eve of Amavasya (New Moon), when the moon’s light is minimal, the darkness symbolizes the illusion of doership. Just as the moon reflects the sun’s light but has no light of its own, the jīva reflects consciousness but is not its source. This night is ideal for contemplating our true role: not as the originator, but as a reflector of divine energy. By meditating on this truth, we weaken the grip of ahankāra and prepare to act with pure detachment.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

adhiṣṭhātari — upon the Supreme; the substratum or basis

parame — supreme, highest

bhartā — maintainer, sustainer

kartāri — the agent, the doer

sarvalokasmāt — from all worlds

na kartāri — not the agent

na karmāṇi — not the action

loke — in the world

sṛjati — creates, produces

niyantā — the controller, regulator
