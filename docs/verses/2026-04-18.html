जन्म कर्म च मे दिव्यमेवं यो वेत्ति तत्त्वतः ।

त्यक्त्वा देहं पुनर्जन्म नैति मामेति सोऽर्जुन ॥ ९ ॥

 

janma karma ca me divyam
evaṁ yo vetti tattvataḥ
tyaktvā dehaṁ punar janma
naiti mām eti so ’rjuna

 

One who knows the transcendental nature of My appearance and activities does not, upon leaving the body, take his birth again in this material world, but attains My eternal abode, O Arjuna.

 

The verse inaugurates Jñāna Yoga with a sledgehammer. Knowledge here is not intellectual accumulation but ontological recognition: knowing the Lord's appearance (janma) and activities (karma) as divyam — divine, not material. This knowledge burns the seed of rebirth.

 

Janma karma ca me divyam — My birth and work are divine. The Lord appears to appear, acts to act. There is no material necessity driving these appearances. They are līlā — play, not reaction. The avatāra principle: descent without descent, transcendence manifesting as immanence.

 

Evaṁ yo vetti tattvataḥ — one who knows thus in truth. Not hearsay, not belief, not philosophical speculation. Recognition at the level of being. The distinction between material and divine birth understood as distinction between prakṛti and puruṣa.

 

Tyaktvā dehaṁ punar janma naiti — having abandoned the body, not again taking birth. Liberation from the cycle. The guṇas no longer have jurisdiction. The kleśa-sequence (YS II.3-9) is broken at its root: the ignorance of identification is removed.

 

Mām eti — attains Me. Not void. Not impersonal dispersion. Personal attainment of the Person. Bhakti-yoga and jñāna-yoga converge: knowledge leads to devotion, devotion crystallizes as knowledge.

 

Today's cosmic alignment: Bharaṇī (Yama) transitions to Kṛttikā (Agni) at 09:40 PM. Yama's domain is death, binding, the cycle of return. Agni's domain is fire that releases, the divine fire that burns the seed of rebirth. The verse teaches what Kṛttikā's fire enables: knowledge that ends rebirth. Chandra Darśan — first crescent after new moon — marks the return of light after dissolution. The knowledge of Bhagavad-gītā is that returning light: recognition of what was never lost.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

janma — birth, appearance; in this context, the Lord's divine descent, not material birth

karma — activities, works; specifically the Lord's pastimes (līlā), not reactions to past actions

divyam — divine, transcendental; of the nature of divinity, not material manifestation

tattvataḥ — in truth, in reality; ontologically, not superficially or philosophically

tyaktvā — having abandoned, given up; the voluntary release of the body at death

punar janma — again taking birth; the cycle of saṁsāra, perpetuated by residual karma

naiti — does not attain; negation of return, cessation of the cycle

mām eti — attains Me; personal attainment of the Supreme Person
