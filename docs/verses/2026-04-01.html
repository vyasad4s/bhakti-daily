🏹 BG 2.15 | April 1, 2026

 

यं हि न व्यथयन्त्येते पुरुषं पुरुषर्षभ ।

समदुःखसुखं धीरं सोऽमृतत्वाय कल्पते ॥ १५ ॥

 

yaṁ hi na vyathayanty ete puruṣaṁ puruṣarṣabha

sama-duḥkha-sukhaṁ dhīraṁ so 'mṛtatvāya kalpate

 

Translation: O best among men [Arjuna], the person who is not disturbed by happiness and distress and is steady in both is certainly eligible for liberation.

 

Here is a precise teaching. Not sentimentality about "staying positive" through hardship. Not toxic positivity that denies suffering exists. Something far more radical: the recognition that experience itself is prakṛtic data, and you are not the processor.

 

The dhīra — patient, steady — is one who has internalized the Sāṅkhya insight that puruṣa (conscious subject) and prakṛti (material matrix) are categorically distinct. Pleasure and pain are modifications of prakṛti. They arise in the field (kṣetra) of body-mind, not in the knower of the field (kṣetra-jña). The yogi witnesses them as weather patterns: real, impactful to the body, but not compromising the sky-like awareness in which they appear and dissolve.

 

Prabhupāda emphasizes this requires determination — a word choice that strips the teaching of any passive interpretation. This isn't waiting for equanimity to descend as a blessing. It's the active cultivation of discrimination (viveka) and dispassion (vairāgya) through sustained practice. Lord Caitanya's example at age twenty-four — abandoning home, young wife, elderly mother for sannyāsa — isn't offered as a universal prescription but as proof-of-concept: when the goal is seen clearly, prakṛtic attachments lose their binding power.

 

The Pūrṇimā moon tonight is fullest illumination, but note: it will wane. The yogi who understands BG 2.15 witnesses both waxing and waning with the same steady gaze. The fullness does not intoxicate; the diminishment does not depress. Both are ete — "these things" — phenomena in the field, not touching the witness.

 

🔥 YOGA SŪTRA CONNECTION:

 

YS II.48: tataḥ dvandvānabhighātaḥ — "From this [practice], there is non-disturbance from the pairs of opposites."

 

The "pairs of opposites" (dvandva) — sukha/duḥkha, success/failure, heat/cold, honor/dishonor — these are the oscillations of prakṛti. The yogi, established in practice with dispassion (vairāgya), no longer registers these fluctuations as spiritual data.

 

Edwin Bryant notes that this is not mere stoicism. It's the result of puruṣa-khyāti — direct perception of the soul's separateness from the guṇa's dance. When you know, experientially, that you are not the mind experiencing pleasure or pain, but the witness (sākṣī) to its activity, the disturbance stops registering as "yours." The mind still feels — this isn't anesthesia. But the appropriation of feeling, the "I am suffering / I am enjoying" — that's the break in identification.

TERM DEFINITIONS:

 

sama (adj.) — equal, same, balanced, equanimous; the quality of buddhi when sattva predominates and rajas/tamas are subdued — the 'zero-drift' state of the mind

 

duḥkha (n.) — pain, distress, suffering; from dus- (difficult) + kha- (space/ground) — "hard ground," existential dissatisfaction — the 'error state' of material existence

 

sukha (n.) — happiness, pleasure, ease; from su- (good) + kha (space) — "good ground" — the 'success output' that still binds

 

dhīra (adj.) — steady, patient, firm; from √dhṛ (to hold, carry, support) — one who holds steady under pressure — the carrier of spiritual weight without system collapse

 

amṛtatva (n.) — liberation, immortality, nectar-state; a-mṛta (without death) — not longevity but freedom from the birth-death cycle — 'root access' to eternal existence

 

kalpate (v.) — is considered eligible, fit, suitable; from √kḷp (to be able) — the technical term for 'qualified practitioner' — having the prerequisites for liberation

 

dvandva (n.) — pairs of opposites; hot-cold, pleasure-pain, honor-dishonor — prakṛti's oscillating expressions that disturb the unsteady mind — the 'binary toggling' of material existence

 

puruṣa-khyāti (n.) — direct perception of the soul; the discriminative knowledge that transforms theoretical Sāṅkhya into realized truth — 'seeing the source code directly'
