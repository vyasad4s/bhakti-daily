🏹 Bhagavad Gita 2.20 | March 31, 2026

 

न जायते म्रियते वा कदाचि-न्नायं भूत्वा भविता वा न भूयः ।

अजो नित्यः शाश्वतोऽयं पुराणो न हन्यते हन्यमाने शरीरे ॥ २० ॥

 

na jāyate mriyate vā kadācin
nāyaṁ bhūtvā bhavitā vā na bhūyaḥ
ajo nityaḥ śāśvato 'yaṁ purāṇo
na hanyate hanyamāne śarīre

 

For the soul there is neither birth nor death at any time. He has not come into being, does not come into being, and will not come into being. He is unborn, eternal, ever-existing and primeval. He is not slain when the body is slain.

 

What the full moon reveals tonight is what consciousness always already is.

The soul does not wax. The soul does not wane. The waxing of tithi from Pratipadā through Caturdaśī, culminating in Pūrṇimā at 9:36 PM tonight—this is a prakṛtic drama of illumination, not a pattern for the ātman. The moon appears to grow, appears to diminish, yet its actual mass remains unchanged. The misidentification is the trick: we take the reflection for the source, the appearance for the reality.

Kṛṣṇa speaks this to Arjuna as śāstra, but the teaching points to something that cannot be taught because it is already the case. Na jāyate mriyate vā—the double negation is not mere emphasis but structural: birth and death are dualistic constructs that apply only to prakṛti, only to the six-fold transformations the body undergoes (birth, growth, production of effects, dwindling, and finally naṣṭa, oblivion). The soul stands outside this chain entirely. Puruṣa has no history. No biography. No arrival. No departure.

This is why Vedanta calls the ātman kūṭa-stha—fixed at the pinnacle. The prefix kūṭa carries the sense of immovable, like a mountain summit unmoved by the weather that rages across its slopes. The body is weather. The soul is the summit itself.

And yet: we mourn. We contract. We build identities around the six-fold transformations as if they defined us. The childhood photograph, the aging face in the mirror, the fear of the last exhalation—each one a vṛtti in the citta, a cloud obscuring the sun that never ceased shining. Yoga begins here: with the recognition that the identification itself is the knot to be cut.

 

🔥 YS II.20: draṣṭā dṛśi-mātraḥ śuddho 'pi pratyayānupaśyaḥ — "The seer is merely the power of seeing; although pure, he witnesses the images of the mind."

Patañjali gives us the architecture. The draṣṭā, the seer, is puruṣa—pure consciousness devoid of content. He is dṛśi-mātraḥ: seeing-essence only, the capacity to illuminate without being stained by what is illuminated. This is precisely what Kṛṣṇa declares: śuddho 'pi—pure even while apparently entangled.

But here is the subtlety: the phrase pratyayānupaśyaḥ introduces the dynamic. The seer witnesses the contents of the mind—the pratyayas, the objects of consciousness—not as their creator or their victim, but as their silent observer. This is the practical bridge between bhakti and rāja-yoga. Bhakti directs attention to Kṛṣṇa as the supreme object; yoga withdraws attention from all objects to reveal the subject. Yet both arrive at the same threshold: the recognition that the one who chants, the one who sits in āsana, the one who reads these words—this apparent self is a constellation of pratyayas that will dissolve like moonlight at dawn, leaving only the witness that was never separate from what it witnessed.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

na jāyate — never takes birth; the denial of causal origination for the ātman

kadācit — at any time; temporal marker emphasizing eternity beyond past/present/future

ajaḥ — unborn; literally "not-born," the atemporal nature of puruṣa

śāśvataḥ — permanent, ever-existing; from śas (to leave residue), indicating that which remains when all changes cease

purāṇaḥ — the oldest, primeval, ancient; predating creation itself

hanyamāne śarīre — when the body is being killed; present participle emphasizing the simultaneity of body's death and soul's persistence
