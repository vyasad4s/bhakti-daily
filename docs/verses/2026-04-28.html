🏹 BG 4.6 | April 28, 2026

 

अजोऽपि सन्नव्ययात्मा भूतानामीश्वरोऽपि सन् ।

प्रकृतिं स्वामधिष्ठाय सम्भवाम्यात्ममायया ॥ ६ ॥

 

अजोऽपि सन्नव्ययात्मा
भूतानामीश्वरोऽपि सन्
प्रकृतिं स्वामधिष्ठाय
सम्भवाम्यात्ममायया

 

Although I am unborn, My transcendental body never deteriorates, and I am the Lord of all living entities, still I appear in every millennium in My original transcendental form.

 

The verse presents the ultimate paradox: the unborn appears, the undecaying manifests, the greatest becomes accessible. This is not a contradiction but the supreme mystery of the avatāra.

 

Ajaḥ api san — unborn and yet present. The Lord is not created by nature nor subject to time. He is āja, unborn, without beginning. Yet He appears. This is sambhava — manifestation of what already IS.

 

Avyayātmā — imperishable, never deteriorating. The Lord's body is not material; it doesn't age, decay, or dissolve. Unlike our bodies which cycle, His form is nitya (eternal) and sac-cid-ānanda.

 

Prakṛtiṁ svām adhiṣṭhāya — standing upon His own nature. The Lord does not enter material nature but stands upon His own prakṛti, His supreme energy. This is yoga-māyā which conceals yet reveals.

 

Sambhavāmi ātma-māyayā — I manifest by My internal potency. The ātma-māyā is the Lord's own power, not the external illusion that binds the jīva. It is the self-delight by which He plays while remaining fully Himself.

 

🔥 YS III.42 — kāya-rūpa-saṁyama-tat-grāhya-śakti-stambhe cakṣuḥ-prakāśa-saṁyogāntar-dhānam

"By saṁyama on the form, the power of light ceasing to reflect, invisibility is gained."

 

The siddhi of invisibility corresponds to the Lord's capacity to conceal and reveal — His divine form is ever-present but only visible when He wishes. The avatāra is the opposite: what was hidden becomes apparent.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

ajaḥ — unborn, without beginning; the absolute source

avyayātmā — imperishable self; transcendental body never deteriorating

īśvaraḥ — the Lord, the supreme controller

adhiṣṭhāya — standing upon, residing in

ātma-māyayā — by His internal potency/power
