🏹 BG 8.20 | 2026-06-29

 

परस्तस्मात्तु भावोऽन्योऽव्यक्तोऽव्यक्तात्सनातन: । य: स सर्वेषु भूतेषु नश्यत्सु न विनश्यति ॥ २० ॥

 

paras tasmāt tu bhāvo ’nyo ’vyakto ’vyaktāt sanātanaḥ yaḥ sa sarveṣu bhūteṣu naśyatsu na vinaśyati

 

Yet there is another unmanifest nature, which is eternal and is transcendental to this manifested and unmanifested matter. It is supreme and is never annihilated. When all in this world is annihilated, that part remains as it is.

 

This verse reveals the existence of a supreme, eternal reality that lies beyond both the manifest (vyakta) and unmanifest (avyakta) aspects of material nature. The manifest universe consists of the endless cycles of creation and dissolution—what Vedanta calls the days and nights of Brahmā—where all conditioned entities arise and fade. The unmanifest aspect referred to earlier in the chapter (the avyakta from which all beings emerge at the beginning of Brahmā’s day and into which they dissolve at night) is itself a subtle, potential state of prakṛti. Yet verse 8.20 declares that there is yet another level—a paraṁ bhāvaḥ, a transcendental nature—that is not only beyond these dual aspects but is also sanātana (eternal) and never subject to transformation or destruction. This transcendental nature is the internal potency of the Supreme Lord (cit‑śakti), the realm of pure consciousness where the jīva (individual soul) exists in its original, blissful, and eternal form as a servant of Kṛṣṇa. While the material energy (mahā‑māyā) undergoes perpetual transformation—manifestation, maintenance, and annihilation—the spiritual energy remains immutable, unaffected by the cosmic cycles. Even when the entire material universe is withdrawn into the body of Mahā‑Viṣṇu during the great dissolution (pralaya), this spiritual substratum persists unchanged, like the unaltered source code of a program that remains intact regardless of how many times the compiled binary is deleted and recompiled. From a Sāṅkhya viewpoint, the duality of puruṣa (consciousness) and prakṛti (matter) is extended here to include a third, superior category: the transcendental energy of the Lord that is neither puruṣa nor prakṛti in the mundane sense, but the potency through which both are sustained. The individual puruṣa, when situated in this transcendental nature through bhakti‑yoga, attains a state of permanent liberation (mukti) where there is no possibility of fall‑down, because the bond is one of loving service rather than mere knowledge. The verse’s declaration that this nature “is never annihilated” (na vinaśyati) assures the devotee that once established in this relationship, the connection is eternal and irreversible.

 

PRACTICE FOR TODAY

Contemplate the Eternal: Reflect on the distinction between the material unmanifest (which is still part of prakṛti) and the transcendental nature (cit-śakti). Realize that the former is temporary, while the latter is the only true home for the soul.

Fixed Remembrance: Throughout the day, try to hold a steady thought of Kṛṣṇa's form. Recognize that this remembrance is a link to the eternal, transcendental reality that survives all cosmic dissolutions.

Transcending the Cycles: Meditate on the idea that while the universe is created and destroyed repeatedly, your relationship with the Divine is immutable. Let this give you a sense of stability amidst the flux of daily life.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

avyakta (n.) – unmanifest; the subtle, potential state of material nature

sanātana (adj.) – eternal; that which does not undergo transformation or destruction

paraṁ bhāvaḥ (n.) – transcendental nature; the supreme, eternal reality beyond material existence

cit-śakti (n.) – spiritual potency; the internal energy of the Supreme Lord

jīva (n.) – individual soul; the eternal servant of Kṛṣṇa

pralaya (n.) – cosmic dissolution; the withdrawal of the universe into Mahā‑Viṣṇu

mukti (n.) – liberation; permanent release from material entanglement

viṣṇu (n.) – Mahā‑Viṣṇu; the first expansion who maintains the universe
