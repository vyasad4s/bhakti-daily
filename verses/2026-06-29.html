Bhagavad Gita Chapter 8, Verse 20

परस्तस्मात्तु भावोऽन्योऽव्यक्तोऽव्यक्तात्सनातन: । य: स सर्वेषु भूतेषु नश्यत्सु न विनश्यति ॥ २० ॥
paras tasmāt tu bhāvo ’nyo ’vyakto ’vyaktāt sanātanaḥ yaḥ sa sarveṣu bhūteṣu naśyatsu na vinaśyati
Yet there is another unmanifest nature, which is eternal and is transcendental to this manifested and unmanifested matter. It is supreme and is never annihilated. When all in this world is annihilated, that part remains as it is.
This verse reveals the existence of a supreme, eternal reality that lies beyond both the manifest (vyakta) and unmanifest (avyakta) aspects of material nature. The manifest universe consists of the endless cycles of creation and dissolution—what Vedanta calls the days and nights of Brahmā—where all conditioned entities arise and fade. The unmanifest aspect referred to earlier in the chapter (the avyakta from which all beings emerge at the beginning of Brahmā’s day and into which they dissolve at night) is itself a subtle, potential state of prakṛti. Yet verse 8.20 declares that there is yet another level—a paraṁ bhāvaḥ, a transcendental nature—that is not only beyond these dual aspects but is also sanātana (eternal) and never subject to transformation or destruction. This transcendental nature is the internal potency of the Supreme Lord (cit‑śakti), the realm of pure consciousness where the jīva (individual soul) exists in its original, blissful, and eternal form as a servant of Kṛṣṇa. While the material energy (mahā‑māyā) undergoes perpetual transformation—manifestation, maintenance, and annihilation—the spiritual energy remains immutable, unaffected by the cosmic cycles. Even when the entire material universe is withdrawn into the body of Mahā‑Viṣṇu during the great dissolution (pralaya), this spiritual substratum persists unchanged, like the unaltered source code of a program that remains intact regardless of how many times the compiled binary is deleted and recompiled. From a Sāṅkhya viewpoint, the duality of puruṣa (consciousness) and prakṛti (matter) is extended here to include a third, superior category: the transcendental energy of the Lord that is neither puruṣa nor prakṛti in the mundane sense, but the potency through which both are sustained. The individual puruṣa, when situated in this transcendental nature through bhakti‑yoga, attains a state of permanent liberation (mukti) where there is no possibility of fall‑down, because the bond is one of loving service rather than mere knowledge. The verse’s declaration that this nature “is never annihilated” (na vinaśyati) assures the devotee that once established in this relationship, the connection is eternal and irreversible. A fitting analogy from computer science is the distinction between volatile memory (RAM), non‑volatile storage (SSD/ROM), and the firmware etched into the hardware itself. The manifest universe resembles RAM—data that is constantly read, written, and erased with each clock cycle. The unmanifest (avyakta) is akin to firmware stored in flash memory: it persists across power cycles but can still be rewritten or corrupted. The transcendental nature described here, however, is like the hard‑wired logic gates of the CPU—an immutable foundation that defines the very architecture of computation. No amount of power cycling, reprogramming, or physical damage to volatile storage can alter this base layer; it is the bedrock upon which all operations depend. For the practitioner, the implication is clear: reliance on temporary, material configurations—even elevated spiritual states such as merging into the brahma‑jyotir—offers no guarantee of permanence. Only by establishing a direct, loving relationship with the Supreme Personality of Godhead, whose essence is this eternal transcendental nature, can one secure an enduring position that survives not only the dissolution of a single universe but the endless cycles of cosmic creation and annihilation. The chanting of the Hare Kṛṣṇa mahā‑mantra, which vibrates the transcendental sound, serves as the direct link to this unchanging reality, allowing the soul to tune its frequency to the eternal wavelength of the divine and thereby attain a state of lasting stability amidst the flux of existence.
