यदा संहरते चायं कूर्मोऽङ्गानीव सर्वशः ।

इन्द्रियाणीन्द्रियार्थेभ्यस्तस्य प्रज्ञा प्रतिष्ठिता ॥ ५८ ॥

 

yadā saṁharate cāyaṁ kūrmo 'ṅgānīva sarvaśaḥ
indriyāṇīndriyārthebhyas tasya prajñā pratiṣṭhitā

 

"One who is able to withdraw his senses from sense objects, as the tortoise draws its limbs within the shell, is firmly fixed in perfect consciousness."

 

Prabhupāda's purport cuts to the bone: "The test of a yogī, devotee or self-realized soul is that he is able to control the senses according to his plan. Most people, however, are servants of the senses." This is the Dhṛti yoga of today's panchāṅga — not suppression, but sovereignty. The senses are not the enemy. Compulsive servitude to them is.

The tortoise metaphor is precise. Notice: the tortoise does not amputate its limbs. It keeps them. It uses them when needed — to walk, to eat, to act. But it is not defined by them. Its essential nature is what remains when the limbs are withdrawn. So too the soul: not the body, not the senses, not even the mind's chatter, but the prajñā — the knowing that stands steady whether senses extend or retract.

This is the bridge between Bhakti and Raja Yoga that today's cosmic energy demands. On Kamada Ekādaśī, the devotee fasts not to torture the body, but to demonstrate: I am not this. The hunger comes and goes. The greeting to Viṣṇu remains. The body craves; the self observes. This is viveka in action — the discernment that knows puruṣa from prakṛti even in the field of sensation.

Aśleṣā nakṣatra's serpent energy is traditionally considered "difficult" — binding, entangling, producing the knots that keep awareness trapped in the subtle body. But the same serpent, when mastered, becomes the raised kuṇḍalinī. The Ekādaśī fast, practiced with awareness, becomes the pressure-release valve: the senses, starved of their usual fuel, have nothing to grasp, and the mind — for a precious window — remembers it can function without them.

Śanivār adds the final dimension: karma. Every sensory engagement plants seed. Every withdrawal withholds the rain of attention that would sprout it. The yogi on Saturday practices vairāgya not as philosophy but as damage control — stopping the production of new binding karma by stopping the compulsive response to sense-stimulus.

The result: pratiṣṭhitā prajñā — wisdom firmly established. Not the flickering insight of retreat, but the steady flame of one who can move through the world with senses extended, yet never lose the center.

 

🔥 Patañjali's Pratyāhāra: Here the disciplines converge. Kṛṣṇa's tortoise is Patañjali's pratyāhāra made visible. YS 2.54: svaviṣayāsamprayoge cittasya svarūpānukāra ivendriyāṇāṁ pratyāhāraḥ — "Pratyāhāra is the withdrawal of the senses from their objects by the mind's non-contact with them, corresponding to the mind's own nature." YS 2.55: tataḥ paramā vaśyatendriyāṇām — "From this comes the highest control of the senses."

The senses do not need to be restrained separately — when the mind is fixed, the senses withdraw automatically. This is not the ascetic's denial but the yogi's retractability: senses used only for service, withdrawn otherwise. The English word "retract" shares root with Sanskrit saṁ-hṛ: to draw together, to gather back. The tortoise does not amputate its limbs — it keeps them, deploys them when needed, but lives essentially inside, self-contained.

Vyāsa comments that the senses are "venomous serpents" — the Aśleṣā energy itself. They want to act loosely and without restriction. The yogi must be strong like a snake-charmer, never allowing the serpent-mind to strike independently. On Ekādaśī, this charisma is practiced through the liminal window: the world is neither rejected nor indulged, but transcended in place.

 

🏹 Practice for Today

For those fasting: the hunger itself is the teacher. When it arises, do not suppress — observe. Who is hungry? The body. Who observes the body? That which withdraws from the hunger as easily as from satisfaction.

For all: Practice the tortoise. Before engaging any sense-object today — a taste, a sound, a scroll through the feed — ask: Is this extension necessary? If not, retract. If yes, extend consciously. The goal is not to become a hermit. The goal is to become retractable: free to engage, free to withdraw, never compelled.

This is Kamada Ekādaśī's gift: the desire (kāma) for liberation becomes, on this day, accessible even to the conditioned soul. The senses that normally bind become the proof of what transcends them — when their objects are removed, the self remains, steady, watching, pratiṣṭhitā.

prajñā (noun) — discriminative wisdom; the steady consciousness that knows puruṣa from prakṛti. The debugger's eye that sees through the stack trace to the source.

pratiṣṭhitā (adj) — firmly established; rooted, unmoving. System status: daemon running, priority locked, uninterruptible.
