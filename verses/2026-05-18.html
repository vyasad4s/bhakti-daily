🏹 BG 6.1 | 2026-05-18

 

श्रीभगवानुवाच

अनाश्रित: कर्मफलं कार्यं कर्म करोति य: ।

स सन्न्यासी च योगी च न निरग्न‍िर्न चाक्रिय: ॥ १ ॥

 

śrī-bhagavān uvāca / anāśritaḥ karma-phalaṁ kāryaṁ karma karoti yaḥ / sa sannyāsī ca yogī ca na niragnir na cākriyaḥ

 

The Supreme Personality of Godhead said: One who is unattached to the fruits of his work and who works as he is obligated is in the renounced order of life, and he is the true mystic, not he who lights no fire and performs no duty.

 

Chapter 6 opens the Dhyāna Yoga — the yoga of meditation. But Kṛṣṇa doesn't begin with breath control or sitting postures. He begins with a surgical strike on the very definition of renunciation. The world thinks sannyāsa means quitting. Kṛṣṇa says the opposite: the real sannyāsī is the one who acts without attachment to results. The one who performs kāryaṁ karma — obligatory duty — while remaining anāśritaḥ karma-phalam — unsheltered by the fruits of action. This is not inaction dressed in ochre robes. This is action purified by detachment.

 

From the Sāṅkhya lens, this verse cuts to the root error: identifying the doer (ahaṅkāra) with the action (karma). When prakṛti acts through the gunas, the puruṣa merely witnesses. The "kāryaṁ karma" happens anyway — the body moves, the mind thinks, the senses engage. The question is whether you claim ownership of the output. The true yogī doesn't. They let prakṛti execute while puruṣa rests in awareness. This is the "zero-attachment" protocol — full engagement, zero proprietorship.

 

The final clause — "na niragnir na cākriyaḥ" — is devastating. The person who lights no fire and performs no duty is explicitly excluded from both sannyāsa and yoga. Cessation of activity is not renunciation; it is avoidance. The spiritual path requires engagement, not escape. You cannot debug the system by unplugging the machine.

 

🔥 YOGA SUTRA CONNECTION: YS 1.28 — tad-japas tad-artha-bhāvanam — The repetition of Om should be accompanied by contemplation of its meaning.

 

This sutra mirrors BG 6.1's core insight: mere mechanical performance — whether of japa or of karma — is insufficient. What matters is the bhāvanā, the contemplative awareness behind the action. The yogī repeats Om not as ritual but as conscious connection with Īśvara. Similarly, the karma-yogī performs duty not as obligation but as offering. In both cases, the outer form is identical to the world's action; the inner orientation is radically different. BG 6.1 gives the framework (act without claiming fruits); YS 1.28 gives the method (infuse each action with awareness of the Sacred). Together they describe the same state: full engagement, rooted in transcendence.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

Anāśritaḥ (adjective) — without taking shelter in; not relying upon; the state of not anchoring one's identity in the results of action

Karma-phalam (neuter noun) — the fruit of action; the result or outcome that binds the doer to saṃsāra when claimed as "mine"

Kāryam karma (neuter compound) — obligatory duty; prescribed work that must be performed regardless of personal desire

Sannyāsī (masculine noun) — one in the renounced order; here redefined by Kṛṣṇa as one who acts without attachment, not one who ceases action

Na niragniḥ (negative compound) — not without fire; one who does not abandon the fire of sacrifice and duty
