🏹 Bhagavad-gītā 4.1 | Monday, April 27, 2026

 

śrī-bhagavān uvāca

imaṁ vivasvate yogaṁ

proktavān aham avyayam

vivasvān manave prāha

manur ikṣvākave 'bravīt

 

śrī-bhagavān uvāca — the Supreme Personality of Godhead said; imam — this; vivasvate — unto the sun-god; yogam — the science of one's relationship to the Supreme; proktavān — instructed; aham — I; avyayam — imperishable; vivasvān — Vivasvān (the sun-god's name); manave — unto the father of mankind; prāha — told; manuḥ — the father of mankind; ikṣvākave — unto King Ikṣvāku; abravīt — said.

 

The Personality of Godhead, Lord Śrī Kṛṣṇa, said: I instructed this imperishable science of yoga to the sun-god, Vivasvān, and Vivasvān instructed it to Manu, the father of mankind, and Manu in turn instructed it to Ikṣvāku.

 

The Gītā does not begin with speculation—it begins with history. Not human history measured in centuries, but cosmic history stretching across millions of years. This verse establishes the paramparā, the disciplic succession, that carries spiritual knowledge like a torch passed from hand to hand across time. When the Lord says this science is avyayam—imperishable—He declares that it cannot be destroyed by time, diluted by interpretation, or corrupted by the mind's cleverness. It simply awaits rediscovery.

 

Today's cosmic configuration reflects this theme of restoration. We stand on Mohinī Ekādaśī, the day Vishnu assumed His enchanting Mohinī form to distribute nectar to the gods while denying it to the demons. Knowledge discriminates—between the deva and asura within us, between what sustains and what poisons. The fasting of Ekādaśī creates exactly this discrimination: we abstain from sensory gratification so the intellect can perceive clearly. The sun-god Vivasvān is the original recipient of this knowledge—the sun being the source of all light, physical and spiritual.

 

The verse traces an unbroken chain: Kṛṣṇa to Vivasvān, Vivasvān to Manu, Manu to Ikṣvāku. This is not mythology—it is methodology. Real knowledge must be received from one who has realized it. The Bhagavad-gītā was lost through the break in this succession, and Kṛṣṇa speaks it again to Arjuna to reestablish the line. Five thousand years later, we are the beneficiaries of that restoration.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

paramparā (n.) — The disciplic succession; the traditional method of transmitting spiritual knowledge from guru to disciple. Unlike academic knowledge that can be obtained from books alone, transcendental knowledge must be received through this living chain to maintain its potency. BG 4.2 states this succession was broken and the science "appears to be lost"—though it was only hidden, awaiting the proper vessel.

avyaya (adj.) — Imperishable, inexhaustible, eternal. Unlike material knowledge which degrades, is forgotten, or becomes obsolete, spiritual knowledge is avyaya—it maintains its full potency regardless of time. The sun has been shining for billions of years without diminishment; this science is equally inexhaustible.

vivasvān (n.) — The sun-god, name of the present deity presiding over the sun planet. In every millennium, the sun has a different deity; in the current age, he is Vivasvān. The sun represents the eye of the Lord and traverses his orbit under the Lord's order.

 

🔥 YS 1.23: īśvara-praṇidhānād vā — "Or [samādhi is achieved] by devotion to Īśvara [the Supreme Lord]." Patañjali offers multiple paths to cessation of mental fluctuations. Among them is īśvara-praṇidhāna—surrender to the Supreme. BG 4.1 reveals what this devotion connects to: not an abstract concept, but a personal Being who instructs, cares about transmission, and re-establishes lost knowledge. The paramparā is the practical mechanism of īśvara-praṇidhāna.

 

🔥 YS 2.1: tapas-svādhyāyeśvara-praṇidhānāni kriyā-yogaḥ — "Discipline, self-study, and devotion to God comprise Kriyā Yoga." The threefold practice corresponds to BG 4.1: tapas (the austerity of receiving knowledge), svādhyāya (hearing from authority), and īśvara-praṇidhāna (humility to be instructed).
