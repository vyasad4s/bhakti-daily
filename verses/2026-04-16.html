Bhagavad Gita 3.27

The Anatomy of False Claim

प्रकृतेः क्रियमाणानि गुणैः कर्माणि सर्वशः ।

अहङ्कारविमूढात्मा कर्ताहमिति मन्यते ॥ २७ ॥

prakṛteḥ kriyamāṇāni guṇaiḥ karmāṇi sarvaśaḥ
ahaṅkāra-vimūḍhātmā kartāham iti manyate

Translation: The spirit soul bewildered by the influence of false ego thinks himself the doer of activities that are in actuality carried out by the three modes of material nature.

— Bhagavad Gita 3.27, as it is

🕉️ Commentary

Kṛṣṇa's instruction here is not gentle contemplation — it is dismantling surgery. The verse operates like a debugger that reveals where the bug lives: between kartā (doer) and aham (I). The bewildered soul inserts himself into transactions carried out by prakṛti's guṇas. This is not blame — it is diagnosis.

The Sāṅkhya is precise here. Prakṛti is the material matrix, the three-guṇa substance that churns out all manifestation. Puruṣa is pure consciousness, the witness, never actor, never acted upon. Between them stands ahaṅkāra — the ego-function that appropriates experience, that transforms "awareness of action" into "I am acting." This is the only wrong in the system: not the action, not the results, but the false watermark of identity stamped on both.

Consider the mechanism. You decide to raise your arm. Nerve signals fire. Muscles contract. The arm rises. The ego says: "I raised my arm." But trace the chain: where did the decision emerge? From conditioning, from prāṇa, from guṇas in motion. The consciousness never moved — it only witnessed what prakṛti enacted. This is vimoha — the delusion that conflates witnessing with authorship.

The implications are devastating for spiritual materialism. You cannot keep score of your sādhana. You cannot claim "I meditated for an hour" as an achievement. You cannot accumulate spiritual credits. There is no accumulator. There is only action happening, witnessed. This is why āsana becomes yoga only when the practitioner stops performing and starts witnessing. The body moves. Breath moves. Mind observes. None of it is "mine."

On this Amāvāsyā, when the moon dissolves and the day's energies pull toward internalization, the practice is radical: track every "I" that arises. I am doing this. I achieved that. I need this. I want that. Each one is ahaṅkāra-vimoha. Each one is false attribution. Each one is the soul asleep, dreaming it is the dreamer.

🔬 Key Terms

prakṛti
"nature, matrix" — The primordial material substance. Code analogy: The operating system, the platform, the material substrate upon which all apps run. It is not conscious — it only appears conscious when reflecting puruṣa's light.
guṇa
"strand, quality" — The three constituent qualities of prakṛti: sattva (lucidity), rajas (activity), and tamas (inertia). Code analogy: The three basic states of any system — processing, clarity, or stasis.
ahaṅkāra
"I-maker" — The ego-function that creates identification, appropriating experience as "mine." Code analogy: A recursive function that calls itself until stack overflow.
vimoha
"delusion, bewilderment" — The state of being confused by false identification. Code analogy: A process that thinks it's the kernel when it's actually just a user-space application.
kartā
"doer, agent" — The subject performing action. In reality, only prakṛti through the guṇas is the agent. Code analogy: The user thinking they compiled the code when actually the compiler did all the work.
manyate
"thinks, imagines" — From man, the root of mind. The ego's primary activity: constructing narratives of authorship. Code analogy: The frontend displaying a dashboard the user thinks represents causation.

📿 Yoga Sūtra: YS II.1

tapaḥ-svādhyāyeśvara-praṇidhānāni kriyā-yogaḥ
Austerity, self-study, and surrender to Īśvara constitute the practice of yoga.

Patañjali offers the antidote: tapas burns identification, svādhyāya reveals puruṣa/prakṛti distinction, praṇidhāna redirects the ego's claim toward divine surrender.

⚡ Practice: Ego-Debugging

Not suppression — watching. Observe the "I" claims:

  • "I am hungry" → Who is aware of hunger?
  • "I am doing japa" → Who witnesses the mantra?
  • "I need rest" → Who notices fatigue?

Prakṛti acts. You witness.
