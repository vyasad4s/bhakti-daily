🏹 BG 7.13 | 2026-06-10

 

त्रिभिर्गुणमयैर्भावैरेभि: सर्वमिदं जगत् ।

मोहितं नाभिजानाति मामेभ्य: परमव्ययम् ॥ १३ ॥

tribhir guṇa-mayair bhāvair ebhiḥ sarvam idaṁ jagat mohitaṁ nābhijānāti mām ebhyaḥ param avyayam

Deluded by the three modes [goodness, passion and ignorance], the whole world does not know Me, who am above the modes and inexhaustible.

Commentary

This is one of the most devastating statements in the entire Gītā. Kṛṣṇa does not say "some people are deluded" or "certain cultures are confused." He says sarvam idaṁ jagat — the entire world. Every living entity, every civilization, every philosophy that operates within the three modes is mohitam — enchanted, bewitched, under a spell. This includes the mode of goodness. The brāhmaṇa absorbed in Vedic study is as deluded as the śūdra lost in drink — only the flavor of delusion differs. Sattva says "I am pure." Rajas says "I am powerful." Tamas says "I don't care." All three are walls of the same prison.

And what is the content of this delusion? Nābhijānāti mām — "they do not know Me." The delusion is not about ethics or productivity or consciousness — it is about the Lord. The three modes prevent the recognition of Kṛṣṇa as the Supreme Person beyond prakṛti. The modes are prakṛti's operating system; Kṛṣṇa is the programmer. The user who only ever interacts with the application layer — no matter how sophisticated their use — has no idea what the kernel contains. Mām ebhyaḥ param avyayam — He is beyond these modes, inexhaustible, imperishable. The modes cycle, combine, recombine. The Lord does not cycle. He is the changeless witness of all change.

Today's Ekādaśī transition at 3:28 PM is the living praxis of this verse. The first half of the day (Dashami) represents the world operating normally under the three modes — completing material tasks, fulfilling obligations, running the default program. The second half (Ekādaśī) represents the conscious interruption of that program — fasting, withdrawing from sense gratification, turning the awareness toward what is beyond the modes. Ekādaśī is not a ritual; it is a system interrupt. It is the deliberate suspension of the three modes' grip, a proof-of-concept that the living entity is not the modes but the witness of them.

Yoga Sūtra Bridge: YS II.18

प्रकाशक्रियास्थितिशीलं भूतेन्द्रियात्मकं भोगापवर्गार्थं दृश्यम्

prakāśa-kriyā-sthiti-śīlaṁ bhūta-indriya-ātmakaṁ bhoga-apavarga-arthaṁ dṛśyam

The seen (prakṛti) has the qualities of luminosity, activity, and inertia, is constituted of elements and sense organs, and exists for the purpose of experience and liberation.

BG 7.13 and YS II.18 describe the same architecture from opposite angles. Kṛṣṇa names the three modes that delude the world — sattva (luminosity/prakāśa), rajas (activity/kriyā), and tamas (inertia/sthiti). Patañjali names these exact three qualities as the fundamental properties of dṛśyam (the seen, prakṛti): prakāśa-kriyā-sthiti-śīlam. The Gītā declares that these three modes conceal the Lord who is beyond them. The Yoga Sūtras declare that prakṛti exists for two purposes: bhoga (experience) and apavarga (liberation). When the world is seen only as bhoga — as experience to be consumed — it becomes moha, delusion. When the same three modes are recognized as the machinery of prakṛti operating for the puruṣa's liberation, the delusion breaks. The transition from bhoga to apavarga is the moment when one moves from "deluded by the modes" to "knowing the Lord beyond the modes." This is why Ekādaśī matters: it is the conscious withdrawal from bhoga (eating, sense gratification) toward apavarga (fasting, spiritual focus). Ekādaśī is the embodied praxis of YS II.18's promise — that the three modes exist not merely for experience but for liberation.

TERM DEFINITIONS:

tribhiḥ — By three. Like the three fundamental system states — sattva (read/execute), rajas (write/modify), tamas (sleep/hibernate) — that govern all processes in the cosmic operating system.

guṇa-mayaiḥ — Consisting of the modes of nature. Like the three permission layers that compose every file in the system — no file exists outside the read-write-execute triad. The entire filesystem is guṇa-maya.

mohitam — Deluded, bewildered, enchanted. Like a process trapped in an infinite loop — it executes instructions but cannot see the shell that launched it. The loop feels like the whole system; it is not.

abhijānāti — Does not know, does not recognize. Like a user who runs applications daily but has no idea what the kernel does — they interact with the surface, never the source.

param — Supreme, beyond, transcendental. Like the hypervisor that sits above all virtual machines — it is not subject to any guest OS's rules or states.

avyayam — Inexhaustible, imperishable. Like the power supply that never drains — it is the source from which all system resources flow, and it is never diminished by what it powers.
