🏹 BG 4.37-4.38 | May 2, 2026

 

यथैधांसि समिद्धोऽग्न‍िर्भस्मसात्कुरुतेऽर्जुन ।

ज्ञानाग्न‍िः सर्वकर्माणि भस्मसात्कुरुते तथा ॥ ३७ ॥

न हि ज्ञानेन सदृशं पवित्रमिह विद्यते ।

तत्स्वयं योगसंसिद्धः कालेनात्मनि विन्दति ॥ ३८ ॥

 

yathāidhāṁsi samiddho ’gnir bhasma-sāt kurute ’rjuna
jñānāgniḥ sarva-karmāṇi bhasma-sāt kurute tathā

na hi jñānena sadṛśaṁ pavitram iha vidyate
tat svayaṁ yoga-saṁsiddhaḥ kālenātmani vindati

 

Translation: As a blazing fire turns firewood to ashes, O Arjuna, so does the fire of knowledge burn to ashes all reactions to material activities. In this world, there is nothing so sublime and pure as transcendental knowledge. Such knowledge is the mature fruit of all mysticism. And one who has become accomplished in the practice of devotional service enjoys this knowledge within himself in due course of time.

 

This is the core promise of Jñāna Yoga—the path of spiritual knowledge. Most spiritual systems offer accumulation: more scripture, more wisdom, more "attainment." But Kṛṣṇa’s method is subtraction. The "firewood" of our accumulated karma—past actions, attachments, and ego—cannot be healed by adding more piety. It can only be transmuted by fire. The fire of knowledge here is Tat-tvam-asi—realizing your identity as the witness (puruṣa) disentangles you from the drama of prakriti.

 

The imagery is deliberate: Agni is the god of transformation—not preservation. When this fire burns, nothing remains but ashes. The ash is the ego-structure dissolved, and in that dissolution, the practitioner finds the final purity (pavitram) that science and religion alike seek.

 

🔥 Yoga Sutra Connection: This incineration is the essence of Pantajali's Kaivalya Pada, 4.29-4.30, where one loses interest in the gunas, attains dharma-megha samādhi, and dissolves the kleshas. In both systems, liberation is not an acquisition—it is a combustive realization that "I am not this body, I am not even this designation."
