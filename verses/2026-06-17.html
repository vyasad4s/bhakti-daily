🏹 BG 8.9 | 2026-06-17

 

कविं पुराणमनुशासितार- मणोरणीयांसमनुस्मरेद्य: । सर्वस्य धातरमचिन्त्यरूप- मादित्यवर्णं तमस: परस्तात् ॥ ९ ॥

 

kavim purāṇam anuśāsitāram
aṇor aṇīyāṁsam anusmared yaḥ
sarvasya dhātāram acintya-rūpam
āditya-varṇaṁ tamasaḥ parastāt

 

One should meditate upon the Supreme Person as the one who knows everything, as He who is the oldest, who is the controller, who is smaller than the smallest, who is the maintainer of everything, who is beyond all material conception, who is inconceivable, and who is always a person. He is luminous like the sun, and He is transcendental, beyond this material nature.

 

From the Sankhya perspective, puruṣa is pure consciousness, attributeless and inactive, yet the scriptures ascribe attributes to aid meditation (upāsana). This verse enumerates such attributes: omniscience (kavi), primal origin (purāṇam), supreme controller (anuśāsitāram), subtler than the subtlest (aṇor aṇīyāṁsam), sustainer of all (sarvasya dhātāram), beyond material conception (acintya), luminous like the sun (āditya-varṇam), and transcendental person (tamasaḥ parastāt). Meditating on these qualities (anusmara) turns the mind inward, loosening identification with the body-mind complex (prakṛti). As the Yoga Sutra affirms, devotion to Īśvara is a direct path to samādhi (YS I.23). Thus the verse serves as both object of meditation and reminder of the practitioner’s true nature as puruṣa, distinct yet dependent on the conscious principle.

 

🔥 Yoga Sūtra Connection: YS I.23 Īśvara-praṇidhānāt vā Or [samādhi is attained] by devotion to the Lord
