🏹 Bhagavad-gītā 3.1 | February 24, 2026

 

अर्जुन उवाच

ज्यायसी चेत्कर्मणस्ते मता बुद्धिर्जनार्दन

तत्किं कर्मणि घोरे मां नियोजयसि केशव

 

arjuna uvāca
jyāyasī cet karmaṇas te matā buddhir janārdana
tat kiṁ karmaṇi ghore māṁ niyojayasi keśava

 

"Arjuna said: O Janārdana, if You consider that knowledge is better than action, why then do You urge me to engage in this terrible action?"

 

The question of action versus knowledge—this is where most seeker's stumble. After the profound establishment of Chapter 2's wisdom (brahmī-sthitiḥ), Arjuna returns to pragmatic confusion: if I'm to realize my true nature, why fight? Why act at all?

 

Rohinī energy today—the star of growth, the nurturing embrace of the divine feminine. Prajāpati presides. This is not the fire of Kṛttikā but the steady, stable growth of the nurturing principle. Aṣṭamī is the eighth day—protection, preservation, the maternal energy holding what has been established. The question Arjuna asks is the question of the householder, the practitioner with responsibilities: how do I maintain my inner realization while engaging in outer action?

 

Karmaṇi ghore—in terrible, fearsome action. The battlefield. The difficult conversation. The hard decision. Arjuna doesn't want to escape to a cave; he wants clarity on HOW to act with wisdom. This is the bridge between renunciation (Chapter 2) and engaged action (Chapter 3).

 

🔥 YS 2.1 — tapas-svādhyāyeśvara-praṇidhānāni kriyā-yogaḥ — "Kriyā Yoga consists of discipline, self-study, and devotion to God."

 

The answer to Arjuna's question: action transformed by wisdom becomes Yoga in action. Not escape from life but engagement with transformed awareness.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

Jyāyasī (comparative) — better, superior; the comparative of jyāya (elder/greater)

Mata (feminine noun) — thought, opinion, doctrine; from man (to think)

Ghora (adjective) — terrible, dreadful, fierce; the quality of the battlefield

Niyojayasi (verb, causative) — you cause to engage, you employ; from yuj (to yoke)

Keśava (vocative) — O Kṛṣṇa; keśa (hair) + va (possessor); literally "one with beautiful hair"
