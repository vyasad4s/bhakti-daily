Bhagavad Gita Chapter 5, Verse 1

अर्जुन उवाच सन्न्यासं कर्मणां कृष्ण पुनर्योगं च शंससि । यच्छ्रेय एतयोरेकं तन्मे ब्रूहि सुनिश्चितम् ॥ १ ॥
arjuna uvāca sannyāsaṁ karmaṇāṁ kṛṣṇa punar yogaṁ ca śaṁsasi yac chreya etayor ekaṁ tan me brūhi su-niścitam
Arjuna said: O Kṛṣṇa, first of all You ask me to renounce work, and then again You recommend work with devotion. Now will You kindly tell me definitely which of the two is more beneficial?

This verse captures the moment of pure discernment (viveka) before action. Arjuna, situated in buddhi (intelligence), does not speculate or assume—he seeks definitive knowledge (niścaya) from the source of truth, Kṛṣṇa. His question is not theoretical but existential: which path truly leads to the supreme goal? This mirrors the Sankhyan principle that liberation arises from discriminating between puruṣa (the conscious self) and prakṛti (the material modes), not from blind adherence to prescribed roles.

Cosmically, today’s Pratipadā tithi (first lunar day) and Chandra Darśana (first sight of the waxing moon) symbolize the initial impulse of creation—the point where unmanifest potential begins to stir. Just as the moon begins its luminous ascent from darkness, the spiritual practitioner begins with a question, not an answer. The verse asks us to pause at this threshold, to engage our buddhi in rigorous inquiry before committing to action or renunciation.

From the terminal of the Sankhya-Yoga system, we see that both sannyāsa (renunciation) and karma‑yoga (action in devotion) are modifications of prakṛti. The binding factor is not the external form of activity but the internal state of attachment (saṅga) or detachment (vairagya). Arjuna’s question implicitly seeks the criteria for discriminating between actions that bind and actions that liberate—a discrimination that can only arise when the intellect is established in sattva (purity) and guided by the inner witness (puruṣa).

Thus, the verse is not a mere historical exchange; it is an algorithm for sadhana: when faced with competing paths, return to the source of truth, interrogate with a desire for definitive knowledge, and let the answer arise from the clarity of a mind established in sameness (samatā)—the very state celebrated later in this chapter (BG 5.19). Today’s cosmic energy supports this pause: the waxing moon’s first light invites us to begin not with blind action, but with clear seeing.
