अर्जुन उवाच सन्न्यासं कर्मणां कृष्ण पुनर्योगं च शंससि । यच्छ्रेय एतयोरेकं तन्मे ब्रूहि सुनिश्चितम् ॥ १ ॥

 

arjuna uvāca sannyāsaṁ karmaṇāṁ kṛṣṇa punar yogaṁ ca śaṁsasi yac chreya etayor ekaṁ tan me brūhi su-niścitam

 

Translation: Arjuna said: O Kṛṣṇa, first of all You ask me to renounce work, and then again You recommend work with devotion. Now will You kindly tell me definitely which of the two is more beneficial?

 

This verse represents the "T=0" moment of spiritual discernment. Arjuna is not merely asking a question; he is experiencing a collision of two operational modes: the path of total cessation (Sannyāsa) and the path of integrated action (Karma-yoga). From a first-principles perspective, this confusion is a modification of the buddhi (intellect) still entangled in the dualities of prakṛti. Arjuna sees the paths as contradictory because he is still viewing "action" as a binding force. Cosmically, today is Pratipadā—the first lunar day—and Chandra Darśana, the first sighting of the waxing moon. This is the "Zero-Point" of the lunar cycle, the exact moment where unmanifest potential begins its ascent back into light. Just as the moon emerges from the void of Amāvāsyā, the practitioner must emerge from the void of doubt. Arjuna's inquiry is the spiritual equivalent of this first sliver of light: it is the impulse of viveka (discrimination) beginning to pierce through the darkness of moha (delusion). The "superior path" is not found in the external form of the activity—whether one sits in a cave or leads an army—but in the internal state of the Sāksin (the Witness). The superior path is the one that transforms the material machine of prakṛti into a transparent instrument for puruṣa. By asking "which is more beneficial," Arjuna is seeking the algorithm for liberation: the method by which action ceases to be a source of karma (reaction) and becomes a vehicle for mukti (freedom). Today's energy supports this initiation—beginning not with blind activity, but with the sharp, disciplined inquiry of the soul.
