🏹 Bhagavad-gītā 3.8 | April 12, 2026

 

नियतं कुरु कर्म त्वं कर्म ज्यायो ह्यकर्मणः ।

शरीरयात्रापि च ते न प्रसिद्ध्येदकर्मणः ॥

 

niyataṁ kuru karma tvaṁ karma jyāyo hy akarmaṇaḥ
śarīra-yātrāpi ca te na prasidhyed akarmaṇaḥ

 

Perform your prescribed duty, for doing so is better than not working. One cannot even maintain one's physical body without work.

 

The Gītā continues its surgical strike against spiritual pretension. Arjuna has just discovered that the path of jñāna (knowledge) appears to offer an elegant exit from his dilemma — why kill anyone when all is one, when the eternal soul cannot be slain? It sounds sophisticated. It sounds advanced. It is also completely wrong for him.

 

Kṛṣṇa's response cuts through the sophistry like a blade. Niyatam kuru karma tvam — "perform your prescribed duty." Not any duty. Not someone else's duty. Not the duty you imagine a more enlightened person might have. Your duty, Arjuna — the duty burning inside you right now, the duty encoded in your station, your time, your capacity.

 

The verse delivers a devastatingly practical irrefutable: even your body's maintenance demands action. You cannot opt out. Renunciation without purification is just parasitism wearing spiritual clothing. The pseudo-yogī who sits in meditation funded by someone else's labor trades one illusion for another.

 

Karma jyāyo hy akarmaṇaḥ — work is better than non-work. This is not a platitude about productivity. This is recognition that the gunas are in motion, that prakṛti's machinery is churning, that standing still is not an option. The only question is whether you steer consciously or are thrown around by forces you pretend aren't there.

 

🔥 YS II.1: tapas-svādhyāya-īśvara-praṇidhānāni kriyā-yogaḥ — "Austerity, self-study, and surrender to God are the kriyā-yoga." Patañjali's definition is not an alternative to karma-yoga — it is its internal architecture. The triple engine transforms mechanical action into liberating action. Tapas (discipline) corresponds to niyatam — prescribed duty. Svādhyāya (self-inquiry) prevents ritualism. Īśvara-praṇidhāna (surrender) is the escape velocity mechanism. Without these three, karma produces bondage. With them, the same actions become the runway for takeoff.

 

Practice: Today, identify one obligation you are currently avoiding through spiritual rationalization. The pattern matters more than the content — the tendency to use "I'm working on myself" as cover for aversion. Do it. The prescribed action is whatever is in front of you. Before you can transcend action, you must master it.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

niyatam (masc. acc. sg.) — prescribed, ordained, regulated; from ni-yam (to restrain, regulate). Compiled binary of duty — specific, non-negotiable code for your system.

karma (neuter nom./acc. sg.) — action, work; derived from kṛ (to do). System state: prakṛti's perpetual motion.

jyāyaḥ (masc. nom. sg. comp.) — better, superior; from ji (to conquer). Comparative advantage: right work trumps attempted transcendence.

śarīra-yātrā (fem. nom. sg.) — bodily maintenance; śarīra (body) + yātrā (journey). The minimum viable product: even the avatar requires resource allocation.

prasidhyet (3rd sg. optative) — would be accomplished; from pra-sidh. Execution status: without input, no output.
