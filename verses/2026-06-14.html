🏹 BG 7.16 | 2026-06-14

 

चतुर्विधा भजन्ते मां जना: सुकृतिनोऽर्जुन । > आर्तो जिज्ञासुरर्थार्थी ज्ञानी च भरतर्षभ ॥ १६ ॥

 

catur-vidhā bhajante māṁ janāḥ su-kṛtino ’rjuna ārto jijñāsur arthārthī jñānī ca bharata-ṛṣabha

 

O best among the Bhāratas, four kinds of pious men begin to render devotional service unto Me – the distressed, the desirer of wealth, the inquisitive, and he who is searching for knowledge of the Absolute.

 

Today’s pañcāṅga marks Amavasya (new moon) during the Krishna Paksha of Jyeshta, a time when the moon is invisible, symbolizing introspection, release, and the seed of new beginnings. The Nakṣatra Rohini (ruled by Brahma, god of creation) governs until 12:43 PM IST, nurturing growth and creativity, transitioning to Mrigashirsha (ruled by Soma, the moon) after, bringing curiosity and exploration. The Yoga Soola (pain, torment) prevails until 11:25 PM IST, highlighting the discomfort of material existence, followed by Ganda (obstacle, mischief) yoga, which reminds us that spiritual progress faces challenges. The Sun and Moon in Taurus (until evening) provide grounded stability, while the shift to Gemini later invites mental agility.

In this context, BG 7.16 beautifully describes the four categories of sincere seekers who approach the Lord: the distressed (ārtā) seeking relief from suffering, the desirer of wealth (arthārthī) seeking material prosperity, the inquisitive (jijñāsu) seeking knowledge, and the seeker of absolute knowledge (jñānī) seeking liberation. Amavasya’s darkness mirrors the inner state of the ārtā—those tormented by material pain who turn to the Lord as their refuge. Rohini’s creative energy supports the arthārthī’s desire for growth and abundance, while Mrigashirsha’s exploratory nature fuels the jijñāsu’s quest for understanding. The Soola yoga’s anguish is transmuted through devotion, and Ganda’s obstacles are overcome by persistent jñāna (knowledge) of the Absolute.

The Lord welcomes all four, not rejecting any because of their material motivations, for even a desire for wealth or knowledge can be purified through association with Him. As the verse declares, these are su-kṛtinaḥ—those who follow scriptural principles—and their initial motives, though mixed, become stepping stones to pure bhakti. The Amavasya sky, devoid of moonlight, reminds us that true light comes from within: the Lord’s presence in the heart. Just as the moon will wax again after this night, so too can our devotion grow from humble beginnings.

 

🔥 Yoga Sūtra Connection: YS 1.23 ईश्वरप्रणिधानात वा īśvara-praṇidhānād vā Or, this previously mentioned state is attainable from devotion to the lord. BG 7.16 and YS 1.23 both emphasize that devotion to the Lord is a means to attain higher states. In the Gītā, Krishna affirms that He accepts the service of all sincere seekers, regardless of their initial motivation, and gradually purifies their hearts. In the Yoga Sūtras, īśvara-praṇidhāna (devotion to the Lord) is presented as a direct path to samādhi. The connection lies in the understanding that when we offer our actions and aspirations to the Lord—whether seeking relief, wealth, knowledge, or liberation—He transforms our motivation into pure love. Thus, the practice of devotion to Īśvara is the inner attitude that BG 7.16 describes from the Lord’s side: He welcomes all who come to Him with sincerity.
