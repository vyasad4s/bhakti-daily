🏹 BG 2.28 | April 2, 2026

 

अव्यक्तादीनि भूतानि व्यक्तमध्यानि भारत ।

अव्यक्तनिधनान्येव तत्र का परिदेवना ॥

 

avyaktādīni bhūtāni vyakta-madhyāni bhārata

avyakta-nidhanāny eva tatra kā paridevanā

 

All created beings are unmanifest in their beginning, manifest in their interim state, and unmanifest again when annihilated. So what need is there for lamentation?

 

Kṛṣṇa is being deliberately thorough here. He has already established the soul as indestructible, unborn, eternal, and immutable. Now He addresses Arjuna from another angle: even if you reject the soul's existence entirely—even if you are a materialist who believes consciousness is merely chemical interaction—still, you have no reason to lament. Why? Because matter itself follows the same cycle.

 

The materialist cannot argue with conservation of energy. Before the skyscraper existed, its constituent elements were diffused in earth, water, fire. After the building's demolition, those same elements return to their unmanifest state. The form was temporary; the substance was conserved. Whether one accepts a transcendental soul or denies it, the pattern remains: manifestation, duration, dissolution. This is not philosophy—this is observable physics.

 

But here is where Patañjali and the Sāṅkhya tradition sharpen the point. The manifested form (vyakta) is prakṛti under the influence of the guṇas. The unmanifest source (avyakta) is prakṛti in equilibrium, before the churning begins. The soul (puruṣa) witnesses both states but belongs to neither. Arjuna is lamenting the destruction of forms—Bhīṣma's form, Droṇa's form, his cousins' forms. But forms are precisely what undergo the cycle Kṛṣṇa describes. To grieve for a form is to grieve for a garment that was always destined to be changed.

 

🔥 YOGA SŪTRA CONNECTION:

 

YS I.4: vṛtti-sārūpyam itaratra — "Otherwise, at other times, [the seer] is absorbed in the changing states [of the mind]."

 

Patañjali describes the fundamental error that generates all lamentation: the soul's apparent absorption in prakṛti's transformations. The vṛttis—the mental fluctuations that perceive birth and death, gain and loss—are modifications of prakṛti, not of puruṣa. Yet when consciousness reflects through the mind's agitations, the soul appears to experience those changes as its own.

 

Kṛṣṇa and Patañjali harmonize here. For the yogī practicing sāṁyama on the distinction between seer and seen (YS III.35), the drama of manifestation/unmanifestation becomes a movie projected on the screen of awareness. The yogī does not lament when a character dies in a film, knowing it's light and shadow.

 

🔥 PRACTICE FOR TODAY:

 

Morning: Upon waking, observe your body with this understanding. It was unmanifest in deep sleep, manifests now in waking, will become unmanifest again in tonight's sleep—and eventually in death. Witness without drama.

 

Throughout the day: When you encounter any form you're attached to—person, possession, status—mentally trace its avyakta-vyakta cycle. Where was this before it appeared? Where will it be after it disappears?

 

Evening: Contemplate what remains when all vyakta dissolves. Not nothing—a witness remains.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

 

avyakta (n.) — "unmanifest, invisible, latent" — the state of prakṛti before guṇic differentiation, or after dissolution. Like code existing as potential before compilation. The source from which all forms emerge.

 

vyakta (n.) — "manifest, visible, expressed" — prakṛti under active guṇic modification, the material world as experienced. The compiled program running.

 

paridevanā (n.) — "lamentation, excessive wailing" — grief based on the delusion of loss; theatrical mourning. Kṛṣṇa's question is rhetorical: when did you ever actually lose anything?

 

bhūtāni (n., plural) — "created beings, elements" — from √bhū (to be, to become). All things that come into existence through the cycle of manifestation.
