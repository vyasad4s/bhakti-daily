🏹 BG 2.70 | March 18, 2026

 

आपूर्यमाणमचलप्रतिष्ठं

समुद्रमापः प्रविशन्ति यद्वत् ।

तद्वत्कामा यं प्रविशन्ति सर्वे

स शान्तिमाप्नोति न कामकामी ॥ ७० ॥

 

āpūryamāṇam acala-pratiṣṭhaṁ

samudram āpaḥ praviśanti yadvat

tadvat kāmā yaṁ praviśanti sarve

sa śāntim āpnoti na kāma-kāmī

 

A person who is not disturbed by the incessant flow of desires—that enter like rivers into the ocean, which is ever being filled but is always still—can alone achieve peace, and not the man who strives to satisfy such desires.

 

The ocean metaphor completes the sequence of verses on sense restraint and inner stability. The kṛṣṇa-conscious person is like the ocean—always complete, never lacking. Rivers of desire flow in continuously, yet the ocean remains undisturbed, unagitated, not seeking more. This is completeness that needs nothing.

 

The term āpūryamāṇam is precise—"being filled continuously." The ocean doesn't say "no more" to the rivers; it receives them without being changed by them. The yogī doesn't block the senses but becomes so full that sense objects cannot disturb the fullness. This is not sensory deprivation but sensory transcendence through inner abundance.

 

Śānti—peace—is not the absence of disturbance but the presence of completeness. The one who runs after kāma (desires) is kāma-kāmī—the desire-desirer, locked in the feedback loop of 2.62-63. The ocean-like person has achieved what vairāgya promises: contentment independent of external conditions.

 

🔥 YS II.42 — saṁtoṣād anuttamaḥ sukha-lābhaḥ

"From contentment, unsurpassed happiness is obtained."

 

Patañjali's saṁtoṣa (contentment) is one of the niyamas—cultivated satisfaction with what one has. The ocean's contentment is active, not passive. It doesn't reject the rivers; it integrates them without chasing them. BG 2.70 gives the metaphysical foundation: the ocean's fullness is natural, not acquired. The yogī discovers an analogous fullness in ātman—always complete, always still, regardless of what flows in.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

āpūryamāṇam (present passive participle) — being filled continuously; from ā-pṛ (to fill completely) — receiving without depletion, the natural state of the complete

acala-pratiṣṭham (compound adjective) — immovably established; acala (unmoving) + pratiṣṭhā (foundation) — root access no process can revoke, the unshakable platform

samudra (masculine noun) — ocean, the gathering of waters; sam (together) + udra (water/place of water) — the field containing all phenomena without modification

āpaḥ (feminine noun, nominative plural) — waters, rivers; that which flows toward and fills

praviśanti (verb, 3rd plural) — they enter; pra-viś (to enter forth) — the continuous inflow of sensory data

yadvat (indeclinable) — just as, in the manner of; the comparative framework

tadvat (indeclinable) — likewise, in that manner; applying the ocean metaphor to the self

kāmāḥ (masculine noun, nominative plural) — desires, sense-pleasures, objects of longing

yaṁ praviśanti (relative clause) — into whom they enter; the one who receives without being changed

śāntim (feminine noun, accusative) — peace, tranquility, the absence of agitation

āpnoti (verb, 3rd singular) — he attains, obtains, reaches; the realization of completion

kāma-kāmī (compound noun) — the desire-desirer, one who goes after desires; stuck in the feedback loop of acquisition
