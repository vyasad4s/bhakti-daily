नैते सृती पार्थ जानन्योगी मुह्यति कश्चन ।

तस्मात्सर्वेषु कालेषु योगयुक्तो भवार्जुन ॥ २७ ॥

 

naite sṛtī pārtha jānan
yogī muhyati kaścana
tasmāt sarveṣu kāleṣu
yoga-yukto bhavārjuna

 

Although the devotees know these two paths, O Arjuna, they are never bewildered. Therefore be always fixed in devotion.

 

The two paths refer to the luminous (śukla) and dark (kṛṣṇa) ways of departing from the material world—one leads to no return, the other to rebirth. A devotee engaged in Kṛṣṇa consciousness (yoga‑yukta) remains steady (sthira) and undisturbed by these alternatives because his mind is fixed on the Supreme. This steadiness is the fruit of abhyāsa (consistent practice) and vairāgya (detachment from results), as stated in Yoga Sutra I.12: “abhyāsa‑vairāgyābhyāṁ tannirodhaḥ.” The mind’s modifications (vṛttis) cease when attachment to outcomes is relinquished and practice is unwavering. In Sankhya terms, this is viveka—discrimination between puruṣa (consciousness) and prakṛti (matter)—allowing the puruṣa to shine through prakṛti’s veil. The devotee’s unwavering focus on Kṛṣṇa is akin to a process with CPU affinity locked to the divine core, never preempted by material interrupts. Thus, the teaching is clear: maintain single‑pointed devotion (ananya bhakti) regardless of external dualities, and the Supreme will be attained without confusion. Hare Kṛṣṇa. 🏹

 

🔥 YS I.12: abhyāsa‑vairāgyābhyāṁ tannirodhaḥ — Through practice and non‑attachment, the cessation of mental modifications. The devotee’s unwavering focus (yoga‑yukta) exemplifies this sutra: steady practice (abhyāsa) and detachment from results (vairāgya) still the mind’s fluctuations (citta‑vṛtti‑nirodhaḥ), allowing puruṣa to shine through.
