🏹 BG 5.22 | May 12, 2026

 

ये हि संस्पर्शजा भोगा दु:खयोनय एव ते ।
आद्यन्तवन्त: कौन्तेय न तेषु रमते बुध: ॥ २२ ॥

 

ye hi saṁsparśa-jā bhogā
duḥkha-yonaya eva te
ādy-antavantaḥ kaunteya
na teṣu ramate budhaḥ

 

Translation: An intelligent person does not take delight in the sources of misery, which are due to contact with the material senses. O son of Kuntī, such pleasures have a beginning and an end, and so the wise man does not delight in them.

 

The material world operates on a principle of exchange: the senses reach out, make contact (saṁsparśa) with an object, and a fleeting sensation arises. This sensation is mistaken for happiness, but it is actually the beginning of a cycle of misery. Why? Because every material contact has a beginning and an end (ādy-antavantaḥ). The pleasure spikes, then fades, leaving a void that triggers renewed craving. The intelligent person (budhaḥ) sees through this illusion. They recognize that seeking happiness in sense contact is like trying to fill a leaking bucket—no matter how much water you pour in, it never stays full.

 

Aparā Ekādaśī, occurring today, is a divine arrangement for breaking this cycle. Ekādaśī means "the eleventh"—the eleventh day of both the waxing and waning moon. On this day, the influence of the moon on the mind is particularly pronounced, making it easier to withdraw the senses from their usual objects. By observing a fast from grains and beans, we voluntarily reduce the stimulation of the taste and hunger senses, creating a gap in the sensory input stream. This gap allows the mind to turn inward, to hear the subtle vibration of the Holy Name within.

 

The great ācāryas have emphasized that Ekādaśī fasting is not mere ritualism, but a scientific spiritual practice. Śrīla Rūpa Gosvāmī writes in the Bhakti-rasāmṛta-sindhu that regulative principles like fasting are favorable to devotion when they help control the senses. Śrīla Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura explains that on Ekādaśī, the merit of fasting is multiplied a hundredfold, making it an especially potent day for spiritual advancement. By engaging in this practice, we are not torturing the body; we are resetting the consciousness to its natural state of joy, which is independent of external circumstances.

 

Moreover, BG 5.22 is not a call for austere renunciation that tortures the body, but for intelligent engagement with the world. The verse does not say to avoid all sense contact—that would be impossible while inhabiting a material body. It says the intelligent person does not delight in the sources of misery. One can still use the senses in the service of the Lord—offering food, decorating the temple, listening to kīrtan—without becoming attached to the sensations themselves. This is the higher standard: to engage with the world while remaining situated in the unchanging joy of the soul.

 

The Loop of Temporary Gratification: Modern neuroscience confirms what the śāstras have declared for millennia: sensory pleasures trigger dopamine release in the brain's reward pathways. This creates a short-lived feeling of euphoria, followed by a drop below baseline as the brain seeks to restore equilibrium. The result is a craving for more stimulation to regain the high—a cycle that underlies addiction to everything from sugar to social media. The Bhagavad Gītā identifies the root of this cycle not in the objects themselves, but in our misidentification with the body and mind. When we believe "I am this body," we naturally seek to protect and pleasure it. When we understand "I am the eternal soul," we see the body as a vehicle to be used in divine service, not as the source of our identity.

 

The Ekādaśī fast is a practical exercise in viveka (discrimination between the eternal and the temporary). By voluntarily limiting certain sensory inputs, we create the conditions to experience a higher taste: the joy of loving service to Śrī Kṛṣṇa. This is not a negative denial, but a positive discovery. As we fast, we may notice that the urges for food or entertainment do not disappear, but they lose their compulsive force. We can observe them as passing clouds in the sky of consciousness, rather than identifying with them. This shift in perspective is the essence of yoga—the union of the individual consciousness with the Supreme.

 

Consider the analogy of a computer running unnecessary background processes that consume CPU and memory, slowing down the system. The senses, when uncontrolled, are like those processes—constantly demanding attention and draining our spiritual energy. Ekādaśī is like opening the task manager and ending those processes, freeing up resources for what truly matters: hearing, chanting, and remembering the Lord. When we return to normal eating the next day, we do so with greater mindfulness, having experienced the freedom that comes from sensory discipline.

 

Yoga Sūtra Connection: Yoga Sūtra 1.15: dṛṣṭānushravika-vishaya-vitṛshnasya vashīkāra-samjñā vairāgyam ("Dispassion is the controlled consciousness of one who is without craving for sense objects, whether these are actually perceived, or described.")

Patañjali defines Vairāgyam (dispassion) not as a state of misery or hatred toward the world, but as a state of "non-craving." While BG 5.22 warns us that material pleasures are the sources of misery, YS 1.15 provides the technical state to overcome them. When we realize that the pleasure is temporary (BG 5.22), the craving naturally evaporates, and we enter the state of Vairagya. This is the "root access" to stability: once the mind stops hunting for external spikes, it settles into its own inherent luminosity.

 

Furthermore, YS 2.54 explicitly defines pratyāhāra as the withdrawal of the senses from their objects—the very practice we undertake on Ekādaśī. Pratyāhāra is the fifth limb of aṣṭāṅga yoga, serving as the bridge between the external practices (yama, niyama, āsana, prāṇāyāma) and the internal practices (dhāraṇa, dhyāna, samādhi). By mastering pratyāhāra, the yogi gains the ability to sit in meditation without being disturbed by itches, noises, or hunger pangs. The Ekādaśī fast, therefore, is not an isolated ritual, but a vital step in the eight-limbed path to samādhi.

 

Today's Practice Guide: If your health permits, observe a full or partial fast from grains and beans today. This means avoiding items like wheat, rice, oats, corn, lentils, and beans. You may eat fruits, nuts, milk, and root vegetables. The purpose is not to punish the body, but to reduce the stimulation of the lower senses, particularly taste and hunger. If you are unable to fast due to health reasons, you can still observe the spirit of the day by reducing unnecessary sensory indulgence—limiting social media, watching less television, and avoiding junk food. Whenever you notice a craving arise, pause and label it: "This is a temporary sensation with a beginning and an end." Then, consciously shift your attention to a spiritual alternative: chanting the Hare Kṛṣṇa mahā-mantra, reading a verse from the Gītā, or listening to a kīrtan recording. By doing this repeatedly, you weaken the neural pathways that link sensory input to compulsive behavior.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

saṁsparśa (contact) — The interface between a sense organ and a material object; the "input" that triggers a sensory reaction

bhogāḥ (enjoyments) — The outputs of sensory stimulation; the temporary "highs" produced by material interaction

budhaḥ (intelligent person) — One who possesses Viveka (discernment) and can debug the illusion of temporary pleasure

ekādaśī — The eleventh day of the lunar fortnight, a day prescribed for fasting and increased devotion

pratyāhāra — The withdrawal of the senses from their objects; the fifth limb of aṣṭāṅga yoga

vairāgyam — Dispassion or non-attachment; freedom from craving for sense objects
