BG 2.61 | February 12, 2026

 

तानि सर्वाणि संयम्य

युक्त आसीत मत्परः

वशे हि यस्येन्द्रियाणि

तस्य प्रज्ञा प्रतिष्ठिता

 

tāni sarvāṇi saṁyamya
yukta āsīta mat-paraḥ
vaśe hi yasyendriyāṇi
tasya prajñā pratiṣṭhitā

 

"Having restrained them all, one should sit concentrated; intent on Me. His wisdom is truly established who has his senses under control."

 

The struggle of yesterday becomes the rootedness of today. Saṁyamya — not suppression but unified direction, all senses gathered and consecrated. Yukta — the mind yoked to Īśvara, neither scattered nor grasping. This is the Mūla energy: the root found. Where yesterday the senses dragged by force, today they rest (āsīta) — seated, settled, obedient (vaśe). Wisdom (prajñā) becomes pratiṣṭhitā — not fleeting insight but founded understanding, the unshakeable discrimination that knows puruṣa distinct from prakṛti.

 

Yoga Sūtra 1.2 — Yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ — "Yoga is the cessation of mental modifications." BG 2.61 gives the method: mat-paraḥ (intent on Me) is the viveka that ultimately stills citta-vṛtti. The citta becomes nirodha not by effort alone but by buddhi's exclusive devotion to the Supreme.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

Saṁyamya (participle) — restraining, unifying, coalescing; gathering the scattered senses into one direction

Yukta (adj.) — yoked, united; one who has accomplished yoga; the conquered mind

Mat-paraḥ (compound) — having Me (mat-) as the highest/ultimate (paraḥ); single-pointed devotion

Pratiṣṭhitā (adj.) — well-founded, established, rooted; wisdom becomes stable like a tree with deep roots
