🏹 BG 2.14 | March 19, 2026

 

मात्रास्पर्शास्तु कौन्तेय शीतोष्णसुखदुःखदाः ।

आगमापायिनोऽनित्यास्तांस्तितिक्षस्व भारत ॥ १४ ॥

 

mātrā-sparśās tu kaunteya śītoṣṇa-sukha-duḥkha-dāḥ
āgamāpāyino 'nityās tāṁs titikṣasva bhārata

 

O son of Kuntī, the nonpermanent appearance of happiness and distress, and their disappearance in due course, are like the appearance and disappearance of winter and summer seasons. They arise from sense perception, O scion of Bharata, and one must learn to tolerate them without being disturbed.

 

The conditioned soul imagines pleasure and pain to be personal enemies and allies. But this is the logic of māyā — the simulation runs on cycles, and seasons change because they must. What arises from sense perception (mātrā-sparśa) is by definition temporary (anitya). Winter cannot last; neither can summer. The temperature of prakṛti fluctuates between śīta and uṣṇa, cold and heat, but the puruṣa remains unchanged beneath it all.

 

Kṛṣṇa uses two names for Arjuna here with precision. Kaunteya — son of Kuntī — recalls the maternal lineage of strength and sacrifice. Bhārata — descendant of Bharata — invokes the royal duty of the great ancestor from whom the very land takes its name. Both remind Arjuna: you have a heritage of endurance. The kings of your line did not collapse when seasons turned.

 

The word titikṣasva is an imperative — not a suggestion, a command. Practice tolerance. Not because the suffering isn't real, but because it's not you. The body's shivering or sweating, the mind's elation or despair — these are transactions between sense organs and their objects. They happen to the instrument, not in the seer. To tolerate them is not stoic denial; it is viveka, discrimination between the foreground of prakṛti's display and the background of puruṣa's witness. Winter ends. So does summer. What remains?

 

🔥 YS I.33 — maitrī-karuṇā-mudito-pekṣāṇāṁ sukha-duḥkha-puṇya-apuṇya-viṣayāṇāṁ bhāvanātaś citta-prasādanam

 

Patañjali hands us four tools for the four types of beings we encounter: friendliness toward the happy, compassion toward the suffering, joy toward the virtuous — and equanimity (upekṣā) toward the wicked. But sukha and duḥkha are categories too, and the yogi must cultivate the same mental attitude toward both.

 

BG 2.14 prescribes titikṣā — endurance, tolerance. YS I.33 prescribes upekṣā — equanimity, impartiality. These are the same practice from different angles. Where the Gītā says "endure the opposites," the Yoga Sūtras say "neither cling nor recoil." Both point to the same truth: the vṛttis of pleasure and pain are equal in their impermanence. They are not events in consciousness; they are contents of the citta, mirrored on its surface like passing clouds. The yogi who cultivates upekṣā toward duḥkha or sukha finds the citta-prasādana — the clarity and tranquility — that allows the true seer to abide in its own nature.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

mātrā-sparśāḥ (m. pl.) — sensory perception, "touching of the objects"; the contact between sense organ and sense object that generates experience; the API boundary where puruṣa's witness meets prakṛti's display

anitya (adj.) — impermanent, non-eternal; fleeting by nature, like all phenomena arising from prakṛti; the truth that all vṛttis are temporary processes

titikṣā (f.) — forbearance, endurance, tolerance; the capacity to bear opposites without disturbance; not stoic denial but viveka in action

āgama-apāyin (adj.) — "coming and going"; appearing and disappearing, arising and subsiding; the natural oscillation of all prakṛtic phenomena

upekṣā (f.) — equanimity, indifference, overlooking; one of the four attitudes in YS I.33, specifically toward the sukha/duḥkha of conditioned existence; the emotional Neutral gear
