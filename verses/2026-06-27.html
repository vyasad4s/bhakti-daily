🏹 BG 8.24 | 2026-06-27

 

अग्निर्ज्योतिरह: शुक्लः षण्मासा उत्तरायणम् । तत्र प्रयाता गच्छन्ति ब्रह्म ब्रह्मविदो जनाः ॥ २४ ॥

 

agnir jyotir ahaḥ śuklaḥ
ṣaṇ-māsā uttarāyaṇam
tatra prayātā gacchanti
brahma brahma-vido janāḥ

 

Those who know the Supreme Brahman attain that Supreme by passing away from the world during the influence of the fiery god, in the light, at an auspicious moment of the day, during the fortnight of the waxing moon, or during the six months when the sun travels in the north.

 

This verse outlines the materially auspicious conditions for attaining the impersonal Brahman at the time of death: the influence of Agni (the fiery god), light (Jyoti), daytime, the waxing moon fortnight (Śukla pakṣa), and the sun’s northern course (Uttarāyaṇa). These conditions are considered favorable for the yogī seeking liberation through the path of knowledge (jñāna-yoga) or impersonal meditation. However, Śrī Kṛṣṇa’s teaching throughout the Bhagavad-gītā emphasizes that pure devotion (bhakti-yoga) transcends such temporal and astrological calculations. A devotee whose consciousness is fixed on the Supreme Personality of Godhead, Kṛṣṇa, attains the spiritual realm regardless of the moment of departure, because the Lord’s grace is not bound by material modes or planetary positions. From a Sāṅkhya perspective, the verse describes the interplay of puruṣa (consciousness) and prakṛti (material nature) at the critical juncture of death. The elements mentioned—fire, light, day, waxing moon, and northern solstice—represent specific configurations of prakṛti that facilitate the subtle body’s journey toward higher realms. Yet, the puruṣa that is fully absorbed in Vāsudeva (Kṛṣṇa) operates independently of these configurations, much like a process running with real‑time priority (nice = -20) that remains unaffected by scheduler fluctuations. The fiery god (Agni) symbolizes the transformative power of tapas (austerity), which burns away material designations; light (Jyoti) represents the illumination of jñāna that dispels ignorance; the waxing moon reflects the gradual increase of sattva‑guṇa through devotion; and the sun’s northern course signifies the progressive ascent toward the celestial plane. Nevertheless, the bhakta’s consciousness, saturated with prema‑love for Kṛṣṇa, functions as a kernel‑level driver that bypasses all material interrupts and directly accesses the spiritual memory space (Vaikuṇṭha or Goloka).

 

🔥 YS I.3: tadā draṣṭuḥ svarūpe ‘vasthānam – "When that is accomplished, the seer abides in its own true nature." Just as verse 8.24 describes the conditions under which the knowledgeable attain Brahman, the Yoga Sūtra teaches that when the mind’s fluctuations (citta‑vṛtti) are completely restrained, the seer (draṣṭu) rests in its own svarūpa (true form). The auspicious moments listed in the verse—fire, light, day, waxing moon, Uttarāyaṇa—can be seen as external aids that help quiet the mind’s disturbances, much like a supportive environment facilitates meditation. However, the ultimate state described in YS I.3 is independent of such external crutches; it is the intrinsic state of the pure puruṣa. For the devotee, this true nature is realized as the eternal servant of Kṛṣṇa, where the seer’s identity is fully absorbed in the divine service, achieving a state of saṁprajñāta samādhi that surpasses even the impersonal Brahman realization.
