🏹 BG 6.29 | 2026-05-21

 

सर्वभूतस्थमात्मानं सर्वभूतानि चात्मनि ।

ईक्षते योगयुक्तात्मा सर्वत्र समदर्शन: ॥ २९ ॥

 

sarva-bhūta-stham ātmānaṁ sarva-bhūtāni cātmani
īkṣate yoga-yuktātmā sarvatra sama-darśanaḥ

 

A true yogī observes Me in all beings and also sees every being in Me. Indeed, the self-realized person sees Me, the same Supreme Lord, everywhere.

 

This verse is the summit of Dhyāna Yoga's inner logic. Kṛṣṇa has walked us through the architecture of meditation — the seat, the posture, the regulation of body and mind, the withdrawal from desire, the lamp unwavering in windless air — and now He delivers the result: sama-darśana, equal vision. Not tolerance. Not sympathy. Not ethical obligation. Sama-darśana — the structural recognition that the same Paramātmā dwells in the heart of the brāhmaṇa and the dog, the believer and the atheist, the friend and the enemy. The yogī does not cultivate this vision; he discovers it as the natural output of regulated practice. When the mind stops flickering (6.26), when regulation becomes natural (6.17), when the self is fixed in transcendence (6.18) — sama-darśana is what you see. It is not a conclusion you reason toward. It is the landscape that appears when the fog burns off.

 

Prabhupāda's purport cuts to the metaphysical core: the distinction between the individual soul (jīva) and the Supersoul (Paramātmā). The jīva is present in one heart; Paramātmā is present in all hearts simultaneously. The yogī's equal vision is not a philosophical position — it is the direct perception of Paramātmā's omnipresence. The Lord is "like the mother and the maintainer" — neutral to all His children, transcendental even while situated within material bodies. This is the supreme neutrality of the Absolute: present everywhere, conditioned by nothing. The yogī who sees this sees the fundamental architecture of reality — sarva-bhūta-stham ātmānam, the Self situated in all beings, and sarva-bhūtāni cātmani, all beings situated in the Self. Two directions of one vision. Inward: Kṛṣṇa in every heart. Outward: every heart in Kṛṣṇa's energy. The circle is complete.

 

🔥 YOGA SŪTRA CONNECTION: YS I.45 — sūkṣmā-viṣayatvaṁ ca-liṅga-paryavasānam
"The subtle object reaches up to pradhāna (the unmanifest prakṛti)." This sūtra describes the final stage of sabīja-samādhi — when the yogī's meditative absorption penetrates from the grossest elements all the way to the primordial matter itself. The trajectory of Patañjali's samāpatti is from coarse to subtle, from visible to invisible, from the skin of reality to its kernel. BG 6.29 is what happens when that trajectory completes and reverses direction: having penetrated inward to the subtlest ground, the yogī now looks outward and sees the same Self that he found within reflected in every form without. Patañjali maps the inward journey; Kṛṣṇa describes the outward vision that results. YS I.45 is the microscope; BG 6.29 is the panopticon. One penetrates; the other comprehends. Both are the same seeing — just different directions of the same eye.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

Sarva-bhūta-stha (adjective compound) — situated in all beings; sarva (all) + bhūta (being/existence) + stha (standing/situated); the prefix captures Paramātmā's omnipresence — not "visiting" all beings but permanently standing within each one; the jīva stands in one body, Paramātmā stands in all bodies simultaneously; this is the structural difference between local consciousness and universal consciousness

Yoga-yukta-ātmā (masculine noun compound) — the soul dovetailed in yoga; yoga (union) + yukta (yoked/engaged) + ātmā (self/soul); the compound is precise — not merely "a yogī" but a self that has been yoked to the process of union; the yoking is the prerequisite for the vision; sama-darśana does not appear to the unyoked mind

Sama-darśana (masculine noun) — equal vision; sama (equal/same) + darśana (seeing/vision); not "equal treatment" but "equal seeing" — the difference is critical; you cannot treat all beings equally while perceiving them differently; sama-darśana is the perceptual infrastructure upon which ethical equality is built; without it, equality is mere ideology

Īkṣate (verb, present middle) — sees, perceives, beholds; from √īkṣ (to see/perceive); the middle voice is significant — the yogī sees for himself, not as an external observer but as one whose own vision has been transformed; this is not secondhand sight, not inherited doctrine, but direct perception

Paramātmā (masculine noun) — the Supersoul, the Supreme Self; parama (supreme/highest) + ātmā (self); the localized manifestation of Kṛṣṇa within every heart; distinct from the individual jīva who occupies a single body; Paramātmā is the witness, the permitter, and the maintainer of all living entities simultaneously

Darśana (neuter noun) — vision, seeing, philosophical school; from √dṛś (to see); in Indian thought, each philosophical system is called a darśana — a "viewpoint" or "way of seeing"; sama-darśana is thus not just one view among many but the view that contains and transcends all others
