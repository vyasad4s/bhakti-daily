🏹 Bhagavad-gītā | Chapter 2, Verse 26 | March 26, 2026

 

अथ चैनं नित्यजातं नित्यं वा मन्यसे मृतम्

तथापि त्वं महाबाहो नैनं शोचितुमर्हसि ॥ २६ ॥

 

atha cainaṁ nitya-jātaṁ
nityaṁ vā manyase mṛtam
tathāpi tvaṁ mahā-bāho
nainaṁ śocitum arhasi

 

If, however, you think that the soul is always being born and always dying, you still have no reason to lament, O mighty-armed one.

 

Punarvasu means the return of light — the nakṣatra of Aditi, the divine mother who restores what was lost. In this energy, nothing truly ends — it only returns, renewed. This verse presents the "even if" argument. Even if Arjuna subscribed to the Lokāyatika or Vaibhāṣika view — that life is merely temporary consciousness arising from material combination, that there is no eternal soul, only perpetual cycles of birth and death — still: no grief is warranted. If souls constantly generate from matter and dissolve back into it, why lament? One kills only chemicals. No one weeps when wood burns or water evaporates.

 

Kṛṣṇa addresses him as mahā-bāho — "mighty-armed one" — with pointed irony. Arjuna has spent years forging his arms into deadly instruments. Yet now, facing inevitable duty, he falters. His muscles are mighty; his mind is weak. The warrior who could draw the Gāṇḍīva without effort now cannot draw the sword of discrimination. Grief is a luxury born of confusion. Whether one accepts the ātmā as eternal (Vedic view) or transient (materialist view), the prescription remains unchanged. Lamentation serves no purpose. It does not stop death. It does not restore life. It only clouds the intelligence needed to act.

 

🔥 YS 2.12 | kleśa-mūlaḥ karma-āśayo dṛṣṭa-ādṛṣṭa-janma-vedanīyaḥ — "The root of action is the afflictions, and its result is experienced in seen and unseen births." Patañjali maps the mechanics: kleśas → karma → birth cycles. Even without accepting the soul's eternity, the cycle continues. The question is not if cycles exist, but how to transcend them. The kṣatriya's duty is to act without confusion, regardless of metaphysical commitments.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

atha (adv.) — "if, however" — introduces alternative viewpoint; the pivot from previous arguments to new consideration

nitya-jātam (adj. + past part.) — "eternally born" — the materialist doctrine of perpetual re-generation without permanent soul

tathāpi (adv.) — "even then, still" — the crux: regardless of philosophical position, conclusions remain unchanged

mahā-bāhu (adj.) — "mighty-armed" — ironic address; Arjuna's physical might contrasts with mental weakness

na...arhasi (verb phrase) — "you do not deserve" — the verdict: grief is beneath the station of a warrior
