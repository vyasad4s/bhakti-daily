BG 2.60 | February 11, 2026

 

यततो ह्यपि कौन्तेय

पुरुषस्य विपश्चितः

इन्द्रियाणि प्रमाथीनि

हरन्ति प्रसभं मनः

 

yatato hy api kaunteya puruṣasya vipaścitaḥ indriyāṇi pramāthīni haranti prasabhaṁ manaḥ

 

"The senses are so strong and impetuous, O Arjuna, that they forcibly carry away the mind even of a man of discrimination who is endeavoring to control them."

 

The senses are wild horses—powerful, impetuous, demanding. Even the discerning mind can be dragged away against its will. This is not a failure of character but recognition of prakṛti's strength. The one attempting to control the senses is understood—discipline is required precisely because the forces are formidable. This verse honors the struggle: it acknowledges that simply wanting self-mastery is not enough. The senses have momentum, inertia, power. But they can be reined.

 

Yoga Sūtra 2.54 — Pratyāhāraḥ saṁprajñāta-samādhiḥ — "Pratyāhāra is the withdrawal of the senses from their objects, following the nature of the mind." Patañjali's pratyāhāra is the practical response: just as the tortoise withdraws its limbs, the yogi withdraws senses from objects. It is not suppression—it is skillful disengagement so the mind can turn inward toward puruṣa.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

Yatataḥ (participle) — endeavoring, struggling; one who makes effort toward self-control

Vipaścitaḥ (adj.) — possessed of discrimination, wise; from vi + paśya, "seeing clearly"

Pramāthīni (adj.) — agitating, turbulent, violently active; from pra + math, "to churn violently"

Prasabham (adv.) — forcibly, violently, by force; the senses drag the mind against its will
