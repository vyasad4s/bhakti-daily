🏹 BG 8.8 | 2026-06-24

 

अभ्यासयोगयुक्तेन चेतसा नान्यगामिना । परमं पुरुषं दिव्यं याति पार्थानुचिन्तयन् ॥ ८ ॥

 

abhyāsa-yoga-yuktena
cetasā nānya-gāminā
paramaṁ puruṣaṁ divyaṁ
yāti pārthānucintayan

 

He who meditates on Me as the Supreme Personality of Godhead, his mind constantly engaged in remembering Me, undeviated from the path, he, O Pārtha, is sure to reach Me.

 

In this verse Lord Kṛṣṇa stresses the importance of remembering Him. One’s memory of Kṛṣṇa is revived by chanting the mahā-mantra, Hare Kṛṣṇa. By this practice of chanting and hearing the sound vibration of the Supreme Lord, one’s ear, tongue and mind are engaged. This mystic meditation is very easy to practice, and it helps one attain the Supreme Lord. Puruṣam means enjoyer. Although living entities belong to the marginal energy of the Supreme Lord, they are in material contamination. They think themselves enjoyers, but they are not the supreme enjoyer. Here it is clearly stated that the supreme enjoyer is the Supreme Personality of Godhead in His different manifestations and plenary expansions as Nārāyaṇa, Vāsudeva, etc. The devotee can constantly think of the object of worship, the Supreme Lord, in any of His features – Nārāyaṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Rāma, etc. – by chanting Hare Kṛṣṇa. This practice will purify him, and at the end of his life, due to his constant chanting, he will be transferred to the kingdom of God. Yoga practice is meditation on the Supersoul within; similarly, by chanting Hare Kṛṣṇa one fixes his mind always on the Supreme Lord. The mind is fickle, and therefore it is necessary to engage the mind by force to think of Kṛṣṇa. One example often given is that of the caterpillar that thinks of becoming a butterfly and so is transformed into a butterfly in the same life. Similarly, if we constantly think of Kṛṣṇa, it is certain that at the end of our lives we shall have the same bodily constitution as Kṛṣṇa. This call for constant remembrance (abhyāsa) and an undeviated mind (anya‑gāminā) finds direct support in Yoga Sutra I.12, which teaches that the vṛttis (mind‑fluctuations) are stilled through abhyāsa (consistent practice) and vairāgya (detachment from sense objects). The “undeviated” aspect is the abhyāsā—returning again and again to the point of focus like a process that never leaves its polling loop. Furthermore, Yoga Sutra I.23 affirms that devotion to Īśvara is a direct route to the samādhi state, echoing the verse’s promise that such remembrance makes the Supreme “sure to reach.” Together, these sutras outline a steady, disciplined focus on the divine object, coupled with the offering of one’s actions to that object.

 

PRACTICE FOR TODAY

Sustain Constant Remembrance: Practice the "infinite loop" of remembrance by chanting the Hare Krishna mantra, treating it as a high-priority background process that never yields.

Mind-Focusing (Abhyasa): Identify a "distraction interrupt" in your day and consciously redirect your attention back to the divine object, practicing an "undeviated mind".

Devotional Surrender: Reflect on the "supreme enjoyer" and offer your daily actions as service, moving from "user-space" ego to "kernel-space" devotion.
