🏹 BG 8.21 | 2026-06-19

 

अव्यक्तोऽक्षर इत्युक्तस्तमाहु: परमां गतिम् । यं प्राप्य न निवर्तन्ते तद्धाम परमं मम ॥ २१ ॥

 

avyakto ’kṣara ity uktas tam āhuḥ paramāṁ gatim yam prāpya na nivartante tad dhāma paramaṁ mama

 

That which the Vedāntists describe as unmanifest and infallible, that which is known as the supreme destination, that place from which, having attained it, one never returns – that is My supreme abode.

 

The Vedāntists speak of an unmanifest (avyakta) and infallible (akṣara) reality as the supreme destination (paramā gati). This verse identifies that very reality as the supreme abode of the Personality of Godhead, Kṛṣṇa Himself. From the Sankhya perspective, puruṣa is pure consciousness, attributeless and inactive, yet the scriptures ascribe attributes to aid meditation (upāsana). The unmanifest nature of the Supreme is beyond the fluctuating modes of prakṛti (sattva, rajas, tamas) and is the eternal ground of all existence. One who attains this abode never returns to the material world because they have realized their true nature as puruṣa, distinct yet dependent on the conscious principle. The Yoga Sutra affirms that through constant practice and dispassion (abhyāsa-vairāgyābhyāṁ), the modifications of the mind are stilled, leading to the realization of the puruṣa (YS I.2‑I.3). Thus the verse serves as both a description of the ultimate goal and a reminder that the unmanifest, infallible reality is none other than the Supreme Person.

 

The verse invites us to realize the unmanifest (avyakta) and infallible (akṣara) as the supreme destination (paramā gati) and the abode of the Supreme (tad dhāma paramaṁ mama). In the language of the energy centers, this is the realization of the sahasrāra-cakra—the thousand‑petaled lotus—where the individual consciousness merges with the divine will. As the Yoga Sutra affirms, samādhi arises when the modifications of the mind are restrained (YS 1.2), and here, the unmanifest infallible reality is attained through steady practice and dispassion, leading to the supreme abode where one never returns.
