🏹 BG 9.7 | 2026-07-14

 

सर्वभूतानि कौन्तेय प्रकृतिं यान्ति मामिकाम्

कल्पक्षये पुनस्तानि कल्पादौ विसृजाम्यहम् ॥ ७ ॥

 

sarva-bhūtāni kaunteya
prakṛtiṁ yānti māmikām
kalpa-kṣaye punas tāni
kalpādau visṛjāmy aham

 

O son of Kuntī, at the end of the millennium all material manifestations enter into My nature, and at the beginning of another millennium, by My potency, I create them again.

 

Amāvāsyā — the dark moon. The sky opens to reveal what the full moon's brilliance had concealed: the vast, unstructured void from which all things arise and into which they dissolve. Today is Bhaumvati Amavasya, the new moon falling on a Tuesday, governed by Maṅgala — the planet of fire, transformation, and confrontation. This is not a gentle dissolution. This is the fierce, purifying void where prakṛti returns to its source.

BG 9.7 speaks precisely to this moment. Kalpa-kṣaye — at the end of a cosmic cycle. The word kalpa denotes an eon, Brahmā's single day, spanning billions of our years. When it ends, all material beings (sarva-bhūtāni) dissolve back into the Lord's own nature (māmikām prakṛtim). The Sanskrit is precise: they don't vanish into nothingness — they enter into the divine substance. Prakṛti doesn't cease to exist; it folds back into its resting state, passive and undifferentiated, awaiting the next command. And when the new cycle begins (kalpa-ādau), the Lord projects them forth again (visṛjāmi) by His potency alone.

The day's nakṣatra sequence mirrors this cosmic rhythm perfectly. Punarvasu — "the star of return" — dominates until mid-afternoon. Its deity is Aditi, the cosmic mother, the unbounded. Punarvasu literally means "returning light," and its symbol is a quiver of arrows — potential energy stored, waiting to be released. This is the exact energetics of BG 9.7: dissolution is not destruction but potential. Beings return to prakṛti's quiver, and at the appointed moment, the Divine releases them again. Then Pushya arrives — the most auspicious of nakṣatras, ruled by Bṛhaspati, the guru of the gods. Pushya's symbol is the udder of a cow — nourishment, sustenance, the divine providing for what has been newly created. The cycle is complete: dissolution (Punarvasu/Amāvāsyā) → re-manifestation (Pushya).

The yoga Harshana — "joy-giving" — further reinforces this. After dissolution comes the subtle joy of renewal, the happiness of what returns rather than grief for what was lost. The human mind panics at endings. The cosmic mind recognizes them as the pause between breaths — necessary, rhythmic, generative. When Pushya arrives, the vajra yoga (arising late evening) adds a thunderbolt clarity: the flash of understanding that breaks through all illusion of permanence.

Tuesday — Maṅgalavāra — adds the fire element. Maṅgala (Mars) rules confrontation, courage, and the willingness to face what is difficult. Under Bhaumvati Amavasya, this fire is turned inward: it burns away the false identifications, the ahaṅkāra that clings to form and continuity. Amāvāsyā is traditionally the day for ancestral offerings (piṇḍa), acknowledging that we ourselves are but momentary expressions in an endless cycle — kalpa after kalpa. The Lord's promise in this verse is not merely cosmological; it is personal. Everything you see, including yourself, will dissolve. And from that dissolution, the Divine will manifest again. The question is not whether you return, but how you return — whether with accumulated saṃskāras driving the next cycle, or with the purified vision of one who has seen through the game.

Here is the hacker metaphor, precise and unsentimental: prakṛti is the codebase. At kalpa-kṣaya, the runtime halts and all processes return to disk — uncompiled, latent, waiting. The Divine is the developer who redeploys. The question for the practitioner is not about stopping the deployment — that's automatic — but about what code you've committed in this cycle. At the next kalpa-ādau, your saṃskāras compile again into a new build. What have you shipped?

 

🔥 YS 3.9-3.12 – vyutthāna-nirodha-saṁskārayor abhibhava-prādurbhāvau nirodha-kṣaṇa-cittānvayo nirodha-pariṇāmaḥ – "The transformation of restraint (nirodha-pariṇāma) is when outgoing saṁskāras are suppressed and restraining saṁskāras arise, moment by moment linked to the citta." Patañjali describes a microcosmic version of exactly what Kṛṣṇa reveals cosmologically in BG 9.7. At kalpa-kṣaye, all active processes (vyutthāna-saṁskāras — outgoing tendencies) dissolve, and the mind-stuff returns to latent potential. At kalpa-ādau, new tendencies arise from the restraining substrate. The yogi who masters nirodha-pariṇāma participates consciously in this same cycle — not at cosmic scale, but in the laboratory of meditation — dissolving the outgoing saṁskāras that bind them to saṁsāra and allowing the nirodha-saṁskāra (the saṁskāra of cessation) to flower into samādhi. BG 9.7 is the macrocosm; YS 3.9 is the microcosm. Same principle, two scales.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

prakṛti (n.) – primordial material nature; the divine energy into which all beings dissolve and from which they are re-manifested; the codebase of existence

kalpa (n.) – a cosmic eon, one day of Brahmā (~4.3 billion years); the cycle of creation and dissolution; one compile-deploy cycle of the universe

kalpa-kṣaya (n.) – "the end of a kalpa"; cosmic dissolution when all material beings return to prakṛti; the runtime halting

visṛjāmi (v.) – "I create / I emit"; the Divine's act of projecting beings into manifestation; the redeploy command

sarva-bhūtāni (n.) – "all created entities"; every material form — gross and subtle — that exists within prakṛti

māmikām (adj.) – "Mine, belonging to Me"; the Lord's own nature, indicating that prakṛti is not independent but is His energy
