🏹 BG 6.28 | Saturday, May 30, 2026

 

युञ्जन्नेवं सदात्मानं योगी विगतकल्मष: ।

सुखेन ब्रह्मसंस्पर्शमत्यन्तं सुखमश्न‍ुते ॥ २८ ॥

 

yuñjann evaṁ sadātmānaṁ yogī vigata-kalmaṣaḥ
sukhena brahma-saṁsparśam atyantaṁ sukham aśnute

 

Thus the self-controlled yogī, constantly engaged in yoga practice, becomes free from all material contamination and achieves the highest stage of perfect happiness in transcendental loving service to the Lord.

 

Pūrṇimā is the moon's answer to a question the aspirant has been asking all month: does the practice actually work? The full moon doesn't negotiate. It arrives. And BG 6.28 arrives with the same certainty — the yogi who has been yuñjan, continuously engaging, becomes vigata-kalmaṣaḥ, free from contamination. Not theoretically. Not aspirationally. The impurity is gone. The karmic residue has been burned through tapas, through sustained practice, through showing up when the mind screamed for distraction and the body begged for sleep.

And what remains when the contamination clears? Brahma-saṁsparśam — contact with the Supreme. Not knowledge about the Supreme. Not a concept of the Supreme. Contact. Saṁsparśa means touch. This is the experiential core of the Gītā's teaching: the goal of yoga is not information, it is intimacy. The yogi touches the Absolute the way a hand touches fire — directly, undeniably, transformatively. And from that touch, atyantaṁ sukham — the highest happiness — flows without effort. This is not the pleasure of the senses, which by definition requires external input and degrades over time. This is the bliss of the Self recognizing its source.

Anurādhā nakṣatra deepens this reading. Ruled by Mitra — the Vedic deity of friendship, contracts, and devotional partnership — Anurādhā governs the space between two beings who have committed to each other. The yogi and the Supreme are not strangers meeting for the first time. They are Mitra — companions who have been in relationship all along. The practice doesn't create the connection; it reveals what was always there, hidden beneath the noise of the guṇas. Pūrṇimā is the day that noise goes silent.

 

🔥 YOGA SŪTRA CONNECTION: YS II.28

yogāṅgānuṣṭhānād aśuddhi-kṣaye jñāna-dīptir-āviveka-khyāteḥ — "Upon the destruction of impurities as a result of the practice of yoga, the lamp of knowledge arises. This culminates in discriminative discernment." (YS II.28)

Patañjali and Kṛṣṇa are describing the same process from different vantage points. Kṛṣṇa names the fruit as brahma-saṁsparśa — the heart's direct contact with the Supreme. Patañjali names it as jñāna-dīptiḥ — the intellect's lamp of knowledge blazing forth. Both arise from the same cause: the destruction of impurity (vigata-kalmaṣaḥ / aśuddhi-kṣaye). The Gītā emphasizes the feeling of liberation — supreme happiness. The Sūtras emphasize the knowing of liberation — discriminative discernment. On this Pūrṇimā, both are available. The full moon illuminates both the heart and the intellect simultaneously. Shadow — the gap between them — is zero.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

brahma-saṁsparśa (n.) — "contact with the Supreme Absolute"; not intellectual understanding — direct experiential touch; like plugging into the source current: not reading about electricity, but getting lit by it

vigata-kalmaṣaḥ (adj.) — "freed from all material contamination"; the karmic cache has been cleared; no residual processes running in the background; the system is clean

yuñjan (v.) — "engaging in yoga practice"; active, continuous, present tense; not "having practiced" — practicing; the code is being executed, not just compiled

sadā (adv.) — "always"; not sometimes, not when convenient; continuous uptime for the soul's connection

atyantam sukham (n.) — "the highest, ultimate happiness"; not the dopamine hit of sense gratification — the stable, permanent bliss of the Self's contact with the Supreme; root access joy

aśuddhi-kṣaya (n.) — "destruction of impurities"; the five kleśas (ignorance, ego, attachment, aversion, clinging to life) — the malware of consciousness — eliminated through sustained practice

 

Practice for Pūrṇimā: Before sunrise, during Brahma Muhūrta (3:56–4:44 AM), sit in meditation and offer your practice to the Supreme. The Anurādhā energy favors devotional connection — Mitra, the divine friend, meets you halfway. Chant BG 6.28 silently three times, then sit in the silence that follows. That silence is the brahma-saṁsparśa. At moonrise (8:13 PM), step outside and look at the full moon. Let it reflect back to you the completion of practice. Not perfection — completion. The yogi doesn't arrive; the yogi keeps arriving. That is yuñjan — the ongoing engagement.
