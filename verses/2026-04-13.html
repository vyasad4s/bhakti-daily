🏹 BG 3.6 | 2026-04-13

 

karmendriyāṇi saṃyamya ya āste manasā smaran

indriyārthān vimūḍhātmā mithyācāraḥ sa ucyate

 

"One who restrains the organs of action but whose mind dwells on sense objects, that deluded man is called a hypocrite."

 

Now this is ṛṣi Bhārata's surgical strike — and on Varuthinī Ekādaśī, it cuts precisely where we pretend it doesn't matter. How many times have you restrained the hand from reaching the phone while the mind scrolled endlessly? How many fasting days have passed where you didn't eat grains but devoured fantasies? This is not sense control. This is performance.

 

Today's cosmic configuration amplifies this teaching mercilessly. Varuthinī Ekādaśī specifically targets the viṣayī tendency, the object-chasing reflex. Śatabhiṣā nakṣatra carries Varuna's gaze, the cosmic physician who sees through all pretense to the root dis-ease. Varuna rules the celestial waters, and here the metaphor becomes explicit: external fasting without internal purification is like washing the cup while the wine remains poisoned. Sukla yoga — "the bright" — offers clarity precisely to expose this split between action and intention.

 

The Sanskrit mithyā-ācāraḥ is worth contemplating. Mithyā is not merely "false" but that which appears real but lacks ultimate substance. Combined: practiced illusion. This is the practitioner who performs sādhana while the citta still runs toward viṣaya like water downhill. The body sits in meditation; the manas runs markets. The mouth doesn't speak gossip; the mind composes drama. This is worse than no practice at all, says Kṛṣṇa, because it breeds vimūḍhatā — delusion compounded upon delusion.

 

🔥 YOGA SŪTRA CONNECTION: pratyāhāra tat samprayāṇe cittasya svarūpānukāra eva indriyāṇāṃ pratyāhāraḥ || 2.54 || — "When the senses withdraw from their objects as the mind follows the self (svarūpa), that is pratyāhāra."

 

Patañjali describes true sense-withdrawal: not mechanical restraint of organs only, but integration — indriyāṇām following cittasya following svarūpa. The grammar encodes this hierarchy: each level subordinates to the next. Objects depend on senses, senses on manas, manas on buddhi, buddhi on the īśvara-sākṣī. To withdraw only at the sense-level without bringing citta into svarūpa-alignment is precisely the mithyācāra Kṛṣṇa exposes.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

karma-indriyāṇi (noun) — organs of action: hands (pāṇi), legs (pāda), speech (vāk), genitals (upastha), anus (pāyu); the five instruments of outward movement. Hacker metaphor: These are the API endpoints your ātmā exposes to prakṛti. If you firewall the endpoints but leave the internal variables exposed, you've got a security theater problem.

manasā smaran (participial phrase) — "remembering within the mind"; mental dwelling upon sense-objects even during physical restraint. System view: This is the background process consuming your CPU cycles while you think the main thread is suspended. Real saṃyama requires process termination, not just pausing the GUI.

mithyā-ācāraḥ (masculine compound) — false practice; conduct that appears spiritual but lacks internal substance. The root: mithyā (illusion) + ācāra (practice). Compare to mayā — the simulation rendering as real. This is the deadliest vṛtti: not knowing that you don't know your manas is still running while your body poses in meditation.

vimūḍha-ātmā (bahuvrīhi compound) — one whose ātmā (true self) is completely deluded (vi- intensive prefix + mūḍha). Being mūḍha is bad; being vi-mūḍha means your delusion has root privileges. You've installed maya.exe with admin access.

pratyāhāra (noun) — withdrawal of the senses from their objects; the fifth limb of aṣṭāṅga yoga. Not suppression but redirected flow — indriyāṇām following cittasya toward svarūpa. This is the firewall rule that actually works: send all sense-traffic to internal Reviewer (the ātmā) before routing anywhere.
