🏹 BG 6.17 | 2026-05-20

 

युक्ताहारविहारस्य युक्तचेष्टस्य कर्मसु ।

युक्तस्वप्नावबोधस्य योगो भवति दु:खहा ॥ १७ ॥

 

yuktāhāra-vihārasya yukta-ceṣṭasya karmasu
yukta-svapnāvabodhasya yogo bhavati duḥkha-hā

 

He who is regulated in his habits of eating, sleeping, recreation and work can mitigate all material pains by practicing the yoga system.

 

Four words repeat in this verse: yukta — regulated, measured, appropriate. Not extreme. Not lax. Yukta. Kṛṣṇa does not say "fast intensely" or "sleep minimally" or "work without rest." He says yukta — the middle path calibrated precisely to the body's role as instrument, not master. This is not austerity for its own sake. This is systems optimization: the body is the hardware through which consciousness operates, and yuktāhāra (regulated eating), yukta-vihāra (regulated recreation), yukta-ceṣṭā (regulated effort), yukta-svapnāvabodha (regulated sleep and wakefulness) are the maintenance protocols that keep the hardware from degrading into an obstacle.

 

The result is stated with clinical precision: yogo bhavati duḥkha-hā — yoga becomes the destroyer of duḥkha. Not "reduces stress." Not "improves wellbeing." Duḥkha-hā — the removal of suffering at its root. Prabhupāda's purport makes this explicit: the Kṛṣṇa conscious person accepts the bare necessities, makes the best use of a bad bargain, and enjoys transcendental happiness. He is callous toward incidental occurrences — accidents, disease, scarcity, even the death of a dear relative — because he knows they come and go without affecting his duty. This is not coldness. This is the natural consequence of regulation: when the system runs clean, the noise doesn't register as signal.

 

🔥 YOGA SŪTRA CONNECTION: YS II.1 — tapaḥ-svādhyāyeśvara-praṇidhānāni kriyā-yogaḥ
"Discipline, self-study, and devotion to Īśvara constitute the practice of kriyā yoga." Patañjali's threefold kriyā yoga is the operational framework behind Kṛṣṇa's fourfold regulation. Tapaḥ (discipline) is the principle of yukta itself — the willingness to regulate rather than indulge. Svādhyāya (self-study) is the diagnostic that tells you what "yukta" means for your particular body-mind system — because regulation is not one-size-fits-all; it requires self-knowledge. Īśvara-praṇidhāna (devotion) is the orientation that transforms mere discipline into yoga — because regulation without devotion is just self-improvement, which still binds. Regulation with devotion becomes duḥkha-hā, the destruction of suffering. The BG and YS converge: yukta without īśvara-praṇidhāna is health coaching. Yukta with īśvara-praṇidhāna is liberation engineering.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

Yukta (adjective) — regulated, measured, appropriate, yoked; from yuj (to yoke/join); the precise calibration between excess and deficiency — not moderation as compromise but regulation as skillful means; the operating mode where the body serves consciousness rather than consuming it

Āhāra (masculine noun) — food, nourishment, intake; from ā-√hṛ (to take in); not just what goes into the mouth but what enters the senses — prasādam is yuktāhāra because it is first offered to Kṛṣṇa, converting consumption into devotion

Vihāra (masculine noun) — recreation, movement, enjoyment; from vi-√hṛ (to roam about); the regulated engagement with the world that refreshes without entangling — Kṛṣṇa conscious recreation is never sense gratification, it is joyful service

Ceṣṭā (feminine noun) — effort, exertion, activity; from √ciṣ (to strive); the energy expenditure in work — yukta-ceṣṭā means every calorie of effort is directed toward Kṛṣṇa's interest, none wasted on ego-projects

Svapna-avabodha (masculine compound) — sleep and wakefulness; svapna (dream/sleep) + avabodha (awakening); the full cycle of consciousness — regulated means neither excess sleep (tamas) nor forced wakefulness (rajasic ambition) but the rhythm that keeps sattva dominant

Duḥkha-hā (masculine noun) — destroyer of suffering; duḥkha (suffering, wrong-churning) + hā (from √hā, to remove/destroy); yoga doesn't manage pain — it eliminates the structural cause: misidentification with prakṛti; when the system is yukta, the error signals stop
