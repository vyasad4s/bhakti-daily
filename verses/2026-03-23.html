🏹 BG 2.11 | 2026-03-23 | Śukla Pañcamī | Kṛttikā→Rohiṇī

 

अशोच्यानन्वशोचस्त्वं प्रज्ञावादांश्च भाषसे ।

गतासूनगतासूंश्च नानुशोचन्ति पण्डिताः ॥११॥

 

aśocyān anvaśocas tvaṁ prajñā-vādāṁś ca bhāṣase
gatāsūn agatāsūṁś ca nānuśocanti paṇḍitāḥ

 

The Supreme Personality of Godhead said: While speaking learned words, you are mourning for what is not worthy of grief. Those who are wise lament neither for the living nor for the dead.

 

At last, Kṛṣṇa speaks as teacher. For ten verses, Arjuna has poured forth his anguish—arguments clothed in dharma, appeals to compassion, fears of sin. And now the Lord cuts through it all with one surgical statement: you are crying for what is not worth crying over.

 

This is not cruelty. This is the beginning of Sāṅkhya—the analytical discrimination between what changes and what never does. Arjuna speaks like a man of knowledge, yet his heart is gripped by ignorance of the self. He knows the scriptures but not their essence. He can debate ethics but cannot see past the body's temporary condition.

 

The paṇḍita—the truly learned—does not lament for the living or the dead because the paṇḍita knows: the ātmā was never born and never dies. It simply changes vehicles, as one changes clothes. The body that Arjuna so desperately wants to preserve will perish today or tomorrow regardless. His tears cannot stop this.

 

This is the first lesson of the Gītā: knowledge must precede action. We cannot perform our duty while identifying with what changes. Arjuna must become the dhīra—the sober one who is not bewildered by bodily transformation. The battlefield is the perfect classroom because it forces the question: what is worth protecting? What endures?

 

🔥 Patañjali Yoga Sūtra I.2 & I.4: yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ — "Yoga is the stilling of the changing states of the mind." vṛtti-sārūpyam itaratra — "Otherwise, the seer is absorbed in the changing states of the mind."

 

Patañjali describes the problem: the seer (puruṣa) misidentifies with the mind's fluctuations (vṛttis), becoming absorbed in the drama of thought and emotion. This is precisely Arjuna's condition—overwhelmed by vṛttis of grief and confusion, he has lost discrimination between himself as the eternal witness and the temporary circumstances he witnesses. Bondage comes from sārūpya—taking on the form of the mind's modifications. Liberation comes from nirodha—stilling these modifications through discriminative knowledge.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

aśocyān (adj.) — not worthy of lamentation; that which should not be grieved over. From a- (negative) + śocya (worthy of lamentation).

anvaśocaḥ (verb) — you are lamenting; you are mourning; from anu-śuc, to grieve after, to follow with sorrow.

prajñā-vādān (noun) — learned talks, words of wisdom; prajñā (discriminative wisdom) + vāda (speech, discussion).

gatāsūn (noun) — those whose lives have ended; the dead; gata (gone) + asūn (life, breath).

agatāsūn (noun) — those who have not ended their lives; the living; a- (negative) + gata (gone) + asūn (life).

paṇḍitāḥ (noun) — the learned, the wise, those with discriminating knowledge; from root paṇḍ—to accumulate wisdom.
