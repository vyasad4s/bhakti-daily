🏹 Bhagavad Gita Chapter 10, Verse 21

 

आदित्यानामहं विष्णुर्ज्योतिषां रविरंशुमान् । मरीचिर्मरुतास्मि नक्षत्राणामहं शशी ॥ २१ ॥

 

ādityānām ahaṁ viṣṇur
jyotiṣāṁ ravir aṁśumān
marīcir marutām asmi
nakṣatrāṇām ahaṁ śaśī

 

Of the Ādityas I am Viṣṇu, of lights I am the radiant sun, of the Maruts I am Marīci, and among the stars I am the moon.

 

Today's cosmic configuration is a direct invocation of BG 10.21 — the verse where Kṛṣṇa reveals Himself as the divine source behind celestial luminaries. The nakṣatra is Chitra, whose very name means "the bright, shining one." Ruled by Tvastar, the celestial architect who fashions form and beauty, Chitra embodies the principle of divine craftsmanship in the heavens. When Kṛṣṇa says nakṣatrāṇām ahaṁ śaśī ("among the stars I am the moon"), He is declaring His presence as the luminous intelligence that gives light its meaning — a truth made vivid today as the Moon transits from Hasta (the Hand) to Chitra (the radiant). The yoga for today transitions from Śiva to Siddha at 9:06 AM. Śiva yoga carries the energy of conscious dissolution — the recognition that form is temporary, that all luminous appearances depend on a deeper ground. Siddha yoga follows as the state of perfection attained — the realization that what remains after dissolution is the eternal substratum. This sequence mirrors the verse's movement: from the manifest lights (sun, moon, winds) to their divine source. First we see the dissolution of identification with temporary radiance (Śiva); then we rest in the recognition of the eternal Light-giver (Siddha). The tithi is Śukla Saptami → Aṣṭami (transition 6:33 PM). Saptami carries the energy of structured, purposeful growth — the seventh day where momentum builds with intention. Aṣṭami, the eighth, carries the energy of overcoming obstacles through strength and resolution. Together they describe the soul's journey: first recognizing the divine order behind celestial phenomena (Saptami), then having the resolve to see beyond the lights to the Light (Aṣṭami). This is amplified by Somavāra (Monday), ruled by the Moon itself — the very luminary Kṛṣṇa identifies with in this verse. Moon currently in Kanyā (Virgo) → Tulā (Libra) after 10:24 PM adds further resonance. Kanyā's precision and analytical discrimination allow us to distinguish the transient lights from their eternal source. Tulā's balance and relationship-consciousness remind us that all lights exist in harmony — they are not competing glories but expressions of one divine opulence. The analytical Virgo mind sees the distinctions; the Libra heart sees the unity.

 

The sāṅkhya precision here is exquisite. Kṛṣṇa does not say "I am the sun" in an identificatory way; He says "of lights I am the radiant sun" — establishing a hierarchy of dependence. The sun (and moon, and winds) are manifestations (vibhūti), expressions of His opulence, not independent sources. This is the crucial sāṅkhya distinction: puruṣa (consciousness) versus prakṛti (manifestation). The lights are prakṛti in motion; Kṛṣṇa is the puruṣa whose presence makes them meaningful. Just as the moon has no light of its own but reflects the sun's, so all luminous phenomena reflect the light of consciousness. This is directly reflected in the Yoga Sūtras. YS I.23 (īśvara-praṇidhānād vā) states that samādhi is attained through surrender to the Lord. When we recognize that the sun is not self-luminous but shines by borrowed light, we naturally turn toward the Source. YS II.45 (samādhi-siddhiḥ īśvara-praṇidhānāt) says perfection of samādhi comes from īśvara-praṇidhāna — exactly what this verse cultivates. By seeing Kṛṣṇa as the moon among stars, we transfer our dependence from the reflected light to the Light itself. The mental modification (vṛtti) of seeking security in transient luminosities ceases when we recognize the unchanging Light-giver. For the bhakti-yogī, this is not abstract astronomy — it is lived dṛṣṭi (vision). Every time we see sunlight, moonlight, starlight, or the radiance of a wise person, we can trace it back: "This light is not its own; it is an expression of the Divine." The practice becomes constant remembrance: wherever there is light, there is Kṛṣṇa. This transforms ordinary perception into sacred seeing — the world becomes a temple of opulences, each photon a whisper of the Beloved.

 

PRACTICE FOR TODAY

Light Awareness: The Chitra nakṣatra carries the energy of radiant beauty and divine craftsmanship. Today, notice light in all its forms — sunlight, moonlight, electric light, even the light in someone's eyes. Each time you perceive light, remember: nakṣatrāṇām ahaṁ śaśī — among the stars, I am the moon. Let physical perception become spiritual recognition.

Śiva to Siddha Transition: During the Śiva yoga period (until 9:06 AM), spend 5 minutes contemplating the impermanence of all luminous phenomena. Then, during Siddha yoga (9:06 AM onward), spend 5 minutes resting in the recognition of the eternal Light-giver behind all lights.

Moon-Specific Remembrance: Since today is Somavāra (Moon's day) and the verse explicitly identifies Kṛṣṇa with the moon, whenever you see the moon (or even think of it), silently offer: "O Moon, You are but a reflection; Your true Source is Kṛṣṇa." Let this deepen your devotional vision.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

ādityānām (genitive plural) — of the Ādityas (the twelve solar deities, principal manifestations of solar energy)

viṣṇuḥ (nominative) — Viṣṇu; the Supreme Lord as sustainer and permeator of the universe

jyotiṣāṁ (genitive plural) — of lights, of luminaries; all sources of illumination in the cosmos

raviḥ (nominative) — the sun; the chief luminary, source of daylight and vitality

aṁśumān (adjective) — radiant, shining with rays; possessing effulgent glory

marīciḥ (nominative) — Marīci; one of the seven great sages, controller of the Maruts (storm deities)

marutām (genitive plural) — of the Maruts; the storm gods, personifications of wind and vital breath

asmi (verb) — I am; asserting identity and presence

nakṣatrāṇām (genitive plural) — of the stars; the fixed luminaries in the celestial sphere

śaśī (nominative) — the moon; reflector of solar light, ruler of mind and tides
