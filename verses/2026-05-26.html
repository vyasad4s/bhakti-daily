🏹 BG 6.10 | 2026-05-26

 

योगी युञ्जीत सततमात्मानं रहसि स्थित: ।

एकाकी यतचित्तात्मा निराशीरपरिग्रह: ॥ १० ॥

 

yogī yuñjīta satatam ātmānaṁ rahasi sthitaḥ
ekākī yata-cittātmā nirāśīr aparigrahaḥ

 

A transcendentalist should always engage his body, mind and self in relationship with the Supreme; he should live alone in a secluded place and should always carefully control his mind. He should be free from desires and feelings of possessiveness.

 

Five commands in a single verse. Kṛṣṇa does not suggest — He instructs. Yuñjīta — must engage. Satatam — constantly. Rahasi sthitaḥ — situated in seclusion. Ekākī — alone. Yata-cittātmā — with controlled mind and self. Each command is a separate discipline, and each discipline is a compression chamber for the others. You cannot practice constantly without seclusion — the world will not permit it. You cannot sit in seclusion without mental control — the mind will not permit it. You cannot control the mind without being free from desire — the senses will not permit it. The five commands are not a checklist. They are a single mechanism — each gear driving the next, the whole assembly turning toward one end: ātmānaṁ, the self engaged in relationship with the Supreme.

 

The verse begins with yuñjīta — the imperative of yuj, to yoke, to join. This is not "try yoga." This is "engage the self in union — now, always, without interruption." The root yuj is the same root that gives us yoga itself. The command is structural: the yogī must become the yoke. Not someone who practices yoga occasionally, but someone whose very mode of existence is joining — body, mind, and self, perpetually connected to the Supreme. Prabhupāda's purport makes the hierarchy clear: the Kṛṣṇa conscious person is the topmost transcendentalist because such a devotee is already engaged. The impersonalist meditates on an abstraction. The haṭha-yogī perfects posture. The bhakta yuñjīta-s — engages — directly with the Person. The seclusion and solitude are not ends in themselves. They are the conditions that make constant engagement possible. A fire burns brightest when wind is excluded. The mind connects most deeply when distraction is removed. The solitary practice is not escapism — it is concentration of force.

 

🔥 YOGA SŪTRA CONNECTION: YS II.45 — samādhi-siddhiḥ īśvara-praṇidhānāt
Patañjali places īśvara-praṇidhāna as the final niyama — the last of the personal observances before the āsana-prāṇāyāma-pratyāhāra sequence even begins. And he says: surrender to Īśvara brings about the perfection of samādhi. Not "contributes to." Not "supports." Siddhiḥ — accomplishment, perfection, the finished work. The causal relationship is direct: praṇidhāna produces samādhi-siddhi. Now read BG 6.10 alongside this. Kṛṣṇa says: yuñjīta satatam ātmānaṁ — engage the self constantly. How? By yata-cittātmā — controlling the mind and self. And what is the most effective form of this control? Prabhupāda answers: the Kṛṣṇa conscious person, who is always thinking of Kṛṣṇa, is already in samādhi. He does not need to withdraw from the world to find the Supreme — he has found the Supreme and therefore naturally withdraws from what is not the Supreme. The sequence in the verse maps exactly to Patañjali's architecture: nirāśīḥ (free from desire) and aparigrahaḥ (free from possessiveness) correspond to vairāgya (dispassion). Yata-cittātmā corresponds to abhyāsa (sustained practice). And the constant engagement — yuñjīta satatam — corresponds to īśvara-praṇidhāna, the surrender that completes the mechanism and triggers samādhi-siddhi. Both Kṛṣṇa and Patañjali agree: the path is not technique alone. The path is engagement with the Supreme — and that engagement, when constant and undistracted, produces the perfection that technique alone cannot reach.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

Yuñjīta (verb, 3rd person singular, imperative, parasmaipada) — must engage, must yoke, must join; from root yuj (to yoke, to unite, to connect); the imperative mood is not a suggestion — it is a command from the Supreme Person to the aspiring yogī; yuj is the same root that produces yoga itself; the instruction is thus self-referential: the yogī must yoga — must become the very process of joining; not "do yoga as an activity" but "become yoga as a state" — the imperative demands transformation of mode, not addition of practice

Satatam (adverb) — constantly, always, perpetually, without interruption; from sadā (always) + intensifying suffix; this is not "most of the time" or "when convenient" — it is the demand for unbroken continuity; the hacker analogy: satatam is the daemon process that runs in the background continuously, not the cron job that fires once a day; the yogī's engagement with the Supreme is not scheduled — it is the default state from which all other activities branch

Rahasi (locative, neuter) — in seclusion, in a secluded place; from rahas (secret, secluded, private); the locative case indicates the location of practice — not a psychological state but a physical condition; seclusion is not loneliness — loneliness is the pain of unwanted isolation; seclusion is the chosen withdrawal that makes concentration possible; the solitary place is the firewall that blocks the world's noise while the inner connection is established

Ekākī (adjective, nominative) — alone, solitary; from eka (one, single) + augment; this is not merely being physically alone — it is being one-pointed, undivided, single in purpose; the yogī who is ekākī has eliminated the internal crowd — the multiple voices, the competing desires, the divided loyalties — and stands as one intelligence directed toward one object: the Supreme; the paradox: the one who is alone with Kṛṣṇa is never truly alone

Nirāśīḥ (adjective, nominative) — free from desire, without expectation; from nis (without) + āśā (hope, desire, expectation); this is not nihilism — it is the liberation that comes when the self is fully engaged with the Supreme and therefore has no room for secondary desires; the one who is yuñjīta satatam in Kṛṣṇa does not need to also desire material things because the supreme engagement has already filled the space that desire would occupy; nirāśīḥ is not the absence of wanting — it is the presence of complete fulfillment

Aparigrahaḥ (adjective, nominative) — free from possessiveness, non-grasping; from a (not) + parigraha (grasping, taking, possessiveness); this is the fifth yama in Patañjali's system — the vow of non-accumulation; the aparigraha practitioner does not hoard, does not cling, does not claim ownership over what was never truly his; Prabhupāda makes the bhakti application explicit: the Kṛṣṇa conscious person knows everything belongs to Kṛṣṇa and is therefore always free from feelings of personal possession; aparigraha is not poverty — it is correct ownership: nothing is mine, everything is His
