🏹 BG 6.47 | 2026-05-25

 

योगिनामपि सर्वेषां मद्गेनान्तरात्मना ।

श्रद्धावान्भजते यो मां स मे युक्ततमो मत: ॥ ४७ ॥

 

yoginām api sarveṣāṁ mad-gatenāntar-ātmanā
śraddhāvān bhajate yo māṁ sa me yukta-tamo mataḥ

 

And of all yogīs, the one with great faith who always abides in Me, thinks of Me within himself and renders transcendental loving service to Me – he is the most intimately united with Me in yoga and is the highest of all. That is My opinion.

 

This is the final verse of Dhyāna Yoga — the last word of an entire chapter on meditation, mind-control, and the eightfold yoga system. After forty-six verses detailing āsana, prāṇāyāma, pratyāhāra, dhāraṇā, dhyāna, and samādhi — after describing the lamp in a windless place, the mind that wanders and must be returned, the regulated habits of eating and sleeping, the equal vision toward friend and enemy, the terrible fall of the unsuccessful yogī — Kṛṣṇa delivers the verdict. Yoginām api sarveṣām: of ALL yogīs — the haṭha-yogī, the jñāna-yogī, the dhyāna-yogī, the aṣṭāṅga-yogī, every single one — the greatest is not the one who sits longest, controls the mind most tightly, or achieves the deepest trance. The greatest is the one who mad-gatenāntar-ātmanā — abides in Kṛṣṇa within, with śraddhāvān — full faith — and bhajate — renders transcendental loving service. Sa me yukta-tamaḥ. He is the most intimately united. The superlative yukta-tamaḥ does not mean "best at yoga" in the technical sense. It means the most profoundly joined — the most intimately connected to the Supreme. The whole chapter has been building to this: every description of meditation, every instruction on mind-control, every warning about the mind's fickerness, has been preparing the ground for this single declaration. Bhakti is not one type of yoga among many. Bhakti is the fulfillment of every yoga. The other yogas are progressions toward this destination. The Himalayan range has many peaks, but Everest is the culmination. Every other peak points toward it.

 

Prabhupāda's purport makes the hierarchy explicit: "The culmination of all kinds of yoga practices lies in bhakti yoga. All other yogas are but means to come to the point of bhakti in bhakti-yoga." This is not sectarianism. This is structural analysis. Karma-yoga without fruitive results is the beginning. When it increases in knowledge and renunciation, it becomes jñāna-yoga. When jñāna-yoga increases in meditation on the Supersoul through physical processes, it becomes aṣṭāṅga-yoga. When one surpasses aṣṭāṅga-yoga and comes to the point of the Supreme Person, Kṛṣṇa — that is bhakti-yoga. Each stage is real. Each stage is valid. But each stage is a stage — a waystation, not the destination. The word bhajate is the hinge. Prabhupāda emphasizes that bhajate comes from the root bhaj, which implies service — not merely worship, not merely respect, not merely meditation upon an abstract principle. Bhaj means to serve with love and faith. You can worship a demigod. You can respect a teacher. But you bhaj only the Supreme Lord. The distinction is not linguistic pedantry — it is ontological. The living entity is constitutionally a servant of Kṛṣṇa. Service is not something added to the soul's nature; it is the soul's nature. To fail in this service is to fall from one's constitutional position, as the Bhāgavatam confirms: ya eṣāṁ puruṣaṁ sākṣād ātma-prabhavam īśvaram na bhajanty avajānanty sthānād bhraṣṭāḥ patanty adhaḥ — anyone who neglects service to the primeval Lord falls down from their position.

 

🔥 YOGA SŪTRA CONNECTION: YS I.23 — īśvara-praṇidhānād vā
Patañjali's twenty-third sūtra is the most explosive line in the entire Yoga Sūtra: īśvara-praṇidhānād vā — "Or [samādhi is attained] by complete surrender unto Īśvara." That single word vā — "or" — is a trapdoor. Patañjali has just spent twenty-two sūtras laying out the method of samādhi through sustained practice (abhyāsa) and dispassion (vairāgya), detailing the stages of samprajñāta and asamprajñāta samādhi, the types of concentration, the gradations of absorption. Then he says: vā. Or. Or you can just surrender to the Lord. This is not an afterthought. This is the alternative path — the direct route that bypasses the entire technical apparatus. The structural parallel to BG 6.47 is exact. Kṛṣṇa has spent forty-six verses on the eightfold system — the seat, the posture, the breath, the mind, the withdrawal, the return — and then declares: the greatest yogī is the one who bhajate Mām, renders loving service. Patañjali's vā is Kṛṣṇa's yukta-tamaḥ. Both point to the same truth: the technical path is real, but it is not the only path. The path of surrender — īśvara-praṇidhāna, bhakti — reaches the same summit by a different route. And Kṛṣṇa adds something Patañjali does not: the path of bhakti reaches higher. The one who abides in Kṛṣṇa with faith and devotion is not just a yogī among yogīs — he is yukta-tamaḥ, the most intimately united, the summit of all summits. Prabhupāda confirms: "Therefore, to become Kṛṣṇa conscious is the highest stage of yoga, just as, when we speak of Himālayan, we refer to the world's highest mountains, of which the highest peak, Mount Everest, is considered to be the culmination."

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

Yukta-tamaḥ (adjective, superlative) — the most intimately united, the greatest yogī, the supreme among the joined; from yuj (to yoke, to join) + superlative suffix -tama; this is not merely "best" — it is the superlative of union itself; where yuktaḥ means "joined, united in yoga," yukta-tamaḥ means joined most intimately — the one whose union is the deepest, most complete, most irreversibly established; the comparative form would be "more united than others"; the superlative says: there is no union beyond this

Bhajate (verb, 3rd person singular, present) — renders loving service, engages in devotional worship; from root bhaj (to share, to partake, to serve with love); this is not generic worship — pūjā is worship, arcana is formal worship — bhaj specifically implies service motivated by love and constitutional relationship; one can worship a demigod, one can respect a teacher, but one bhaj-s only the Supreme; the distinction is ontological: the living entity is constitutionally a servant of Kṛṣṇa, and bhajate names the activation of that constitutional truth

Mad-gatena (instrumental, neuter) — by absorption in Me, with mind abiding in Me; from mat (in Me, of Me) + gata (gone, arrived, absorbed); the one whose consciousness has arrived in Kṛṣṇa — not visiting, not occasionally thinking of, but gata, arrived, settled, dwelling; the instrumental case indicates this is the means by which the yogī becomes yukta-tamaḥ: absorption in Kṛṣṇa is both the path and the destination

Śraddhā-vān (adjective, nominative) — endowed with faith, full of śraddhā; from śraddhā (faith, reverential trust, the conviction that the path leads to the goal) + vat (possessing); śraddhā is not blind belief — it is the trust that arises when the intelligence recognizes truth and the heart commits to it; Prabhupāda translates it as "firm faith" — the faith that survives the mind's wandering, the doubt's return, the long plateau where nothing seems to happen; without śraddhā, the bhakta cannot sustain the absorption that mad-gatena requires

Antar-ātmanā (instrumental, masculine) — within himself, in the inner self; from antara (within, inner) + ātman (self, soul); the Lord is not encountered by going outward — He is found within; the bhakta who renders service does so from the innermost chamber of the heart, where Paramātmā dwells; this is the same location as the dhyāna-yogī's meditation — but the bhakta does not merely meditate upon the Lord within; the bhakta serves the Lord within
