🏹 VERSE CONTENT

Source: BG 2.62-63

Date: March 12, 2026

Sanskrit:

ध्यायतो विषयान्पुंसः सङ्गस्तेषूपजायते

सङ्गात्सञ्जायते कामः कामात्क्रोधोऽभिजायते

॥ ६२ ॥

क्रोधाद्भ‍वति सम्मोहः सम्मोहात्स्मृतिविभ्रमः

स्मृतिभ्रंशाद्बुद्धिनाशो बुद्धिनाशात्प्रणश्यति

॥ ६३ ॥

Transliteration:

dhyāyato viṣayān puṁsaḥ saṅgas teṣūpajāyate

saṅgāt sañjāyate kāmaḥ kāmāt krodho 'bhijāyate

krodhād bhavati sammohaḥ sammohāt smṛti-vibhramaḥ

smṛti-bhraṁśād buddhi-nāśo buddhi-nāśāt praṇaśyati

Translation:

While contemplating the objects of the senses, a person develops attachment for them, and from such attachment lust develops, and from lust anger arises.

From anger, complete delusion arises, and from delusion bewilderment of memory. When memory is bewildered, intelligence is lost, and when intelligence is lost one falls down again into the material pool.

Commentary:

This is not metaphor. This is the operating manual for exactly how consciousness degrades—step by step, inexorable, inevitable. The Bhagavad-gītā doesn't present this as theory but as functional architecture: the causality chain is precise, mechanical, unavoidable. The sequence begins not with action but with dhyāyataḥ—mere contemplation. Just thinking about sense objects initiates the cascade. The mind, that interface between puruṣa and prakṛti, serves as both ambassador and Trojan horse.

The Sāṅkhya perspective reveals the mechanics: this entire chain operates within prakṛti, pulling the puruṣa ever deeper into misidentification. Buddhi, that discriminative intelligence which should be reflecting consciousness back toward its source, instead becomes clouded by the vṛttis—those modifications of the mind-stuff generated by sensory engagement. Each step compounds the error: rāga (attachment) produces kāma (desire), which produces krodha (anger) when frustrated. Anger breeds sammoha (delusion), which corrupts smṛti (memory/connection to truth), which destroys buddhi, which results in praṇaśyati—total ruin, the fall from any possibility of liberation.

The hacker metaphor isn't decorative here—it's structural. These verses describe a buffer overflow attack on consciousness. Contemplation of sense objects is the injection vector. Attachment is the escalation of privileges. By the time anger executes, root access to the buddhi has been compromised. The system isn't just malfunctioning—it's executing malware. The only defense is input validation at the API boundary: pratyāhāra—withdrawal of senses from their objects. Not repression, which is just loading more processes to slow the inevitable breach, but complete redirection of the mind's attention away from the prakṛtic toward the puruṣic.

Yoga Sutra Connection:

🔥 YS II.54 — svaviṣayāsamprayoge cittasya svarūpānukāra ivendriyāṇāṁ pratyāhāraḥ

Translation: Pratyāhāra, withdrawal from the sense objects, occurs when the senses do not come into contact with their respective sense objects. It corresponds, as it were, to the nature of the mind [when it is withdrawn from the sense objects].

Kṛṣṇa in BG 2.62-63 traces the attack vector from contemplation to ruin; Patañjali in YS II.54 identifies the firewall architecture. The connection is surgical: the Gītā shows how sense objects breach the perimeter, and the Yoga Sūtra prescribes the protocol—asamprayoga, non-contact. When the mind is fixed on its true object (puruṣa/īśvara), the senses cease functioning automatically. No separate restraint is needed because the mind itself is no longer generating the vṛttis that propagate outward. This is pratyāhāra—the mind mimicking (anukāra) its own true nature (sva-rūpa), reflecting consciousness inward rather than scattering it through sensory engagement. The Acharyas note: the mind is the reins in the chariot analogy—control the mind, and the horses (senses) follow. Fail to control the mind, and even the strongest will is dragged into the mud. The two verses are one teaching: BG 2.62-63 shows the vulnerability; YS II.54 provides the patch.

Sanskrit Terms:

  • dhyāyataḥ (present participle, genitive) — "while contemplating, pondering"; the gerundive form of √dhyāi indicating continuous mental engagement with an object. In Sāṅkhya/Yoga psychology, this is the first vṛtti that externalizes consciousness.
  • saṅga (m. noun) — "association, attachment"; the psychological residue of repeated contemplation; forms saṁskāras or latent impressions that condition future perception.
  • kāma (m. noun) — "desire, wish, longing"; one of the five kleśas (afflictions) in Yoga Sūtra II.3; the fundamental disturbance that arises when buddhi becomes clouded by rajas.
  • krodha (m. noun) — "anger, wrath"; the destructive energy that manifests when kāma is frustrated; rajas at its most turbulent.
  • sammoha (m. noun) — "complete delusion, bewilderment"; the loss of discriminative clarity; buddhi operating under dense tamas.
  • smṛti (f. noun) — "memory"; in this context, memory of one's true nature (puruṣa) and the connection to truth; distinct from ordinary recollection—this is the capacity to remember the Self.
  • buddhi-nāśa (m. noun, compound) — "destruction of intelligence"; the loss of discriminative capacity; when buddhi can no longer distinguish between puruṣa and prakṛti, between eternal and temporary.
  • praṇaśyati (verb, 3rd person singular, present) — "one falls down, is ruined, perishes"; the inevitable consequence when the entire chain executes—total loss of spiritual position.
  • pratyāhāra (m. noun) — "withdrawal, retraction"; from prati-ā-√hṛ (to draw back toward oneself); the fifth limb of aṣṭāṅga yoga; sense withdrawal not by suppression but by mind-control; corresponding to internalization when the mind, fixed on the object of meditation, naturally withdraws from sensory engagement.
