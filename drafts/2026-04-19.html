Bhagavad-gītā 4.10

वीतरागभयक्रोधा मन्मया मामुपाश्रिताः ।
बहवो ज्ञानतपसा पूता मद्भावमागताः ॥ १० ॥

vīta-rāga-bhaya-krodhā man-mayā mām upāśritāḥ
bahavo jñāna-tapasā pūtā mad-bhāvam āgatāḥ

Translation

Being freed from attachment, fear and anger, being fully absorbed in Me and taking refuge in Me, many, many persons in the past became purified by knowledge of Me — and thus they all attained transcendental love for Me.

Purport

The path from conditioned existence to transcendental love is not theoretical — it is a practical methodology revealed here by the Supreme Lord. Three obstacles must be cleared: rāga (attachment to material objects and relationships), bhaya (fear of losing material security or the mistaken fear of losing individuality in spiritual liberation), and krodha (anger born of frustrated desire or philosophical confusion). These are not addressed separately but simultaneously dissolved through jñāna-tapasā — the fire of knowledge.

This knowledge is not mere information. It is transformative discernment that severs the roots of misidentification. The word tapasā is critical: knowledge must be heated in the fire of austerity and disciplined practice. Information without transformation is merely intellectual accumulation. But when knowledge is welded to tapas, it becomes a purifying force that burns the accumulated residue of countless lifetimes.

The result is mad-bhāvam — not merely liberation, but transcendental love for the Lord. This is the imperishable fruit promised on Akshaya Tritiya: what is attained through this purification never diminishes. It is akṣaya — inexhaustible. The destination is not void or impersonal absorption, but conscious, individual participation in the Lord's eternal loving exchanges. Many have walked this path. It is not speculation. It is demonstrated fact.

Cosmic Interpretation

Akshaya Tritiya: The third lunar day of the waxing moon — akṣaya means "that which never decays." Meritorious acts performed today yield imperishable results. The tradition holds that any spiritual practice undertaken on this day becomes permanent capital in the soul's account — never subject to the fluctuations of time or circumstance.

Paraśurāma Jayanti: The sixth avatāra of Viṣṇu, who wielded the axe to destroy the corrupt kṣatriya dynasties twenty-one times. Today commemorates the removal of obstacles — not external enemies, but the internal ones: attachment, fear, and anger (rāga-bhaya-krodha). The axe of knowledge severs the knot of material entanglement.

Kṛttikā → Rohiṇī: The day begins in the nakṣatra of Agni, the purifying fire — perfect for jñāna-tapasā. It transitions to Rohiṇī, the "growing" or "fertile" star — the abode of growth and sustenance. Knowledge first burns, then nurtures. The purification yields fruit.

Saubhagya Yoga: Beginning mid-morning, this yoga promises good fortune flourishing through the day. The timing signals that the path of knowledge — though requiring discipline — ultimately leads to auspicious outcomes, not deprivation.

Yoga Sutra Connection

Patañjali Yoga Sūtra 2.4

avidyā kṣetram uttareṣāṃ prasupta-tanu-vicchinnodārāṇām

Avidyā is the breeding ground for the other kleśas (afflictions), whether they are dormant, attenuated, interrupted, or fully active.

For the three obstacles named in BG 4.10 — rāga, bhaya, krodha — Patañjali offers the deeper diagnosis: avidyā, spiritual ignorance. These afflictions are not root causes but symptomatic growths in the soil of misidentification. As long as one sees the body as self, the material world as permanent, and sense pleasure as the highest good, rāga-attachment proliferates. As long as one fears death or annihilation, bhaya dominates. As long as frustration arises from the collision between desire and reality, krodha flares.

The jñāna-tapasā of the Gita is Patañjali's viveka-khyāti — the clear discernment of what is real from what is merely apparent. This is not academic knowledge but penetrative wisdom that alters one's fundamental stance toward experience. When avidyā is addressed at the root, the kleśas cannot sustain themselves. They wither like plants pulled from their soil.

Both systems agree: purification comes not from suppression but from transformation through knowledge. The fire burns away the substrate of ignorance itself.

Practice

Contemplation

Identify one domain where rāga, bhaya, or krodha operates in your life. Not intellectually — where do you feel it as constriction, contraction, or heat? Today, practice seeing through it rather than acting from it.

Mantra

vīta-rāga-bhaya-krodhā — silently repeat, feeling each obstacle dissolve into clarity.

Sādhanā

Perform any act of bhakti today knowing it yields imperishable results. This is Akshaya Tritiya's gift: the determination that one's practice is never wasted.

Terms Explained

vīta — "freed from, gone beyond." From the root √vī (to go). In the hacker metaphor: you've exited the legacy system. You're no longer running old code. No longer subject to the constraints that bound previous iterations.

rāga — Attachment; the coloring of consciousness by pleasant experience leading to clinging. Not merely "liking" something but the mental formation that says "this is mine, this is necessary for my happiness." In yogic psychology, rāga is paired with its opposite force dveṣa (aversion), together forming the pendulum of craving.

bhaya — Fear; specifically the existential fear that drives avoidance behaviors. In this verse, it refers to fear of spiritual existence — the terror of losing separate identity that prevents impersonalists from approaching the Personal Absolute. Also encompasses the mundane fear of death and loss.

krodha — Anger; the feverish agitation that arises when desire is obstructed. From frustrated rāga comes burning krodha. Patañjali identifies this as the most destructive kleśa because it obliterates discrimination when active, making one resemble "a madman."

jñāna-tapasā — "By the austerity of knowledge." The compound is instrumental: the means of purification. Not tapas imposed upon the body alone, but the fire of knowledge itself. Tapas is heat, friction, effort that transforms. When applied to jñāna, it becomes a laser cutting through illusion. The austerity is not deprivation — it is the discipline of sustained attention.

pūtā — "purified, cleansed." From √pū (to cleanse, purify). The state of being rendered pure — not inherently pure (which the soul always is) but freed from the accretions of material association. Like gold separated from ore through smelting.

mad-bhāvam — "My state, My mood, transcendental love for Me." The culmination of the path: not merely knowledge of God, but participation in His nature. Bhāva is the preliminary stage of prema — the condition of the heart that makes spontaneous love possible. This is the imperishable attainment, the akṣaya fruit of Akshaya Tritiya.
