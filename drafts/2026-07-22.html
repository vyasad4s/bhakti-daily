Bhagavad Gita Chapter 10, Verse 33

अक्षराणामकारोऽस्मि द्वन्द्व: सामासिकस्य च । अहमेवाक्षय: कालो धाताहं विश्वतोमुख: ॥ ३३ ॥

 

akṣarāṇām a-kāro ’smi
dvandvaḥ sāmāsikasya ca
aham evākṣayaḥ kālo
dhātāhaṁ viśvato-mukhaḥ

 

Of letters I am the letter A, and among compound words I am the dual compound. I am also inexhaustible time, and of creators I am Brahmā.

 

Today's cosmic configuration aligns precisely with BG 10.33 — the verse where Kṛṣṇa reveals Himself as the first letter, the dual compound, inexhaustible time, and Brahmā the creator. The nakṣatra transition from Svāti (wind/independent) to Viśākhā (forked branch, determination) occurs at 1:33 PM. Viśākhā's symbol is a triumphal arch decorated with leaves, representing goal-oriented energy and the power to achieve objectives through focused effort. This mirrors the verse's "dvandvaḥ sāmāsikasya ca" — the dual compound — where two distinct elements (like rāma-kṛṣṇa) are bonded yet retain individual identity, much like the forked path that ultimately converges toward a single destination. The yoga transitions from Sādhya to Śubha at 9:11 AM. Śubha yoga ("auspicious") carries the energy of purity, goodness, and favorable beginnings — perfect for engaging with the eternal aspects of the Divine. As Śubha governs the morning, we begin the day aligned with the inexhaustible time (akṣayaḥ kālaḥ) that Kṛṣṇa claims as His own. This is not mere clock-time but the timeless principle that underlies all manifestation — the eternal now that contains past, present, and future. The tithi is Śukla Navami (until 9:33 PM), then Daśamī after. Navami carries the energy of accomplishment, fulfillment, and the power to complete what was begun. Daśamī (tenth tithi) adds the quality of dharma — righteous action in accordance with cosmic law. Together they describe the soul's journey: first, recognize the eternal principle at work (Navami); then, align action with that principle through discriminative effort (Daśamī). Moon currently in Tula (Libra) — the sign of balance, justice, and cosmic harmony — adds profound resonance. Tula's symbol is the scales, weighing actions against dharma. This aligns perfectly with the verse's presentation of Kṛṣṇa as both the beginning (a-kāra) and the eternal time principle — the Alpha and Omega that maintains cosmic balance. Libra's ruling planet Venus adds the quality of refined discrimination, helping us discern the eternal amidst the temporal. The sāṅkhya precision here is stunning in its simplicity. Kṛṣṇa does not say "I am the letter A" in an identificatory way; He says "of letters I am the letter A" — establishing a hierarchy of dependence. Letters (as sound, as vibration, as the building blocks of language) are prakṛti in motion; Kṛṣṇa is the puruṣa whose consciousness makes them meaningful. Just as letters have no existence apart from the consciousness that gives them meaning, so all linguistic forms reflect the light of consciousness. This is directly reflected in the Yoga Sūtras. YS I.27 states: tasya vācakaḥ praṇavaḥ — "His designator is praṇava (oṁ)." The syllable oṁ is not a conventional designation but an eternal vibration inherent in consciousness itself — the sound that designates the Divine. When we recognize that even the letter A (the first sound) depends on consciousness for its significance, we naturally turn toward the Source. YS I.28 continues: taj-japas tad-artha-bhāvanam — "Its repetition and the contemplation of its meaning should be performed." Through japa of praṇava, we saturate the mind with samskāras associated with God, transforming ordinary sound into sacred remembrance. For the bhakti-yogī, this is not abstract linguistics — it is lived dṛṣṭi (vision). Every time we speak a letter, form a word, or experience communication, we can trace it back: "This expression is not its own; it is an expression of the Divine." The practice becomes constant remembrance: wherever there is communication, there is Kṛṣṇa as the first letter. This transforms ordinary perception into sacred hearing — the world becomes a vibration of opulences, each spoken sound a whisper of the Beloved wielding His creative word.

 

PRACTICE FOR TODAY

First Letter Awareness: The Viśākhā nakṣatra carries the energy of determination, goal-seeking, and the forked path. Today, notice the first letter of every word you speak or write. Each time you write. Each time you perceive language — spoken, written, even thought — remember: akṣarāṇām a-kāro ’smi — of letters, I am the letter A. Let ordinary perception become spiritual recognition of the Divine as the source of all expression.

Sādhya to Śubha Transition: During the Sādhya yoga period (until 9:11 AM), spend 5 minutes recognizing the auspicious beginnings already present in your life. Then, during Śubha yoga (9:11 AM onward), spend 5 minutes resolving to align action with eternal principles — engaging in sadhana that reflects the inexhaustible time principle.

Let this deepen your vision of unity amidst diversity.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

akṣarāṇām (genitive plural) — of letters, of alphabetic characters; the building blocks of written and spoken language

a-kāraḥ (nominative) — the first letter; in Sanskrit, the vowel 'a' (अ) as the primordial sound from which all other sounds emerge

asmi (verb) — I am; asserting identity and presence

dvandvaḥ (nominative) — the dual; a compound word where two elements of equal weight are bonded (e.g., rāma-kṛṣṇa)

sāmāsikasya (genitive) — of compound words; words formed by combining two or more stems

aham (pronoun) — I; the pure consciousness asserting identity

eva (adverb) — certainly, indeed; emphasizing certainty

akṣayaḥ (adjective) — inexhaustible, eternal, imperishable; that which does not diminish or decay

kālaḥ (nominative) — time; not mere clock-time but the eternal principle underlying all manifestation

dhātā (nominative) — the creator, sustainer; one who brings forth and maintains

viśvato-mukhaḥ (compound) — facing in all directions; having universal vision or omnidirectional awareness
