🏹 VERSE CONTENT

Source: BG 2.13

Date: March 8, 2026

Sanskrit:

देहिनोऽस्मिन्यथा देहे कौमारं यौवनं जरा ।

तथा देहान्तरप्राप्तिर्धीरस्तत्र न मुह्यति ॥

Transliteration:

dehino 'smin yathā dehe kaumāraṁ yauvanaṁ jarā
tathā dehāntara-prāptir dhīras tatra na muhyati

Translation:

As the embodied soul continuously passes, in this body, from boyhood to youth to old age, the soul similarly passes into another body at death. A sober person is not bewildered by such a change.

Commentary:

The soul doesn't upgrade or downgrade. It doesn't patch, version, or deprecate. It simply is — while the interface (the body) cycles through states: kaumāraṁ (childhood), yauvanaṁ (youth), jarā (old age). These are not upgrades to the soul. They're skin changes on a character that never levels up or down because it's not part of the leveling system.

This is the first drill-bit verse of the Gītā: the beginning of viveka — discrimination between puruṣa (consciousness) and prakṛti (matter). Arjuna stands at the edge of battle seeing bodies everywhere, and Kṛṣṇa drops the payload: "You're seeing version numbers, not source code." The body is the compiled binary. The soul is the developer. Binaries crash, get corrupted, get replaced. The developer doesn't.

Dhīra — the sober, the steady — is the one who doesn't panic when the binary changes. This isn't stoicism or emotional suppression. It's seeing clearly. The body feels pain? So be it. The body ages? Naturally. The body dies? Expected behavior. The soul, however, continues executing elsewhere. No data loss. No corrupt files. Just a fresh install.

Yoga Sutra Connection:

🔥 YS I.2 — yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ — "Yoga is the stilling of the changing states of the mind."

Both the Gītā and the Yoga Sūtras point to the same architecture: consciousness (puruṣa) sits beneath the changing layers (prakṛti). The body's states — childhood, youth, old age, death, reinstallation — are citta-vṛttis writ large. The mind's fluctuations and the body's transitions are the same phenomenon at different scales. Yoga is the practice of not identifying with the runtime errors. BG 2.13 gives the metaphysical proof; YS I.2 gives the method. Together: Don't debug your illusions. See through them.

Sanskrit Terms:

  • dehī (nominative singular from dehin) — "the embodied one" — the conscious self temporarily inhabiting a body; like a process (soul) running on hardware (body) that gets swapped out
  • deha (masculine noun) — "body" — the physical form, prakṛtic wrapper; the biodegradable interface between soul and world
  • dehāntara-prāptiḥ (feminine compound) — "attainment of another body" — death not as termination but as migration; no data lost, just new hardware
  • dhīra (nominative singular) — "the sober, the steady" — one whose intelligence (buddhi) remains stable through changing conditions; the unperturbed observer who sees through the simulation
  • muhyati (present indicative, 3rd person singular) — "becomes deluded, confused" — to mistake the temporary for the eternal, the wrapper for the content

Special Observances:

Rang Panchami — the festival of colors, typically associated with Holi. The cosmic energy today carries the theme of burning what must be burned — ego, identification with the temporary, illusion. Viśākhā's name comes from vi- (apart) + śākhā (branch) — "branched" or "divided." The nakṣatra is ruled by Indra and Agni, representing the piercing, analytical fire that discriminates and divides the real from the unreal.

Cosmic Interpretation:

Viśākhā means "divided" or "branched" — the energy of piercing through to see distinctions. This is viveka-energy: the capacity to tell the difference between the eternal soul and the temporary body. On Rang Panchami, we throw colored powders as a play of illusion — temporary, beautiful, not to be clung to. The day's energy asks: Can you see through the colors? Can you enjoy the play without forgetting what outlasts it?

Vyāghāta yoga means "striking" or "attacking" — it's an energy of breaking through resistance. Whatever obstacle has been between you and clear perception, today it can be shattered. The combination of Viśākhā (piercing discernment) + Vyāghāta (obstacle-breaking) creates a rare window: the fog can lift. The illusion can be seen through. The soul beneath the body's changes can be recognized.

But note: the moon is waning (Krishnapaksha), moving toward dissolution. This is not the energy of building up but of burning away. What identification with the body is ready to be released? What "I am this form" can you surrender to the fire?

Verse Selection Rationale:

BG 2.13 — dehino 'smin yathā dehe...

The soul passes through bodies like changing clothes. Viśākhā "pierces" — it sees through the different "outfits" to the one wearing them. Vyāghāta "strikes" — it shatters the wall of identification. This verse is the essence of both energies: the discrimination (Viśākhā) that reveals the unchanging seer behind the changing seen, and the force (Vyāghāta) to break through the wall of ignorance that keeps us clinging to what must be surrendered.
