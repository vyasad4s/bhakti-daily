🏹 VERSE CONTENT

Source: BG 4.38-39

Date: April 26, 2026

Sanskrit:

न हि ज्ञानेन सदृशं पवित्रमिह विद्यते ।

तत्स्वयं योगसंसिद्धः कालेनात्मनि विन्दति ॥ ३८ ॥

श्रद्धावाँल्ल‍भते ज्ञानं तत्परः संयतेन्द्रियः ।

ज्ञानं लब्ध्वा परां शान्तिमचिरेणाधिगच्छति ॥ ३९ ॥

Transliteration:

न हि ज्ञानेन सदृशं
पवित्रमिह विद्यते
तत्स्वयं योगसंसिद्धः
कालेनात्मनि विन्दति

श्रद्धावाँल्ल‍भते ज्ञानं
तत्परः संयतेन्द्रियः
ज्ञानं लब्ध्वा परां शान्तिम्
अचिरेणाधिगच्छति

Translation:

In this world, there is nothing so sublime and pure as transcendental knowledge. Such knowledge is the mature fruit of all mysticism. And one who has become accomplished in the practice of devotional service enjoys this knowledge within himself in due course of time. A faithful man who is dedicated to transcendental knowledge and who subdues his senses is eligible to achieve such knowledge, and having achieved it he quickly attains the supreme spiritual peace.

Commentary:

Kṛṣṇa delivers a thunderbolt: na hi jñānena sadṛśaṁ pavitram iha vidyate — "In this world, there is nothing so sublime and pure as transcendental knowledge." Not ritual. Not austerity in isolation. Not philosophical speculation. Knowledge — specifically tattva-jñāna, knowledge of the Absolute Truth — stands alone as the supreme purifier.

The first verse establishes knowledge as the "mature fruit of all mysticism" (yoga-saṁsiddhaḥ). This is crucial. Knowledge is not the starting point; it is what ripens when yoga — the integrated practice of devotion, discipline, and discernment — reaches maturity. It is not theoretical download. It is organic growth, the harvest of sustained cultivation.

The second verse reveals the conditions for eligibility (tat-paraḥ saṁyatendriyaḥ): one must be dedicated (tat-paraḥ) and one must subdue the senses (saṁyata-indriyaḥ). This is the Ekādaśī principle embodied — not mere abstention from grains, but comprehensive sense control that creates the inner silence where knowledge can land. Faith (śraddhā) opens the door; disciplined senses keep it open.

The result is immediate and decisive: acireṇa — "very soon" — one attains supreme peace. Not eventually. Not after lifetimes of additional effort. Once knowledge is achieved through the proper process, peace follows as surely as dawn follows the darkest hour.

Sāṅkhya-Yoga Connection:

These verses bridge the apparent gap between jñāna-mārga (path of knowledge) and bhakti-mārga (path of devotion). Sāṅkhya provides the intellectual framework — discrimination between puruṣa and prakṛti — but this is not mere abstract philosophy. The yoga of devotion (yoga-saṁsiddhaḥ) provides the laboratory where this knowledge becomes direct experience. Knowledge without practice remains theory; practice without knowledge remains ritual. Together, they constitute the complete method.

Yoga Sūtra Connection:

🔥 YS I.20: śraddhā-vīrya-smṛti-samādhi-prajñā-pūrvakaḥ itareṣām

"For others, this state is preceded by faith, vigor, memory, absorption, and discernment."

This maps precisely onto Kṛṣṇa's teaching:

  • Śraddhā (faith) → śraddhāvān — the faithful man
  • Vīrya (vigor) → tat-paraḥ — dedicated, one-pointed effort
  • Smṛti (memory) → sustained remembrance of the goal
  • Samādhi (absorption) → yoga-saṁsiddhi — mature perfection in yoga
  • Prajñā (discernment) → jñāna — the knowledge itself

Patañjali's five prerequisites mirror Kṛṣṇa's two-verse ecosystem: faith and dedication open the path; sense control and sustained practice mature into knowledge; knowledge flowers into peace. This is not sequential but simultaneous — the five elements functioning as integrated circuit.

Sanskrit Terms:

  • jñānam — knowledge, wisdom; especially spiritual knowledge of the Absolute Truth. The mature fruit of yoga practice.
  • pavitram — pure, sanctifying; that which purifies
  • yoga-saṁsiddhiḥ — mature perfection or accomplishment in yoga; the completed state when practice bears its fruit
  • kālena — in due course of time; gradually through time
  • ātmāni — in the self; within one's own being
  • śraddhāvān — one endowed with faith; the faithful person
  • tat-paraḥ — dedicated to that; one-pointed, fixed in purpose
  • saṁyatendriyaḥ — one who has subdued the senses; sense control as prerequisite
  • acireṇa — very soon, without delay, quickly; immediacy
  • parā śāntiḥ — supreme, transcendental peace; ontological stillness
