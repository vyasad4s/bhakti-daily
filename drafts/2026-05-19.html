उद्धरेदात्मनात्मानं नात्मानमवसादयेत् ।

आत्मैव ह्यात्मनो बन्धुरात्मैव रिपुरात्मन: ॥ ५ ॥

 

uddhared ātmanātmānaṁ nātmānam avasādayet
ātmaiva hy ātmano bandhur ātmaiva ripur ātmanaḥ

 

One must deliver himself with the help of his mind, and not degrade himself. The mind is the friend of the conditioned soul, and his enemy as well.

 

The mind is not a passive observer — it is the instrument of liberation or the mechanism of bondage. Kṛṣṇa doesn't say the mind *can* be your friend; He says it *is* your friend, and it *is* your enemy. The same instrument. The difference? Direction. When the mind flows toward discriminative awareness — when buddhi governs manas — it becomes bandhu, your ally. When it cascades unchecked toward sense objects, it becomes ripu, the saboteur within your own fortress. This is Sāṅkhya's architecture made explicit: puruṣa is bound not by external forces but by its own misidentification with the very tool meant to serve it. The mind is prakṛti's sharpest blade — it cuts both ways.

 

Prabhupāda's purport draws on the Amṛta-bindu Upaniṣad: mana eva manuṣyāṇāṁ kāraṇaṁ bandha-mokṣayoḥ — "For man, mind is the cause of bondage and mind is the cause of liberation." This is not philosophy. This is engineering. The mind is a system that processes input. Feed it viṣaya (sense data) without discriminative filtering, and it compiles karma. Direct it through abhyāsa (practice) and vairāgya (detachment), and it becomes the bridge to puruṣa. There is no third option. You are either uddharet — elevating yourself — or avasādayet — degrading yourself. The mind doesn't pause in neutral.

 

🔥 YOGA SŪTRA CONNECTION: YS I.12 — abhyāsa-vairāgyābhyāṁ tan-nirodhaḥ
"The fluctuations of the mind are stilled by practice and dispassion." Patañjali offers the precise method for what Kṛṣṇa states as principle. The mind is either your friend (stilled, sāttvic, reflecting puruṣa) or your enemy (turbulent, rājasic, entangled in prakṛti). Vyāsa's bhāṣya on this sūtra says: the stream of citta can flow two ways — toward upliftment or toward downfall. This is not metaphor. It is the operational reality of consciousness. BG 6.35 confirms the method: abhyāsena tu kaunteya vairāgyeṇa ca gṛhyate — "By practice and detachment, the mind can be controlled." The Gītā and the Sūtras converge: practice redirects the mind, detachment removes its gravitational pull toward matter. Together, they transform ripu into bandhu.
