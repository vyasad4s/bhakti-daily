🏹 BG 6.25-26 | 2026-05-24

 

शनैः शनैरुपरमेद्‍बुद्ध्या धृतिगृहीतया ।

आत्मसंस्थं मनः कृत्वा न किञ्चिदपि चिन्तयेत् ॥ २५ ॥

यतो यतो निश्चलति मनश्चञ्चलमस्थिरम् ।

ततस्ततो नियम्यैतदात्मन्येव वशं नयेत् ॥ २६ ॥

 

śanaiḥ śanair uparamed buddhyā dhṛti-gṛhītayā
ātma-saṁsthaṁ manaḥ kṛtvā na kiñcid api cintayet

yato yato niścalati manaś cañcalam asthiram
tatas tato niyamyaitad ātmany eva vaśaṁ nayet

 

Gradually, step by step, one should become situated in trance by means of intelligence sustained by full conviction, and thus the mind should be fixed on the Self alone and should think of nothing else.

From wherever the mind wanders due to its flickering and unsteady nature, one must certainly withdraw it and bring it back under the control of the Self.

 

Two verses. One method. Verse 25 gives the architecture — śanaiḥ śanaiḥ, gradually, step by step, the yogī should withdraw into trance through intelligence held by conviction (dhṛti-gṛhītayā buddhyā), fix the mind on the Self (ātma-saṁsthaṁ manaḥ kṛtvā), and think of nothing else (na kiñcid api cintayet). This is the ideal — the steady-state of samādhi. Then verse 26 gives the reality: the mind will wander. Yato yato — from wherever, to wherever — the mind goes astray, because it is cañcalam, flickering, asthiram, unsteady. And when it does? Tatas tato niyamya — from there, from there, regulate it. Ātmany eva vaśaṁ nayet — bring it back under the control of the Self alone. These two verses are the complete operating procedure. 25 is the specification: what the finished state looks like. 26 is the debug protocol: what to do when the system crashes — because it will crash. The mind is not defective for wandering; wandering is its nature. The yogī is not the one whose mind never wanders. The yogī is the one who always brings it back. The repetition is the teaching: śanaiḥ śanaiḥ — not once, not forcefully, but gradually, repeatedly. Yataḥ yataḥ / tataḥ tataḥ — wherever it goes, from there, from there, bring it back. The doubled adverbs are not poetic emphasis. They are the practical instruction: this will happen again. And again. And the method is the same each time. Return. Return. Return.

 

Prabhupāda's purport on 6.25 names this withdrawal pratyāhāra — the fifth limb of Patañjali's aṣṭāṅga system. This is not casual commentary; it is structural identification. What Kṛṣṇa describes as fixing the mind on the Self and withdrawing from all other thought is exactly what Patañjali codifies as the senses following the mind's lead when it disengages from objects. And on 6.26, Prabhupāda draws the critical distinction: one who controls the mind is gosvāmī, master of the senses; one controlled by the mind is go-dāsa, servant of the senses. There is no third category. You are either returning the mind to the Self or being dragged by it through the sense fields. The flickering mind is not the problem — the unreturned mind is the problem. Verse 26 does not say "prevent wandering." It says: when wandering happens, niyamya — regulate, restrain — and vaśaṁ nayet — bring under control. The instruction presupposes failure and builds the response into the method itself. This is not a system for perfect practitioners. This is a system for the cañcala mind — which is all of us.

 

🔥 YOGA SŪTRA CONNECTION: YS II.54-55 — pratyāhāra, the bridge limb
Patañjali's definition: sva-viṣayāsamprayoge cittasya svarūpānukāra ivendriyāṇāṁ pratyāhāraḥ — when the mind disengages from its objects, the senses follow, imitating the mind's own nature. This is pratyāhāra. Then: tataḥ paramā vaśyatendriyāṇām — from this comes the supreme mastery of the senses. The structural parallel to BG 6.25-26 is exact. Verse 25 describes the mind disengaging: ātma-saṁsthaṁ manaḥ kṛtvā — the mind placed in the Self, thinking of nothing else. This is the citta withdrawing from viṣaya, which triggers pratyāhāra automatically — the senses cease functioning because the mind that drives them has gone silent. Verse 26 then provides the real-time implementation: when the mind disengages and then re-engages (as it will, because cañcalam), the yogī detects the wander and redirects — ātmany eva vaśaṁ nayet. Each redirection is a micro-pratyāhāra. Each return is the mind disengaging again. Over time, the returns accumulate. The śanaiḥ śanaiḥ — the gradualness — is not weakness. It is the architecture of sustainable transformation. Pratyāhāra is not a one-time event; it is a continuous loop of disengagement, detection of re-engagement, and disengagement again. The yogī who masters this loop attains paramā vaśyatā — supreme sensory mastery. Not by suppressing the senses, but by withdrawing the mind that feeds them. Starve the command channel; the peripherals power down.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

Śanaiḥ śanaiḥ (adverb, reduplicated) — gradually, slowly, step by step; from śana (slowly, gently); the reduplication is not redundant — it encodes the instruction that this process is iterative, not instantaneous; each attempt builds on the last; the mind is not conquered in a single stroke but tamed through accumulated returns; the same root appears in śānti (peace) — the gradual one arrives at peace

Dhṛti-gṛhītayā (instrumental, feminine) — sustained by conviction, held by steadfastness; from dhṛti (steadfastness, endurance, firm conviction) + gṛhītayā (grasped, held); the intelligence (buddhyā) that drives the withdrawal must itself be held firm by dhṛti — insight alone is not enough; it must be locked in by conviction; fleeting understanding without enduring commitment cannot sustain the gradual process

Ātma-saṁstham (adjective) — situated in the Self, established in transcendence; from ātman (Self, the supreme soul) + saṁstha (situated, established); the mind's destination is not a concept or a feeling but the Self itself — the conscious witness beyond prakṛti; this is not "thinking about the Self" but placing the mind in the Self, like a river entering the ocean

Cañcalam (adjective) — flickering, restless, volatile; from cañc (to move to and fro, to be unsteady); the mind's default operating mode — not occasionally distracted but constitutionally restless; the same word Kṛṣṇa used in 6.35 (mano durnigrahaṁ calam); the flickering is not a bug — it is the mind's nature; the method must account for it, not pretend it doesn't exist

Niyamya (gerund, absolute) — having regulated, having restrained; from ni (down, firmly) + yam (to restrain, to govern); not destruction of the mind's motion but its governance; the river is not dried up — it is channeled; the energy of the mind is redirected, not eliminated; the gerund form indicates this is a prerequisite action — restraint must happen before the mind can be led back to the Self

Vaśaṁ nayet (verb phrase) — should bring under control, should lead to subjugation; from vaśa (control, subjection, authority) + nayet (should lead, should guide); the mind is not forced — it is led; the prefix ni in niyamya is the firm restraint, but nayet is the gentle guidance that follows; first restrain, then lead; first the leash, then the path home
