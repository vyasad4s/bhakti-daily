प्रशान्तमनसं ह्येनं योगिनं सुखमुत्तमम् ।

उपैति शान्तरजसं ब्रह्मभूतमकल्मषम् ॥ २७ ॥

 

praśānta-manasaṁ hy enaṁ yoginaṁ sukham uttamam / upaiti śānta-rajasaṁ brahma-bhūtam akalmaṣam

 

The yogī whose mind is fixed on Me verily attains the highest perfection of transcendental happiness. He is beyond the mode of passion, he realizes his qualitative identity with the Supreme, and thus he is freed from all reactions to past deeds.

 

Three stages unfold in this single verse — and they are not simultaneous gifts but sequential consequences. First: praśānta-manas, the thoroughly pacified mind. Not merely quiet — pacified. The distinction matters. A quiet mind can be merely dormant, a suppressed mind merely repressed. But a pacified mind is one whose rajas has been brought to stillness through sustained engagement, not through force. The prefix pra- intensifies śānta: thoroughly, completely, definitively settled. This is the mind that has stopped thrashing.

Second: sukham uttamam, the highest happiness. Not pleasant sensation, not temporary relief, not the dopamine cycle of acquisition and consumption. This is uttama — beyond measure, beyond comparison, beyond the reach of guṇa-fluctuation. Vyāsa's commentary on Yoga Sūtra II.42 defines this precisely: santoṣād anuttamaḥ sukha-lābhaḥ — "From contentment, the highest happiness is attained." The causal chain is explicit: contentment → cessation of hankering → sattva undisturbed → anuttama-sukha manifests spontaneously. BG 6.27 and YS II.42 describe the same interior event from two angles of vision.

Third: brahma-bhūtam akalmaṣam — liberated from material contamination, freed from all past reactions. This is not a bonus. It is the necessary outcome of the first two. When the mind is settled on Kṛṣṇa and happiness flows from contentment rather than acquisition, the karmic ledger simply stops generating new entries. Past sanskāras exhaust themselves without replenishment. The fire doesn't go out — it burns so cleanly that no residue remains.

 

🔥 YOGA SŪTRA CONNECTION: YS II.42

santoṣād anuttamaḥ sukha-lābhaḥ — "From contentment, the highest happiness is attained."

Patañjali places this in the niyama sequence — after cleanliness (śauca) and before austerity (tapaḥ). The order is not arbitrary. Purity prepares the ground; contentment is the harvest; austerity deepens what contentment has revealed. Vyāsa's commentary makes the Sāṅkhya mechanism explicit: when hankering ceases, sattva becomes undisturbed, and anuttama-sukha — the same sukham uttamam of BG 6.27 — manifests on its own. This is not two traditions agreeing politely. This is the same metaphysical engine described from two seats of observation. The yogī settles the mind → rajas pacifies → contentment replaces craving → the highest happiness is not pursued, it reveals itself. Puruṣa shines through buddhi when prakṛti's static subsides.
