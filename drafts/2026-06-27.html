Bhagavad Gita Chapter 8, Verse 24

अग्निर्ज्योतिरह: शुक्लः षण्मासा उत्तरायणम् । तत्र प्रयाता गच्छन्ति ब्रह्म ब्रह्मविदो जना: ॥ २४ ॥
agnir jyotir ahaḥ śuklaḥ
ṣaṇ-māsā uttarāyaṇam
tatra prayātā gacchanti
brahma brahma-vido janāḥ
Those who know the Supreme Brahman attain that Supreme by passing away from the world during the influence of the fiery god, in the light, at an auspicious moment of the day, during the fortnight of the waxing moon, or during the six months when the sun travels in the north.
The verse outlines the auspicious conditions for attaining the impersonal Brahman at the time of death: the influence of Agni (fire), light (Jyoti), daytime, the waxing moon fortnight, and the sun’s northern course (Uttarayana). For the devotee in Kṛṣṇa consciousness, however, there is no fear of returning to birth and death regardless of the time of departure, because the Lord’s grace transcends material calculations. The fiery god represents the transformative energy of tapas (austerity) that purifies the consciousness, while light symbolizes jnana (knowledge) that dispels ignorance. The waxing moon (Shukla paksha) reflects the growing illumination of the mind through devotion, and the sun’s northern course (Uttarayana) signifies the progressive journey toward liberation. Thus, even though the verse outlines material conditions for attaining Brahman, the underlying principle is that consciousness oriented toward the Supreme transcends temporal constraints—a truth realized through unalloyed devotion (ananya bhakti), which is the ultimate auspicious condition.
