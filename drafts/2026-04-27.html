Bhagavad-gītā Chapter 4, Verse 1

śrī-bhagavān uvāca
imaṁ vivasvate yogaṁ
proktavān aham avyayam
vivasvān manave prāha
manur ikṣvākave 'bravīt

śrī-bhagavān uvāca — the Supreme Personality of Godhead said; imam — this; vivasvate — unto the sun-god; yogam — the science of one's relationship to the Supreme; proktavān — instructed; aham — I; avyayam — imperishable; vivasvān — Vivasvān (the sun-god's name); manave — unto the father of mankind; prāha — told; manuḥ — the father of mankind; ikṣvākave — unto King Ikṣvāku; abravīt — said.

The Personality of Godhead, Lord Śrī Kṛṣṇa, said: I instructed this imperishable science of yoga to the sun-god, Vivasvān, and Vivasvān instructed it to Manu, the father of mankind, and Manu in turn instructed it to Ikṣvāku.

The Imperishable Science Restored

The Gītā does not begin with speculation—it begins with history. Not human history measured in centuries, but cosmic history stretching across millions of years. This verse establishes the paramparā, the disciplic succession, that carries spiritual knowledge like a torch passed from hand to hand across time. When the Lord says this science is avyayam—imperishable—He declares that it cannot be destroyed by time, diluted by interpretation, or corrupted by the mind's cleverness. It simply awaits rediscovery.

Today's cosmic configuration reflects this theme of restoration. We stand on Mohinī Ekādaśī, the day Vishnu assumed His enchanting Mohinī form to distribute nectar to the gods while denying it to the demons. Knowledge discriminates—between the deva and asura within us, between what sustains and what poisons. The fasting of Ekādaśī creates exactly this discrimination: we abstain from sensory gratification so the intellect can perceive clearly. The sun-god Vivasvān is the original recipient of this knowledge—the sun being the source of all light, physical and spiritual.

The verse traces an unbroken chain: Kṛṣṇa to Vivasvān, Vivasvān to Manu, Manu to Ikṣvāku. This is not mythology—it is methodology. Real knowledge must be received from one who has realized it. The Bhagavad-gītā was lost through the break in this succession, and Kṛṣṇa speaks it again to Arjuna to reestablish the line. Five thousand years later, we are the beneficiaries of that restoration.

paramparā — (Sanskrit: param + parā, "from one to another") The disciplic succession; the traditional method of transmitting spiritual knowledge from guru to disciple. Unlike academic knowledge that can be obtained from books alone, transcendental knowledge must be received through this living chain to maintain its potency. The BG 4.2 states this succession was broken and the science "appears to be lost"—though it was only hidden, awaiting the proper vessel.

avyaya — (Sanskrit: a + vyaya, "without expenditure") Imperishable, inexhaustible, eternal. Unlike material knowledge which degrades, is forgotten, or becomes obsolete, spiritual knowledge is avyaya—it maintains its full potency regardless of time. The sun has been shining for billions of years without diminishment; this science is equally inexhaustible.

vivasvān — The sun-god, name of the present deity presiding over the sun planet. In every millennium, the sun has a different deity; in the current age, he is Vivasvān. The sun represents the eye of the Lord and traverses his orbit under the Lord's order—he is therefore the natural first student of spiritual science, being closest to the source of illumination.

Yoga Sūtra Connection

YS 1.23: īśvara-praṇidhānād vā — "Or [samādhi is achieved] by devotion to Īśvara [the Supreme Lord]."

Patañjali offers multiple paths to the cessation of mental fluctuations (YS 1.2: yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ). Among them is īśvara-praṇidhāna—surrender, devotion, and dedication to the Supreme. BG 4.1 reveals what this devotion connects to: not an abstract concept, but a personal Being who instructs, cares about transmission, and re-establishes lost knowledge. The paramparā of BG 4.1 is the practical mechanism of īśvara-praṇidhāna—it is how devotion becomes effective.

YS 2.1: tapas-svādhyāyeśvara-praṇidhānāni kriyā-yogaḥ — "Discipline, self-study, and devotion to God comprise Kriyā Yoga [the yoga of action]."

The threefold practice of Kriyā Yoga corresponds to BG 4.1: tapas (the austerity of receiving knowledge), svādhyāya (hearing from authority), and īśvara-praṇidhāna (the humility to be instructed). These three elements define the proper student on the path of knowledge.

Cosmic Context: Mohinī Ekādaśī, Uttara Phālgunī

Tithi: Śukla Pakṣa Ekādaśī → Dvādaśī at 8:46 AM (Mohinī Ekādaśī fasting observance)

Nakṣatra: Pūrva Phālgunī → Uttara Phālgunī at 11:48 AM (stability, sustenance, fixed energy)

Yoga: Dhruva (fixed) → Vyāghāta (conflict/effort) at 12:05 PM

This Ekādaśī observes the līlā where Mohinī distributed amṛta, the nectar of immortality, exclusively to the devas while denying the asuras. The spiritual science of BG 4.1 operates similarly—it is received only by those whose consciousness has been purified through discipline. The asura-nature cannot digest this knowledge; it slips through their fingers like the nectar slipping from Rāhu's throat. Today's portal invites the sādhaka to examine: am I receiving this knowledge as a deva (with surrender and discrimination) or as an asura (for power, control, or sensory gratification)?

Uttara Phālgunī stabilizes the energy. Where Pūrva Phālgunī initiates creative activity, Uttara completes and sustains it. The paramparā is this sustaining force—knowledge held stable across generations by those willing to transmit it without distortion. When Vyāghāta yoga arrives at noon, it represents the friction of transmission: the effort required to pass this science from one generation to the next, overcoming the entropy that causes loss.

Today's Practice

Discernment: As you fast or simplify food today (Ekādaśī observance), practice the discrimination of Mohinī: what am I consuming mentally and spiritually? Am I taking in what nourishes the soul or what feeds the senses? Hold the clear-eyed discrimination that separates deva from asura within your own mind.

Humility: Acknowledge your place in the paramparā. You did not generate this knowledge—you received it. Sit with gratitude for every teacher who carried this torch before you, all the way back to Vivasvān and the original source.
