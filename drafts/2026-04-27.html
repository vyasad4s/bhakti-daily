Bhagavad-gītā 4.1

श्रीभगवानुवाच
इमं विवस्वते योगं प्रोक्तवानहमव्ययम् ।
विवस्वान्मनवे प्राह मनुरिक्ष्वाकवेऽब्रवीत् ॥ १ ॥

śrī-bhagavān uvāca
imaṁ vivasvate yogaṁ proktavān aham avyayam
vivasvān manave prāha manur ikṣvākave 'bravīt

The Personality of Godhead, Lord Śrī Kṛṣṇa, said: I instructed this imperishable science of yoga to the sun-god, Vivasvān, and Vivasvān instructed it to Manu, the father of mankind, and Manu in turn instructed it to Ikṣvāku.

— Translation by A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupāda

Commentary

The Bhagavad-gītā is not a speculative philosophy. It is avyayam — imperishable knowledge that exists outside the flow of time. On this Mohinī Ekādaśī, the verse speaks directly to the theme of divine restoration: just as the Lord took the form of Mohinī to restore the nectar of immortality to the devas, here He restores the lost science of yoga through the paramparā system.

The paramparā is not sentimental tradition. It is the recognition that true knowledge cannot be manufactured; it must be received. Vivasvān, the sun-god, is the current presiding deity of this knowledge cycle — appropriate for a Monday morning when the moon (Soma, born from the churning that produced the nectar) still carries the energy of the previous night's Ekādaśī fast. The science passes from god to man to king: an unbroken transmission requiring vessel, not creator.

Arjuna receives this same knowledge millions of years later. The chain had been "broken" (naṣṭaḥ) — not destroyed, but scattered, like the Vedas during the cosmic dissolution. The Lord's appearance to restate what was lost is precisely what Ekādaśī commemorates: the restoration of what is essential when the world forgets.

Consider the gravity: Kṛṣṇa speaks to Vivasvān. Vivasvān speaks to Manu. Manu speaks to Ikṣvāku. Each link receives and becomes capable of transmitting. Each link is simultaneously servant and master — servant to the one above, master to the one below. This is not hierarchy of power; it is chain of custody. The knowledge remains pure only when passed through authorized hands.

Yoga Sūtra Connection

YS 1.26 — sa eṣa pūrvāṣmān śāstraḥ
"He is the Teacher of the ancients; the Teacher of all Teachers."

Patañjali's formulation echoes the same principle Kṛṣṇa declares: the highest knowledge comes from the ādi-puruṣa, the original person. The Yoga Sūtras emphasize that Īśvara is not merely an object of devotion but śāstra — the primal instructor whose teachings require no authentication because He is the source of all authority.

This verse explains why bhakti and Patañjali's rāja-yoga are not separate paths. The classical yoga system — with its eight limbs culminating in samādhi — is the same yogaḥ Kṛṣṇa taught Vivasvān. The disciplic succession (paramparā-prāptam) is not optional ornamentation; it is epistemological necessity. Knowledge of the Absolute cannot be intuited; it must be heard (śravaṇa), contemplated (manana), and realized through practice under guidance.

On Ekādaśī, when the senses are controlled through fasting, the mind becomes fit to receive what the Ācāryas have preserved. The day itself is a paramparā — transmitted from Vivasvān's era to this very morning.

Ekādaśī Portal: Knowledge Restored

Ekādaśī is the eleventh day — the number that exceeds the completeness of ten, representing transcendence. The Śukla Pakṣa (waxing moon) Ekādaśī carries ascension energy: moving from darkness toward light. The specific name Mohinī Ekādaśī commemorates the Lord's appearance as the enchanting female form who distributed nectar to the devas — a līlā of divine discrimination that separates the worthy from the unworthy, much as this verse distinguishes the rājarṣayaḥ (saintly kings) who preserved knowledge from those who allowed it to scatter.

The transition from Pūrva Phālgunī (the "former" bed, representing beginnings, fertility, new creation) to Uttara Phālgunī (the "latter" bed, representing stability, sustenance, established order) mirrors the verse's movement: from the Lord's first instruction to Vivasvān, to the establishment of the paramparā system, and its preservation through Manu and Ikṣvāku. What begins must be stabilized. What is spoken must be transmitted.

The Vyāghāta yoga that commences at 12:05 PM means "strike" or "blow" — the necessary disruption that restores what has been scattered. Kṛṣṇa appears when the succession is broken. The Ekādaśī fast prepares the body-mind to receive this striking wisdom without the interference of digestive demands.

Timing: The Rāhu Kāla is 7:46–9:28 AM. Complete observances before 7:46 AM or wait until after 9:28 AM. The Abhijit Muhurat (12:26–1:20 PM) is ideal for deep study.

Key Terms

avyayam Imperishable, inexhaustible, not subject to decay. The knowledge of yoga exists outside time — like source code that never compiles to obsolescence. — Eternal, immutable, free from deterioration.
paramparā The disciplic succession through which Vedic knowledge is transmitted. Lit. "one after another." An unbroken chain back to the original speaker. — System of knowledge transmission by successive generation of teachers.
manuḥ The father of mankind, the progenitor and lawgiver for human civilization in each age. — Bridge between divine instruction and its application in human society.
vivasvān The sun-god, presiding deity of the current millennium. Represents illuminative intelligence that makes all growth possible. The original student (śiṣya) who became the original teacher. — Sun deity; name of the sun-god to whom Bhagavad-gītā was originally spoken.
ikṣvākuḥ The first king of the solar dynasty (sūryavaṁśa) to receive the science of yoga from Manu. Forefather of Lord Rāmacandra's dynasty. — Royal receptacle of transcendental knowledge; progenitor of the Ikṣvāku dynasty.
proktavān Having spoken/proclaimed — the perfect active participle emphasizing the act of transmission as completed and authoritative. From the root √vac (to speak). — One who has spoken; denotes authoritative declaration.

Today's Practice

Ekādaśī observance today creates the bodily and mental conditions for receiving paramparā knowledge. The fast reduces tamas and rajas, allowing sattva to predominate.

Morning: Before 7:46 AM or after 9:28 AM, chant the verse aloud eleven times — once for each link in the paramparā. Feel each syllable as transmission, not recitation.

Midday: In the Abhijit Muhurat (12:26–1:20 PM), study the purport with the question: What have I received that I am obligated to transmit?

Evening: As Moon enters Kanyā at 6:05 PM, reflect on your position in whatever chains of knowledge you belong to. Service as student. Responsibility as future teacher.
