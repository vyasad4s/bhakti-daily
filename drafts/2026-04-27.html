Bhagavad-gītā 4.6
अजोऽपि सन्नव्ययात्मा भूतानामीश्वरोऽपि सन् ।
प्रकृतिं स्वामधिष्ठाय सम्भवाम्यात्ममायया ॥ ६ ॥
ajo 'pi sann avyayātmā
bhūtānām īśvaro 'pi san
prakṛtiṁ svām adhiṣṭhāya
sambhavāmy ātma-māyayā

ajaḥ — unborn / beginningless; avyaya-ātmā — without deterioration, self / body that never deteriorates; bhūtānām — of all those born; īśvaraḥ — the Supreme Lord; prakṛtim — nature / form; svām — My own; adhiṣṭhāya — taking shelter of / being situated in; sambhavāmi — I appear / incarnate; ātma-māyayā — by My internal (spiritual) energy

"Although I am unborn and My transcendental body never deteriorates, and although I am the Lord of all living entities, by My internal energy I still appear in every millennium in My original transcendental form."

— Prabhupāda, Bhagavad-gītā As It Is

The Ekādaśī that breaks illusion observes the day when Viṣṇu assumed Mohinī-mūrti — the form of divine enchantment that outwitted the demons and secured amṛta for the devas. Māyā here is not ignorance but transcendental power: the Lord's capacity to appear in ways that serve His devotees and thwart the undeserving.

Vyāghātā Yoga (destructive/obstacle) begins at 12:05 PM — the yoga that cuts through obstacles by meeting them head-on. Combined with Uttara Phālgunī (the latter part of the marriage-bed asterism, ruled by Aryaman, deity of contracts and commitments), today carries the energy of decisive transformation.

BG 4.6 speaks of ātma-māyayā — not the māyā of illusion but the Lord's internal potency by which He appears while remaining unborn, unchanged, and utterly distinct from material conditioned nature. The demons at the churning of the ocean saw Mohinī as external beauty to be possessed; the devas recognized the divine intention behind the form. Such is the function of true knowledge: to penetrate appearance and perceive the Lord's transcendental nature even when He accommodates Himself to our limited vision.

Discipline, Discernment, Devotion

This is not speculation about metaphysics. This is the structure of reality, revealed.

Sāṅkhya first principles: Ordinary living entities transmigrate because we are conditioned by prakṛti — we take one body, discard it, take another. The material covering changes. Memory is lost. We forget even what we did yesterday.

The Lord is different. He is ajaḥ — unborn — which means He never enters the materialconditioned cycle of birth and death. He is avyayātmā — His body never deteriorates. He does not age. Kṛṣṇa appeared as an infant, grew to youth, and remained there. By material calculation He had grandchildren at Kurukṣetra. Yet He appeared as a young man of twenty-five.

This is not poetic imagery. This is the ontological distinction between Puruṣa and prakṛti applied to the Supreme Person. The conditioned soul is bound by prakṛti's laws. The Lord adhiṣṭhāya — takes shelter of, is situated within — His own prakṛti, His own nature. He does not acquire a body made of material elements. He manifests His own eternal form through His own internal potency.

Ātma-māyayā — this is the critical term. In conditioned existence, māyā refers to the deluding potency that makes us identify with what we are not. Here, ātma-māyayā refers to the Lord's internal, spiritual energy that allows Him to appear to the conditioned vision while remaining absolutely transcendental.

The appearance is real. The form is eternal. The perception is accommodated to our limited senses — just as the sun appears to rise and set while actually remaining fixed. The Lord appears to take birth like an ordinary being while remaining unborn. This is the essence of avyakta (unmanifest) appearing within the manifest without being touched by it.

On Mohini Ekādaśī, this principle becomes visible in narrative form. The Lord assumed a form calculated to serve His devotees. The form was beautiful, captivating, irresistible — but it was not material beauty subject to time and decay. It was the beauty of transcendental power deployed for protection. The demons were enchanted by the covering and missed the substance. The devas recognized the protection even within the enchantment.

Discernment: The same capacity is required of the sādhaka today. The material world presents endless coverings — bodies, possessions, identities, achievements. The question is not whether they exist. The question is whether we recognize the transcendental source standing behind and within them.

Yoga Sūtra Connection

YS 1.24: kleśa-karma-vipākāśayair aparāmṛṣṭaḥ puruṣaviśeṣa īśvaraḥ

"The Lord is a special Puruṣa, untouched by afflictions (kleśa), karma, results of actions (vipāka), and latent impressions (āśaya)."

Patañjali here establishes īśvara as a distinct ontological category — not merely a powerful being within the material universe, but a Puruṣa whose very nature excludes the binding factors that condition ordinary souls. This is the same distinction Krishna makes in BG 4.6: I appear, but I am not subject to the laws that bind you.

The Lord's appearance is therefore not incarnation in the sense of spirit entering matter. It is manifestation — the eternal becoming visible without changing its essential nature. Patañjali's "puruṣaviśeṣa" (special Puruṣa) is Krishna's "īśvaraḥ" — the Lord of all living entities who governs the world while remaining utterly distinct from it.

The sādhaka who understands this distinction no longer seeks liberation through dissolving into impersonal being. Instead, one seeks svarūpa — one's own original form in relationship with that transcendental Person who appears and disappears according to His own will, not according to material compulsion.

Practice

Ekādaśī observance is fasting from material engagement to focus on what is permanent. Today's practice:

Discipline: Fast from one form of sensory indulgence today — not as deprivation, but as discernment training. Can you recognize the desire without being ruled by it? This is viveka in action.

Discernment: During the Vyāghātā yoga period (12:05 PM onward), identify one area where you have been confusing the covering with the substance. Where have you taken temporary identification as permanent self?

Devotion: Chant with attention to the difference between mechanical repetition and conscious offering. The Lord appears to those who seek Him with knowledge — not mere intellectual knowledge, but the practical knowledge that manifests as recognition. Recognize the difference between praying for material acquisition and praying for the capacity to perceive His transcendental presence.
