🏹 VERSE CONTENT

Source: Bhagavad Gita 3.8

Date: March 4, 2026

Sanskrit:

नियतं कुरु कर्म त्वं कर्म ज्यायो ह्यकर्मणः ।

शरीरयात्रापि च ते न प्रसिद्ध्येदकर्मणः ॥ ८ ॥

Transliteration:

niyataṁ kuru karma tvaṁ karma jyāyo hy akarmaṇaḥ

śarīra-yātrāpi ca te na prasidhyed akarmaṇaḥ

Translation:

Perform your prescribed duty, for doing so is better than not working. One cannot even maintain one's physical body without work.

Commentary:

This verse cuts through the spiritual theater of false renunciation. Kṛṣṇa, operating as buddhi itself, delivers a surgical correction to those who mistake inaction for liberation. The word niyata is precise: it refers to duty bound by the dharma-śāstra, by one's station in the varṇāśrama system, and by the cosmic order itself. This is not busywork. This is karma that purifies because it is performed without the fever of doership.

The argument builds from pragmatic necessity to spiritual consequence. First: you cannot maintain the body without work—this is Sankhya realism, acknowledging that prakṛti demands engagement. Second: karma is jyāyaḥ (superior) to akarma—not because activity is inherently good, but because the path of the pretender, the one who claims liberation while depending on others, is pure poison. The term akarma here is not niṣkāma-karma (dispassionate action) but the refusal of responsibility masquerading as wisdom.

This is the path between the fire of rajas and the stagnation of tamas. The kṣatriya who would renounce the battlefield to meditate in a cave has not transcended ego—he has merely relocated it. Real vairāgya is not the abandonment of duty but the abandonment of the proprietary claim upon its fruit. The "prescribed duty" (niyata-karma) becomes the anvil upon which false identification is hammered away. You work because the body exists. You work without attachment because puruṣa cannot claim the results. This is the discipline that precedes discernment.

Yoga Sutra Connection:

🔥 YS 2.1 — tapaḥsvādhyāyeśvarapraṇidhānāni kriyā-yogaḥ

"Austerity, self-study, and surrender to the Supreme constitute the yoga of action (kriyā-yoga)."

Patañjali's definition of kriyā-yoga mirrors Kṛṣṇa's teaching exactly. Niyata-karma (prescribed duty) is the field in which tapas (austerity/heat) operates. Svādhyāya (self-study) is the discrimination that prevents work from becoming karma-bandhana (bondage). Īśvara-praṇidhāna (surrender to the Supreme) is the non-proprietary attitude that transforms akarma (inaction/renunciation) into the higher akarma (action without binding effect). The kriyā-yogin performs fire-sacrifice (yajña) in every action, burning the saṅkalpa (intention) that creates new sañcita-karma (stored impressions). The body is maintained not as an end but as an instrument for this burning.

Sanskrit Terms:

  • niyata (noun, nom. sg.) — prescribed, regulated, bound by cosmic/dharmic order; from ni-yam (to restrain/fix) — duty not chosen but inherited through circumstance and station
  • karma (noun, nom. sg.) — action, work, ritual performance; from kṛ (to do) — here specifically niyata-karma: duty performed without attachment to result
  • jyāyaḥ (adjective, nom. sg., m.) — superior, better, more excellent; comparative of jyā (to overpower) — the moral hierarchy between performed duty and renunciatory evasion
  • akarma (noun, nom. sg.) — non-action, inaction, renunciation without qualification; a-karma (negated action) — here refers to the false renunciation of the immature practitioner
  • śarīra-yātrā (compound noun, nom. sg., f.) — bodily maintenance, journey of the body; śarīra (that which decays) + yātrā (going) — the minimum requirement of prakṛti that cannot be evaded
  • prasidhyed (verb, optative 3rd sg., passive) — would be accomplished, effected, made successful; from pra-sidh (to execute/complete) — the impossibility of bodily maintenance without engagement

Cosmic Interpretation:

The energies of this morning speak to the transition between mastery and skillful execution. Uttara Phalguni, ruled by Aryaman—the deity of contracts and lasting relationships—gives way to Hasta, whose deity Savitar governs the creative hand and craftsmanship. This is the liminal space between having established the structure (Uttara Phalguni's stability) and executing with precision (Hasta's dexterity). The task is not to abandon the work but to perform it with stainless attention.

The yoga transitions from Sula (thorn/weapon) to Ganda (knot/bond). Sula brings precision, sometimes piercing discomfort—the sharp recognition that one's evasion of duty has been noted. Ganda binds, but this binding is not merely restriction; it is the binding of attention required for skillful action. The thorn punctures illusion. The knot ties the practitioner to the practice. Between 9:15 PM when Ganda begins and the Sarvartha Siddhi yoga taking hold, the evening opens for work that aligns with ultimate purpose.

Krishna Dwitiya—the second day of the waning moon—is uniquely suited to BG 3.8. The waxing moon (Shukla) builds; the waning moon (Krishna) strips away. Dwitiya energy is about beginnings within decline, establishing structure within diminishment. To perform niyata-karma on this tithi is to acknowledge that even in the reduction of the visible, the invisible duty remains. The body must be maintained. The work must be done. The moon wanes but the rotation of dharma continues.

Verse Selection Rationale:

BG 3.8 — नियतं कुरु कर्म त्वं

Following BG 3.7 (March 2)—the verse distinguishing the superior yogi who acts without attachment—today's cosmic signature demanded the practical instruction: HOW to act. Uttara Phalguni's contractual, enduring nature combined with Hasta's hand-craftsmanship points to karma performed as craft, as duty-bound artistry. The Sula yoga that demands precision found its match in the verse that demands performance.
