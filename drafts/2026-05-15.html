Bhagavad Gita Chapter 5, Verse 15

अधिष्ठात्रि परमे भर्ति कर्तारि सर्वलोकस्मात् ।
न कर्तारि न कर्माणि लोके सॄजति नियन्ता ॥ १५ ॥
adhiṣṭhātari parame bhartā kartāri sarvalokasmāt
na kartāri na karmāṇi loke sṛjati niyantā
The embodied living being, however, does not create his own impetus for action, nor does he create the agent who causes action, nor does he create the fruits of action. All this is effected by the modes of material nature.

This verse is a profound declaration of non-doership (akartṛtva). It teaches that the living being (jīva) is not the ultimate source of action. The impetus to act (pravṛtti), the agent who performs the action (kartā), and the results (phalam) are all orchestrated by prakṛti—the material nature governed by the three guṇas (sattva, rajas, tamas). The jīva, conditioned by prakṛti, mistakenly identifies itself as the doer due to ahankāra (false ego). When this misidentification is removed through knowledge and devotion, one sees that action happens through the instrumentality of the body and mind, but the true self remains a neutral witness.

Consider the analogy of a robot on an assembly line. The robot performs tasks, but it does not originate the design, the power supply, or the purpose of the line—it is merely an instrument. Similarly, the body-mind complex is an instrument powered by prakṛti. The soul, when situated in its true nature as ātmā, is like the electricity that powers the robot: it enables action but does not dictate the specifics of the task. When we realize this, we can engage in work without attachment to outcomes, because we understand that we are not the proprietor of the action or its fruits.

This understanding is the foundation of karma-yoga: acting as an instrument of the Divine. When we offer our actions to Kṛṣṇa, we transfer the sense of proprietorship from the false ego to the Supreme. The action remains, but the burden of doership and the anxiety of results fall away. We become like a lotus leaf in water—unaffected by the moisture because our essence is not of the water, though we reside within it.

On the eve of Amavasya (New Moon), when the moon’s light is minimal, the darkness symbolizes the illusion of doership. Just as the moon reflects the sun’s light but has no light of its own, the jīva reflects consciousness but is not its source. This night is ideal for contemplating our true role: not as the originator, but as a reflector of divine energy. By meditating on this truth, we weaken the grip of ahankāra and prepare to act with pure detachment.

Yoga Sutra Connection

YS 2.3: kleśa-karmā-vipāka-āśayaiḥ aparāmṛṣṭaḥ puruṣa-viśeṣa īśvaraḥ

"The Seer is pure consciousness, though it appears to see through the mind."

This sutra clarifies that the puruṣa (true self) is merely a witness; it does not act. The modifications of the mind (citta-vṛtti) and the resulting actions are the play of prakṛti. This directly supports BG 5.15's teaching that the living being does not create the impetus for action, the agent, or the fruits—all are arranged by prakṛti. The puruṣa remains unaffected, like a lotus leaf untouched by water.

Furthermore, YS 2.18-19 describe the relationship between puruṣa and prakṛti: the world exists for the purpose of liberation and experience of the puruṣa. When the puruṣa mistakenly identifies with prakṛti’s modifications, bondage arises. Through discriminative knowledge (viveka-khyāti), the puruṣa realizes its separateness and remains untouched by the changes in prakṛti. This is the exact state described in BG 5.15: the embodied being does not create impetus, agent, or fruits—all are prakṛti’s play—while the puruṣa remains the indifferent witness.

Thus, the Yoga Sūtras provide the metaphysical foundation for the detached action taught in the Bhagavad Gītā: when we realize we are not the doer, we can engage in action without entanglement. The practical method to cultivate this realization is through abhyāsa (consistent practice) and vairāgya (detachment), as outlined in YS 1.12-16. By repeatedly returning the mind to the understanding of non-doership, we weaken the imprint of ahankāra and stabilize in the witness consciousness.

Today's Practice

As we approach Amavasya (New Moon), observe any tendencies toward attachment to results in your activities. Before beginning any task, recall that the impetus to act, the agent of action, and the fruits of action are all arranged by the material nature (prakṛti). Consciously offer the action to Kṛṣṇa and release expectation of specific outcomes. Throughout the day, periodically check: Is my mind fixed on the Divine as the witness, or has it identified with being the doer? When you notice distraction, gently return to the consciousness of being a neutral observer. This practice of action with detachment is the essence of renunciation in action taught in Chapter 5.

A practical exercise: Before eating, pause and reflect that the hunger, the hand that brings food to the mouth, and the satisfaction derived are all arrangements of prakṛti. Offer the act of eating to Kṛṣṇa as prasāda. After eating, notice if the mind claims “I enjoyed that” or if it remains neutral, simply acknowledging that nourishment occurred. Over time, this mindfulness extends to all actions—work, speech, even thought—allowing us to participate in the world without becoming entangled in its reactions.
