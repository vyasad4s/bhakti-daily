Bhagavad Gita Chapter 5, Verse 7

yogayukto viśuddhātmā vijitātmā jitendriyaḥ
sarva-bhūtātma-bhūtātmā kurvann api na lipyate
yoga-yukto viśuddhātmā vijitātmā jitendriyaḥ
sarva-bhūtātma-bhūtātmā kurvann api na lipyate
One who works in devotion, who is a pure soul, and who controls his mind and senses is dear to everyone, and everyone is dear to him. Though always working, such a man is never entangled.
One who is on the path of liberation by Kṛṣṇa consciousness is very dear to every living being, and every living being is dear to him. This is due to his Kṛṣṇa consciousness. Such a person cannot think of any living being as separate from Kṛṣṇa, just as the leaves and branches of a tree are not separate from the tree. He knows very well that by pouring water on the root of the tree, the water will be distributed to all the leaves and branches, or by supplying food to the stomach, the energy is automatically distributed throughout the body. Because one who works in Kṛṣṇa consciousness is servant to all, he is very dear to everyone. And because everyone is satisfied by his work, he is pure in consciousness. Because he is pure in consciousness, his mind is completely controlled. And because his mind is controlled, his senses are also controlled. Because his mind is always fixed on Kṛṣṇa, there is no chance of his being deviated from Kṛṣṇa. Nor is there a chance that he will engage his senses in matters other than the service of the Lord. He does not like to hear anything except topics relating to Kṛṣṇa; he does not like to eat anything which is not offered to Kṛṣṇa; and he does not wish to go anywhere if Kṛṣṇa is not involved. Therefore, his senses are controlled. A man of controlled senses cannot be offensive to anyone. One may ask, “Why then was Arjuna offensive (in battle) to others? Wasn’t he in Kṛṣṇa consciousness?” Arjuna was only superficially offensive because (as has already been explained in the Second Chapter) all the assembled persons on the battlefield would continue to live individually, as the soul cannot be slain. So, spiritually, no one was killed on the Battlefield of Kurukṣetra. Only their dresses were changed by the order of Kṛṣṇa, who was personally present. Therefore Arjuna, while fighting on the Battlefield of Kurukṣetra, was not really fighting at all; he was simply carrying out the orders of Kṛṣṇa in full Kṛṣṇa consciousness. Such a person is never entangled in the reactions of work.

The eve of Amavasya carries the energy of completion and release. As the moon wanes to darkness, we are invited to let go of what no longer serves us. This aligns perfectly with Chapter 5's teachings on renunciation - not the renunciation of action, but renunciation of attachment to the fruits of action.

Today's nakṣatra transition from Ashwini to Bharani at 10:44 AM brings a shift from the energy of swift beginnings (Ashwini, ruled by Ketu) to the energy of purification and bearing fruit (Bharani, ruled by Venus). This supports the Gita's message that true freedom comes not from inaction, but from action performed with devotion and sense control.

The Saubhagya yoga (good fortune) prevailing until late night emphasizes that auspicious results come to those who act with purity of heart and self-mastery - exactly the state described in verse 5.7.

Yoga Sutra Connection

YS 1.2: yogaḥ citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ

"Yoga is the cessation of the modifications of the mind."

This sutra provides the psychological foundation for BG 5.7's description of the self-controlled devotee. When citta-vṛtti (mental modifications) are restrained through abhyāsa (practice) and vairāgya (detachment), the mind becomes fixed on the Divine. This is precisely what enables one to "work in devotion" while remaining "never entangled" - the actions are performed, but the mind remains undisturbed by their results because it is established in its true nature as the servant of Kṛṣṇa.

The progression is clear: through the practice of bhakti-yoga (BG 5), the mind attains the state of citta-vṛtti-nirodha (YS 1.2), leading to the liberation described in both texts - freedom from entanglement in the reactions of work while engaged in benevolent action.

Sanskrit Terms

yoga-yuktaḥ
engaged in devotional service; one whose consciousness is united with the Divine through bhakti
viśuddhātmā
purified soul; consciousness cleansed of material designation
vijitātmā
self-controlled; mastery over the mind
jitendriyaḥ
having conquered the senses; sense control as a result of mind control
sarva-bhūtātma-bhūtātmā
seeing the self in all beings and all beings in the self; vision of unity in diversity
lipyate
is entangled, affected; the state of being bound by karma

Today's Practice

As we approach Amavasya (New Moon), observe any tendencies toward attachment to results in your activities. Before beginning any task, consciously offer the action to Kṛṣṇa and release expectation of specific outcomes. Throughout the day, periodically check: Is my mind fixed on the Divine, or has it wandered to concerns about success or failure? When you notice distraction, gently return to the consciousness of being Kṛṣṇa's instrument. This practice of action with detachment is the essence of renunciation in action taught in Chapter 5.
