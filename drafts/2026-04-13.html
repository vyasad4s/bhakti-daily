The Hypocrite's Trap

BG 3.6 | Varuthini Ekadashi | Shatabhisha Nakshatra

karmendriyāṇi saṃyamya ya āste manasā smaran indriyārthān vimūḍhātmā mithyācāraḥ sa ucyate
"One who restrains the organs of action but whose mind dwells on sense objects, that deluded man is called a hypocrite."

karma-indriyāṇi — organs of action (hands, legs, mouth, genitals, anus); saṃyamya — controlling; manasā smaran — remembering within the mind; indriya-arthān — objects of the senses; vimūḍha-ātmā — deluded in self; mithyā-ācāraḥ — false/hypocritical conduct.

☸ Commentary

Now this is Ṛṣi Bhārata's surgical strike — and on Varuthinī Ekādaśī, it cuts precisely where we pretend it doesn't matter. How many times have you restrained the hand from reaching the phone while the mind scrolled endlessly? How many fasting days have passed where you didn't eat grains but devoured fantasies? This is not sense control. This is performance.

Today's cosmic configuration amplifies this teaching mercilessly. Varuthinī Ekādaśī specifically targets the viṣayī tendency, the object-chasing reflex. Śatabhiṣā nakṣatra carries Varuna's gaze, the cosmic physician who sees through all pretense to the root dis-ease. Varuna rules the celestial waters, and here the metaphor becomes explicit: external fasting without internal purification is like washing the cup while the wine remains poisoned. Sukla yoga — "the bright" — offers clarity precisely to expose this split between action and intention.

The Sanskrit mithyā-ācāraḥ is worth contemplating. Mithyā is not merely "false" but that which appears real but lacks ultimate substance. Combined: practiced illusion. This is the practitioner who performs sādhana while the citta still runs toward viṣaya like water downhill. The body sits in meditation; the manas runs markets. The mouth doesn't speak gossip; the mind composes drama. This is worse than no practice at all, says Kṛṣṇa, because it breeds vimūḍhatā — delusion compounded upon delusion.

Arjuna's specific condition here matters. In Chapter 1, he wanted to renounce from confusion — mistaking bodily kinship for spiritual truth. Now Kṛṣṇa warns him against the opposite trap: mistaking bodily renunciation for spiritual achievement. Neither false activity nor false inactivity reaches yoga. True karma-yoga requires saṃyama where prāṇa, indriya, manas, buddhi, and ātmā align.

On this Ekādaśī, as śukla-bala peaks toward dvādaśī at 3:38 PM, the teaching presses: what are you actually fasting from? The stomach? Or the tendency to seek completion in external objects? Varuna's healing waters wash away what is artificial. But they require exposure.

🧘 Yoga Sūtra Connection

pratyāhāraḥ tat samprayāṇe cittasya svarūpānukāra eva indriyāṇāṃ pratyāhāraḥ || 2.54 ||

"When the senses withdraw from their objects as the mind follows the self (svarūpa), that is pratyāhāra." — YS 2.54

This is the technical precision missing in Bhagavad-gītā 3.6's vimūḍhatā. Patañjali describes true sense-withdrawal: not mechanical restraint of organs only, but integration — indriyāṇāṃ following cittasya following svarūpa. Where the Gītā's mithyācāraḥ fails is exactly here: external withdrawal without internal alignment.

Patañjali's grammar encodes this hierarchy: indriyāṇāṃ depends on cittasya, which depends on svarūpa. Each level subordinates to the next. Bhīṣma taught this exact sequence to Dhṛtarāṣṭra: objects depend on senses, senses on manas, manas on buddhi, buddhi on the Īśvara-sākṣī. To withdraw only at the sense-level without bringing citta into svarūpa-alignment is precisely the mithyācāra Kṛṣṇa exposes.

Śatabhiṣā's empty circle symbol — the void that contains hidden medicines — becomes the metaphor: pratyāhāra is not suppression but redirected flow. Varuthinī Ekādaśī's power is said to transform even the pañca-mahāpātaka — which are ultimately just this: external restraint masking internal possession.

🎯 Practice

The Honesty Check (Ekādaśī Protocol)

Every time you restrain an action today, ask: Where is my manas dwelling?

  • Phone not touched → mind checking it mentally? (mithyācāra)
  • Food not eaten → mind planning the feast after? (vimūḍhatā)
  • Words restrained → mind composing the speech? (saṃyama failed)

This is not guilt-seeking. It's diagnosis. Varuna's medicine only works on exposed wounds.

The Śatabhiṣā Practice: When you notice the mind chasing sense-objects while senses are restrained, perform one round of:

  1. Nāma-smaran — verbal: Hare Kṛṣṇa (redirects mouth)
  2. Viṣṇu-dhyāna — mental: visualize the four-armed form (redirects manas)
  3. Cakra-focus — subtle: bring awareness to ājñā (redirects indriya-aspect)

This is pratyāhāra — the indriyāṇām following cittasya toward svarūpa. This is upavasa — sitting near the Lord. This is real Ekādaśī.

The hundred physicians of Śatabhiṣā prescribe one medicine today: the congruence of outer restraint and inner devotion. Anything less is mithyā. Anything more is yoga.
