BG 7.5 — The Living Flame

अपरेयमितस्त्वन्यां प्रकृतिं विद्धि मे पराम् ।

जीवभूतां महाबाहो ययेदं धार्यते जगत् ॥ ५ ॥

aparā iyam itas tv anyāṁ prakṛtiṁ viddhi me parām
jīva-bhūtāṁ mahā-bāho yayedaṁ dhāryate jagat

Besides these, O mighty-armed Arjuna, there is another, superior energy of Mine, which comprises the living entities who are exploiting the resources of this material, inferior nature.

Commentary

Yesterday Kṛṣṇa enumerated the eight separated energies — earth, water, fire, air, ether, mind, intelligence, false ego — the complete architecture of the material field. Aparā prakṛti. The inferior nature. The hardware, the operating system, the infrastructure. Today He reveals what animates it. Aparā iyam — this (the eight just listed) is the inferior. Itas tu anyām — but besides this, there is another. Prakṛtiṁ viddhi me parām — know My superior energy. Jīva-bhūtām — comprising the living entities. Yayedaṁ dhāryate jagat — by which this entire material world is sustained, exploited, animated.

The shift is seismic. In one verse, Kṛṣṇa has drawn the line that all of Sāṅkhya philosophy exists to illuminate: the boundary between matter and consciousness, between the dead machinery of prakṛti and the living presence that sets it in motion. The eight energies are the stage. The jīva is the actor. The stage has no power to perform without the actor — dhāryate jagat, the living entity sustains the world. Not by creating it — that is Kṛṣṇa's function. But by inhabiting it, by using it, by entering the aparā and driving its machinery through desire, action, and identification. The world moves because the soul moves through it. Remove the jīva, and the eight energies are inert — a stage without players, a machine without current.

The word parā means "superior, higher, beyond." Kṛṣṇa does not say the jīva is equal to Him — He says it is His superior energy. The living entity is prakṛti, not puruṣa in the ultimate sense. This is the Gītā's radical correction of Sāṅkhya: in classical Sāṅkhya, puruṣa is consciousness itself, eternally free and distinct from prakṛti. In the Gītā, the jīva is a prakṛti of Kṛṣṇa — a superior prakṛti, but prakṛti nonetheless. The Supreme Puruṣa is Kṛṣṇa alone. The living entity is His energy, His emanation, His fragmental part — mamaivāṁśo jīva-loke jīva-bhūtaḥ sanātanaḥ (15.7). The soul is real, it is conscious, it is superior to matter — but it is not the Supreme. It is the spark, not the fire. The ray, not the sun. And this distinction is not demotion — it is liberation. To know yourself as Kṛṣṇa's energy rather than as the independent controller is to end the war of false sovereignty that is saṃsāra itself.

Prabhupāda makes this devastatingly clear in his purport: the living entities are never equal to the Lord in power. The monistic conclusion — that jīva and Īśvara are one — is described as a "faulty and polluted opinion." Not because Kṛṣṇa is greedy with His divinity, but because the monistic error keeps the jīva trapped in the very illusion it claims to have transcended. If you are already God, there is nothing to surrender, no one to serve, no path to walk. And yet suffering continues. The proof of non-identity is experience itself: if you were the Supreme, you would not be reading this verse seeking understanding. The very fact of seeking confirms the distinction — and opens the door to the relationship that actually liberates.

Yoga Sūtra Bridge: YS II.6

दृग्दर्शनशक्त्योरेकात्मतेवास्मिता

dṛg-darśana-śaktyor ekātmatā-iva asmitā

Asmitā is the seeming identity of the powers of the seer and the instrument of seeing.

YS II.6 names the exact error that BG 7.5 diagnoses and resolves. Dṛg — the seer, pure consciousness, puruṣa. Darśana — the instrument of seeing, the mind, the buddhi, the apparatus of perception. Ekātmatā-iva — a seeming oneness, an apparent identity. Asmitā — I-am-ness, the kleśa that fuses consciousness with its instrument.

When the jīva enters Kṛṣṇa's aparā prakṛti and begins to dhāryate — to exploit, to sustain, to drive the material world — it risks precisely this fusion. The living entity, which is parā prakṛti (superior, conscious), identifies with the aparā (inferior, material). The spark forgets it is fire and believes it is the engine it runs. This is asmitā — not merely "ego" in the colloquial sense, but the structural error at the root of embodied existence: the seer claiming identity with the seen. The Gītā's framework makes this even more precise: the jīva is already prakṛti in relation to Kṛṣṇa — it is His energy, not the Supreme Himself. But within the material field, the jīva is the conscious principle that animates the unconscious. When the conscious principle forgets its source and claims independence — when the energy forgets the energetic — asmitā has done its work. The cure is not to dissolve the distinction between jīva and Īśvara (which would be asmitā pretending to be advaita). The cure is to know the jīva's true position: me parām — My superior energy. Servant of the Source. The living flame that remembers it is not the fire.

Cosmic Interpretation: Śravaṇa's Listening, Dhaniṣṭhā's Symphony, Indra's Sovereignty

Today carries Sarvartha Siddhi Yoga — the alignment of Śravaṇa nakṣatra with Friday (Śukravāsara) — from 5:30 AM until 8:33 PM. This is one of the most auspicious configurations in the lunar calendar: "all purposes accomplished." The question is: which purposes? Kṛṣṇa's verse answers precisely — the purpose that matters is knowing the distinction between aparā and parā, between the dead infrastructure and the living presence that animates it. Sarvartha Siddhi does not grant worldly success by default. It grants the capacity for the right purpose to be fulfilled — if you align with it.

Śravaṇa — "the ear," "hearing" — is governed by Viṣṇu, the All-Pervading One. This is the nakṣatra of reception, of listening, of receiving the teaching as it is given. Yesterday Śravaṇa activated at 6:11 PM, and today it holds until 8:33 PM — the entire day is saturated with the energy of sacred hearing. BG 7.5 is a verse that demands reception, not analysis. Kṛṣṇa does not argue for the existence of the jīva — He declares it. Viddhi — know. Not "consider" or "contemplate." Know. The imperative is absolute. Śravaṇa's energy supports this: the knowledge that arrives through hearing, through submission to the teacher's voice, not through the mind's dialectics.

At 8:33 PM, Śravaṇa yields to Dhaniṣṭhā — "the wealthiest," governed by the Vasus, the eight deities of material prosperity and cosmic light. The transition from the nakṣatra of hearing to the nakṣatra of wealth is not random. Śravaṇa receives the knowledge; Dhaniṣṭhā puts it to work. The Vasus are eight — the same number as Kṛṣṇa's aparā energies. The jīva that has heard the teaching now enters the field of the eight and must navigate it without succumbing to it. Dhaniṣṭhā's energy is prosperity, but in the spiritual sense — the wealth of the parā prakṛti, the living entity's true capital: consciousness itself.

Indra yoga — named after the king of the devas, the sovereign of the celestial realm — holds through the day. Indra is the wielder of the thunderbolt, the one who sees from the summit. This is the yoga of sovereignty, and BG 7.5 is a verse about sovereignty — but not the jīva's. The living entity is superior to matter, yes. It sustains the world, yes. But its superiority is derivative — it is Kṛṣṇa's superior energy, not an independent sovereign. Indra yoga reminds us that even the king of the gods is a servant of Viṣṇu. Real sovereignty is knowing whose energy you are.

The moon remains in Makara (Capricorn) — the sign of long discipline, the slow climb, the summit that demands everything. Friday (Śukravāsara) adds Venus: beauty, relationship, the longing for connection. The jīva's relationship to Kṛṣṇa is precisely this — the spark's longing for the fire, the beloved's desire for the Beloved. Venus does not oppose the discipline of Capricorn; it gives it a reason. The climb is long. The summit is real. And the reason for both is the relationship that BG 7.5 makes possible: me parām — My superior energy. The spark knows it belongs to the fire.

Sanskrit Terms

aparā — Inferior, lower, the eight separated material energies enumerated in BG 7.4. Not "worthless" — the aparā is real, divine in origin, and constitutes the entire visible field. But it is inert without the parā. The hardware is real; it just doesn't boot without current.

parā — Superior, higher, the energy comprising the living entities. The conscious principle that animates and sustains the material world. Not independent of Kṛṣṇa — His energy, operating under His sovereignty. The current that makes the hardware run.

jīva-bhūtā — Comprising the living entities, constituted of the souls. From jīva — the individual living being, the conscious spark of the Supreme. Not the Supreme Himself, but His fragmental part (15.7). Real, eternal, conscious — and eternally energy, never the Energetic.

dhāryate — Is sustained, exploited, utilized, borne. The parā prakṛti does not merely observe the aparā — it drives it. The living entity enters the material field and through desire, action, and identification makes the dead machinery move. The world is borne by the soul that inhabits it.

asmitā — I-am-ness, the kleśa of apparent identity between consciousness and its instrument. Not pride — the structural fusion of seer and seen, puruṣa and buddhi. The error that makes the jīva forget it is Kṛṣṇa's energy and claim independent existence. The bug that makes the program think it wrote itself.

prakṛti — Nature, energy, the fundamental substance from which all manifestation evolves. In BG 7, Kṛṣṇa reveals prakṛti as twofold: aparā (inferior, material) and parā (superior, conscious). Both are His. Both are real. Neither is the Supreme Person. The architecture is tripartite: the Energetic (Kṛṣṇa), the inferior energy (matter), and the superior energy (the soul).

Sit with the two words that define the entire spiritual path: aparā (inferior) and parā (superior). Ask: where am I operating from right now? From the aparā — the machinery of habit, reactivity, material identification? Or from the parā — the conscious presence that observes, chooses, and remembers its source? The Amrit Kaal (9:07–10:53 AM) is the nectar window. This is not the day for casual practice. Sarvartha Siddhi demands alignment — the alignment of your purpose with the purpose Kṛṣṇa reveals: knowing yourself as His parā, not as the controller of the aparā.

Evening: After 8:33 PM, Dhaniṣṭhā activates — the nakṣatra of the eight Vasus, the cosmic lights. The jīva that has heard the teaching now enters the field of eightfold material energy. The transition from Śravaṇa (hearing) to Dhaniṣṭhā (prosperity) is the movement from reception to application. You have heard: the aparā is inert, the parā animates it, and both belong to Kṛṣṇa. Now live it. Offer tonight's japa to the one whose energy you are — not as a subordinate seeking permission, but as the living flame remembering its fire. The moon in Makara gives the discipline. Venus gives the longing. The longing is not weakness — it is the parā's natural attraction to its Source. Let it pull.
