Bhagavad Gita Chapter 7, Verse 6

एतद्योनीनि भूतानि सर्वाणीत्युपधारय । अहं कृत्स्नस्य जगत: प्रभव: प्रलयस्तथा ॥ ६ ॥
etad-yonīni bhūtāni
sarvāṇīty upadhāraya
ahaṁ kṛtsnasya jagataḥ
prabhavaḥ pralayas tathā
All created beings have their source in these two natures.
Of all that is material and all that is spiritual in this world,
know for certain that I am both the origin and the dissolution.
Everything that exists is a product of matter and spirit. Spirit is the basic field of creation, and matter is created by spirit. Spirit is not created at a certain stage of material development. Rather, this material world is manifested only on the basis of spiritual energy. This material body is developed because spirit is present within matter; a child grows gradually to boyhood and then to manhood because that superior energy, spirit soul, is present. Similarly, the entire cosmic manifestation of the gigantic universe is developed because of the presence of the Supersoul, Viṣṇu. Therefore spirit and matter, which combine to manifest this gigantic universal form, are originally two energies of the Lord, and consequently the Lord is the original cause of everything. A fragmental part and parcel of the Lord, namely the living entity, may be the cause of a big skyscraper, a big factory, or even a big city, but he cannot be the cause of a big universe. The cause of the big universe is the big soul, or the Supersoul. And Kṛṣṇa, the Supreme, is the cause of both the big and small souls. Therefore He is the original cause of all causes. This is confirmed in the Kaṭha Upaniṣad (2.2.13). Nityo nityānāṁ cetanaś cetanānām.

Cosmic Interpretation

The day begins with Pratipada (first tithi of waxing moon) under Mrigashirsha nakshatra (symbolized by deer's head, representing seeking and searching), under the influence of Ganda yoga (obstacles/poison). This represents the initial stage of spiritual seeking where one encounters obstacles and confusion.

At 9:38 AM, the nakshatra shifts to Ardra (ruled by Rudra, the storm god), representing destruction and transformation - the necessary breaking down of old patterns to make way for new growth.

At 7:01 PM, tithi shifts from Pratipada to Dwitiya, and at 7:08 PM yoga shifts from Ganda (obstacle) to Vridhi (growth/increase). This marks the transition from struggle to progress, from dissolution to manifestation.

The day features Chandra Darshan (first sighting of the new moon), symbolizing the renewal of consciousness after the dark period of Amavasya. Somvar Vrat (Monday fasting) honors Lord Shiva as the destroyer and transformer. Mithuna Sankranti (sun entering Gemini) represents the transition into a new solar month, emphasizing duality and communication.

BG 7.6 perfectly encapsulates this cosmic rhythm: "All created beings have their source in these two natures. Of all that is material and all that is spiritual in this world, know for certain that I am both the origin and the dissolution." The verse reminds us that behind the cycles of creation and destruction, seeking and fulfillment, obstacle and growth, lies the unchanging source - Krishna as both prabhava (origin) and pralaya (dissolution).

Just as the moon disappears in Amavasya only to return as Chandra Darshan, just as Ardra's storm clears the way for renewal, just as Ganda's obstacles give way to Vridhi's growth - the devotee learns to see the divine hand in all transitions, recognizing that every ending is a new beginning in the eternal cycle of cosmic manifestation.

Connections & Key Concepts

Yoga Sutra Connection

YS I.3: tadā draṣṭuḥ svarūpe ‘vasthānam
When that is accomplished, the seer abides in its own true nature

Connection: Just as BG 7.6 reveals Krishna as the source and dissolution of all natures, Yoga Sutra I.3 describes the state where pure consciousness (purusha) rests in its true nature, separate from the changing manifestations of prakriti. Both point to realizing the unchanging reality behind cosmic fluctuations.

Sankhya Concepts

  • purusha-prakriti dualism
  • prabhava-pralaya (origin-dissolution)
  • maya as divine energy
  • the two natures as superior and inferior prakriti (BG 7.4-5)

Key Sanskrit Terms

  • etad-yonīni — these two natures (material and spiritual)
  • bhūtāni — all created beings, elements
  • prabhavaḥ — source of manifestation, origin
  • pralayaḥ — annihilation, dissolution
  • jagataḥ — the world, cosmic manifestation
  • kṛtsnasya — all-inclusive, total
  • upadhāraya — know for certain, realize
