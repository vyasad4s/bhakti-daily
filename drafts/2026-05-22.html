सुहृन्मित्रार्युदासीनमध्यस्थद्वेष्यबन्धुषु ।

साधुष्वपि च पापेषु समबुद्धिर्विशिष्यते ॥ ९ ॥

 

suhṛn-mitrāry-udāsīna-
madhyastha-dveṣya-bandhuṣu
sādhuṣv api ca pāpeṣu
sama-buddhir viśiṣyate

 

A person is considered still further advanced when he regards honest well-wishers, affectionate benefactors, the neutral, mediators, the envious, friends and enemies, the pious and the sinners all with an equal mind.

 

Kṛṣṇa catalogs the entire social field in a single verse: well-wishers, benefactors, enemies, neutrals, mediators, the envious, relatives, the pious, the sinners — nine categories of human relationship compressed into one Sanskrit compound. This is not a list. This is a topology. Every person you encounter falls into one of these slots. Your mother: su-hṛt. Your rival at work: dveṣya. The coworker who neither helps nor harms: udāsīna. The friend who mediates your conflict: madhya-stha. The relative who envies your success: paradoxically both bandhu and dveṣya — and that paradox is precisely the point. The mind categorizes. It sorts people into threat and resource, friend and foe, worthy and unworthy. This sorting is prakṛti's default — the guṇas allocating attachment and aversion with mechanical efficiency. Kṛṣṇa's demand is surgical: sama-buddhiḥ. Not sama-darśana (equal vision, verse 29) — that comes later. This is equal intelligence. The buddhi — the very instrument of discrimination — must hold all categories as one. The mind's sorting algorithm must be overwritten. Not destroyed — transcended.

 

This verse sits between 6.7 (the self-controlled yogī finds sukha in snow and sun) and 6.10 (the yogī must sit alone in seclusion). The sequence is precise: first, equanimity toward external conditions (6.7); then, equanimity toward people (6.9); then, withdrawal into solitude (6.10). Kṛṣṇa builds the architecture from outside in — first you stabilize against weather and circumstance, then you stabilize against the far more destabilizing force of human relationship, and only then are you ready for the inner work of seated meditation. Most aspiring yogīs invert this sequence: they want the cushion before they've handled the conflict. Kṛṣṇa says: deal with the envious relative first. Then sit. The purport drives the same point through the example of the advanced devotee — one who has transcended the dualities of honor and dishonor, friends and enemies. This is not passive tolerance. Viśiṣyate means "surpasses" — the yogī doesn't merely endure all these types; he is elevated beyond the framework that makes them different. The categories still exist in prakṛti. The yogī simply no longer operates by their logic.

 

🔥 YOGA SŪTRA CONNECTION: YS I.33 — maitrī-karuṇā-muditā-upekṣāṇāṁ sukha-duḥkha-puṇyāpuṇya-viṣayāṇāṁ bhāvanātaś citta-prasādanam
"By cultivating attitudes of friendliness toward the happy, compassion toward the suffering, gladness toward the virtuous, and equanimity toward the non-virtuous, the mind becomes lucid and pleasant." Patañjali's fourfold practice maps directly onto Kṛṣṇa's nine-category field. Where Kṛṣṇa gives the topology (who exists in your world), Patañjali gives the method (what attitude to cultivate toward each). The happy: maitrī (friendliness — no jealousy). The suffering: karuṇā (compassion — no contempt). The virtuous: muditā (gladness — no rivalry). The non-virtuous: upekṣā (equanimity — no condemnation). YS I.33 is the how; BG 6.9 is the what. Together they form a complete protocol: recognize the categories (Kṛṣṇa), apply the correct attitude (Patañjali), and the result is citta-prasādanam — a lucid, pleasant mind. This is not optional psychology. This is the prerequisite for every subsequent step in both systems. A mind still flickering between attachment and aversion toward people cannot meditate. Period.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

Sama-buddhi (feminine noun compound) — equal intelligence; sama (equal/same) + buddhi (intelligence/discriminative faculty); not "equal feeling" — feelings can lie; buddhi is the discriminative faculty that precedes feeling; when buddhi holds all categories as one, feeling follows; the yogī doesn't suppress emotion — he restructures the cognitive architecture that generates it

Su-hṛt (masculine noun) — well-wisher by nature; su (good) + hṛt (heart); the person whose heart naturally wishes you well — not through obligation or contract but through disposition; this is the deepest category of ally because it operates below consciousness

Madhya-stha (masculine noun) — mediator, neutral party; madhya (middle) + stha (standing); the one who stands in the middle; not passive but positioned — the mediator chooses the center, which is itself a stance; the yogī's sama-buddhi mirrors this position: standing at the center of all polarities without being pulled to either pole

Dveṣya (adjective, used as noun) — the envious, the hateful; from dveṣa (hatred/aversion); those who direct rāga-dveṣa at you; the critical test of sama-buddhi is not how you treat the su-hṛt but how you regard the dveṣya — the mind's default is retaliation; the yogī's override is equanimity

Viśiṣyate (verb, passive) — is distinguished, surpasses, excels; from viśiṣ (to distinguish); the yogī doesn't merely achieve equanimity — he surpasses the entire framework of comparison; the verb is passive because this surpassing is not the ego's achievement but the natural consequence of buddhi's restructuring

Upekṣā (feminine noun) — equanimity, overlooking, non-reactive awareness; from upa (near) + īkṣ (to look at); to look closely at something without reacting; not avoidance — attentive neutrality; you see the non-virtuous person clearly, you understand their condition, and you withhold the reactive judgment that would destabilize your own citta
