Today's Verse

Bhagavad-gītā 3.9
yajñārthāt karmaṇo 'nyatra loko 'yaṁ karma-bandhanaḥ tad-arthaṁ karma kaunteya mukta-saṅgaḥ samācara
Work done as a sacrifice for Viṣṇu has to be performed; otherwise work causes bondage in this material world. Therefore, O son of Kuntī, perform your prescribed duties for His satisfaction, and in that way you will always remain free from bondage.
All work produces reactions — this is the mechanics of karma. Good work binds; evil work binds. Only work offered as sacrifice to Viṣṇu becomes liberating. The varṇāśrama system, all Vedic rituals, and ultimately Kṛṣṇa consciousness itself exist for this single purpose: to transform binding action into liberating service. Perform your duty not for results, but for His satisfaction — this is the art of true freedom.

Pūrva Aṣāḍhā means "first unconquered" — presided over by the Waters (Āpaḥ), the primordial medium that cannot be destroyed. Water conquers fire yet nourishes it; it takes the shape of any vessel yet remains itself; it flows around obstacles yet reaches the ocean inevitably. This is the spirit of karma-yoga: to work without being conquered by the results.

Śiva Yoga is among the most auspicious of the twenty-seven yogas — it transforms adverse conditions into fortunate outcomes. Combined with Aṣāḍhā's invincible water-energy, today's configuration says: no circumstance can defeat the one who offers action as sacrifice.

The transition into Kṛṣṇa Aṣṭamī (Kalāṣṭamī) honors Kṛṣṇa as Kāla, time itself, the all-attractive dissolution that swallows all binding reactions for the surrendered soul.

Why this verse today? BG 3.9 reveals the alchemy of sacrifice — how ordinary karma becomes yajña. Pūrva Aṣāḍhā's "unconquered" quality requires this teaching: work performed for Viṣṇu cannot be bound, just as water cannot be destroyed. The Śiva yoga ensures transformation of reaction into liberation. This is not theory — Arjuna stood on the battlefield when Kṛṣṇa spoke these words.

Yoga Sūtra Connection

YS II.1 — Kriyā Yoga Defined
tapaḥ-svādhyāyeśvara-praṇidhānāni kriyā-yogaḥ
Kriyā yoga consists of tapas (discipline/austerity), svādhyāya (self-study/scriptural study), and īśvara praṇidhāna (surrender to the Supreme).

Patañjali's kriyā yoga is precisely what Kṛṣṇa prescribes in BG 3.9. Tapas — performing duty despite pain or pleasure. Svādhyāya — understanding through Vedic knowledge that Viṣṇu is the ultimate enjoyer. Īśvara praṇidhāna — the actual offering of work for His satisfaction.

Where modern yoga dilutes kriyā to mere physical techniques, Patañjali and Kṛṣṇa agree: it is the triad of transformative action that burns the seeds of karma. The Bhakti-marga of BG 3.9 fulfills the Raja-yoga of YS 2.1 — both demand the same three elements, both lead to mukti-saṅga (liberation from attachment).

Sanskrit Terms

yajña — sacrifice, literally "worship" or "offering." In Vedic culture, the central act of cosmic exchange: humans give to the gods, gods give to humans. In Kṛṣṇa consciousness, it means any action performed for Viṣṇu's satisfaction.
karma-bandhanaḥ — the bondage of action. Every act not offered to Viṣṇu produces a reaction that binds the soul to the cycle of birth and death. Like legacy code that keeps generating dependencies you never asked for.
mukta-saṅgaḥ — liberated from attachment. Mukta = freed; saṅga = association/attachment. The one who works while remaining unbound by results — like a programmer who ships code without ego-attachment to features.
samācara — perform perfectly, execute completely. Not half-measures. The Sanskrit implies thoroughness — do your duty as it is meant to be done, not as convenience permits.
kaunteya — son of Kuntī, addressing Arjuna. Kuntī means "spear" — the piercing intellect that can penetrate māyā. Arjuna represents the jīva with discriminative capacity who still needs instruction.

The Practice

Today's sādhana: Identify one duty you've been avoiding or one action you've been performing for results. Transform it into yajña.

Method: Before acting, internally offer: "This is for You, not for me." After acting, release attachment to outcome. The Aṣāḍhā energy supports this — water doesn't cling to the riverbank, it flows toward the sea. Your work should flow toward Viṣṇu without clinging to the shores of "my success" or "my failure."

Warning: This is not an excuse for sloppy work or spiritual bypassing. Samācara means execute with excellence — then release the results. The Śiva yoga today means even difficult duties become auspicious when offered.
