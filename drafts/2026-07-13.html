Bhagavad Gita Chapter 9, Verse 22

अनन्याश्चिन्तयन्तो मां ये जना: पर्युपासते । तेषां नित्याभियुक्तानां योगक्षेमं वहाम्यहम् ॥ २२ ॥
ananyāś cintayanto māṁ ye janāḥ paryupāsate
teṣāṁ nityābhiyuktānāṁ yoga-kṣemaṁ vahāmy aham
But those who always worship Me with exclusive devotion, meditating on My transcendental form — to them I carry what they lack, and I preserve what they have.

This verse captures the reciprocal nature of bhakti-yoga: the devotee offers ananya (exclusive, undivided) devotion, and the Lord reciprocates by providing yoga (what is lacking for spiritual progress) and protecting kṣema (what has already been attained).

Today’s pañcāṅga highlights a powerful interplay of energies that mirrors this exchange. The day begins under the nakṣatra Ārdrā — the star of Rudra, the storm god whose fierce winds clear away obstruction and impurity. This corresponds to the vyāghāta (obstacle‑removing) yoga that dominates the early morning, a force that breaks through the knots of karma that bind the heart. As the day progresses, the gentle, restorative nakṣatra Punarvasu takes over, bringing the promise of return and renewal — the very kṣema (preservation) that the Lord promises to those who remain steadfast.

The weekday, Somavāra (Monday), is governed by the Moon, the presiding deity of the mind and emotions. With the Moon transiting Mithuna (Gemini), the dual nature of the mind is highlighted: it can be pulled between distraction and focus, between doubt and faith. Yet the steady, unshakable quality of Dhruva yoga (present in the early hours) offers a fixed point — like the pole star — around which the mind can anchor in unwavering remembrance of the Divine.

Thus the day’s cosmic rhythm outlines a clear path: first, the turbulent Ārdrā/vyāghāta forces dissolve the accumulated impurities that obscure devotion; then, the stabilizing Dhruva sustains the practitioner’s resolve; finally, the nurturing Punarvasu/Harshana cycle brings the joy of fulfilled aspiration and the preservation of gains. In this flow, the practitioner’s exclusive devotion (ananya bhakti) is not a one‑sided offering but a dynamic exchange: the clearing of obstacles makes space for the divine to flow in, and the sustaining grace protects the fruits of that flow.

This is not a passive promise but an active, intimate reciprocity. The Lord does not merely watch from a distance; He carries (vahāmi) what is lacking — whether it be courage, understanding, or material support — and He guards what has been attained, preventing regression. The devotee’s role is to maintain the unbroken stream of remembrance, trusting that the universe itself conspires to support that surrender.
