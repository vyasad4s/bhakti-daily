🏹 BG 7.6 | 2026-06-16
एतद्योनीनि भूतानि सर्वाणीत्युपधारय । > अहं कृत्स्नस्य जगत: प्रभव: प्रलयस्तथा ॥ ६ ॥
etad-yonīni bhūtāni sarvāṇīty upadhāraya ahaṁ kṛtsnasya jagataḥ prabhavaḥ pralayas tathā
All created beings have their source in these two natures. Of all that is material and all that is spiritual in this world, know for certain that I am both the origin and the dissolution.
Today’s tithi is Dwitiya (second day of waxing moon), symbolizing the initial stage of manifestation after the new moon’s darkness. The nakṣatra Ardra (ruled by Rudra, the storm god) governs, representing destruction and transformation—the necessary breaking down of old patterns to make way for new growth. The yoga Vriddhi (growth/increase) is active, signifying expansion, prosperity, and the fruition of efforts initiated during the previous phase. Dwitiya’s energy of beginnings aligns with BG 7.6’s revelation that Krishna is both the source (prabhava) and dissolution (pralaya) of all material and spiritual energies. Ardra’s transformative force mirrors the verse’s declaration that everything originates from and returns to the divine duality of prakṛti (matter) and puruṣa (spirit). Vriddhi yoga’s expansive quality reflects how recognizing Krishna as the ultimate cause leads to spiritual growth and the dissolution of illusion. Just as Dwitiya follows Amavasya’s darkness with the first sliver of moonlight, Ardra’s storm clears stagnation, and Vriddhi brings increase—so too does the devotee progress from ignorance to knowledge by realizing that all existence rests in Krishna’s twofold nature. The verse invites us to “know for certain” (upadhāraya) this unchanging truth behind cosmic fluctuations.
🔥 Yoga Sūtra Connection: YS II.28 yogāṅgānuṣṭhānādam śuddhiḥ kṣaye vijñāna-dīptiḥ By the practice of the limbs of yoga, the impurities dwindle and there dawns the light of wisdom. BG 7.6 and YS II.28 both address the removal of ignorance to reveal true knowledge. In the Gītā, Krishna declares Himself as the origin and dissolution of all natures, urging the seeker to realize this truth definitively. In the Yoga Sūtras, the consistent practice of the eight limbs purifies the mind, allowing the light of wisdom (vijñāna-dīptiḥ) to emerge. The connection lies in the understanding that when we dissolve false identifications with matter and spirit through disciplined practice, we abide in the awareness of Krishna as the sole source—just as the verse declares, “know for certain that I am both the origin and the dissolution.”
