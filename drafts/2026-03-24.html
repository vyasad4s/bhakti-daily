आश्चर्यवत्पश्यति कश्चिदेन
माश्चर्यवद्वदिति तथैव चान्यः ।
आश्चर्यवच्चैनमन्यः श‍ृणोति
श्रुत्वाप्येनं वेद न चैव कश्चित् ॥२९॥

 

āścarya-vat paśyati kaścid enam
āścarya-vad vadati tathaiva cānyaḥ
āścarya-vac cainam anyaḥ śṛṇoti
śrutvāpy enaṁ veda na caiva kaścit

 

Some look upon the soul as amazing, some describe him as amazing, and some hear of him as amazing, while others, even after hearing about him, cannot understand him at all.

 

Kṛṣṇa has been describing the soul's nature: unbreakable, unburnable, invisible, inconceivable. Now He speaks of how beings encounter this truth. The soul is āścarya — the astonishing, the marvelous, that which stops the mind in wonder. Not everyone meets this wonder in the same way.

 

The progression is deliberate. First paśyati — someone sees, directly perceives the soul's nature. This is the highest: direct darshan, the flash of recognition where puruṣa reveals itself to itself through purified buddhi. Then vadati — someone speaks of this vision, passing it through the medium of words, becoming the spokesman of the truth. Then śṛṇoti — another hears these words, receiving the testimony second-hand, building understanding through sound and transmission.

 

But here is the crux: śrutvāpy enaṁ veda na caiva kaścit — even after hearing, some do not know. The soul cannot be captured in transmission. Words can point but not contain. Hearsay knowledge is not direct knowledge. Mrigashīrṣa nakṣatra — the deer's head — captures this perfectly: the deer catches the scent but cannot locate its source. It wanders, following the fragrance, always near but never arriving. This is the condition of those who hear of the soul but do not put in the work of austere practice to know it directly.

 

Saubhāgya yoga dominates today's energy — auspiciousness, divine grace that makes progress possible. But grace meets us where we are. Some are granted the eyes to see. Others receive the gift of speech, becoming vessels for the teachings. Others still are given the ears to hear, planting seeds that may sprout in futures beyond this life. And some — perhaps protected by their own unreadiness — are given the blessing of not-yet-knowing, preserved for a time when they can truly receive.

 

🔥 Patañjali Yoga Sūtra I.7: pratyakṣānumānāgamāḥ pramāṇāni — "Right knowledge consists of sense perception, inference, and verbal testimony." The three pramāṇas correspond to the three modes of encountering the soul: pratyakṣa (direct seeing) for those who look with wonder, anumāna (discriminative reasoning) for those who describe with precision, and āgama (authoritative testimony) for those who hear with faith. Yet Patañjali's fourth state in I.8 — viparyaya, error born of ignorance — explains why some, even after hearing, cannot understand. The wrong knowledge of identifying self-as-body blocks recognition. Until this fundamental error is corrected through sustained practice, the soul remains hidden in plain sight.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

āścarya (noun) — that which causes astonishment; the marvelous; the incomprehensible wonder that shatters ordinary perception.

paśyati (verb) — to see, perceive directly; the root dṛṣṭi carries the weight of immediate vision, not mediated by concept.

vadati (verb) — to speak, describe, articulate; from the root vad, implying the competent expression of what has been perceived.

śṛṇoti (verb) — to hear, attend to, receive through the ear; the root śru means to flow toward, indicating the receptive posture of the student.

veda (verb) — to know with certainty; from the root vid, giving the name to the Vedas themselves; direct knowledge as opposed to hearsay.
