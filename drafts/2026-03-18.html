🏹 VERSE CONTENT

Source: Bhagavad-gītā 2.70

Date: March 18, 2026

Sanskrit:

आपूर्यमाणमचलप्रतिष्ठं

समुद्रमापः प्रविशन्ति यद्वत् ।

तद्वत्कामा यं प्रविशन्ति सर्वे

स शान्तिमाप्‍नोति न कामकामी ॥ ७० ॥

Transliteration:

āpūryamāṇam acala-pratiṣṭhaṁ samudram āpaḥ praviśanti yadvat |
tadvat kāmā yaṁ praviśanti sarve sa śāntim āpnoti na kāma-kāmī ||

Translation:

A person who is not disturbed by the incessant flow of desires — that enter like rivers into the ocean, which is ever being filled but is always still — can alone achieve peace, and not the man who strives to satisfy such desires.

Commentary:

apāpaviddhaḥ (adjective) — "not disturbed," "undisturbed"; lit. "un-struck," "un-violated". In Sāṅkhya terms: the puruṣa (conscious witness) remains unaffected by the perpetual injections of prakṛtic activity. The hacker metaphor: a process running at kernel level that cannot be interrupted by user-space signals. The ocean doesn't refuse the rivers — it receives them without modification.

The rainy season dumps phenomenal volumes into the ocean, yet the ocean keeps its boundaries, its salt content, its vast unsubtractable depth. Rivers enter, are contained, and continue. This is not denial or suppression — it is fullness. The ocean is full already. Desires, like rivers, are prakṛtic phenomena entering the field of puruṣa-consciousness. They affect nothing because they are received by what needs nothing. This is śānti — not the cultivated calm of a vacation, but the unmovable peace of fulfilled existence.

The Kṛṣṇa-conscious devotee is not trying to kill desire. That is the sannyāsī's error — desire denied is desire deferred. The devotee is already full. The ocean doesn't become "more ocean" when rivers pour in. Rivers, noise, traffic, ambitions, rejections — these enter and leave no trace, like water poured into water. This is acala-pratiṣṭha: the steady foundation that does not tremble. Not because it is defended, but because it cannot be reached.

Yoga Sutra Connection:

🔥 YS I.15 — dṛṣṭānuśravika-viṣaya-vitṛṣnasya vaśīkāra-saṁjñā vairāgyam

("Dispassion is the controlled consciousness of one who is without craving for sense objects, whether actually perceived or described in scripture.")

The Bhakta follows the path of īśvara-praṇidhāna—— devotion as surrender. The Rāja-yogī follows abhyāsa-vairāgya—— practice plus dispassion. Here they meet at the shoreline. Vyāsa comments that vairāgya requires indifference to sense objects "whether in their absence or presence." This is the ocean's secret: it doesn't gain or lose when rivers arrive. The vaśīkāra (mastery) of I.15 is not domination — it is the effortless sovereignty of the one who is already complete.

Sanskrit Terms:

  • āpūryamāṇam (present passive participle) — "being filled continuously"; from ā-pṛ, to fill. The ocean's volume is maintained by ceaseless input. Unlike the kāmī (desirer) who empties with every indulgence, the apāpaviddhaḥ remains perpetually resourced.
  • acala-pratiṣṭham (compound adjective) — "steady foundation," "immovable base"; acala (unmoving) + pratiṣṭha (standing firm). The pratiṣṭha is the root access of consciousness, the puruṣa-level permission that no prakṛtic process can revoke.
  • samudra (masculine noun) — "ocean, gathering-place of waters"; from sam (together) + udra (water). The metaphor isn't decorative — it describes saguṇa Brahman as the field containing all phenomena without being modified by them.
  • vaśīkāra (masculine noun) — "mastery, control"; from vaś (to wish, to be subject to) + kāra (making). Not the will-to-power of forceful domination, but the effortless sovereignty of the full. The ocean doesn't "resist" rivers — it is unmodified by them.
  • śānti (feminine noun) — "peace, tranquility, cessation of agitation"; from śam (to calm). Not the absence of stimulus but the presence of completion. The goal of Sāṅkhya and the fruit of yoga.

Special Observances:

Amāvāsyā (New Moon) — The monthly dissolution point. Prakṛti withdraws into her unmanifest state before re-emerging as Śukla Pakṣa. Time of ancestral offering, introspection, and witnessing the guṇas in suspension.

Cosmic Interpretation:

The Amāvāsyā moon is invisible — not absent, but withdrawn. Like the ocean that receives rivers without being seen to change, the dark moon absorbs solar light without reflecting it. This is the cosmic mirror of apāpaviddhaḥ: the capacity to contain phenomena without displaying reaction.

Pūrva Bhādrapadā, ruled by Ajaikapāda (Śiva's one-footed ascetic form), carries the fire of sacrifice and self-discipline. The nakṣatra's deity is known for standing on a single leg — the epitome of acala-pratiṣṭha, unmovable foundation. As the day progresses to Uttara Bhādrapadā, the energy shifts from fierce renunciation to sustained endurance.

The moon's transition from Kumbha (intellectual detachment) to Mīna (emotional surrender) precisely mirrors BG 2.70. Kumbha is the fixed sign of the thinker who stands apart — observing prakṛti without immersion. Mīna is dissolution itself, the oceanic consciousness that contains all rivers. The devotee moves from detached observation to surrendered fullness. Both are stillness.

Verse Selection Rationale:

BG 2.70 — āpūryamāṇam acala-pratiṣṭhaṁ samudram āpaḥ praviśanti...

Amāvāsyā is the ocean receiving rivers. The day's panchāṅga shows the precise conditions described in the verse: dissolution (Amāvāsyā), unmovable foundation (Ajaikapāda's single leg), and the shift from observation to full containment (Kumbha → Mīna). The ocean doesn't become "more wet" when rivers enter. The devotee doesn't become "more disturbed" when phenomena arise. This is not an achievement. It is the recognition of what already is.
