🏹 VERSE CONTENT

Source: BG 2.14

Date: March 9, 2026

Sanskrit:

मात्रास्पर्शास्तु कौन्तेय शीतोष्णसुखदुःखदाः ।

आगमापायिनोऽनित्यास्तांस्तितिक्षस्व भारत ॥

Transliteration:

mātrā-sparśās tu kaunteya śītoṣṇa-sukha-duḥkha-dāḥ
āgamāpāyino 'nityās tāṁs titikṣasva bhārata

Translation:

O son of Kuntī, the nonpermanent appearance of happiness and distress, and their disappearance in due course, are like the appearance and disappearance of winter and summer seasons. They arise from sense perception, O scion of Bharata, and one must learn to tolerate them without being disturbed.

Commentary:

This is the titikṣā verse — not passive endurance but active forbearance as a discipline. Kṛṣṇa tells Arjuna: sensations are weather. Śīta (cold) and uṣṇa (heat) are not moral failings; they are mātrā-sparśa — contact between the senses and their objects. The compiler doesn't crash when the server room temperature fluctuates. The operating system continues executing its dharma.

The metaphor is surgical: seasons arrive (āgama) and depart (apāya) with mechanical regularity. Your duty is not contingent on favorable conditions. A kṣatriya fights in freezing Māgha (January-February) and a woman cooks in boiling Jyeṣṭha (May-June). The body reports sensation; the soul doesn't negotiate with the report.

Titikṣasva — the imperative "you must tolerate" — isn't asking Arjuna to grit his teeth. It's the recognition that viveka (discernment) operates independently of prakṛti's fluctuations. Pain and pleasure are guṇa-vṛttis — modifications of nature's modes — not threats to consciousness itself. The titikṣā of this verse is the prerequisite for BG 2.15's sama-duḥkha-sukha — the equanimity that makes one eligible for liberation. You don't wait for pleasant sensations to practice. You practice through the sensations.

Yoga Sutra Connection:

🔥 YS II.1 — tapaḥ-svādhyāyeśvara-praṇidhānāni kriyā-yogaḥ — "Austerity, study of sacred texts, and surrender to Īśvara constitute the yoga of action."

BG 2.14's titikṣā is the practical application of tapas — not self-mortification but the disciplined tolerance of discomfort without identifying with it. Patañjali's kriyā-yoga begins with tapas: heating, purification, the burning away of impurities. Kṛṣṇa's instruction to "tolerate the nonpermanent" is precisely this tapas — maintaining the direction of consciousness toward its source despite the mātrā-sparśa that would pull it into identification with sensation.

The sāṅkhya architecture is identical in both texts: puruṣa observes, prakṛti fluctuates. BG 2.14 explains why we tolerate — because sensations are anitya (impermanent) and arise from sense-object contact, not from self. YS II.1 prescribes how — through the triple practice of austerity, study, and surrender. Together they form the method: see clearly (viveka), don't identify (vairāgya), and continue the practice (abhyāsa) through all weather.

Sanskrit Terms:

  • mātrā-sparśāḥ (masculine plural, compound) — "sense-object contacts" — the interface where consciousness meets prakṛti; the API boundary where input triggers response
  • titikṣasva (imperative, second person singular) — "you must tolerate/endure" — active forbearance, not passive suffering; the austere patience that maintains practice through all conditions
  • āgama-apāyinaḥ (masculine plural, compound) — "those that come and go" — describing sensations as temporary visitors, not permanent residents
  • anityāḥ (feminine plural, nominative) — "impermanent, non-eternal" — the defining quality of all prakṛtic phenomena; that which cannot be relied upon for lasting satisfaction
  • śīta-uṣṇa (neuter compound) — "cold and heat" — the classic paired opposites; all dvandva (pairs of opposites) that disturb the undiscriminating mind
  • kaunteya / bhārata (masculine vocative singulars) — "O son of Kuntī / O descendant of Bharata" — appellations invoking Arjuna's maternal and paternal lineages, reminding him of his duty to uphold dharma
