BG 3.30 — The Action Without Fever

मयि सर्वाणि कर्माणि सन्न्यस्याध्यात्मचेतसा ।
निराशीर्निर्ममो भूत्वा युध्यस्व विगतज्वरः ॥ ३० ॥

mayi sarvāṇi karmāṇi sannyasyādhyātma-cetasā
nirāśīr nirmamo bhūtvā yudhyasvā vigata-jvaraḥ

"Therefore, O Arjuna, surrendering all your works unto Me, with full knowledge of Me, without desires for profit, with no claims to proprietorship, and free from lethargy, fight."

— Bhagavad-gītā 3.30, Translation by His Divine Grace A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupāda

The Parallel: Śravaṇa is the asterism of hearing — the cosmic ear tuned to divine vibration. Sādhya means "that which can be accomplished." Today's verse bridges them perfectly: the accomplished action is one performed with consciousness surrendered to the divine — adhyātma-cetāsā, with mind fixed on the Supreme. When the ear truly hears, the fever drops away. Vigata-jvaraḥ — without the mental fever of anxiety, calculation, and desire for outcomes. The action simply flows. This is not passivity; it is the highest discipline — executing duty without the bondage of proprietorship. The Machine That Runs Itself

Kṛṣṇa gives Arjuna military orders here — but the real command is about consciousness. Mayi sarvāṇi karmāṇi sannyasya: surrender all actions unto Me. Not "stop acting." Not "meditate while the world burns." Act — but surrender the fruits. This is the razor's edge of Karma Yoga.

The purport clarifies what this looks like practically: a cashier counts millions for his employer but doesn't claim a cent. Why? The system is clear — the money isn't his. His role is to execute, not to own. This is nirmamaḥ — no proprietorship. The bondage comes from the claim, not the action.

The Sanskrit word here that demands attention: vigata-jvaraḥ — without fever. Not without effort. Not without intensity. Without fever. The mental fever of anxiety, of "what if," of second-guessing, of calculating outcomes. The body can work; the mind can calculate; but the fever — that nervous grip on results — must dissolve. This is what makes action sādhya — accomplishable. Not because we control outcomes, but because we've surrendered the need to.

Śravaṇa nakṣatra reinforces this teaching. The ear receives — it doesn't generate sound. It listens, it submits to vibration. Today's cosmic energy invites that same receptive posture: hear the order, surrender the fruits, act without fever. The Sarvartha Siddhi yoga window suggests success comes to this method — but siddhi here is liberation, not victory.

Yoga Sūtra Connection

Tapaḥ svādhyāyeśvarapraṇidhānāni kriyā-yogaḥ
YS 2.1

"Austerity, self-study, and surrender to Īśvara constitute the yoga of action."

Patañjali's kriyā-yoga is action-yoga, and its third pillar is īśvarapraṇidhāna — surrender to the Lord. This isn't optional garnish on the practice; it's foundational. BG 3.30 is the Bhagavad-gītā's exposition of this same principle: surrender becomes the method. Edwin Bryant notes that īśvarapraṇidhāna functions as "the accelerator of the yogic process" — it cuts through obstacles that tapas and svādhyāya alone might take years to dissolve. Arjuna is being offered this accelerator here: stop the feverish mental calculating and simply execute as if Kṛṣṇa were your commanding officer. This is military discipline of the highest order — not grudging submission, but clear-eyed surrender to the structure of reality.

Īśvarapraṇidhānād vā
YS 1.23

"Or [samādhi arises from] surrender to Īśvara."

The vā here is crucial — "or." Patañjali lists multiple paths to samādhi, and surrender is one of them. For householders, for those in the world who cannot renounce all activity, Kṛṣṇa offers BG 3.30: yuḍhyasva — "fight." Vigata-jvaraḥ — without the fever. This is the integration point where Bhakti and Karma Yoga meet. The warrior surrenders to his commander; the devotee surrenders to Kṛṣṇa; the yogī surrenders to Īśvara. Same posture, different names.

Today's Practice

The Vigata-jvaraḥ Check: Before any significant action today, pause. Notice the mental fever — the gripping, the calculating, the anxiety about outcomes. Consciously release it. Say internally: "I surrender the fruits of this action." Then act with full attention.

Śravaṇa Listening: In your next conversation, practice pure receiving. Don't formulate your response while the other speaks. Simply hear. Let the words land without your immediate commentary.

The Cashier Mind: Whatever your work today — emails, decisions, physical labor — execute with precision while holding the cashier's clarity: "none of this is mine." Count the millions for the employer. Take nothing for yourself.

Terms

adhyātma-cetāsā "with consciousness fixed on the self" — the cognitive mode where awareness acknowledges its source in the Divine, like a wave knowing it is water.
vigata-jvaraḥ "without fever" — the absence of nervous mental agitation about outcomes. The body works; the mind calculates; but the grip of anxiety dissolves.
nirmamaḥ "without mine-ness" — the state of executing actions without claiming proprietorship. The cashier counting millions for his employer — executing responsibility while holding no ownership.
nirāśīḥ "without expectation" — acting without attachment to specific results. The sky doesn't rain expecting gratitude; the sun doesn't shine for appreciation.
sannyasya "having fully renounced" — complete surrender of agency, not abandonment of action. Like a symphony musician surrendering to the conductor.
īśvarapraṇidhāna "surrender to the Lord" — the yogic practice of offering all actions and their fruits to the Divine. The accelerator of spiritual practice.
