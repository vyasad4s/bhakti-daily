Bhagavad-gītā 2.28

अव्यक्तादीनि भूतानि व्यक्तमध्यानि भारत ।
अव्यक्तनिधनान्येव तत्र का परिदेवना ॥ २८ ॥

avyaktādīni bhūtāni vyakta-madhyāni bhārata
avyakta-nidhanāny eva tatra kā paridevanā

All created beings are unmanifest in their beginning, manifest in their interim state, and unmanifest again when annihilated. So what need is there for lamentation?

— Bhagavad-gītā As It Is, Chapter 2, Verse 28

Cosmic Context

Nakṣatra: Chitra — The Bright One, ruled by Tvashtā, the celestial architect. This star governs the hidden-to-visible-to-hidden cycle of manifestation. Just as Chitra's splendor appears, dazzles, and dissolves into night, all material forms follow this trajectory.

Tithi: Kṛṣṇa Pratipadā — First day of the waning moon. The full manifestation begins its return to the unmanifest. The cosmic process in miniature: from fullness (vyakta) toward dissolution (avyakta).

Yoga: Vyāghāta — "Shattering." A disruptive force that breaks false permanence. Today the illusion of stable form is shattered, revealing the truth of constant transformation.

Correlation: On a day ruled by the architect of cosmic splendor (Chitra-Tvashtā), during the moon's return to darkness (Pratipadā), under a yoga that breaks attachment (Vyāghāta), the Gītā speaks of the soul beyond manifestation cycles. The timing is surgical precision.

Sāṅkhya Unfolding

Kṛṣṇa is being deliberately thorough here. He has already established the soul as indestructible, unborn, eternal, and immutable. Now He addresses Arjuna from another angle: even if you reject the soul's existence entirely—even if you are a materialist who believes consciousness is merely chemical interaction—still, you have no reason to lament. Why? Because matter itself follows the same cycle.

The materialist cannot argue with conservation of energy. Before the skyscraper existed, its constituent elements were diffused in earth, water, fire. After the building's demolition, those same elements return to their unmanifest state. The form was temporary; the substance was conserved. Whether one accepts a transcendental soul or denies it, the pattern remains: manifestation, duration, dissolution. This is not philosophy—this is observable physics.

But here is where Patañjali and the Sāṅkhya tradition sharpen the point. The manifested form (vyakta) is prakṛti under the influence of the guṇas. The unmanifest source (avyakta) is prakṛti in equilibrium, before the churning begins. The soul (puruṣa) witnesses both states but belongs to neither. Arjuna is lamenting the destruction of forms—Bhīṣma's form, Droṇa's form, his cousins' forms. But forms are precisely what undergo the cycle Kṛṣṇa describes. To grieve for a form is to grieve for a garment that was always destined to be changed.

This verse contains the radical implication: even the materialist's worldview dissolves the basis for lamentation. Only the deluded—those who mistake temporary manifestation for permanent existence—suffer when forms dissolve. The materially sophisticated understand conservation. The spiritually realized understand eternality. Both are spared from paridevanā—the excessive wailing that arises from miśidentification.

Yoga Sūtra Integration

YS I.4: vṛtti-sārūpyam itaratra — "Otherwise, at other times, [the seer] is absorbed in the changing states [of the mind]."

Patañjali describes the fundamental error that generates all lamentation: the soul's apparent absorption in prakṛti's transformations. The vṛttis—the mental fluctuations that perceive birth and death, gain and loss—are modifications of prakṛti, not of puruṣa. Yet when consciousness reflects through the mind's agitations, the soul appears to experience those changes as its own.

Kṛṣṇa and Patañjali harmonize here. For the yogī practicing sāṁyama on the distinction between seer and seen (YS III.35, referenced yesterday), the drama of manifestation/unmanifestation becomes a movie projected on the screen of awareness. The yogī does not lament when a character dies in a film, knowing it's light and shadow. Similarly, when the bodies of Bhīṣma and Droṇa undergo their destined dissolution, the realized soul witnesses without attachment—because it recognizes the cycle Kṛṣṇa describes.

The practice: when grief arises about any loss today, immediately recall this verse. The form you mourn was unmanifest before you knew it, manifested for an interim, and returns to the unmanifest. The substance—whether material elements or the witnessing soul—was never lost.

Practice Today

Morning: Upon waking, observe your body with this understanding. It was unmanifest in deep sleep consciousness, manifests now in waking, will become unmanifest again in tonight's sleep—and eventually in death. Witness this without drama.

Throughout the day: When you encounter any form you are attached to—person, possession, status—mentally trace its avyakta-vyakta cycle. Where was this before it appeared? Where will it be after it disappears? The exercise is not morbid; it is liberating.

Evening: Contemplate what remains when all vyakta dissolves. Not nothing—a witness remains. That witness is you. Not the body. Not the mind. The silent awareness that watched all forms come and go.

Saṃskṛta Terms

avyakta — "unmanifest, invisible, latent" — the state of prakṛti before guṇic differentiation, or after dissolution. Like code that exists as potential before compilation. The source from which all forms emerge and to which they return.

vyakta — "manifest, visible, expressed" — prakṛti under active guṇic modification, the material world as experienced. The compiled program running. Chitra nakṣatra governs this: the bright appearance that captures attention but is destined to fade.

paridevanā — "lamentation, excessive wailing, beating of the breast" — grief based on the delusion of loss. The word implies theatrical mourning, the display of sorrow. Kṛṣṇa's question (tatra kā paridevanā) is rhetorical: when did you ever actually lose anything?

bhūtāni — "created beings, elements, entities" — from √bhū (to be, to become). All things that come into existence through the cycle of manifestation. Entities that were not, are now, will not be—and yet never truly were or weren't.
