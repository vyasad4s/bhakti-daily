🏹 VERSE CONTENT

Source: BG 3.7

Date: March 02, 2026

Sanskrit:

यस्त्विन्द्रियाणि मनसा नियम्यारभतेऽर्जुन ।

कर्मेन्द्रियैः कर्मयोगमसक्तः स विशिष्यते ॥ ३.७ ॥

Transliteration:

yas tv indriyāṇi manasā
niyamyārabhate 'rjuna
karmendriyaiḥ karma-yogam
asaktaḥ sa viśiṣyate

Translation:

"On the other hand, if a sincere person tries to control the active senses by the mind and begins karma-yoga [in Kṛṣṇa consciousness] without attachment, he is by far superior."

Commentary:

The contrast is complete. Yesterday exposed the pretender — one who restrains the body while the mind clings to sense objects. Today reveals the superior path — karma-yoga with asakti (non-attachment). Kṛṣṇa declares this person viśiṣyate — not just better, but by far superior.

The sequence is deliberate: verse 3.6 described the hypocrite (mithyācāraḥ); verse 3.7 reveals the authentic practitioner. The difference is not external restraint but internal alignment. The true yogī uses manasā niyamya — regulating through the mind, which has been submitted to Kṛṣṇa. The senses follow the mind; the mind follows the divine.

Prabhupāda emphasizes: better a sincere sweeper than a charlatan meditator. The varṇāśrama system exists not to suppress life but to transform it into yajña — sacrifice for Viṣṇu. Every ordinary action becomes worship when offered without attachment. The householder who chants Hare Kṛṣṇa while earning livelihood surpasses the pseudo-renunciant who lives at others' expense.

The key lies in the Sanskrit asaktaḥ — without attachment. Not aversion; not suppression. Action without the hooks of desire. Yesterday's verse showed what happens when we try to stop acting while the mind still wants; today's verse shows how to act in freedom. The person who controls senses through buddhi (intelligence) and dedicates every action to the Supreme — this is the superior yogī.

Kṛṣṇa addresses Arjuna directly here (arjuna) — the path is for householders, warriors, and active practitioners. Renunciation is not escape; it's transformation. Karma-yoga is the bridge between saṃsāra and mokṣa. The senses continue — hands work, eyes see, legs move — but the consciousness has shifted from "I will enjoy the fruit" to "This is yajña for Viṣṇu." The flow of karma continues but no longer binds because saṅga (attachment) is severed.

Sankhya Concepts:

The guṇas of prakṛti still operate — this is key. The true yogī does not transcend the guṇas by force; they transform action through consciousness (cit). The puruṣa remains unattached witness while buddhi directs all action toward the Supreme.

Here Sāṅkhya reveals its practical wisdom: prakṛti acts perpetually, but puruṣa suffers only through abhimāna (false identification). When the mind (buddhi) is fixed on Kṛṣṇa, action flows without binding. The senses remain active instruments; the karmic consequence dissolves. This is naiṣkarmya — actionlessness in action, not inaction.

The tri-guṇas manifest through the antaḥkaraṇa (inner instrument): buddhi (intellect), ahaṅkāra (ego), and manas (mind). The superior yogī has purified these, so sattva dominates while rajas becomes the dynamic of service and tamas becomes the stability of detachment.

Yoga Sutra Connection:

🔥 YS 1.12 — abhyāsa-vairāgyābhyāṁ tat-nirodhaḥ — "The fluctuations of the mind are restrained by practice AND detachment."

Karma-yoga combines both. Abhyāsa in the form of regular devotional action; vairāgya through non-attachment to results. Patañjali dual method realized simultaneously. Practice without detachment creates rigid habit; detachment without practice becomes mere philosophy.

🔥 YS 2.2 — samādhi-bhāvana-arthaḥ kleśa-tanū-karaṇa-arthaś ca — "For cultivating samādhi and for thinning the afflictions."

Karma-yoga specifically addresses the kleśas (afflictions). Entrenched attractions and aversions (rāga-dveṣa) are systematically dissolved through detached action.

Sanskrit Terms:

  • Yaḥ (relative pronoun, nominative singular) — one who; the subject being described.
  • Tu (particle, indeclinable) — but; on the other hand. Contrasting particle marking the shift from verse 3.6's false yogī to the genuine practitioner.
  • Indriyāṇi (neuter plural, accusative) — the five senses of perception (jñānendriya): sight, hearing, touch, taste, smell.
  • Manasā (neuter noun, instrumental singular) — by the mind; from manas (mind).
  • Niyamya (gerund, absolutive) — regulating, controlling; from ni-yam.
  • Ārabhate (verb, present middle voice, 3rd person singular) — begins, undertakes; from ā-rabh.
  • Karmendriyaiḥ (neuter plural, instrumental) — by the organs of action: hands, legs, speech, genitals, anus.
  • Karma-yogam (masculine singular, accusative) — the path of duty without attachment.
  • Asaktaḥ (nominalized past passive participle) — unattached, free from clinging; from a-sañj.
  • Sa (demonstrative pronoun, nominative singular) — that person.
  • Viśiṣyate (verb, present passive, 3rd person singular) — is by far superior; from vi-śiṣ.
