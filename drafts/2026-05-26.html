🏹 BG 6.10 | 2026-05-26

 

योगी युञ्जीत सततमात्मानं रहसि स्थित: ।

एकाकी यतचित्तात्मा निराशीरपरिग्रह: ॥ १० ॥

 

yogī yuñjīta satatam ātmānaṁ rahasi sthitaḥ
ekākī yata-cittātmā nirāśīr aparigrahaḥ

 

A transcendentalist should always engage his body, mind and self in relationship with the Supreme; he should live alone in a secluded place and should always carefully control his mind. He should be free from desires and feelings of possessiveness.

 

Five commands in a single verse. Kṛṣṇa does not suggest — He instructs. Yuñjīta — must engage. Satatam — constantly. Rahasi sthitaḥ — situated in seclusion. Ekākī — alone. Yata-cittātmā — with controlled mind and self. Each command is a separate discipline, and each discipline is a compression chamber for the others. You cannot practice constantly without seclusion — the world will not permit it. You cannot sit in seclusion without mental control — the mind will not permit it. You cannot control the mind without being free from desire — the senses will not permit it. The five commands are not a checklist. They are a single mechanism — each gear driving the next, the whole assembly turning toward one end: ātmānaṁ, the self engaged in relationship with the Supreme.

 

The verse begins with yuñjīta — the imperative of yuj, to yoke, to join. This is not "try yoga." This is "engage the self in union — now, always, without interruption." The root yuj is the same root that gives us yoga itself. The command is structural: the yogī must become the yoke. Not someone who practices yoga occasionally, but someone whose very mode of existence is joining — body, mind, and self, perpetually connected to the Supreme. Prabhupāda's purport makes the hierarchy clear: the Kṛṣṇa conscious person is the topmost transcendentalist because such a devotee is already engaged. The impersonalist meditates on an abstraction. The haṭha-yogī perfects posture. The bhakta yuñjīta-s — engages — directly with the Person. The seclusion and solitude are not ends in themselves. They are the conditions that make constant engagement possible. A fire burns brightest when wind is excluded. The mind connects most deeply when distraction is removed. The solitary practice is not escapism — it is concentration of force.

 

🔥 YOGA SŪTRA CONNECTION: YS II.45 — samādhi-siddhiḥ īśvara-praṇidhānāt
Patañjali places īśvara-praṇidhāna as the final niyama — the last of the personal observances before the āsana-prāṇāyāma-pratyāhāra sequence even begins. And he says: surrender to Īśvara brings about the perfection of samādhi. Not "contributes to." Not "supports." Siddhiḥ — accomplishment, perfection, the finished work. The causal relationship is direct: praṇidhāna produces samādhi-siddhi. Now read BG 6.10 alongside this. Kṛṣṇa says: yuñjīta satatam ātmānaṁ — engage the self constantly. How? By yata-cittātmā — controlling the mind and self. And what is the most effective form of this control? Prabhupāda answers: the Kṛṣṇa conscious person, who is always thinking of Kṛṣṇa, is already in samādhi. He does not need to withdraw from the world to find the Supreme — he has found the Supreme and therefore naturally withdraws from what is not the Supreme. The sequence in the verse maps exactly to Patañjali's architecture: nirāśīḥ (free from desire) and aparigrahaḥ (free from possessiveness) correspond to vairāgya (dispassion). Yata-cittātmā corresponds to abhyāsa (sustained practice). And the constant engagement — yuñjīta satatam — corresponds to īśvara-praṇidhāna, the surrender that completes the mechanism and triggers samādhi-siddhi. Both Kṛṣṇa and Patañjali agree: the path is not technique alone. The path is engagement with the Supreme — and that engagement, when constant and undistracted, produces the perfection that technique alone cannot reach.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

Yuñjīta (verb, 3rd person singular, imperative, parasmaipada) — must engage, must yoke, must join; from root yuj (to yoke, to unite, to connect); the imperative mood is not a suggestion — it is a command from the Supreme Person to the aspiring yogī; yuj is the same root that produces yoga itself; the instruction is thus self-referential: the yogī must yoga — must become the very process of joining; not "do yoga as an activity" but "become yoga as a state" — the imperative demands transformation of mode, not addition of practice

Satatam (adverb) — constantly, always, perpetually, without interruption; from sadā (always) + intensifying suffix; this is not "most of the time" or "when convenient" — it is the demand for unbroken continuity; the hacker analogy: satatam is the daemon process that runs in the background continuously, not the cron job that fires once a day; the yogī's engagement with the Supreme is not scheduled — it is the default state from which all other activities branch

Rahasi (locative, neuter) — in seclusion, in a secluded place; from rahas (secret, secluded, private); the locative case indicates the location of practice — not a psychological state but a physical condition; seclusion is not loneliness — loneliness is the pain of unwanted isolation; seclusion is the chosen withdrawal that makes concentration possible; the solitary place is the firewall that blocks the world's noise while the inner connection is established

Ekākī (adjective, nominative) — alone, solitary; from eka (one, single) + augment; this is not merely being physically alone — it is being one-pointed, undivided, single in purpose; the yogī who is ekākī has eliminated the internal crowd — the multiple voices, the competing desires, the divided loyalties — and stands as one intelligence directed toward one object: the Supreme; the paradox: the one who is alone with Kṛṣṇa is never truly alone

Nirāśīḥ (adjective, nominative) — free from desire, without expectation; from nis (without) + āśā (hope, desire, expectation); this is not nihilism — it is the liberation that comes when the self is fully engaged with the Supreme and therefore has no room for secondary desires; the one who is yuñjīta satatam in Kṛṣṇa does not need to also desire material things because the supreme engagement has already filled the space that desire would occupy; nirāśīḥ is not the absence of wanting — it is the presence of complete fulfillment

Aparigrahaḥ (adjective, nominative) — free from possessiveness, non-grasping; from a (not) + parigraha (grasping, taking, possessiveness); this is the fifth yama in Patañjali's system — the vow of non-accumulation; the aparigraha practitioner does not hoard, does not cling, does not claim ownership over what was never truly his; Prabhupāda makes the bhakti application explicit: the Kṛṣṇa conscious person knows everything belongs to Kṛṣṇa and is therefore always free from feelings of personal possession; aparigraha is not poverty — it is correct ownership: nothing is mine, everything is His

⊛ COSMIC INTERPRETATION

 

Hasta → Chitrā: The Hand That Serves, The Form That Radiates
Hasta is the Hand of Savitṛ — the solar deity who impels, who sets all things in motion. The hand that serves. The hand that engages. This is not coincidence — BG 6.10 commands yuñjīta, "engage!" and Hasta provides the cosmic architecture for engagement. The hand is the instrument of action, the mechanism by which intention becomes manifestation. Savitṛ's hand does not merely act — it impels, it initiates the very motion of practice. When Kṛṣṇa says yuñjīta satatam, He is describing the motion that Hasta makes possible: the continuous, skillful engagement of the self with the Supreme. As Hasta yields to Chitrā at 8:26 PM, the energy shifts from the hand that serves to the radiant form that Tvaṣṭā architects. The solitary yogī who has spent the day in engaged practice now enters the night with an inner architecture — the mind shaped by sustained connection, the heart luminous with the form of the Supreme. Hasta builds the practice. Chitrā reveals the result.

 

Siddhi → Vyatipāta: Accomplishment, Then Vigilance
The yoga of Siddhi — accomplishment, perfection, the successful completion of effort — holds the day until 5:40 PM. This is the solar energy of success, the cosmic affirmation that practice bears fruit. But the transition is sharp: Siddhi gives way to Vyatipāta, the most inauspicious yoga in the entire panchāṅga cycle. The name itself signals danger — vyati (beyond, excessive) + pāta (fall, calamity). The accomplished yogī who feels the sweetness of siddhi must immediately double his vigilance. This is the precise teaching of BG 6.10 in cosmic form: yata-cittātmā — controlled mind and self — does not end when success arrives. It especially applies when success arrives. The fall from siddhi is the most dangerous fall because it is the one you don't see coming. Vyatipāta is the cosmic warning: do not relax your guard at the moment of accomplishment. The five commands of BG 6.10 are not for beginners only. They are for the accomplished yogī who must remain ekākī yata-cittātmā — alone, controlled, vigilant — even in success.

 

Padmini Ekādaśī: The Lotus Fast
Padmini — the lotus-bearing — is one of the most celebrated Ekādaśīs in the Vaiṣṇava calendar. The lotus is the symbol of purity rooted in impurity: it grows from mud, rises through water, and opens to the sun without a trace of its origin clinging to its petals. The Padmini fast is the solitary fire of BG 6.10 made ritual: the practitioner withdraws from grain, from excess, from the ordinary flow of consumption, and concentrates the energy inward. The lotus does not leave the water — it transcends it from within. The Padmini observer does not leave the world — they engage the Supreme satatam, constantly, from within the conditions of embodied life. This is precisely the teaching: rahasi sthitaḥ does not require a cave in the Himalayas. It requires the internal seclusion that Padmini Ekādaśī creates through fasting, through prayer, through the deliberate withdrawal of the senses from their objects. The lotus fast is the firewall — it blocks the noise while the inner connection is established.

 

Maṅgalavāra: The Martial Discipline of Tuesday
Tuesday is Bhauma's day — the day of Mars, the planet of discipline, warfare, and the sustained application of force. There is nothing soft about Maṅgalavāra. The energy is martial, focused, relentless. This is not the day for gentle reflection — it is the day for yuñjīta, the imperative command to engage with full force. The warrior does not ask whether he feels like fighting. He fights because the command has been given. The yogī does not ask whether he feels like practicing. He practices because Kṛṣṇa has said yuñjīta satatam — engage, constantly, without interruption. Maṅgalavāra provides the cosmic engine for this imperative. The fire of Mars does not waver. The discipline of Tuesday does not negotiate. On this Padmini Ekādaśī that falls on Maṅgalavāra, the universe is not suggesting practice — it is commanding it.

 

Dvipuṣkar Yoga: The Double Amplification
After 8:52 PM, when the tithi shifts to Śukla Dwādaśī while Chitrā nakṣatra holds and Maṅgalavāra continues, a Dvipuṣkar Yoga forms — a doubly auspicious configuration that amplifies the results of any spiritual action performed during its window. ⊛ SĀṀKHYA LENS

 

From the Sāṅkhya framework, BG 6.10 describes the operation of buddhi when it is correctly oriented toward puruṣa rather than absorbed in prakṛti. The five commands are the five movements of discerning intelligence: (1) Yuñjīta — buddhi turns toward puruṣa (the fundamental reorientation from matter to spirit). (2) Satatam — buddhi maintains this orientation continuously (not intermittently, not partially — the full commitment of discernment). (3) Rahasi sthitaḥ — buddhi withdraws from the field of prakṛti's noise to establish the connection in clarity (the solitary place is not a physical location alone — it is the isolation of discernment from the interference of rajas and tamas). (5) Nirāśīr aparigrahaḥ — buddhi is freed from the two primary distortions: rāga (desire, which pulls toward what prakṛti offers) and abhiniveśa (possessiveness, which clings to what prakṛti has provided). When all five movements are complete, buddhi is in its natural state: sattvic, transparent, reflecting puruṣa's light without distortion. This is the Sāṅkhya architecture of liberation — and BG 6.10 compresses it into a single verse.

⊛ YS BRIDGE DEEP DIVE

 

YS II.45 — samādhi-siddhiḥ īśvara-praṇidhānāt — is the final niyama and the bridge between personal practice and the transcendent goal. Patañjali does not say that īśvara-praṇidhāna is one technique among many. He says it produces samādhi-siddhi — the perfection of samādhi itself. The causal suffix -āt is unambiguous: "from īśvara-praṇidhāna, samādhi-siddhi arises." The commentator Vyāsa explains that this surrender can be of three intensities — mild, medium, or intense — and the fruit corresponds to the intensity. BG 6.10 matches the intense mode: yuñjīta satatam — engage constantly, without interruption. This is not mild surrender. This is the full commitment that Patañjali describes as producing rapid results. The five commands of BG 6.10 are the architecture of intense praṇidhāna: (1) engagement as surrender in action, (2) constancy as surrender in time, (3) seclusion as surrender in space, (4) mental control as surrender in consciousness, (5) desirelessness and non-possessiveness as surrender in substance. When all five are operative simultaneously, the mechanism is complete — and samādhi-siddhi follows as surely as fire follows the striking of a match.

 

The cosmic configuration of 2026-05-26 reinforces this bridge. Siddhi yoga holds the day — the very word siddhi appears in YS II.45 (samādhi-siddhiḥ). The panchāṅga is literally naming the goal while the verse describes the path. Padmini Ekādaśī provides the ritual container. Hasta provides the hand of engagement. Maṅgalavāra provides the martial intensity. The cosmos and the scripture are aligned on a single point: īśvara-praṇidhānāt samādhi-siddhiḥ — from surrender, perfection.
