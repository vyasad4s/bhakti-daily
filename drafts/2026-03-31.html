Bhagavad Gita 2.20

न जायते म्रियते वा कदाचि-न्नायं भूत्वा भविता वा न भूयः ।
अजो नित्यः शाश्वतोऽयं पुराणो न हन्यते हन्यमाने शरीरे ॥ २० ॥
na jayate mriyate va kadacin
nayam bhutva bhavita va na bhuyah
ajo nityah sasvato 'yam purano
na hanyate hanyamane sarire
For the soul there is neither birth nor death at any time. He has not come into being, does not come into being, and will not come into being. He is unborn, eternal, ever-existing and primeval. He is not slain when the body is slain.

na jayate — never takes birth; na — never; mriyate — dies; va — either; kadacit — at any time; nayam — this soul does not; bhutva — having come into being; bhavita — will come to be; ajah — unborn; nityah — eternal; sasvatah — permanent/ever-existing; puranah — the oldest/primeval; na hanyate — is never killed; hanyamane sarire — when the body is being killed.

What the full moon reveals tonight is what consciousness always already is.

The soul does not wax. The soul does not wane. The waxing of tithi from Pratipada through Caturdasi, culminating in Purnima at 9:36 PM tonight—this is a prakrtic drama of illumination, not a pattern for the atman. The moon appears to grow, appears to diminish, yet its actual mass remains unchanged. The misidentification is the trick: we take the reflection for the source, the appearance for the reality.

Krsna speaks this to Arjuna as sastra, but the teaching points to something that cannot be taught because it is already the case. Na jayate mriyate va—the double negation is not mere emphasis but structural: birth and death are dualistic constructs that apply only to prakrti, only to the six-fold transformations the body undergoes (birth, growth, production of effects, dwindling, and finally nasta, oblivion). The soul stands outside this chain entirely. Purusa has no history. No biography. No arrival. No departure.

This is why Vedanta calls the atman kuta-stha—fixed at the pinnacle. The prefix kuta carries the sense of immovable, like a mountain summit unmoved by the weather that rages across its slopes. The body is weather. The soul is the summit itself.

And yet: we mourn. We contract. We build identities around the six-fold transformations as if they defined us. The childhood photograph, the aging face in the mirror, the fear of the last exhalation—each one a vrtti in the citta, a cloud obscuring the sun that never ceased shining. Yoga begins here: with the recognition that the identification itself is the knot to be cut.

Connection to the Yoga Sutras

YS II.20: drasta drsi-matrah suddho 'pi pratyanupasyah

"The seer is merely the power of seeing; although pure, he witnesses the images of the mind."

Patanjali gives us the architecture. The drasta, the seer, is purusa—pure consciousness devoid of content. He is drsi-matrah: seeing-essence only, the capacity to illuminate without being stained by what is illuminated. This is precisely what Krsna declares: suddho 'pi—pure even while apparently entangled.

But here is the subtlety: the phrase pratyaya-anupasyah introduces the dynamic. The seer witnesses the contents of the mind—the pratyayas, the objects of consciousness—not as their creator or their victim, but as their silent observer. This is the practical bridge between bhakti and raja-yoga. Bhakti directs attention to Krsna as the supreme object; yoga withdraws attention from all objects to reveal the subject. Yet both arrive at the same threshold: the recognition that the one who chants, the one who sits in asana, the one who reads these words—this apparent self is a constellation of pratyayas that will dissolve like moonlight at dawn, leaving only the witness that was never separate from what it witnessed.

The Tithi Current:

We are at the final gate. Sukla Caturdasi marks the fourteenth phase—the soul's penultimate step before Purnima's total revelation. The timing is surgical: after 9:36 PM EDT tonight, the lunar cycle achieves fullness. What was accumulating now becomes complete. The atma does not accumulate; it is already full. Yet we require this cosmic mirror to remember what we never forgot, only obscured.

Naksatra Transition:

Moon travels through Uttara Phalguni's second half before entering Hasta at the stroke of noon (12:02 PM). Uttara Phalguni's deity is Aryama, the pitri-lord—an appropriate passage as we consider those who have preceded us, who have also never been born and never died. The passage into Hasta (the hand, ruled by Savitar) brings the active capacity to grasp what has been revealed. The hand of Hasta is the hand that finally understands: I was never in need of grasping at all.

Moon Sign Crossing:

At 12:02 PM, Chandra exits Singha (Leo) and enters Kanya (Virgo). This is significant. From the royal fixed fire of Leo to the mutable earth of Virgo—the analytical, service-oriented, dharma-conscious sign. The soul's illumination now enters the practical domain: how does this realization become reality in action? Virgo asks not "what is the truth?" but "what do I do now that I know?"

Yoga Vrddhi:

The yoga is Vrddhi—increase, expansion, growth. Until 5:20 AM tomorrow, the cosmic wind blows toward amplification. What expands tonight is not ego but recognition. The citta, cleared of its habitual contraction around birth-and-death narratives, can now expand to reflect the full spectrum of purusa's awareness.

Mars Day (Mangala-vara):

Tuesday carries Mars energy—courage, discrimination, the capacity to cut. Mangala is the planetary kuta: the spear that pierces. On the Caturdasi before Purnima, this energy is available to sever the ahankara's final grip. The day asks: do you have the courage to be what you actually are?

Sankhya Concepts

  • kuta-stha — fixed at the summit; the soul's immovable nature despite prakrtic flux
  • anu-atma / vibu-atma — the individual soul and the Supersoul; both unborn
  • sad-vikara — six transformations of matter (birth, growth, duration, production, dwindling, disappearance)
  • drastr / drsya — seer and seen; purusa and prakrti

Daily Practice

At the moment of Purnima (9:36 PM) or during the Amrta Kala (11:17 PM – 12:57 AM), sit in silence and trace the drasta. Not the meditation. Notthe meditator's identity. Just the capacity to witness that has been present through every experience of your life—including the experiences you cannot remember, including the sleep last night, including the moment before the first memory formed.

Notice: the witness did not arrive when you learned to meditate. The witness does not depart when thoughts distract. The witness is kuta-stha—the summit where all weather occurs but which the weather cannot touch.

This is the view from Purnima: not that the soul will someday achieve eternity, but that eternity is the condition in which this sentence is being read.
