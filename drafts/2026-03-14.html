🏹 VERSE CONTENT

Source: BG 2.65

Date: March 14, 2026

Sanskrit:

प्रसादे सर्वदुःखानां हानिरस्योपजायते ।

प्रसन्नचेतसो ह्याशु बुद्धिः पर्यवतिष्ठते ॥ ६५ ॥

Transliteration:

prasāde sarva-duḥkhānāṁ hāniḥ asya upajāyate
prasanna-cetasaḥ hi āśu buddhiḥ paryavatiṣṭhate

Translation:

For one thus situated in the Divine, material miseries are destroyed, and in this happy state, his intelligence soon becomes fixed in the process of self-realization.

Commentary:

Yesterday we established the architecture of prasāda—how rāga-dveṣa-vimukta consciousness settles into grace like a system finding its stable state. Today Kṛṣṇa completes the sequence: what happens when that settlement occurs. The result is not subtle. It is hāniḥ—annihilation, destruction, complete cessation of sarva-duḥkhānām, all miseries.

Sāṅkhya teaches that duḥkha has three sources: ādhyātmika (self-inflicted), ādhibhautika (inflicted by other beings), and ādhidaivika (cosmic forces). When prasāda settles, none of these operate on the puruṣa. Why? Because the puruṣa now knows itself as distinct from prakṛti. The gunas still churn—pain still arrives at the body, praise and blame still circulate in society, storms still gather—but none of it reaches the witness. The duḥkha is destroyed because the misidentification that created it has been debugged.

This is not dissociation. It is discrimination so precise that prakṛti continues its functions while puruṣa remains untroubled. The Yogī is not catatonic—they may be cooking, chanting, working—but internally, prasanna-cetasaḥ: the consciousness is settled, expanded, clear like a mountain lake at dawn. From this clarity, buddhiḥ paryavatiṣṭhate—intelligence becomes established, fixed, unshakeable. The wandering mind that once chased every sensory distraction now holds steady in self-knowledge. This is the fruit of Ekādaśī fasting, of Śravaṇa listening, of the discipline that precedes grace: when grace arrives, it clears the deck completely.

Yoga Sutra Connection:

🔥 YS I.16 — तत्परं पुरुषख्यातेर्गुणवैतृष्ण्यम्
"The highest form of non-attachment is that which follows the discriminative awareness of puruṣa, in which there is complete cessation of even the desire for the guṇas."

Patañjali and Kṛṣṇa describe the same territory. The Sūtra's guṇa-vaitṛṣṇya—thirstlessness for even the subtlest manifestations of prakṛti—is the technical correlate to Kṛṣṇa's hāniḥ sarva-duḥkhānām. In Sāṅkhya, duḥkha arises because puruṣa misidentifies with prakṛti's transformations. When puruṣa-khyāti (awareness of puruṣa) dawns, the guṇas continue their dance, but the witness is no longer thirsty for their effects. This is paravairāgya, supreme detachment—not rejection but recognition that the show was never binding.

Today's verse adds the element of speed: āśu, "very soon." This is not gradual enlightenment through aeons of practice. This is the immediate result of stabilized prasāda. When the conditions are right—the senses regulated, the mind settled, the devotion sincere—the shift can happen now. The Ekādaśī energy accelerates this process. The fasting body, the clear mind, the devotional intent—these create a crucible in which buddhi settles fast.

Sanskrit Terms:

  • prasāde (loc.) — in the grace/mercy of the Lord; the settled state of prasāda (from pra + sad, "to settle firmly")
  • sarva-duḥkhānām (gen. pl.) — of all miseries; sarva (all) + duḥkha (suffering); the totality of material suffering across all three categories (ādhyātmika, ādhibhautika, ādhidaivika)
  • hāniḥ (nom.) — destruction, cessation, annihilation; root han (to kill); technical term for complete elimination, not mere suppression
  • prasanna-cetasaḥ (gen. sg.) — of one whose consciousness is clear/expanded; prasanna (clear, bright, satisfied) + cetas (consciousness); the settled mind that reflects puruṣa like a still lake reflects the moon
  • āśu (adv.) — quickly, immediately, very soon; the speed of transformation when conditions are ripe
  • paryavatiṣṭhate (3sg.) — becomes established, fixed, settled; pari (around/completely) + ava + sthā (to stand); the intelligence becomes firmly situated in self-knowledge

Papamochani Ekādaśī — The Ekādaśī that "liberates from sins." Believed to destroy even the reactions to brahmicide and other heinous acts. The fasting and vigil of this day is said to annihilate accumulated karmic debt and grant liberation. The energy is purification—hāniḥ in action.

Cosmic Interpretation:

Today carries the energy of annihilation and renewal. Papamochani Ekādaśī is not merely about fasting—it is about the complete destruction of accumulated duḥkha. The tithi itself is named for this function: pāpa (sin) + mocana (liberation). The karmic ledger gets wiped clean.

Śravaṇa nakṣatra enters at 07:19 PM—this is the asterism of listening, of śravaṇa in its root sense. Presided over by Viṣṇu and symbolized by three footprints (representing the step of Viṣṇu across the universe), Śravaṇa is the nakṣatra of divine sound and sacred transmission. Combined with Ekādaśī, this is a day for receiving teaching, not merely performing austerity.

The Parigha yoga (iron club/bolt) suggests obstacles being struck down. What seems like a harsh energy is actually protective—it destroys that which would obstruct the spiritual path. The iron club of Parigha clears debris.

The Moon in Makara (Capricorn) approaching Śravaṇa grounds this energy. Makara is the sign of Saturn, of discipline, of temporal authority bending to spiritual law. The moon here is stable, structured, capable of holding the intense Ekādaśī energy without dispersing.

Meena Saṅkrānti at 03:30 PM completes the picture. The sun's entry into Pisces marks the final 30 days of the Vedic year. This is dissolution energy—old structures dissolving before the new year begins. Combined with Papamochani Ekādaśī, today is a complete reset. The old self can be left behind. The accumulated pāpa can be annihilated. The prasāda can settle.

Verse Selection Rationale:

BG 2.65 — प्रसादे सर्वदुःखानां हानिरस्योपजायते

This verse was chosen because it is the natural successor to yesterday's BG 2.64, but more importantly because it contains the word hāniḥ—destruction, annihilation—which is the precise energy of Papamochani Ekādaśī. The tithi's very name means "sin-destroying," and this verse teaches how all duḥkha meets its end: not through effort alone, but through prasāda.

The thematic connection is exact: yesterday we learned how to achieve prasāda (rāga-dveṣa-vimukta, vidheyātmā), today we learn what prasāda delivers (sarva-duḥkhānāṃ hāniḥ). The sequential progression mirrors the Ekādaśī process—fasting creates the conditions, grace completes the work. Śravaṇa nakṣatra supports this through its association with divine sound and hearing—receiving the teaching that destroys suffering.
