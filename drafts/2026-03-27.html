Bhagavad-gītā 2.70 | Śrī Kṛṣṇa Saṃvāda
आपूर्यमाणमचलप्रतिष्ठं
समुद्रमापः प्रविशन्ति यद्वत् ।
तद्वत्कामा यं प्रविशन्ति सर्वे
स शान्तिमाप्‍नोति न कामकामी ॥ ७० ॥
āpūryamāṇam acala-pratiṣṭhaṁ
samudram āpaḥ praviśanti yadvat
tadvat kāmā yaṁ praviśanti sarve
sa śāntim āpnoti na kāma-kāmī
Translation: A person who is not disturbed by the incessant flow of desires — that enter like rivers into the ocean, which is ever being filled but is always still — can alone achieve peace, and not the man who strives to satisfy such desires.

Commentary

The analogy is precise: rivers flow ceaselessly into the ocean during monsoon, yet the ocean remains acala-pratiṣṭham — steadfast, unwavering in its depths. It does not flood. It does not overflow its banks. The surface may churn with storms, but the abyssal waters are still.

So too with the mind established in consciousness of the Absolute. Desires continue to appear — this is the nature of prakṛti, the material matrix. The senses perceive objects, the mind processes sensations, attachment and aversion arise. But these are surface phenomena. They do not reach the depths.

The devotee is already full. Not through sensory gratification, but through bhakti — the fullness that comes from connection to source. Therefore desires arrive like rivers to an already-full ocean: they merge without disturbance. There is nothing to chase. Nothing to resist. Only witnessing.

This is not suppression. This is not the ascetic gritting teeth against temptation. This is abundance. When you are truly full, the arrival of more food does not agitate you. When you are truly established in the self, the arrival of sensory opportunities does not compel you.

Kṛṣṇa draws the contrast sharply: the one who strives to fulfill desires — kāma-kāmī — never attains peace. Each satisfaction breeds new longing. Each new longing demands new pursuit. This is saṃsāra, the cycle of becoming, driven by desire and its temporary fulfillment.

But the devotee who has found fullness in service? Desires enter anddissolve. They do not find purchase. The self is already complete.

Tonight, when the clock strikes 11:16 PM and Ekādaśī begins, the practical application of this teaching arrives. The fast is not deprivation — it is the ocean remaining full despite the river's pause. The stomach may signal hunger, but the self is not hungry. The senses may call for taste, but the devotee is already tasting fullness in remembrance.

This is the promise of the Ekādaśī vow: not that we become something through fasting, but that we recognize what we already are — full, complete, untouched by the coming and going of sensory rivers. The ocean does not need the rivers to be the ocean. The rivers cannot make it more ocean.

Yoga Sūtra Connection

II.54: svaviṣayāsaṃprayoge cittasya svarūpānukāra ivendriyāṇāṃ pratyāhāraḥ

Translation: Pratyāhāra — withdrawal from the sense objects — occurs when the senses do not come into contact with their respective sense objects. It corresponds, as it were, to the nature of the mind when it is withdrawn from the sense objects.

Patañjali describes the fifth limb of yoga: pratyāhāra is not forceful suppression of senses but natural withdrawal when the mind is fixed elsewhere. When citta (mind-field) is absorbed in its object of meditation, senses cease their functioning automatically. They point inward, reflecting the mind's inner direction. This is exactly what Kṛṣṇa describes: the devotee whose mind is fixed in fullness does not need to restrain senses separately — they withdraw naturally, like rivers flowing into a self-satisfied ocean.

Term Definitions

  • āpūryamāṇam — always being filled, constantly replenished. The present passive participle implies continuous reception without action required. Like a server receiving requests but requiring no processing cycles for fulfillment — already at capacity.
  • acala-pratiṣṭham — immovably situated, permanently established. The compound emphasizes unshakeability: acala = unshaken, pratiṣṭham = foundation. The depths remain steady regardless of surface conditions.
  • kāma-kāmī — one who desires desires, the desirer of desire-fulfillment. Pleonastic construction emphasizing compulsive pursuit. The person for whom desire is both means and end, trapped in recursive wanting.
  • pratyāhāra — withdrawal of senses from objects; literally "drawing back toward oneself." The fifth limb of yoga where senses cease outward projection and mirror the mind's inward absorption.
  • śānti — peace, tranquility, the natural state of unperturbed consciousness. Not merely absence of disturbance but positive fullness, completeness, self-satisfaction.
