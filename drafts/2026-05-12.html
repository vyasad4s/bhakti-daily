Bhagavad Gītā 5.22 Tuesday, May 12, 2026
ये हि संस्पर्शजा भोगा दु:खयोनय एव ते ।
आद्यन्तवन्त: कौन्तेय न तेषु रमते बुध: ॥ २२ ॥
*ye hi saṁsparśa-jā bhogā
duḥkha-yonaya eva te
ādy-antavantaḥ kaunteya
na teṣu ramate budhaḥ*
An intelligent person does not take delight in the sources of misery, which are due to contact with the material senses. O son of Kuntī, such pleasures have a beginning and an end, and so the wise man does not delight in them.

The material world operates on a principle of exchange: the senses reach out, make contact (saṁsparśa) with an object, and a fleeting sensation arises. This sensation is mistaken for happiness, but it is actually the beginning of a cycle of misery. Why? Because every material contact has a beginning and an end (ādy-antavantaḥ). The pleasure spikes, then fades, leaving a void that triggers renewed craving. The intelligent person (budhaḥ) sees through this illusion. They recognize that seeking happiness in sense contact is like trying to fill a leaking bucket—no matter how much water you pour in, it never stays full.

Aparā Ekadāśī, occurring today, is a divine arrangement for breaking this cycle. Ekādaśī means "the eleventh"—the eleventh day of both the waxing and waning moon. On this day, the influence of the moon on the mind is particularly pronounced, making it easier to withdraw the senses from their usual objects. By observing a fast from grains and beans, we voluntarily reduce the stimulation of the taste and hunger senses, creating a gap in the sensory input stream. This gap allows the mind to turn inward, to hear the subtle vibration of the Holy Name within.

The great ācāryas have emphasized that Ekādaśī fasting is not mere ritualism, but a scientific spiritual practice. Śrīla Rūpa Gosvāmī writes in the Bhakti-rasāmṛta-sindhu that regulative principles like fasting are favorable to devotion when they help control the senses. Śrīla Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura explains that on Ekādaśī, the merit of fasting is multiplied a hundredfold, making it an especially potent day for spiritual advancement. By engaging in this practice, we are not torturing the body; we are resetting the consciousness to its natural state of joy, which is independent of external circumstances.

Moreover, BG 5.22 is not a call for austere renunciation that tortures the body, but for intelligent engagement with the world. The verse does not say to avoid all sense contact—that would be impossible while inhabiting a material body. It says the intelligent person does not delight in the sources of misery. One can still use the senses in the service of the Lord—offering food, decorating the temple, listening to kīrtan—without becoming attached to the sensations themselves. This is the higher standard: to engage with the world while remaining situated in the unchanging joy of the soul.

The Loop of Temporary Gratification

Modern neuroscience confirms what the śāstras have declared for millennia: sensory pleasures trigger dopamine release in the brain's reward pathways. This creates a short-lived feeling of euphoria, followed by a drop below baseline as the brain seeks to restore equilibrium. The result is a craving for more stimulation to regain the high—a cycle that underlies addiction to everything from sugar to social media. The Bhagavad Gītā identifies the root of this cycle not in the objects themselves, but in our misidentification with the body and mind. When we believe "I am this body," we naturally seek to protect and pleasure it. When we understand "I am the eternal soul," we see the body as a vehicle to be used in divine service, not as the source of our identity.

The Ekādaśī fast is a practical exercise in viveka (discrimination between the eternal and the temporary). By voluntarily limiting certain sensory inputs, we create the conditions to experience a higher taste: the joy of loving service to Śrī Kṛṣṇa. This is not a negative denial, but a positive discovery. As we fast, we may notice that the urges for food or entertainment do not disappear, but they lose their compulsive force. We can observe them as passing clouds in the sky of consciousness, rather than identifying with them. This shift in perspective is the essence of yoga—the union of the individual consciousness with the Supreme.

Consider the analogy of a computer running unnecessary background processes that consume CPU and memory, slowing down the system. The senses, when uncontrolled, are like those processes—constantly demanding attention and draining our spiritual energy. Ekādaśī is like opening the task manager and ending those processes, freeing up resources for what truly matters: hearing, chanting, and remembering the Lord. When we return to normal eating the next day, we do so with greater mindfulness, having experienced the freedom that comes from sensory discipline.

Cosmic Interpretation: Apara Ekadashi

Today is Aparā Ekādaśī, a day specifically dedicated to the purification of the heart and the discipline of the senses. The name "Aparā" means "the greater" or "the unlimited," indicating that the spiritual benefit obtained on this day is extraordinary. In the Vedic calendar, Ekādaśī is a strategic "system reset." By fasting from grains and beans, we intentionally disrupt the physical interface of taste and hunger, forcing the consciousness to withdraw from the material bhogāḥ (enjoyments) and redirect its energy inward.

The ruling nakshatra today is Pūrva Bhādrapadā (until mid-afternoon), then Uttara Bhādrapadā. Bhādrapadā means "the blessed feet," referring to the auspiciousness of those who take shelter at the Divine's feet. This nakshatra is associated with the deity Ajaikapāt, a form of Rudra who grants liberation to devotees. Its energy is fiery and transformative—ideal for burning away the accumulated impressions of sense enjoyment. The yoga (lunar-solar combination) is Vaidhṛti (until early afternoon), then Viṣkambha. Vaidhṛti means "that which sustains," indicating a stabilizing influence that supports the practitioner through the austerity of the fast.

Aligning this with BG 5.22, we see that the "sources of misery" are not to be feared, but understood and transcended. The intelligent person does not blindly reject the world, nor do they indulge blindly. They use the discriminative faculty (viveka) to see that lasting happiness cannot be found in temporary contacts. Today's cosmic energy provides a powerful assist: the disciplined observance of Ekādaśī allows us to empirically test this truth. When we fast, we may notice that the world does not end; in fact, the mind often becomes clearer, more peaceful, and more receptive to spiritual vibration.

The special merit of Aparā Ekādaśī is said to grant the result of observing all Ekādaśīs of the year. This is not a magical loophole, but an acknowledgement that sincere observance of this day can kickstart a lasting change in habit. By making a dedicated effort today, we establish a momentum that can carry us forward in our spiritual practice.

Yoga Sūtra Connection

Yoga Sūtra 1.15: dṛṣṭānushravika-vishaya-vitṛshnasya vashīkāra-samjñā vairāgyam
"Dispassion is the controlled consciousness of one who is without craving for sense objects, whether these are actually perceived, or described."

Patañjali defines Vairāgyam (dispassion) not as a state of misery or hatred toward the world, but as a state of "non-craving." While BG 5.22 warns us that material pleasures are the sources of misery, YS 1.15 provides the technical state to overcome them. When we realize that the pleasure is temporary (BG 5.22), the craving naturally evaporates, and we enter the state of Vairagya. This is the "root access" to stability: once the mind stops hunting for external spikes, it settles into its own inherent luminosity.

Furthermore, YS 2.54 explicitly defines pratyāhāra as the withdrawal of the senses from their objects—the very practice we undertake on Ekādaśī. Pratyāhāra is the fifth limb of aṣṭāṅga yoga, serving as the bridge between the external practices (yama, niyama, āsana, prāṇāyāma) and the internal practices (dhāraṇa, dhyāna, samādhi). By mastering pratyāhāra, the yogi gains the ability to sit in meditation without being disturbed by itches, noises, or hunger pangs. The Ekādaśī fast, therefore, is not an isolated ritual, but a vital step in the eight-limbed path to samādhi.

saṁsparśa (contact) — The interface between a sense organ and a material object; the "input" that triggers a sensory reaction.

bhogāḥ (enjoyments) — The outputs of sensory stimulation; the temporary "highs" produced by material interaction.

budhaḥ (intelligent person) — One who possesses Viveka (discernment) and can debug the illusion of temporary pleasure.

ekādaśī — The eleventh day of the lunar fortnight, a day prescribed for fasting and increased devotion.

pratyāhāra — The withdrawal of the senses from their objects; the fifth limb of aṣṭāṅga yoga.

vairāgyam — Dispassion or non-attachment; freedom from craving for sense objects.

Today's Practice Guide

Observing the Fast: If your health permits, observe a full or partial fast from grains and beans today. This means avoiding items like wheat, rice, oats, corn, lentils, and beans. You may eat fruits, nuts, milk, and root vegetables. The purpose is not to punish the body, but to reduce the stimulation of the lower senses, particularly taste and hunger. If you are unable to fast due to health reasons, you can still observe the spirit of the day by reducing unnecessary sensory indulgence—limiting social media, watching less television, and avoiding junk food.

Sensory Awareness: Throughout the day, whenever you notice a craving arise—for food, for entertainment, for distraction—pause and label it: "This is a temporary sensation with a beginning and an end." Then, consciously shift your attention to a spiritual alternative: chanting the Hare Kṛṣṇa mahā-mantra, reading a verse from the Gītā, or listening to a kīrtan recording. By doing this repeatedly, you weaken the neural pathways that link sensory input to compulsive behavior.

Meditation on Impermanence: Take a few minutes to contemplate the transient nature of all material experiences. Recall a recent pleasure—a delicious meal, a fun outing, a purchase—and observe how the feeling faded, leaving you perhaps wanting more. This reflection reinforces the teaching of BG 5.22 and helps to cultivate vairagya.

Service-Oriented Action: Engage in at least one act of loving service today. This could be offering water to a tulsi plant, helping a family member, or cleaning a sacred space. Perform the action with the mindset that you are not the enjoyer, but an instrument of the Lord. This transforms ordinary activity into karma-yoga, further freeing you from the sense of being the doer.
