Bhagavad Gita Chapter 9, Verse 10

मयाध्यक्षेण प्रकृति: सूयते सचराचरम् । हेतुनानेन कौन्तेय जगद्विपरिवर्तते ॥ १० ॥

 

mayādhyakṣeṇa prakṛtiḥ
sūyate sa-carācaram
hetunānena kaunteya
jagad viparivartate

 

This material nature, which is one of My energies, is working under My direction, O son of Kuntī, producing all moving and nonmoving beings. Under its rule this manifestation is created and annihilated again and again.

 

Today's cosmic configuration is a precision match for BG 9.10 — the verse that reveals the divine Hand behind all material operation. The nakṣatra is Hasta, whose very name means "the Hand." Ruled by Savitṛ — the solar impeller, the deity who stirs creation into motion — Hasta embodies the principle of skilled, directed power. When Kṛṣṇa says mayādhyakṣeṇa ("by My superintendence"), He is describing exactly what Hasta represents: the Hand that guides without itself being bound. Material nature (prakṛtiḥ) moves, produces, creates, annihilates — but always under direction. The Hand is not the mechanism; it is the intelligence behind the mechanism. The yoga for today is Śiva, beginning at 9:52 AM. Śiva yoga here is not merely auspicious timing — it carries the energetic signature of dissolution and regeneration, which is precisely what this verse describes: jagad viparivartate — the cosmic manifestation turns, cycles, is created and annihilated again and again. Śiva is the deity who destroys so that creation may continue. BG 9.10 reveals that this cyclical destruction is not chaos but method — prakṛti operating under divine superintendence, turning the wheel of creation according to law, not random collapse. The tithi transition from Śukla Ṣaṣṭhī to Saptami at 6:00 PM marks a building crescendo. The sixth day carried the warrior energy of Skanda; now Saptami — the seventh — carries the momentum of structured, directed growth. This is not expansion for its own sake but hetunā — for a reason, with purpose. The universe doesn't spin accidentally. It turns because the Hand directs it, and the reason is woven into its very operation. Sunday (Ravivāra), ruled by the Sun, reinforces Savitṛ's presence — the solar impeller whose glance sets prakṛti in motion, just as Kṛṣṇa's glance (īkṣaṇa) activates material nature.

 

The sāṅkhya precision of this verse cannot be overstated. Prakṛti is not independent. She is sūyate — manifested, produced — under the adhyakṣa (superintendence) of the Supreme. The root adhīkṣ means to oversee, to preside. Kṛṣṇa is the adhyakṣa — the presiding consciousness — but He is not entangled in what He presides over. This is the crucial sāṅkhya distinction: puruṣa is the witness, prakṛti is the field, and here we see their relationship made explicit. The Supreme Person directs; material nature executes. The hand shapes the clay; the hand is not the clay. This directly mirrors the Yoga Sūtra's teaching. YS IV.18 reveals that one who sees the guṇas as the sole actor — recognizing the self as non-doer — has attained discriminative wisdom. The practitioner witnesses the three guṇas (sattva, rajas, tamas) churning out all experience, and rather than identifying with their modifications, rests in the awareness that guṇa-pravṛtti (the operations of the modes) is the doer, not the conscious self. BG 8.29 states this same principle: "One who sees that all activities are being performed by prakṛti, and that the self is not the doer, truly sees." BG 9.10 goes one step further — it names the Hand behind prakṛti herself. Not only is prakṛti the doer rather than the soul, but prakṛti herself operates under divine direction. The chain of superintendence: Kṛṣṇa → prakṛti → guṇas → manifestations. For the bhakti-yogī, this is not detached observation — it is Īśvara-praṇidhāna (YS I.23, II.1). Surrender to the Lord means recognizing His hand in everything, including the difficult. When the body suffers, when circumstances frustrate, when the mind churns — prakṛti is operating under direction. This recognition doesn't produce passivity; it produces intelligent cooperation. You stop fighting the mechanism and align with the Mechanic. You stop cursing the clay and trust the Potter. This is the essence of hetunānena — "for this reason" — there is a reason behind the turning, even when we cannot see it.

 

PRACTICE FOR TODAY

Hand Awareness: The Hasta nakṣatra carries the energy of the Hand — skill, dexterity, craftsmanship. Today, notice your own hands in action. Each time you use your hands (typing, cooking, opening, closing), remember: mayādhyakṣeṇa — there is a Hand behind your hand. Let physical awareness become spiritual remembrance.

Śiva Yoga Witnessing: During the Śiva yoga period (9:52 AM onward), spend 10 minutes witnessing the turning of your own mental prakṛti. Thoughts arise, dissolve, arise again. jagad viparivartate — the mind-world turns. Don't stop it. Don't push it. Just see: you are the witness, not the turner. The Hand behind it is Kṛṣṇa's.

Non-Doer Recognition: Three times today, before an action you identify as "mine," pause and offer: "This is being performed by prakṛti under Your direction, not by me." Let this lift the weight of doership. Watch how the action still happens — often more smoothly — without the friction of ego-involvement.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

mayā (instrumental) — by Me; through the agency of the Supreme Lord Kṛṣṇa

adhyakṣeṇa (instrumental) — by superintendence; by presiding oversight; from adhi-īkṣ, to oversee

prakṛtiḥ (nominative) — material nature; the fundamental creative principle that evolves into all manifested reality

sūyate (passive verb) — is manifested; is produced; is brought into being (passive voice emphasizes that prakṛti is acted upon, not self-originating)

sa-carācaram (compound) — both moving (cara) and nonmoving (acara); all living entities from microbes to demigods, plus the inert material world

hetunā (instrumental) — for the reason; by this cause; indicating purposeful, not random, operation

anena (pronoun) — this; referring to the superintendence just described

kaunteya (vocative) — O son of Kuntī; addressing Arjuna by his mother's name, emphasizing personal relationship

jagat (nominative) — the cosmic manifestation; the universe; from gam-jñā, "that which moves" — all that is in motion

viparivartate (verb) — is working; revolves; cycles; turns反复ly — contains the root vṛt (to turn), source of "vṛtti" (mental modification)

Sāṅkhya Lens

From the sāṅkhya perspective, BG 9.10 is the foundational verse on the prakṛti-puruṣa relationship at the cosmic level. Prakṛti is not self-acting — she is sūyate (passive: "is manifested"). She requires the adhyakṣa — the presiding consciousness — to activate her. This is the macrocosmic version of what YS IV.18 describes microcosmically: the guṇas operate, the puruṣa witnesses. At the cosmic level, the Supreme Person is the adhyakṣa; at the individual level, the jīva's recognition of non-doership is the sāṅkhya discrimination (viveka) that leads to liberation.

The verse uses the passive voice deliberately. sūyate — "is manifested" — not "manifests herself." Prakṛti doesn't self-generate; she is generated. The Hand shapes the clay; the clay doesn't shape itself. For the sāṅkhya practitioner, this means: your body, mind, emotions, and circumstances are prakṛti being manifested — they are being done, not doing. The one who sees this (BG 8.29) "truly sees."

The verse's final word — viparivartate (turns, revolves) — contains the root vṛt, the same root as vṛtti (mental modification) in YS I.2 (citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ). The macrocosmic turning of the universe (jagad viparivartate) and the microcosmic turning of the mind (citta-vṛtti) share the same root because they share the same nature: both are prakṛti in motion. And both operate under superintendence. When the hand turns the wheel, the wheel turns. When the mind's modifications are stilled, the Hand behind them becomes visible.

Today's Hasta energy makes this tangible. Feel your hands. They move by the gunas — nervous system, muscle, bone, prāṇa — all prakṛti. But the adhyakṣa — the intelligence that intends the movement — that is the Hand behind your hand. The sāṅkhya discrimination is not abstract metaphysics. It is felt reality: "I am not the doer; the Hand is. I am the witness." This is viveka. This is freedom.
