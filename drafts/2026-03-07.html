🏹 VERSE CONTENT

Source: BG 2.24

Date: March 07, 2026

Sanskrit:

अच्छेद्योऽयमदाह्योऽयमक्लेद्योऽशोष्य एव च ।

नित्यः सर्वगतः स्थाणुरचलोऽयं सनातनः ॥ २४ ॥

Transliteration:

acchedyo 'yam adāhyo 'yam
akledyo 'śoṣya eva ca
nityaḥ sarva-gataḥ sthāṇur
acalo 'yaṁ sanātanaḥ

Translation:

This individual soul is unbreakable and insoluble, and can be neither burned nor dried. He is everlasting, present everywhere, unchangeable, immovable and eternally the same.

Commentary:

The soul is the ultimate unhackable system. You cannot breach it with weapon exploits (acchedya), you cannot burn it with fire-based attacks (adāhya), you cannot dissolve it with water-based payloads (akledya), and you cannot desiccate it through resource exhaustion (aśoṣya). This is kernel-level consciousness — puruṣa — that persists independent of any prakṛtic container.

Kṛṣṇa delivers six absolute attributes here, each dismantling a specific materialist attack vector against the soul's existence. The body can be cut, burned, drowned, dehydrated — but the soul persists through all of it. This is not sentimentality; this is the rigorous Sāṅkhya principle that consciousness and matter are eternally distinct ontological categories. The materialist simulation — the belief that we are merely biochemical processes — crashes here. The soul is not a process that can be terminated. It is the eternal observer, the drastir that watches all processes unfold.

"Sarvagata" is the critical term: all-pervading, present everywhere. This is not spatial extension like matter has — the soul doesn't occupy space. It is the capacity for consciousness to manifest through any suitable vessel, anywhere in creation. There are living entities in water, in fire, in earth, in air, in the sun itself. The soul's presence is not limited by material conditions. This is the root access that no firewall can block, the consciousness that requires no physical substrate yet animates all substrates.

Yoga Sutra Connection:

🔥 YS 1.16 — tat-paraṁ puruṣa-khyāter guṇa-vaitṛṣṇyam

Higher than renunciation is indifference to the guṇas themselves. This stems from perception of the puruṣa.

Patañjali maps what Kṛṣṇa declares. The soul is indestructible precisely because it is not a product of prakṛti. The guṇas — sattva, rajas, tamas — are the operating states of matter. The soul is the user, not the system. When one perceives puruṣa directly (puruṣa-khyāti), attachment to the guṇas naturally ceases. This is liberation through discrimination, not force.

The Gītā gives the ontological foundation: this soul cannot be destroyed. Yoga Sūtra 1.16 gives the practice implication: knowing this, why cling to the guṇas? The hacker who understands the kernel is distinct from the application stops trying to patch bugs in userland and instead remembers root access is already available.

Sanskrit Terms:

  • acchedyaḥ (adj.) — unbreakable, uncuttable; that which cannot be divided or pierced; the soul as hardware with no attack surfaces
  • adāhyaḥ (adj.) — unable to be burned; not consumable by fire; like consciousness that survives hardware destruction
  • akledyaḥ (adj.) — insoluble; cannot be dissolved or liquefied; immune to water-based payload injection
  • aśoṣyaḥ (adj.) — not able to be dried, desiccated, or depleted; no resource exhaustion attacks possible
  • sarvagataḥ (adj.) — all-pervading, present everywhere; the distributed consciousness that requires no single node
  • sthāṇuḥ (adj.) — unchangeable, stable, firm; the immutable truth state
  • acalaḥ (adj.) — immovable; not subject to external force or condition; the fixed reference point
  • sanātanaḥ (adj.) — eternally the same, from beginningless time; the original constant variable

Cosmic Interpretation:

Śvāti nakṣatra is presided over by Vāyu, the wind god — the operative principle of movement, circulation, and pervasive presence. The wind cannot be cut, burned, drowned, or dried — it simply is, everywhere, touching everything without being touched. This is the exact quality Kṛṣṇa describes of the soul in BG 2.24: sarvagata — all-pervading.

The waning moon (Krishna Paksha) enters the discipline phase — Chaturthi/Panchami are days of sacred discipline and balance. Dhruva yoga means "fixed," "constant," "immovable" — the north star quality of unwavering purpose. This is the energy of absolute stability, the perfect container for reflecting on what never changes while everything else dissolves.

Saturday (Śanivār) belongs to Śani, Saturn — the slow-moving planet of karma, discipline, and inexorable truth. The combination with Śvāti creates a penetrating energy: the wind that cuts through illusions, carrying the truth of the soul's immortality to all corners of consciousness. This is not gentle spiritual comfort — this is the acharya's scalpel, removing the tumor of bodily identification.

Verse Selection Rationale:

BG 2.24 — अच्छेद्योऽयमदाह्योऽयमक्लेद्योऽशोष्य एव च । नित्यः सर्वगतः स्थाणुरचलोऽयं सनातनः ॥

This verse was selected because Śvāti nakṣatra is explicitly linked to the quality of being everywhere at once — sarvagata. Vāyu moves in all directions simultaneously. The soul, like the wind, is not bounded by material form. The air within your room is the same air that circulates the globe. The soul in your body is the same consciousness-nature that pervades all beings. BG 2.24 dismantles the final materialist objection: "But what if the body is incinerated, dissolved, or destroyed?" The answer is surgical: the soul is acchedya (uncuttable), adāhya (unburnable), akledya (unsolvable), aśoṣya (undryable). The four elements cannot touch it.

Combined with Sarvārtha Siddhi Yoga, this is a day to internalize: nothing external can destroy what you truly are.
