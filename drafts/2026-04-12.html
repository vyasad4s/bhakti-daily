BG 3.8 | Prescribed Duty

नियतं कुरु कर्म त्वं कर्म ज्यायो ह्यकर्मणः ।
शरीरयात्रापि च ते न प्रसिद्ध्येदकर्मणः ॥ ८ ॥

niyataṁ kuru karma tvaṁ karma jyāyo hy akarmaṇaḥ
śarīra-yātrāpi ca te na prasidhyed akarmaṇaḥ

Translation

Perform your prescribed duty, for doing so is better than not working. One cannot even maintain one's physical body without work.

niyatam — prescribed; kuru — do; karma — duties; tvam — you; karma — work; jyāyaḥ — better; hi — certainly; akarmaṇaḥ — than no work; śarīra — bodily; yātrā — maintenance; api — even; ca — also; te — your; na — never; prasidhyet — is effected; akarmaṇaḥ — without work

Commentary

The Gītā continues its surgical strike against spiritual pretension. Arjuna has just discovered that the path of jñāna (knowledge) appears to offer an elegant exit from his dilemma — why kill anyone when all is one, when the eternal soul cannot be slain? It sounds sophisticated. It sounds advanced. It is also completely wrong for him.

Kṛṣṇa's response cuts through the sophistry like a blade. Niyatam kuru karma tvam — "perform your prescribed duty." Not any duty. Not someone else's duty. Not the duty you imagine a more enlightened person might have. Your duty, Arjuna — the duty burning inside you right now, the duty encoded in your station, your time, your capacity. The kṣatriya's dharma is not the brāhmaṇa's dharma. The householder's path is not the sannyāsī's path. The Gītā relentlessly refuses to let us outsource our particular calling onto some universal template.

The verse delivers a devastatingly practical irrefutable: even your body's maintenance demands action. You cannot opt out. The jīva in this world is not on vacation from prakṛti — it is embedded, obligated, implicated. Renunciation without purification is just parasitism wearing spiritual clothing. The pseudo-yogī who sits in meditation funded by someone else's labor trades one illusion for another.

Karma jyāyo hy akarmaṇaḥ — work is better than non-work. This is not a platitude about productivity. This is recognition that the gunas are in motion, that prakṛti's machinery is churning, that standing still is not an option on a moving train. The only question is whether you are steering consciously or being thrown around by forces you pretend aren't there.

Yoga Sūtra Connection

तपःस्वाध्यायेश्वरप्रणिधानानि क्रियायोगः ॥ YS II.1

tapas-svādhyāya-īśvara-praṇidhānāni kriyā-yogaḥ

"Austerity, self-study, and surrender to God are the kriyā-yoga."

Patañjali's definition of kriyā-yoga is not an alternative to karma-yoga — it is its internal architecture. The three components (tapas, svādhyāya, īśvara-praṇidhāna) are precisely what transforms mechanical action into liberating action. Without them, karma produces bondage. With them, the same actions become the runway for takeoff.

Tapas corresponds to BG 3.8's niyatam — the prescribed nature of duty. There is no yoga without the capacity to do what must be done, especially when the senses protest. Svādhyāya prevents mechanical ritualism — the action must remain conscious, examined, understood. Īśvara-praṇidhāna is the escape velocity mechanism, the offer of results upward that prevents bondage.

BG 3.8 and YS II.1 form the complete instruction: act as kriyā-yoga. Transform obligation into offering through the triple engine of discipline, inquiry, and surrender. This is the technology of liberation embedded in the apparent prison of necessity.

Practice

Today, identify one obligation you are currently avoiding through spiritual rationalization. This could be anything: a conversation you don't want to have, a project you've abandoned, care owed to someone, paperwork that must be filed. The content matters less than the pattern — the tendency to use "I'm working on myself" or "I'm in a contemplative phase" as cover for aversion.

Do it. The prescribed action is whatever is in front of you. Before you can transcend action, you must master it. Before you can renounce, you must have something to renounce. This is the mundane gate through which the extraordinary enters.

Yet this wealth is not hoarded; it is distributed through yajña. The eight Vasus are the divine administrators, just as today's verse reminds us that even body-maintenance requires participation in the cosmic economy.

Śubha Yoga (auspicious) governs from 8:45 AM — a rare alignment blessing all undertakings. The quality of "auspicious" does not mean avoiding work; it means performing prescribed duty with the right consciousness. Śubha combined with Dhaniṣṭhā indicates the manifestation of abundance through right action, not renunciation of it.

The Moon enters Kumbha (Aquarius) at 6:14 PM — the sign of distribution and universal service. This completes the arc: perform your niyatam karma (prescribed duty), enter the śubha (auspicious) quality of consciousness, and become the vessel through which abundance flows to others. This is Karma Yoga's complete cycle.
