Bhagavad Gita Chapter 9, Verse 5

न च मत्स्थानि भूतानि पश्य मे योगमैश्वरम् । भूतभृन्न च भूतस्थो ममात्मा भूतभावन: ॥ ५ ॥

 

na ca mat-sthāni bhūtāni paśya me yogam aiśvaram
bhūta-bhṛn na ca bhūta-stho mamātmā bhūta-bhāvanaḥ

 

And yet everything that is created does not rest in Me. Behold My mystic opulence! Although I am the maintainer of all living entities and although I am everywhere, I am not a part of this cosmic manifestation, for My Self is the very source of creation.

 

This verse strikes at the heart of the most persistent confusion in spiritual philosophy: the relationship between the Absolute and the relative. Krishna declares a paradox — all beings rest in Him, yet He is not contained by them. He maintains everything, yet He is not entangled in what He maintains. He is the source of creation, yet He transcends it. This is yogam aiśvaram — the inconceivable mystic power of the Supreme. The Atlas image is the wrong picture. We imagine the Divine straining under the weight of the cosmos, bearing the burden of universal maintenance like some cosmic weightlifter. Krishna dismantles this anthropomorphic projection entirely. The planetary systems float in space — that space is His energy. But He is different from space. He is bhūta-bhṛt, the maintainer, and simultaneously na bhūta-sthaḥ, not situated in the cosmic manifestation. The maintainer is not maintained by what He maintains. The container is not contained. This is where bhedābheda — simultaneous difference and non-difference — becomes not a philosophical abstraction but a lived reality. The Lord is everywhere (all-pervading) and yet aloof (transcendental). He is the source and the substance and the beyond. His mind and Himself are one — there is no gap between His willing and its accomplishment, unlike our experience where intention and execution diverge. Simply by His supreme will, everything is created, sustained, and annihilated. No friction. No fatigue. No compromise.

 

From a practice perspective, this verse is a direct challenge to our tendency to either collapse the Divine into nature (pantheism) or remove Him so far from nature that He becomes irrelevant (deism). Krishna refuses both errors. He pervades all things — your breath, your thoughts, the screen in front of you — and yet He is not any of those things. This means the Divine is impossibly close and infinitely beyond at the same time. Every moment is a meeting point. Every breath is His energy. But if you try to grab Him through the material, you'll get only material. He renders Himself accessible only through bhakti — the loving, surrendered orientation of consciousness that recognizes the paradox without trying to resolve it into a neat philosophical formula.

TERM DEFINITIONS:

yogam aiśvaram (n.) — inconceivable mystic power; the divine potency by which Krishna pervades all without being contained; the Ultimate "you can't box me" energy

bhūta-bhṛt (n.) — maintainer of all living entities; He who sustains every jīva, every organism, every atom — without strain

na bhūta-sthaḥ (phrase) — "not situated in the cosmic manifestation"; Krishna's transcendental aloofness; He is in all but not of all

bhūta-bhāvanaḥ (n.) — the source of all manifestations; the creative origin from which all beings arise; He who brings forth existence itself

mamātmā (pron.) — "My Self"; Krishna's own self, distinct from the cosmic manifestation it pervades; the transcendental home address

Cosmic Interpretation

Today carries an extraordinary cluster of transitions — solar, lunar, and nakṣatric — all within a single day. Karka Saṅkrānti marks the Sun's entry into Cancer, the summer solstice shift, one of the pivotal solar gateways of the year. This is not mere astronomical data; it is a cosmic saṅkrānti — a crossing point, a liminal threshold where one energy exits and another enters. The Sun, representing the ātma-jyoti (the light of the self in Vedic symbolism), moves from the dualistic, mercurial air of Gemini into the emotional, nurturing waters of Cancer.

Simultaneously, the Moon shifts from Cancer to Leo — from the emotional depths of Karka to the royal authority of Siṃha. And the nakṣatra transitions from Āśleṣā — the coiled serpent, wisdom wrapped around itself, the kuṇḍalinī energy held in latency — to Maghā — the royal throne, the seat of ancestral power and authority. From the serpent's coil to the king's throne. From hidden wisdom to its enthronement.

The yoga begins as Siddhī (accomplishment, perfection, attainment) and transitions to Vyatipāta (upheaval, the great turning, the cosmic swing). The朴 build-up of spiritual effort culminates and releases. Energy peaks and reorganizes. The tithi moves from Tṛtīyā (growth, expansion, the building phase) to Caturthī (the fourth, the earth, the settling into form — also Gaṇeśa's number).

BG 9.5 is the perfect verse for this cosmic moment. Krishna says: everything rests in Me, yet I am aloof. All this cosmic transit — the Sun's saṅkrānti, the Moon's rāśi shift, the nakṣatra's serpent-to-throne transition — all of it rests in His inconceivable energy. But He is not any of these transitions. He is their source, their maintainer, and their beyond. Yogam aiśvaram — the mystic opulence by which the infinite pervades the finite without being confined to it.

On Guruwāra — Jupiter's day, the day of the supreme Teacher — we meditate on the supreme Guru who is pūrveśām api guruḥ kālenānavacchedāt (YS I.26): the teacher of the ancients, unlimited by time. The Saṅkrānti reminds us that while the Sun transits through rāśis, the Supreme never transits. He is the field in which all transit occurs — and He transcends the field.

Verse Selection Rationale

[BG 9.5] Alignment: This verse perfectly mirrors today's cosmic signature. Karka Saṅkrānti is fundamentally about transmission — the Sun crossing from one rāśi to another while being neither fully in one nor fully out. This is Krishna's position: pervading all rāśis, all states, all conditions — yet belonging to none. The Āśleṣā → Maghā nakṣatra shift captures the verse's paradox: the coiled kuṇḍalinī wisdom (Āśleṣā, serpent energy) rises to the royal throne (Maghā, the king's seat) — just as the Lord pervades the hidden depths and the royal heights simultaneously, aloof from both.

The Siddhī → Vyatipāta yoga transition is the release after attainment. Having "accomplished" the verse's understanding, we are ready for the upheaval of Vyatipāta — the dissolution of the neat philosophical boxes we try to put the Divine in. Guruwāra amplifies this: Jupiter is the planet of wisdom, the teacher, and the day's energy supports confronting the paradox of all-pervasion + aloofness without retreating into comfortable doctrine. No other verse in BG Chapter 9 so precisely captures the Lord's simultaneously-immanent-and-transcendent position.

Sāṅkhya Lens

[Puruṣa and prakṛti — the ultimate asymmetry]: BG 9.5 is perhaps the most direct statement of the Sāṅkhya dualism applied to theology. In Sāṅkhya, puruṣa (consciousness) and prakṛti (matter) are eternally distinct. The living entities (bhūtāni) are prakṛtic evolutes — products of the material energy. Krishna declares they rest in Him (He is the source of both puruṣa and prakṛti, being the Supreme Whole), yet He is not within the cosmic manifestation as one more object among objects.

[Yoga Sūtra Bridge — YS I.24]: Patañjali defines Īśvara as puruṣa-viśeṣa — a special puruṣa, untouched by kleśa (afflictions), karma (action), vipāka (fructification), and āśaya (subconscious residues). This is the Yoga Sūtra's equivalent of Krishna's declaration in BG 9.5. The Lord pervades all prakṛtic manifestation as its maintainer (bhūta-bhṛt), yet He is aparāmṛṣṭa — untouched, untainted. The Saṅkrānti energy of today — the Sun crossing thresholds — is the cosmic enactment of this principle: the Divine moves through all states without being moved by any of them.

[Yoga Sūtra I.40 — the mind pervading all]: Patañjali describes how in deep samādhi, the yogi's mind becomes potentially omnipresent — pervading the entirety of prakṛti from the smallest atom to the cosmos (YS I.40). This is the microcosmic reflection of Krishna's macrocosmic position. The yogi's consciousness, being an expression of pure sattva, can pervade all just as the Divine pervades all — yet the yogi remains distinct from what they pervade. This is the practice-dimension of BG 9.5: we are to cultivate a consciousness that touches all things without being entangled by them. The verse is not just theology — it is the template for yoga.

[Bhedābheda — the relationship that resolves nothing]: There is a temptation to resolve the paradox of BG 9.5 — to say "yes, but..." or "it really means..." The Yoga school resists this. Bhedābheda — simultaneous difference and non-difference — is not a philosophical compromise but an honest description of reality that refuses to collapse into either monism (everything is one) or dualism (everything is separate). The Saṅkrānti embodies this: the Sun is both in Gemini and in Cancer during the transit. The nakṣatra is both Āśleṣā and Maghā during the transition. The yoga is both Siddhī and Vyatipāta. Reality is liminal. Krishna is liminal. And that is not confusion — that is yogam aiśvaram.
