🏹 BG 6.30 | Thursday, May 28, 2026

 

यो मां पश्यति सर्वत्र सर्वं च मयि पश्यति ।

तस्याहं न प्रणश्यामि स च मे न प्रणश्यति ॥ ३० ॥

 

yo māṁ paśyati sarvatra sarvaṁ ca mayi paśyati / tasyāhaṁ na praṇaśyāmi sa ca me na praṇaśyati

 

For one who sees Me everywhere and sees everything in Me, I am never lost, nor is he ever lost to Me.

 

This verse is Kṛṣṇa's most intimate declaration of mutual non-separation — and it arrives not as philosophy but as promise. Two movements define it. The first is double-vision: yo māṁ paśyati sarvatra — seeing Kṛṣṇa everywhere; sarvaṁ ca mayi paśyati — seeing everything in Kṛṣṇa. These are not the same perception reversed. They are two distinct modalities of awareness. Seeing Kṛṣṇa everywhere is the transcendental gaze — the yogī perceives the Supersoul (Paramātmā) in every heart, every creature, every blade of grass. The world doesn't disappear; it becomes transparent. Seeing everything in Kṛṣṇa is the reverse — all phenomena, all diversity, all apparently separate objects are recognized as residing within His energy. The first is the path inward; the second is the path outward. Together they form a complete circuit of perception, and the yogī who completes this circuit sees sarvatra sama-darśanaḥ — equally everywhere — which is precisely how BG 6.29 described the prerequisite state.

The second movement is the result — and it is stated with startling directness. Tasyāhaṁ na praṇaśyāmi: "I am never lost to him." Sa ca me na praṇaśyati: "He is never lost to Me." Kṛṣṇa does not say "he attains Me" or "he merges with Me." He says something far more radical: a permanent, mutual, unbreakable visibility. The devotee cannot lose sight of Kṛṣṇa; Kṛṣṇa cannot lose sight of the devotee. This is not liberation as annihilation — it is liberation as relationship eternally confirmed. Prabhupāda makes this explicit in his purport: "To merge in Kṛṣṇa is spiritual annihilation. A devotee takes no such risk." The bhakti path doesn't dissolve the lover into the Beloved. It establishes an intimacy that death itself cannot sever.

The verb praṇaśyāmi carries weight beyond mere "loss." Its root naś means to perish, vanish, be destroyed. Kṛṣṇa is saying: for the yogī who sees this way, I can never perish from his vision, and he can never perish from Mine. This is the ultimate security — not the security of a safe investment or a comfortable life, but the security of knowing that the Supreme Person has permanently registered your existence. You are seen. You are held. You are never forgotten.

 

🔥 YOGA SŪTRA CONNECTION: YS I.36–I.37

viśokā vā jyotiṣmatī — "Or [stability comes from] the luminous [mind] which is free from sorrow." (YS I.36)

vīta-rāga-viṣayaṁ vā cittam — "Or [stability comes from] the mind which has dispassion for its object." (YS I.37)

Patañjali offers a sequence of antidotes for the mind's scattering. YS I.36 points to the sorrowless inner light — the same luminosity the yogī sees when Kṛṣṇa's presence becomes visible everywhere. When the mind is cleared of sorrow (viśokā), it becomes radiant (jyotiṣmatī), and this radiance is not self-generated — it is the reflection of puruṣa shining through purified buddhi. BG 6.30 describes the fruit of this: the one who sees this light in all beings is the one who sees Kṛṣṇa everywhere.

YS I.37 takes the next step: the mind that has dispassion (vīta-rāga) as its object. When rāga — attachment, coloration, the filtering of perception through desire — is removed, the mind sees what is actually there. BG 6.30 is precisely what appears when rāga no longer distorts perception: Kṛṣṇa everywhere, everything in Kṛṣṇa. The Yoga Sūtras describe the method of purifying perception. The Gītā describes what is seen when perception is pure. Same engine. Two angles of vision.

 >>> COSMIC INTERPRETATION <<<

Trayodaśī is the tithi of completion's threshold — the thirteenth day of the waxing fortnight, one step before the fullness of Caturdaśī, two steps before Pūrṇimā. The moon is nearly full, its light almost unobscured. This is the cosmic energy of almost-there — the vision is nearly complete, the seeing is nearly total. BG 6.30 is the verse of one who has arrived at this seeing: Kṛṣṇa everywhere, everything in Kṛṣṇa. Trayodaśī's near-full luminosity mirrors the near-complete perception of the divine yogī.

Svātī, ruled by Vāyu (the wind god), is the nakṣatra of independence and self-direction — the wind that cannot be contained, that moves freely through all space. This is the nakṣatra of one who has shed all dependencies and stands alone in clear perception. The yogī of BG 6.30 has this Svātī independence: his seeing is not dependent on external conditions, not contingent on mood or circumstance. He sees Kṛṣṇa everywhere because his perception is free — as free as wind through an open sky. As Svātī transitions to Viśākhā (ruled by Indrāgnī, the combined fire of Indra and Agni), the energy shifts from solitary clarity to purposeful union — the independent seer moves toward devoted alignment. This is the trajectory of BG 6.30 itself: from seeing Kṛṣṇa everywhere (Svātī's unbounded perception) to being permanently held in Kṛṣṇa's vision (Viśākhā's covenantal fire).

Pradoṣa Vrat — the Trayodaśī observance of Śiva worship at sunset — adds a profound layer. Pradoṣa is the twilight hour when day and night dissolve into each other, when boundaries between realms thin. Śiva, the supreme yogī, is worshipped at this liminal moment. BG 6.30 is itself a pradoṣa verse — it describes the threshold where seeing and being-seen merge, where the boundary between devotee and Lord becomes permeable without dissolving. The Pradoṣa energy reminds us: this vision is not achieved at noon's harsh clarity but at twilight's intimate merging of light and shadow.

Parigha yoga, arriving in the evening, means "crossbar" or "barrier" — but also that which crosses a barrier. The parigha is both the obstacle and the instrument that removes it. This duality maps onto the verse's central paradox: the only barrier to seeing Kṛṣṇa everywhere is the rāga that filters perception; once removed, the same mind becomes the instrument of total vision.

 >>> VERSE SELECTION RATIONALE <<<

BG 6.30 Alignment: Four cosmic vectors converge on this verse:

1. Trayodaśī near-fullness: The thirteenth tithi's almost-complete luminosity mirrors the verse's "almost-total" vision — seeing Kṛṣṇa everywhere is the fullest possible perception, just as the nearly-full moon is the fullest possible light before absolute completeness (Pūrṇimā).

4. Parigha yoga: The crossbar that is both barrier and key — rāga is the parigha that blocks vision; vīta-rāga is the parigha that opens it. Same instrument, opposite function, depending on how it's turned.

 >>> SĀṄKHYA LENS <<<

Prakṛti as Kṛṣṇa's energy: BG 6.30 is the Sāṅkhya realization rendered in devotional terms. In classical Sāṅkhya, all of prakṛti exists for puruṣa's experience — but the Gītā reveals that all of prakṛti exists within Kṛṣṇa's energy. When the yogī sees "everything in Me," he is seeing what Sāṅkhya describes structurally: there is no substance, no form, no phenomenon that exists outside the guṇas, and the guṇas are Kṛṣṇa's delegated power. The verse doesn't contradict Sāṅkhya — it personalizes it. The impersonal mechanics of puruṣa-prakṛti become the intimate recognition that every movement of prakṛti is Kṛṣṇa's movement.

Rāga as the distortion field: The reason most people don't see Kṛṣṇa everywhere is not because He isn't there — it's because rāga distorts perception. In Sāṅkhya terms, rāga is rajas coloring the buddhi so that it reflects desire instead of puruṣa. When rāga is removed (vīta-rāga, YS I.37), the buddhi becomes a clean mirror. And what does the clean mirror reflect? Not emptiness — Kṛṣṇa. Everywhere. In everything.

Mutual non-perishing: The Sāṅkhya framework explains why this mutual visibility is eternal. Puruṣa cannot perish — it is eternal consciousness. When the devotee's buddhi is purified to the point of reflecting Kṛṣṇa without distortion, the devotee's awareness participates in puruṣa's eternity. "He is never lost to Me" is Kṛṣṇa confirming what Sāṅkhya already establishes: once puruṣa is truly seen, it cannot be unseen.
