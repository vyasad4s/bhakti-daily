Bhagavad Gita Chapter 8, Verse 11

यदक्षरं वेदविदो वदन्ति विशन्ति यद्यतयो वीतरागा: । यदिच्छन्तो ब्रह्मचर्यं चरन्ति तत्ते पदं सङ्ग्रहेण प्रवक्ष्ये ॥ ११ ॥
yad akṣaraṁ veda-vido vadanti viśanti yad yatayo vīta-rāgāḥ yad icchanto brahma-caryaṁ caranti tat te padaṁ saṅgraheṇa pravakṣye
Persons who are learned in the Vedas, who utter oṁ-kāra, and who are great sages in the renounced order enter into Brahman. Desiring such perfection, one practices celibacy. I shall now briefly explain to you this process by which one may attain salvation.
Lord Śrī Kṛṣṇa explains that those who are versed in the Vedas and who vibrate the sacred syllable oṁ enter into the impersonal Brahman. Great sages in the renounced order of life, who are free from attachment, also attain this Brahman. Those who desire such perfection practice celibacy (brahmacarya) as a means to spiritual advancement. The Lord promises to explain this process in detail in the following verses. This verse establishes oṁ-kāra as the sonic representation of Brahman, accessible through Vedic knowledge and renunciation. The practice of brahmacarya is presented as a preparatory discipline for those seeking realization of the Absolute. In the current age, however, Lord Caitanya has revealed that the sankirtana movement—chanting the holy names Hare Kṛṣṇa—is the yuga-dharma and the most accessible path to spiritual perfection, especially when traditional Vedic studies and celibate student life are difficult to maintain.

Yoga Sutra Connection

YS I.27: tasya vācakaḥ praṇavaḥ

His word is oṁ.

This sutra directly illuminates BG 8.11 by revealing that oṁ-kāra is not merely a sound but the vibrational symbol (vācaka) of the Supreme Lord (īśvara). Just as the Vedic scholars and sages in the BG verse access Brahman through oṁ, the Yoga Sutras declare that oṁ is the designated sound representing Ishvara itself. The practice of omkāra japa (repetition of oṁ) with feeling of its meaning (YS I.28) leads to direct perception of the inner self and removal of obstacles (YS I.29). Thus, the syllable oṁ serves as the bridge between the impersonal Brahman of BG 8.11 and the personal Ishvara of Yoga philosophy - both pointing to the same ultimate reality accessible through sacred sound.
