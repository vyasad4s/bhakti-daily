BG 7.7 — The Thread That Holds All

मत्त: परतरं नान्यत्किञ्चिदस्ति धनञ्जय ।

मयि सर्वमिदं प्रोतं सूत्रे मणिगणा इव ॥ ७ ॥

mattaḥ parataraṁ nānyat kiñcid asti dhanañ-jaya
mayi sarvam idaṁ protaṁ sūtre maṇi-gaṇā iva

O conqueror of wealth, there is no truth superior to Me. Everything rests upon Me, as pearls are strung on a thread.

Commentary

This is the axis verse of Chapter 7. Everything before it has been preparation — the method (7.1: attach your mind), the promise (7.2: complete knowledge), the rarity of realization (7.3: hardly one knows Me), the classification of natures (7.4–5: lower and higher prakṛti), the declaration of origin and dissolution (7.6: I am the source and the end). Now Kṛṣṇa delivers the statement from which all other statements derive their authority: mattaḥ parataraṁ nānyat kiñcid asti — beyond Me, there is nothing. Not "nothing better." Not "nothing equal." Nothing. The word kiñcit is emphatic — even the smallest thing, the most subtle principle, the most abstract concept — none of it transcends Him.

Then the image that locks the teaching into permanence: sūtre maṇi-gaṇā iva — as pearls on a thread. This is not a philosophical argument. It is a vision. See a necklace — every pearl exists, every pearl is beautiful, every pearl has its place. But remove the thread and what remains? Scattered beads on the floor. No form, no order, no necklace. The pearls do not hold themselves together. The thread is not one pearl among others — it is the condition that makes "pearl" meaningful at all. Kṛṣṇa says: I am that thread. Everything — sarvam idam, all this that you see — is strung upon Me.

The address is significant: dhanañ-jaya — O conqueror of wealth. Arjuna has mastered the material world. He has conquered kingdoms, won treasures, proved himself against every opponent. Kṛṣṇa addresses him by this title deliberately: you who have conquered wealth, understand that all wealth is a pearl. And every pearl needs a thread. Your conquest means nothing if you do not know what holds it all together. The greatest warrior, the wealthiest king, the most accomplished yogī — all are pearls. Beautiful. Valuable. Strung on a thread they did not create and cannot replace. This is not humiliation — it is liberation. When you stop trying to be the thread and take shelter in the One who is, the necklace of your life finds its form.

Yoga Sūtra Bridge: YS II.45

समाधिसिद्धिः ईश्वरप्रणिधानात्

samādhi-siddhiḥ īśvara-praṇidhānāt

Surrender to Īśvara brings about the perfection of samādhi.

Sūtra II.45 is the last of the niyamas — the inner observances that purify the practitioner. It arrives after tapas (discipline, II.43) and svādhyāya (self-study, II.44), and it completes the sequence with a radical shortcut: īśvara-praṇidhānāt — from surrender to the Lord — samādhi-siddhiḥ — the perfection of samādhi. Not "contributes to." Not "supports." Brings about. The causative is absolute.

Vyāsa's bhāṣya explains: this is the most direct path because it targets the thread itself. Tapas works on the body and senses — polishing the pearl. Svādhyāya works on the mind — clarifying the pearl's inner light. But īśvara-praṇidhāna works on the thread — the connecting principle upon which every pearl depends. Why is this more efficient? Because the thread is singular while the pearls are many. You can spend lifetimes polishing individual pearls — each obstacle, each attachment, each impurity. Or you can grasp the thread and recognize that when the thread is held, all pearls are simultaneously ordered.

Kṛṣṇa's image in 7.7 makes Patañjali's method visible. The thread is not an abstract principle — it is a Person. Īśvara is not a concept but the living ground of existence. When Kṛṣṇa says mayi sarvam idaṁ protam — "in Me all this is strung" — He is identifying Himself as the sūtra that Patañjali's practitioners surrender to. The Yoga Sūtras prescribe the technique (surrender). The Gītā reveals the identity of the one surrendered to (Kṛṣṇa). Together: the method and the address. The how and the who. Patañjali says "take hold of the thread." Kṛṣṇa says "I am the thread."

Sanskrit Terms

parataram — Superior to, beyond, higher than. The comparative form of para — supreme, ultimate. Kṛṣṇa does not say He is among the highest — He says there is nothing beyond Him. Not the tallest building, but the ground that makes all buildings possible.

protam — Strung upon, woven through, resting upon. From the root prā — to spread, to extend. Everything is threaded through Kṛṣṇa the way every bead passes through the string. Not adjacent to Him — dependent on Him.

sūtra — Thread, string, the connecting line. Also: the rule, the formula, the concise expression of vast meaning. The Yoga Sūtras are threads of knowledge. Kṛṣṇa is the thread of existence itself. Without the sūtra, the maṇi-gaṇāḥ (pearls) are scattered beads with no coherence.

maṇi-gaṇāḥ — Pearls, gems, a collection of jewels. Every individual entity — every living being, every element, every world — is a pearl. Beautiful in itself, but meaningless without the thread that connects it to all others and gives the necklace its form.

dhanañ-jaya — O conqueror of wealth — an epithet of Arjuna. Kṛṣṇa addresses the one who has conquered material riches, reminding him that all wealth rests on a thread — and the thread is not wealth. It is something else entirely.

īśvara-praṇidhāna — Surrender, devotion, offering of all actions to the Lord. Patañjali's term for the bhakti path. Not passive resignation — the active placement of oneself upon the thread, trusting that the thread holds.

samādhi-siddhi — The perfection of samādhi, the completion of yogic absorption. The fruit of surrender: not a technique achieved, but a state revealed when the practitioner stops grasping at pearls and takes hold of the thread.

On the exhale: mayi sarvam idaṁ protaṁ sūtre maṇi-gaṇā iva — everything rests on You like pearls on a thread.
