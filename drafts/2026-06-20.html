Bhagavad Gita Chapter 8, Verse 15

आब्रह्मभुवनाल्लोकाः punar āvartino ’rjuna । मामुपेत्य पुनर्जन्म दु:खालयमशाश्वतम् । नाप्‍नुवन्ति महात्मान: संसिद्धिं परमां गता: ॥ १५ ॥
ā-brahma-bhuvanāl lokāḥ
punar āvartino ’rjuna
mām upetya punar janma
duḥkhālayam aśāśvatam
nāpnuvanti mahātmānaḥ
saṁsiddhiṁ paramāṁ gatāḥ
From the highest planet in the material world down to the lowest, all are places of misery wherein repeated birth and death take place. But one who attains to My abode, O son of Kuntī, never takes birth again.
From the Sankhya perspective, the material world (prakṛti) is a temporary, miserable place of repeated birth and death (saṃsāra). This verse contrasts the temporary nature of all material planetary systems (even the highest, Brahmaloka) with the eternal abode of the Supreme Personality of Godhead. One who attains Kṛṣṇa’s abode never returns to the cycle of birth and death because they have achieved the highest perfection (saṁsiddhi). The Yoga Sutra affirms that through dispassion (vairāgya) and practice (abhyāsa), the mind becomes fixed on the puruṣa, leading to liberation (YS I.12‑I.16). Thus the verse serves as both a diagnosis of material existence’s insufficiency and a prescription for attaining the eternal goal through bhakti-yoga.

Cosmic Interpretation

The tithi begins as Sukla Paksha Shashthi (until 06:16 AM), the sixth tithi of the waxing moon associated with Kartikeya (the warrior god), courage, and the power to overcome obstacles. As Shashthi yields to Saptami at 06:16 AM, the energy shifts to the seventh tithi, associated with the sun’s vitality and divine grace. The nakṣatra Purva Phalguni, ruled by Bhaga (the god of prosperity), governs the day, representing enjoyment, prosperity, and union. As Purva Phalguni yields to Uttara Phalguni (also ruled by Bhaga) at 12:01 AM on June 21, the energy continues the theme of prosperity and enjoyment. The yoga Siddhi (perfection, accomplishment) prevails from 03:18 AM, signifying fulfillment and success, after which Vyatipata (scattered, disruptive energy) brings the energy of overcoming obstacles through sudden insight.

BG 8.15 beautifully aligns with this cosmic rhythm: “From the highest planet in the material world down to the lowest, all are places of misery wherein repeated birth and death take place. But one who attains to My abode, O son of Kuntī, never takes birth again.” Just as Shashthi’s energy of courage (through Kartikeya’s grace) gives way to Saptami’s vitality, and as Purva Phalguni’s prosperity yields to Uttara Phalguni’s continued enjoyment—so too does the devotee progress by realizing that all material planets, from the highest to the lowest, are temporary places of misery, while the abode of the Supreme is eternal and free from rebirth. Siddhi’s energy of perfection mirrors the verse’s declaration that the great souls attain the highest perfection (saṁsiddhi) and never return. Vyatipata’s disruptive energy represents the breaking free from the cycle of saṁsāra through sudden spiritual insight.

The verse invites us to recognize that all material planetary systems (ā-brahma-bhuvanāl lokāḥ) are temporary places of misery (duḥkhālayam aśāśvatam) where repeated birth and death occur. But one who attains the Supreme’s abode (mām upetya) achieves the highest perfection (saṁsiddhiṁ) and never returns to rebirth (punar janma na vidyate). In the language of the energy centers, this is the realization that even the highest material achievements are temporary, while the supreme goal is eternal liberation through devotion to Kṛṣṇa.

Connections & Key Concepts

Yoga Sutra Connection

YS I.12‑I.16: tapaḥ svādhyāyeśvara-praṇidhānāni kriyā-yogaḥ
Yoga is composed of discipline, self-study, and devotion to the Lord.

Connection: BG 8.15 contrasts the temporary misery of all material worlds with the eternal perfection of attaining the Supreme’s abode. Yoga Sutra I.12 defines kriyā-yoga as composed of discipline (tapas), self-study (svādhyāya), and devotion to the Lord (īśvara-praṇidhāna). Both point to the same essential practice: through disciplined devotion and self-study, the yogi attains the highest perfection and liberation from the cycle of birth and death. The verse’s injunction to attain the abode from which one never returns is the result of kriyā-yoga, where tapas burns away impurities, svādhyāya provides self-knowledge, and īśvara-praṇidhāna sustains devotion to the Supreme.
