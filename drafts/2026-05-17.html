Bhagavad Gita 5.1

Sanskrit:

श्रीभगवानुवाच । सन्न्यासं कर्मयोगं च निरयेक्षते बुधः । तयोर्योऽकर्मसंज्ञस्तु सः सन्न्यासी परोsपि सः ॥ १

Translation:

The Supreme Lord said: O Arjuna, the sannyasa [renunciation] and karma-yoga [the yoga of action]—both lead to the supreme goal. But of the two, karma-yoga is superior to sannyasa.

Purport:

Lord Krishna explains that both renunciation of work and work in devotion lead to the same goal. However, karma-yoga—performing work as an offering to the Divine—is superior because it engages the practitioner in the world without attachment. Renunciation without action can lead to inertia, whereas karma-yoga purifies the heart through selfless service. This verse sets the tone for the chapter, establishing that true renunciation is found in performing one's duty without selfish motive.

Yoga Sutra Connection

YS 2.1: Tapas svādhyāya īśvarapraṇidhānāni kriyāyogaḥ — Austerity, self-study, and surrender to God constitute the yoga of action.

This sutra directly complements BG 5.1 by defining karma-yoga (kriyāyoga) as a threefold practice: tapas (disciplined effort), svādhyāya (self-reflection through scripture), and īśvarapraṇidhānā (surrender to the Divine). Just as Krishna advocates for action in devotion, Patanjali outlines the practical framework for such action. Together, they show that true action is not mere activity but a conscious, offered performance that purifies the practitioner.

Glossary Terms

Sannyasa (संन्यास): Renunciation of prescribed duties motivated by desire for sense gratification. In BG 5.1, Krishna contrasts sannyasa with karma-yoga, showing that true renunciation is not inaction but action without selfish attachment.

Karma-yoga (कर्मयोग): The yoga of action—performing one's duty as an offering to the Divine, free from attachment to results. This is the superior path described in BG 5.1 for those engaged in the world.

Tapas (तपस्): Austerity or disciplined effort; the first component of kriyāyoga in YS 2.1. It involves enduring opposites (heat/cold, pleasure/pain) for spiritual growth.

Svādhyāya (स्वाध्याय): Self-study, particularly of sacred texts; the second component of kriyāyoga. It cultivates discernment (viveka) and connection to spiritual lineage.

Īśvarapraṇidhāna (ईश्वरप्रणिधान): Surrender to the Divine will; the third component of kriyāyoga. It transforms action into worship by dedicating the fruits to God.

Chandra Darshan (चंद्र दर्शन): The first sighting of the moon after Amavasya (new moon). Symbolizes new beginnings, hope, and the gradual return of light—aligning with the Pratipada tithi and the start of the spiritual practice of karma-yoga.
