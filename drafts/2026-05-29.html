अर्जुन उवाच
अयति: श्रद्धयोपेतो योगाच्च‍लितमानस: ।
अप्राप्य योगसंसिद्धिं कां गतिं कृष्ण गच्छति ॥ ३७ ॥

कच्च‍िन्नोभयविभ्रष्टश्छिन्नाभ्रमिव नश्यति ।
अप्रतिष्ठो महाबाहो विमूढो ब्रह्मण: पथि ॥ ३८ ॥

एतन्मे संशयं कृष्ण छेत्तुमर्हस्यशेषत: ।
त्वदन्य: संशयस्यास्य छेत्ता न ह्युपपद्यते ॥ ३९ ॥

श्रीभगवानुवाच
पार्थ नैवेह नामुत्र विनाशस्तस्य विद्यते ।
न हि कल्याणकृत्कश्चिद्दुर्गतिं तात गच्छति ॥ ४० ॥

arjuna uvāca
ayatiḥ śraddhayopeto yogāc calita-mānasaḥ
aprāpya yoga-saṁsiddhiṁ kāṁ gatiṁ kṛṣṇa gacchati

kaccin nobhaya-vibhraṣṭaś chinnābhram iva naśyati
apratiṣṭho mahā-bāho vimūḍho brahmaṇaḥ pathi

etan me saṁśayaṁ kṛṣṇa chettum arhasy aśeṣataḥ
tvad-anyaḥ saṁśayasyāsya chettā na hy upapadyate

śrī-bhagavān uvāca
pārtha naiveha nāmutra vināśas tasya vidyate
na hi kalyāṇa-kṛt kaścid durgatiṁ tāta gacchati

Arjuna said: O Kṛṣṇa, what is the destination of the unsuccessful transcendentalist, who in the beginning takes to the process of self-realization with faith but who later desists due to worldly-mindedness and thus does not attain perfection in mysticism?

O mighty-armed Kṛṣṇa, does not such a man, who is bewildered from the path of transcendence, fall away from both spiritual and material success and perish like a riven cloud, with no position in any sphere?

This is my doubt, O Kṛṣṇa, and I ask You to dispel it completely. But for You, no one is to be found who can destroy this doubt.

The Supreme Personality of Godhead said: Son of Pṛthā, a transcendentalist engaged in auspicious activities does not meet with destruction either in this world or in the spiritual world; one who does good, My friend, is never overcome by evil.

Arjuna asks the question every practitioner fears. Not "how do I succeed?" — but "what happens if I fail?" He's been listening to Kṛṣṇa describe the exalted state of the perfected yogī: mind controlled, senses mastered, established in samādhi, seeing the Self in all beings. And the honest warrior in him recoils. He knows his own mind — cañcalam hi manaḥ kṛṣṇa pramāthi balavad dṛḍham — restless, turbulent, obstinate. What if someone begins with faith, with genuine śraddhā, but the mind drags them off the path? Do they become like a chinnābhram — a riven cloud, torn apart in mid-sky, belonging nowhere? Neither a materialist (because they renounced) nor a yogī (because they failed)? Arjuna calls this being apratīṣṭha — without position, without footing. It's the spiritual nightmare: you left the shore, but you never reached the other side.

Kṛṣṇa's response is one of the most consequential statements in the entire Gītā. Three words land like thunder: na hi kalyāṇa-kṛt kaścid durgatiṁ tāta gacchati — "One who does good, My friend, is never overcome by evil." The address is intimate: tāta — My friend, My dear one. Kṛṣṇa doesn't lecture; He reassures. The principle He establishes is absolute: auspicious effort cannot be destroyed. Not in this world (iha), not in the next (amutra). The spiritual economy doesn't work like the material one. In material transactions, half-finished work yields zero return. In spiritual practice, every sincere effort is recorded, preserved, and carried forward. The buddhi-saṁyoga — the revival of divine consciousness from a previous life — is the mechanism. What you learned, what you practiced, what you tasted of transcendence — that data is never overwritten. It reboots in the next incarnation.

This is where Patañjali's Yoga Sūtra I.23 intersects with surgical precision: Īśvara-praṇidhānād vā — "Or, [the state of yoga is attainable] by devotion to the Lord." The vā is critical. It means "alternatively" — or rather, it means there is another way. You don't have to climb the entire eightfold ladder by brute force alone. You can surrender. And Vyāsa's bhāṣya on this sutra explains: devotion to Īśvara involves bhakti-viśeṣa — a particular type of longing — and God bestows His grace upon the yogī. The path of bhakti is favored, says Vyāsa, because it doesn't require one to be "solely dependent on one's own steam and resources." Kṛṣṇa is saying the same thing in BG 6.40. The failed yogī doesn't fail because of insufficient effort — they "fail" because the timeline of human life ran out. But the effort itself is banked. Īśvara remembers. Grace picks up where effort left off. The kalyāṇa-kṛt — the one who performs auspicious action — has an advocate. The system is rigged in favor of sincerity.

Yoga: Parigha → Śiva (transition ~7:05 PM). Parigha = the bar of a door, an obstacle — but also the bar that holds the door shut. Śiva = auspiciousness, the destroyer of obstacles. The day transitions from blockage to blessing.
Moon: Tula (Libra) → Vṛścika (Scorpio, after 9:08 PM). The Moon in Tula seeks balance, equilibrium — the exact state the failed yogī fears they've lost. The transition into Vṛścika, a sign of depth, transformation, and hidden reserves, mirrors the promise of renewal.

The cosmic architecture of this day is a living commentary on BG 6.37-40. Viśākhā's forked branches represent Arjuna's doubt: two paths, neither fully taken, the fear of being ubhaya-vibhraṣṭa — fallen from both. But the nakṣatra transitions to Anurādhā, the star of Mitra — the divine friend. And Kṛṣṇa's answer is precisely that: tāta — My friend. The universe doesn't abandon the sincere seeker; Mitra's energy reaches through. The Caturdaśī tithi — maximum waxing charge, one step from fullness — reflects the state of the yogāc calita-mānasaḥ: they were almost there. The effort reached near-completion. And the yoga transitions from Parigha (obstruction) to Śiva (auspiciousness) — the very movement of Kṛṣṇa's answer. The door-bar lifts. The failed yogī is not failed at all. They are paused, not lost.

YS Bridge: Patañjali I.23 (Īśvara-praṇidhānād vā) + I.26 (pūrveṣām api guruḥ kālenānavacchedāt) — Īśvara is the guru of the ancients, unlimited by time. The failed yogī's practice is never time-barred because Īśvara transcends time. The buddhi-saṁyoga of BG 6.43 is the grace mechanism: what you invested in previous practice is retrieved, not by your own memory, but by Īśvara's omniscience. The kalyāṇa-kṛt has an eternal advocate.

yogāc calita-mānasaḥ — "deviated from yoga due to restless mind" — the spiritual version of a dropped connection. The signal was there; the bandwidth ran out. Not failure — interruption.
ubhaya-vibhraṣṭa — "fallen from both" — the fear of belonging nowhere: neither the material world (you already left) nor the spiritual (you didn't arrive). Kṛṣṇa's answer: you were never fallen. You were in transit.
chinnābhram — "riven cloud" — Arjuna's nightmare image: a cloud torn apart, dissolving into nothing. Kṛṣṇa's implicit counter: even a riven cloud doesn't vanish — its moisture returns to the cycle. Nothing spiritual is lost.
kalyāṇa-kṛt — "one who performs auspicious action" — the key qualification. Not perfection. Not completion. Auspicious effort. The criterion is sincerity of direction, not distance traveled.
buddhi-saṁyoga — "revival of divine consciousness" — the spiritual checksum. Your past practice carries a signature that re-authenticates in the next life. Like saved game data — the progress file persists across incarnations.
Īśvara-praṇidhāna — "devotion/surrender to the Lord" — the alternative path (YS I.23). Not a backup plan — the preferred route. The one path where grace supplements effort, where the system works for you, not just through you.

Practice for Today: The Caturdaśī threshold — the eve of fullness — is the moment to audit what you've started and not finished. Not to shame yourself. To recognize: every sincere round you've chanted, every verse you've studied, every moment you've turned toward Kṛṣṇa — that effort is banked. It cannot be destroyed. The fear of being "too late" or "not far enough" is māyā's last argument. Kṛṣṇa says: na hi kalyāṇa-kṛt kaścid durgatiṁ gacchati. One who does good is never overcome by evil. Let Anurādhā's Mitra-energy remind you: you have a friend. Let the transition from Parigha to Śiva remind you: the bar lifts. You are not a riven cloud. You are a paused process. Resume now.
