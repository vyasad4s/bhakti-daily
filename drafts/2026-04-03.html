Bhagavad-gītā 3.1

अर्जुन उवाच

ज्यायसी चेत्कर्मणस्ते मता बुद्धिर्जनार्दन ।
तत्किं कर्मणि घोरे मां नियोजयसि केशव ॥ १ ॥

arjuna uvāca

jyāyasī cet karmaṇas te matā buddhir janārdana
tat kiṁ karmaṇi ghore māṁ niyojayasi keśava

Translation

Arjuna said: O Janārdana, O Keśava, if You consider knowledge superior to fruitive work, why do You want to engage me in this ghastly warfare?

Prabhupāda's Purport (Excerpt)

The Supreme Personality of Godhead Śrī Kṛṣṇa has very elaborately described the constitution of the soul in the previous chapter, with a view to delivering His intimate friend Arjuna from the ocean of material grief. And the path of realization has been recommended: buddhi-yoga, or Kṛṣṇa consciousness. Sometimes Kṛṣṇa consciousness is misunderstood to be inertia, and one with such a misunderstanding often withdraws to a secluded place to become fully Kṛṣṇa conscious by chanting the holy name of Lord Kṛṣṇa. But without being trained in the philosophy of Kṛṣṇa consciousness, it is not advisable to chant the holy name of Kṛṣṇa in a secluded place, where one may acquire only cheap adoration from the innocent public.

Arjuna also thought of Kṛṣṇa consciousness or buddhi-yoga, or intelligence in spiritual advancement of knowledge, as something like retirement from active life and the practice of penance and austerity at a secluded place. In other words, he wanted to skillfully avoid the fighting by using Kṛṣṇa consciousness as an excuse. But as a sincere student, he placed the matter before his master and questioned Kṛṣṇa as to his best course of action. In answer, Lord Kṛṣṇa elaborately explained karma-yoga, or work in Kṛṣṇa consciousness, in this Third Chapter.

Commentary

Arjuna stands at the precipice of battle, but more significantly, he stands at the precipice of understanding. Having heard Kṛṣṇa's exposition on the immutable, unburnable soul in Chapter 2, he now faces a crisis of application. If the soul is above all material action—if knowledge of this truth liberates—then why engage in action at all? Why not retreat to contemplation?

This is the eternal question of the spiritual practitioner: Does liberation come through withdrawal or through engagement? Through the silence of meditation or through the noise of duty? Arjuna is not arguing against wisdom—he is seeking clarity on its proper expression. His question reveals the sophistication of his seeking; he will not accept superficial answers or spiritual bypassing.

Kṛṣṇa's response will unfold across eighteen verses that form the foundation of Karma Yoga: action performed without attachment to results, action as worship, action purified by consciousness. The path of knowledge (jñāna-yoga) and the path of action (karma-yoga) are not opponents—they are stages, approaches, expressions suited to different temperaments and different phases of development.

Yoga Sutra Connection

YS II.1 — Kriyā Yoga

tapas svādhyāya īśvara-praṇidhānāni kriyā-yogaḥ

Kriyā Yoga consists of discipline (tapas), self-study (svādhyāya), and devotion to the Supreme (īśvara-praṇidhāna).

Patañjali outlines three paths in one practice: the path of action (tapas, discipline/effort), the path of knowledge (svādhyāya, study/self-inquiry), and the path of devotion (īśvara-praṇidhāna, surrender to the Divine). Arjuna's dilemma rests on a false dichotomy between knowledge and action. The Yoga Sūtras reveal that these paths are interdependent—religion without philosophy is sentiment; philosophy without religion is mental speculation. True Karma Yoga unites them: action performed with understanding, discipline informed by wisdom, and both offered in devotion.

Sanskrit Terms

jyāyasī — "better, superior" (comparative of jya, "venerable"): Arjuna is not asking casually—he is seeking the higher path. Like a viveka-seeker debugging two approaches, he wants the optimal spiritual algorithm.

karma — "action, work": From the root kṛ (to do). In the Gītā, karma has layers: fruitive action (binding), prescribed duty (purifying), and karma-yoga (liberating). The practitioner must discriminate between them—this is viveka applied to activity.

buddhi — "intelligence, discernment": The faculty of judgment, the interface between puruṣa and prakṛti. When pure sattva dominates buddhi, it becomes capable of recognizing the soul's true nature. It is not mere intellect—it is awakened intelligence.

janārdana / keśava — Names of Kṛṣṇa: Janārdana ("cherisher of the people") emphasizes divine compassion; Keśava ("slayer of the demon Keśī") emphasizes divine power. Arjuna invokes both aspects—he seeks both understanding and protection.

Cosmic Interpretation

Today's Energy: Kṛṣṇa Pakṣa Dwitīyā — the second day of the waning moon. After Pūrṇimā's fullness, we enter the phase of release, of letting go, of introspection. This is fertile ground for asking: what must I release? What actions serve dissolution versus what actions bind?

Nakṣatra Transition: Chitrā → Svāti. Chitrā, ruled by Tvaṣṭṛ (the cosmic architect), represents structure, brilliant manifestation, the perfected form. Svāti, ruled by Vāyu (wind), represents independence, movement, adaptation. The transition from Chitrā to Svāti mirrors the shift from the Sāṅkhya-knowledge of Chapter 2 (understanding structure) to the Karma-yoga action of Chapter 3 (engaged movement).

Harṣaṇa Yoga: "Bringing joy." This yoga supports clear decisions and beneficial undertakings. The question of action versus renunciation is not a burden to bear—it is an inquiry that, properly pursued, brings joy through clarity.

Chapter Transition: Today marks Day 1 of BG Chapter 3 (Karma Yoga). Just as the moon transitions from full to new, we transition from the analytical knowledge of the soul to the practical application of duty. This is not a step down—it is the necessary completion of what came before.

Practice

Morning: Identify one "ghastly warfare" in your life today—a duty you are avoiding or an action you perform with resentment. Instead of seeking escape, examine: Can this action be performed with awareness? With detachment from results? As service rather than burden?

Through the Day: When you wish to withdraw from activity, ask: Is this true renunciation or avoidance? When you rush into activity, ask: Is this fulfillment or compulsion? Notice the mind's tendency toward either extreme.

Evening: Reflect: Was there a moment today when action and knowledge merged—when you knew what to do and did it without attachment? Or when you knew enough not to act? In Kṛṣṇa Pakṣa, we practice the courage of decisive action tempered by the wisdom of discernment.
