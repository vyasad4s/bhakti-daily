Bhagavad Gita Chapter 9, Verse 17

पिताहमस्य जगतो माता धाता पितामह: । वेद्यं पवित्रम् ॐकार ऋक् साम यजुरेव च ॥ १७ ॥

 

pitāham asya jagato mātā dhātā pitāmahaḥ
vedyam pavitram oṁ-kāra ṛk sāma yajur eva ca

 

I am the father of this universe, the mother, the support and the grandsire. I am the object of knowledge, the purifier and the syllable oṁ. I am also the Ṛg, the Sāma and the Yajur Vedas.

 

This verse reveals the intimate, all-encompassing nature of the Divine — not as a distant ruler, but as the very fabric of relational existence itself. Krishna declares Himself to be the father, mother, supporter, and grandfather of the universe. This is not metaphorical poetry; it is an ontological statement about how the Divine permeates every role we recognize in cosmic and familial relationship. The brilliance here is in the progression: father (source of lineage), mother (source of nourishment and care), dhātā (the supporter/sustainer — the one who holds everything in being), and pitāmaha (grandfather, representing wisdom and ancestral continuity). Krishna claims to be all these simultaneously — not as separate entities playing roles, but as the singular reality that appears as these relationships to conditioned beings. Furthermore, He is vedyam (the object of knowledge) — what we seek to know through science, philosophy, and spirituality. He is pavitram (the purifier) — the agent that cleanses our consciousness. He is oṁ-kāra — the primordial vibration from which all manifestation emerges. And He is the three Vedas themselves — Ṛg (knowledge/hymns), Sāma (melody/devotion), and Yajur (action/ritual) — the complete spectrum of human spiritual endeavor. This verse dismantles the illusion of separation between the seeker and the sought. When we look for our father, we find Krishna. When we seek nourishment, we find Krishna. When we search for support in crisis, we find Krishna. When we pursue knowledge, we find Krishna. When we chant oṁ, we vibrate with Krishna. When we study the Vedas, we study Krishna. The Divine is not "up there" or "out there" — He is the very substance of our seeking and the object of our search.

 

From a practice perspective, this verse offers a profound shift in consciousness: instead of seeking God as an object "out there," we begin to recognize the Divine face in every relationship and every role we play. The father who disciplines us? That is Krishna's protective aspect. The mother who nourishes us? That is Krishna's sustaining grace. The teacher who guides us? That is Krishna's wisdom. The friend who stands by us? That is Krishna's companionship. Even in modern contexts, this mapping continues to work. The employer who provides our livelihood? Krishna as dhātā (supporter). The government that maintains order? Krishna as the sustaining principle of society. The earth that feeds us? Krishna as the nurturing mother. The cosmos that holds our planet in orbit? Krishna as the grand architect. The verse also contains a subtle methodological insight: spiritual realization is not about rejecting relationships but about seeing the Divine within them. We don't need to abandon our roles as children, parents, workers, or citizens to find God — we find God by recognizing His presence in how we fulfill these roles with love, integrity, and awareness. The kitchen becomes a temple when we cook for our family as an offering to the Divine Mother. The workplace becomes a sangha when we conduct business with honesty as service to the Divine Supporter. This is the essence of bhakti-yoga: not escapism, but engagement with the world as a field of divine revelation. Every relationship becomes an opportunity to recognize and honor the Beloved who wears countless faces.

 

PRACTICE FOR TODAY

See the Divine in Relationships: Throughout your day, pause before interacting with others and silently recognize: "This being is a manifestation of Krishna." Whether it's the barista making your coffee, the colleague asking for help, or your life partner sharing breakfast — see Krishna in their eyes, their voice, their presence. Let this awareness transform ordinary interaction into sacred encounter.

Chant with Feeling: Today, when you chant oṁ (whether in meditation, kirtan, or silently), feel it not just as a sound but as the vibration of Krishna as oṁ-kāra — the primordial pulse from which all manifestation arises and into which it returns. Let the chant remind you that the sound emerging from your throat is the same sound that vibrates in every atom.

Serve as Krishna: In your actions today, consciously embody one of Krishna's roles from this verse. Be the supporter (dhātā) for someone who needs help. Be the source of knowledge (vedyam) by sharing wisdom kindly. Be the purifier (pavitra) by uplifting a conversation that has turned negative. By serving in these ways, you become a transparent medium for the Divine to work through you.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

pitāham (compound) — "I am the father"; the divine source of lineage, protection, and initiation

mātā (noun) — mother; the divine nourisher, sustainer, and source of unconditional care

dhātā (noun) — supporter/sustainer; the divine maintainer who holds all beings in existence

pitāmahaḥ (noun) — grandfather; the divine source of wisdom, tradition, and ancestral continuity

vedyam (noun) — object of knowledge; that which is to be known, understood, and realized

pavitram (noun) — purifier; that which cleanses, purifies, and elevates consciousness

oṁ-kāra (noun) — the syllable oṁ; the primordial vibration, the sonic embodiment of the Absolute

ṛk (noun) — Ṛg Veda; the Veda of hymns, knowledge, and praise

sāma (noun) — Sāma Veda; the Veda of melody, devotion, and musical expression

yajuḥ (noun) — Yajur Veda; the Veda of rituals, action, and ceremonial practice

Sāṅkhya Lens

[The Divine as the Ground of Relations]: In Sāṅkhya philosophy, puruṣa (consciousness) and prakṛti (matter) are eternally distinct principles. The manifest universe arises from their interplay, yet puruṣa remains inactive, witnessing while prakṛti evolves through its modifications (vikārās). BG 9.17 presents a theistic interpretation that complements this framework: Krishna, as the Supreme Puruṣa (purusha-viśeṣa per YS I.24), is not merely a passive witness but the intelligent source from which the diversified functions of prakṛti emerge. When He says "I am the father, mother, supporter, grandsire," He is describing how the singular puruṣa-consciousness appears to conditioned jīvas as the multifaceted ground of all relational experience.

[Yoga Sūtra Bridge — YS I.24 & I.27]: Patañjali defines Īśvara as puruṣa-viśeṣa — a special puruṣa untouched by kleśa (afflictions), karma (action), vipāka (fructification), and āśaya (subconscious residues) (YS I.24). This is the Yoga Sūtra's precise counterpart to Krishna's declaration in BG 9.17. The Lord is not just another puruṣa caught in the machinery of prakṛti; He is the transcendental consciousness that gives meaning and direction to prakṛti's evolution. Furthermore, YS I.27 identifies oṁ (praṇava) as the sound symbol of Īśvara — making Krishna's statement "oṁ-kāra" a direct declaration of His identity as the object of meditation and the vibrational ground of all existence. The morning's Brahma Muhūrta practice of oṁ-japa is thus a direct communion with the oṁ-kāra whom Krishna proclaims Himself to be.

[The Three Vedas as Gunas Expression]: Krishna's identification as ṛk, sāma, and yajuḥ reveals how the Divine operates through the three gunas that modulate prakṛti's expression. The Ṛg Veda (ṛk), emphasizing knowledge and hymns, aligns with sattva-guṇa (clarity, wisdom, harmony). The Sāma Veda (sāma), focused on melody and devotion, expresses sattva mixed with rajas (the dynamic energy of loving engagement). The Yajur Veda (yajuḥ), dealing with ritual and action, embodies the necessary interplay of all three gunas — sattva for proper intention, rajas for energetic execution, and tamas for the material substrate of ritual objects. In this way, the Vedas are not merely texts but vibrational patterns through which the Divine structures and enlightens phenomenal existence.

[Bhedābheda in Relationship]: There is a beautiful bhedābheda (simultaneous difference and non-difference) at work in this verse. When Krishna says "I am the father," He is not claiming to be a limited, conditional father figure like the jīvas who play that role. Rather, He is the infinite Fatherhood itself — the archetypal source of all fatherhood — yet appearing as the particular father in each individual's experience. The same applies to mother, supporter, grandfather, and so on. This is not modalism (where God merely plays different roles) but rather the recognition that the infinite Divine contains all finite expressions within Himself while remaining eternally distinct from them. The jīva experiences relationship; the Divine is the Ground of Relationship.
