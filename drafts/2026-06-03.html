BG 7.3 — Hardly One Knows Me in Truth

मनुष्याणां सहस्रेषु कश्चिद्यतति सिद्धये ।

यततामपि सिद्धानां कश्चिन्मां वेत्ति तत्त्वत: ॥ ३ ॥

manuṣyāṇāṁ sahasreṣu kaścid yatati siddhaye
yatatām api siddhānāṁ kaścin māṁ vetti tattvataḥ

Out of many thousands among men, one may endeavor for perfection, and of those who have achieved perfection, hardly one knows Me in truth.

Commentary

Yesterday Kṛṣṇa opened Chapter 7 with a promise: attach your mind to Me, take shelter in Me, and you will know Me completely, without doubt. Today He reveals the statistics. Manuṣyāṇāṁ sahasreṣu — out of many thousands of human beings. Kaścid yatati siddhaye — one endeavors for perfection. Not one in ten. Not one in a hundred. One in thousands. And then the filter tightens: yatatām api siddhānāṁ — of those who have already achieved that perfection — kaścin māṁ vetti tattvataḥ — hardly one knows Me in truth. Two funnels stacked. The first separates the indifferent from the seekers. The second separates the accomplished from the realized. At the narrow end stands the rarest of beings: one who knows the Supreme as He is.

This is not elitism. Kṛṣṇa is not gatekeeping — He is describing an observable fact about consciousness. The first filter is yatati, endeavor. Most human beings never begin. The animal propensities — eating, sleeping, mating, defending — consume the entire bandwidth of attention. There is no surplus energy directed toward the question "what am I?" The second filter is tattvataḥ, in truth. Many achieve some form of perfection — Brahman realization, Paramātmā awareness, siddhis, elevated consciousness. But these are intermediate stations. To know Kṛṣṇa as the Supreme Person — not as an abstraction, not as a localized presence, but as the complete, personal, all-attractive source of all that exists — requires a further step that even most accomplished yogīs do not take. Not because it is denied to them, but because the architecture of their seeking keeps them at the level of the impersonal. The impersonal is easier. The personal demands surrender, not just mastery.

The key word is tattvataḥ — in truth, in fact, as things actually are. This is not theoretical knowledge. Prabhupāda makes this devastatingly clear: one can be a great scholar, a powerful yogī, an accomplished philosopher, and still not know Kṛṣṇa. The sages and demigods themselves are sometimes confused about Him. Knowledge of the Supreme is not earned by accumulation — it is received by surrender. The funnel narrows not because the path becomes harder, but because the demand shifts from effort to grace. You can effort your way to Brahman. You can meditate your way to Paramātmā. But to know Kṛṣṇa tattvataḥ — that requires the one thing no technique can produce: the voluntary offering of the self to the Self.

Yoga Sūtra Bridge: YS II.28

योगाङ्गानुष्ठानादशुद्धिक्षये ज्ञानदीप्तिराविवेकख्यातेः

yogāṅgānuṣṭhānād aśuddhi-kṣaye jñāna-dīptir-āviveka-khyāteḥ

Upon the destruction of impurities as a result of the practice of yoga, the lamp of knowledge arises. This culminates in discriminative discernment.

Patañjali's II.28 provides the mechanics behind Kṛṣṇa's statistics. Why is the knower of the Supreme so rare? Because the process has stages, and each stage eliminates more candidates than it passes. Yogāṅgānuṣṭhānāt — from the practice of the limbs of yoga. Aśuddhi-kṣaye — upon the destruction of impurities. Jñāna-dīptiḥ — the lamp of knowledge arises. Viveka-khyāteḥ — culminating in discriminative discernment. Four stages: practice, purification, illumination, discrimination. Each is a filter, and each filter narrows the field.

The correspondence to BG 7.3 is precise. Yatati siddhaye — one endeavors for perfection — maps to the first two stages: practice and purification. Siddhānām — those who have achieved perfection — maps to illumination. Tattvataḥ vetti — knows in truth — maps to viveka-khyāti, discriminative discernment. Patañjali confirms what Kṛṣṇa states: the lamp of knowledge does not light while impurities remain. The rarity is not a mystery — it is the math of a process that demands everything at each stage. And yet Patañjali adds a critical detail: the lamp arises not from the impurities alone being destroyed, but as a result of the practice. The effort matters. The purification matters. The funnel is real, but it is traversable — for the one who keeps walking.

Cosmic Interpretation: Sankashti's Funnel, Pūrvāṣāḍhā's Waters → Uttara Aṣāḍhā's Confirmation

Today carries a rare triple signature: tithi transition, nakṣatra transition, and a major observance. Kṛṣṇa Tritiyā yields to Chaturthi at 11:51 AM — and this is not ordinary Chaturthi but Sankashti, the monthly observance dedicated to Gaṇeśa, remover of obstacles. The cosmic question Sankashti poses is exactly the question BG 7.3 answers: what stands between the seeker and the goal? Kṛṣṇa's response is surgical — the obstacle is not external. It is the rarity of aspiration itself, and the further rarity of surrender beyond accomplishment.

The nakṣatra transition reinforces this. Pūrvāṣāḍhā — "the early victory," governed by Āpaḥ, the water deities — yields to Uttara Aṣāḍhā — "the later victory," governed by the Viśvadeva, the universal gods. The movement is from initial breakthrough to confirmed attainment. Pūrvāṣāḍhā is yatati siddhaye — the effort that tastes the first fruits. Uttara Aṣāḍhā is tattvataḥ vetti — the rarer, deeper knowing that does not merely approach the truth but stands within it. The waters of Pūrvāṣāḍhā cleanse and prepare. The universal gods of Uttara Aṣāḍhā confirm and universalize. The one who knows Kṛṣṇa in truth attains not a sectarian vision but a universal one — the Viśvadeva are precisely the principle of cosmic universality.

Budhavāsara — Wednesday, Mercury's day — governs intellect, communication, the processing of information. But BG 7.3 warns: information is not knowledge, and knowledge is not realization. Mercury can catalog the eight separated energies of prakṛti (BG 7.4). It can map the eightfold nature of material existence. But it cannot produce tattvataḥ — knowledge in truth. That requires a different faculty: not intellect parsing its objects, but consciousness recognizing its Source. Śukla yoga (bright, luminous) holds through the day — the path is illuminated. Then Brahma yoga (sacred knowledge) activates at 11:32 PM. The sequence is exact: first the light, then the sacred knowledge it reveals. The moon's entry into Makara (Capricorn) at 10:11 PM confirms the terrain — the sign of long discipline, the slow climb, the summit that is reached not by speed but by persistence. The one who endures the funnel is the one who reaches the narrow end.

Sanskrit Terms

sahasreṣu — Out of many thousands. Kṛṣṇa's statistical frame — not hyperbole but precise observation. The path narrows at each stage. Thousands enter, one arrives. The funnel is not cruelty; it is the architecture of reality.

siddhaye — For perfection, for accomplishment. From siddhi — completion, fulfillment, the attainment of a goal. Not mere success in the worldly sense — the perfection of consciousness itself. The difference between compiling without errors and actually executing the program.

siddhānām — Of those who have achieved perfection. The same root as siddhaye, but now in the past participle — those who have already completed the journey. Even among these, Kṛṣṇa says, hardly one knows Him. Perfection is necessary but not sufficient.

tattvataḥ — In truth, in fact, as reality actually is. From tattva — that-ness, the essential nature of a thing. Not partial knowledge, not theoretical knowledge, not knowledge-by-description. Knowledge-by-acquaintance. The difference between reading the documentation and directly experiencing the source code running.

aśuddhi — Impurities, contamination. The obstacles that obscure the lamp of knowledge — the residues of avidyā, asmitā, rāga, dveṣa, and abhiniveśa. The five kleśas are the background code that prevents the system from booting cleanly.

viveka-khyāti — Discriminative discernment, the highest form of knowledge in classical yoga. The direct perception of the distinction between puruṣa and prakṛti. When this fires, the funnel collapses to its final stage.

Practice for Kṛṣṇa Chaturthi / Sankashti

Morning: Before 11:51 AM, Kṛṣṇa Tritiyā still holds — yesterday's energy of forward motion carries into the morning. During Brahma Muhūrta (3:55–4:43 AM), chant BG 7.3 slowly. Sit with the two numbers: sahasreṣu (thousands) and kaścit (one). Where are you on this funnel? Have you begun to endeavor? Have you achieved some measure of siddhi? What impurities (aśuddhi) still remain between you and tattvataḥ? The Amrit Kaal (10:06–11:53 AM) is a potent window for contemplation — the nectar of time when effort bears fruit. Use it.

Evening: After 11:51 AM, Sankashti Chaturthi activates — Gaṇeśa's day of obstacle removal. The obstacle BG 7.3 identifies is not a wall but a funnel: the path narrows not because it is blocked, but because it demands more at each stage. After 3:29 PM, Uttara Aṣāḍhā (Viśvadeva — the universal gods) activates, suggesting that the one who does know Kṛṣṇa tattvataḥ attains something universal — not sectarian, not limited, but the knowledge that contains all knowledge. The moon enters Makara (Capricorn) at 10:11 PM — the sign of discipline, structure, the long climb. Offer tonight's japa to the one who makes the funnel possible: Gaṇeśa removes the obstacles; Kṛṣṇa reveals what lies at the narrow end.
