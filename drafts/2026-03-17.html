🏹 VERSE CONTENT

Source: Bhagavad-gītā 2.68

Date: March 17, 2026

Sanskrit:

तस्माद्यस्य महाबाहो निगृहीतानि सर्वशः।

इन्द्रियाणीन्द्रियार्थेभ्यस्तस्य प्रज्ञा प्रतिष्ठिता॥

Transliteration:

tasmād yasya mahā-bāho nigṛhītāni sarvaśaḥ
indriyāṇīndriyārthebhyas tasya prajñā pratiṣṭhitā

Translation:

Therefore, O mighty-armed one, one whose senses are restrained from their objects is certainly of steady intelligence.

Commentary:

The Lord concludes this profound section on sense control with a conclusion that couldn't be more direct: the intellect is only stable when the senses are withdrawn from their objects. This is not a recommendation—it's a statement of fact about the mechanics of consciousness. The previous verse (2.67) warned how a single sense running loose can capsize the whole vessel of intelligence, like wind overturning a boat. Here the prescription follows the diagnosis.

The term nigṛhītāni—"restrained, curbed down"—implies active, deliberate control, not passive disinterest. This is the tortoise withdrawing its limbs not out of fear but out of mastery. The senses are not enemies to be destroyed but instruments to be directed. Pratiṣṭhitā—"established, fixed, firmly established"—describes intelligence that has achieved operational stability, like a system running without crashes or memory leaks. This is the prerequisite for any real spiritual accomplishment.

Yoga Sutra Connection:

🔥 YS II.54 — svaviṣayāsaṃprayoge cittasya svarūpānukāra ivendriyāṇāṃ pratyāhāraḥ

Here Patañjali defines pratyāhāra as the withdrawal of senses from their objects, mimicking the nature of citta. The Gita's prescription aligns perfectly: restraint (nigṛhītāni) creates the condition for steady intelligence (prajñā pratiṣṭhitā). Bhakti provides the higher taste that makes this withdrawal natural rather than forced.

Sanskrit Terms:

  • nigṛhītāni (past passive participle, neut. pl.) — restrained, curbed down; from ni- (down) + grah (to seize); the senses actively withdrawn from objects like a skilled administrator recalling agents from the field
  • indriyārthebhyaḥ (noun, abl. pl.) — from sense objects; indriya (sense) + artha (object/meaning); the domain where senses seek engagement
  • prajñā (noun, fem.) — wisdom, intelligence, discriminative knowledge; the awakened buddhi that distinguishes puruṣa from prakṛti
  • pratiṣṭhitā (past passive participle, fem.) — established, fixed, firmly situated; from prati- (against) + sthā (to stand); intelligence grounded in its true nature
