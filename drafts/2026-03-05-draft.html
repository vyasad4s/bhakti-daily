🏹 VERSE CONTENT

Source: BG 2.22

Date: March 5, 2026

Sanskrit:

वासांसि जीर्णानि यथा विहाय

नवानि गृह्णाति नरोऽपराणि ।

तथा शरीराणि विहाय जीर्णा-

न्यन्यानि संयाति नवानि देही ॥ २२ ॥

Transliteration:

vāsāṁsi jīrṇāni yathā vihāya navāni gṛhṇāti naro 'parāṇi tathā śarīrāṇi vihāya jīrṇāny anyāni saṁyāti navāni dehī

Translation:

As a person puts on new garments, giving up old ones, the soul similarly accepts new material bodies, giving up the old and useless ones.

Commentary:

This verse delivers the kernel truth at the heart of all spiritual practice: you are not the body you inhabit. The dehī—the embodied one—is an eternal conscious entity that simply occupies successive vehicles of flesh. Like discarding worn garments for fresh ones, the soul changes bodies, leaving behind what has become unusable while remaining itself unchanged.

But here's the issue: most of us operate as if we're the clothes, not the wearer. We identify so completely with our current vessel—its sensations, its preferences, its accumulated history—that we assume its dissolution means our dissolution. This is the root error (mithyā-jñāna) that binds us. The Bhagavad-gītā isn't offering comfort here; it's offering surgical precision. Recognize the distinction or remain trapped. The choice is yours, but the clock is running.

In Sāṅkhya terms, this verse points directly to the dualism between puruṣa (conscious spirit) and prakṛti (material nature including bodies). The gunas—sattva, rajas, tamas—churn out new vehicles according to accumulated saṅskāras, yet the witness-consciousness merely observes the exchange. Hasta nakṣatra governs today: "the hand" that crafts and transforms. What will you craft with this knowledge? Will you remain identified with the garb, or will you cultivate the discernment to recognize the wearer?

Yoga Sutra Connection:

🔥 YS I.3-4 — tadā draṣṭuḥ svarūpe 'vasthānam | vṛtti-sārūpyam itaratra

"When that is accomplished, the seer abides in its own true nature. Otherwise, at other times, [the seer] is absorbed in the changing states [of the mind]."

Here is the thread connecting Bhakti and Raja Yoga paths. BG 2.22 describes the mechanics: soul changing bodies like garments. YS I.3-4 describes the consequence of not understanding this mechanics: the seer (puruṣa) becomes absorbed (sārūpyam) in the vṛttis—the changing states—rather than abiding in its own nature. When we fail to discriminate between body and soul, we don't merely observe embodiment; we become the embodiment. We experience the body's aging, its pain, its death, as if they were happening to us fundamentally. But the dehī sees the śarīra as a worn garment, not as self. This is viveka in action: the discernment that frees.

Sanskrit Terms:

  • vāsāṁsi (noun, pl.) — garments, clothes; in the hacker metaphor: temporary user accounts that the root administrator creates and discards
  • jīrṇāni (adj.) — worn out, old, decayed; describes the material body after its utility is exhausted
  • vihāya (gerund) — having abandoned, given up; implies voluntary relinquishment, not forced deprivation
  • dehī (noun) — the embodied one, the soul; literally "possessing a body"; distinct from deha (the body itself)
  • saṁyāti (verb) — verily accepts, enters into; denotes the soul's active assumption of new embodiment according to karma
