Bg. 3.5

na hi kaścit kṣaṇam api
jātu tiṣṭhaty akarma-kṛt
kāryate hy avaśaḥ karma
sarvaḥ prakṛti-jair guṇaiḥ

na — nor; hi — certainly; kaścit — anyone; kṣaṇam — a moment; api — also; jātu — at any time; tiṣṭhati — remains; akarma-kṛt — without doing something; kāryate — is forced to do; hi — certainly; avaśaḥ — helplessly; karma — work; sarvaḥ — all; prakṛti-jaiḥ — born of the modes of material nature; guṇaiḥ — by the qualities

Everyone is forced to act helplessly according to the qualities he has acquired from the modes of material nature; therefore no one can refrain from doing something, not even for a moment.

The Inescapable Truth

Arjuna wants to quit. The battlefield is too much. The consequences too horrifying. He thinks if he just sits down and stops, the problem will go away. But Krishna cuts to the root — Mūla-style — with devastating clarity: you cannot stop. The soul is constitutionally active. Even to maintain this body requires work. You are not choosing between action and inaction. You are choosing between conscious action and compelled action.

This is Sāṅkhya as a debugging tool. Your current "code" — the mental and physical habits accumulated across lifetimes — compiles and executes automatically. The guṇas (sattva, rajas, tamas) are the runtime environment, the background processes you rarely examine but which determine every output. Rajas drives you to chase, to build, to accumulate. Tamas drags you to sleep, to resist, to decay. Sattva rare clarity and peace. But none of them are you. They are the matrix in which you operate.

The word avaśaḥ — helplessly — is the key. Most think they are free. They choose careers, partners, meals. But check the logs: how many choices were compulsive? Driven by hunger, fear, habit, inherited patterns you never consented to? The gita doesn't flatter you with illusions of autonomy. It says: acknowledge the constraint first. Only then can you work within it intelligently.

Mūla nakṣatra confronts us with roots — the deep structures underlying surface phenomena. Today asks: what is really running your show? Not the story you tell, but the actual processes? Can you sit for sixty seconds without shifting, planning, checking, reacting? The answer reveals the depth of compulsion Krishna points to. But this is not despair. It is the necessary diagnosis before the cure.

Yoga Sūtra Connection: I.4

vṛtti-sārūpyam itaratra
Otherwise, at other times, [the seer] is absorbed in the changing states [of the mind].

Patañjali names our condition precisely. When we do not abide as pure consciousness — when we are not in samādhi — we are sārūpyam, absorbed in, literally "one-formed with," the fluctuations of mind. BG 3.5 describes the mechanics: the soul is bound to act through the guṇas, helplessly identified with prakṛti's processes. YS I.4 describes the experience: we become what we perceive. The anger arises, and suddenly "I am angry." The thought appears, and "I think." The pattern runs, and "I choose."

The bridge is practice itself. Karma-yoga is the recognition that since action is unavoidable, we might as well make it aware. Every moment of intentional action — offering the work rather than claiming the result — is a loosening of vṛtti-sārūpyam. We do not abdicate the body. We reclaim it as instrument rather than identity.

Practice Today

Set a timer for 10 minutes. Sit still. Watch the compulsion to move, adjust, check your phone, plan your day. Do not resist. Just observe. Each impulse is prakṛti-jair guṇaiḥ — the modes working through you. After 10 minutes, choose one action you would ordinarily do unconsciously (checking email, the next bite of food, a reactive word) and perform it deliberately. Offer it before acting.

The root cannot be uprooted by the root. Only consciousness can see the pattern. Only practice can change it.

na: nor — negation particle
kaścit: anyone — indefinite pronoun emphasizing universality
kṣaṇam: a moment — atomic unit of time, "blink"
akarma-kṛt: non-doer of action — the impossible state Arjuna imagined
avaśaḥ: helpless — from a (not) + vaśa (control/sway), "without control"
prakṛti-jaiḥ: born of prakṛti — referring to the guṇas as evolutes of material nature
guṇaiḥ: by the qualities — the three-fold patterning of material nature (sattva, rajas, tamas)
