Bhagavad Gita Chapter 9, Verse 4

मया ततमिदं सर्वं जगदव्यक्तमूर्तिना । मत्स्थानि सर्वभूतानि न चाहं तेष्ववस्थित: ॥ ४ ॥
mayā tatam idaṁ sarvaṁ jagad avyakta-mūrtinā mat-sthāni sarva-bhūtāni na cāhaṁ teṣv avasthitaḥ
By Me, in My unmanifested form, this entire universe is pervaded. All beings are in Me, but I am not in them.
This verse reveals the paradox of the Supreme Lord’s simultaneous immanence and transcendence. Krishna declares that He pervades the entire universe through His unmanifested form (avyakta-mūrti)—His all‑pervading energy—yet remains distinct as the Supreme Personality. All living entities exist within His energy, but He is not confined within them. This avoids both impersonalism (which would deny His personal existence) and crude anthropomorphism (which would limit Him to the material realm). Śrīla Prabhupāda explains that just as sunshine pervades the universe while the sun globe remains distinct, the Lord’s energy fills creation while He retains His personal identity. The material world is a manifestation of His inferior (material) energy, whereas the spiritual world is His superior (spiritual) energy. Both rest upon Him, yet He is aloof as the enjoyer and controller. Realization of this truth comes through pure devotional service, when the senses are engaged in loving communion with Krishna; only then is He revealed as the personal source of everything. Practically, this understanding prevents two extremes: thinking that God is merely an impersonal force (leading to indifference) or imagining that He is limited to a particular form or location (leading to sectarianism). By fixing the mind on the personal all‑pervasive Lord, one avoids material entanglement and progresses toward pure bhakti.
