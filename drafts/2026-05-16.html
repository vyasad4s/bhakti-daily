Bhagavad Gita Chapter 5, Verse 23

शक्नोतीह वै रागद्वेषौ विगतः सुखिनु भूनुतः ।
तस्माद्योBुस्तनतः शान्तिं लभते स सुखिनः स्मृतः ॥ २३ ॥
śaknoti haivaṃ yasmād ūrjato dveṣo vittasya kāma-krodhodbhavaḥ
sa yuktaḥ sa sukhaḥ naraḥ
One who is able to withstand the impulse of lust and anger before leaving the body is a yogi and is happy.

This verse identifies the primary "system noise" that prevents the soul from accessing its inherent peace: kāma (lust/desire) and krodha (anger). In the architecture of the mind, these are not merely emotions but high-amplitude spikes of Rajasic and Tamasic energy. Desire is the projection of a perceived lack; anger is the reaction to the frustration of that desire. Together, they form a feedback loop that traps the consciousness in a state of constant agitation, which we mistakenly call "living."

The key word here is śaknoti—the capacity or ability to withstand. This is not a passive avoidance but an active, disciplined resistance. It is the spiritual equivalent of a high-pass filter, allowing the essential frequency of the soul to pass through while blocking the low-frequency noise of impulse. This capacity is developed through tapas (austerity), which burns away the dross of old saṃskāras (legacy code) that trigger these impulses automatically.

When a practitioner is "yukta" (united/connected), they no longer react blindly to the impulse. They observe the spike of anger or the pull of desire as a purely material event—a chemical surge in the brain, a contraction in the chest—without identifying with it. By maintaining this gap between the impulse and the reaction, the yogi remains sukhaḥ (happy). This happiness is not the fleeting pleasure of a satisfied desire, but the stable joy of a mind that is no longer a slave to its own biology.

On the occasion of Shani Jayanti, the birthday of Lord Saturn, this teaching takes on added gravity. Shani is the great taskmaster, the cosmic auditor who enforces the law of karma with cold, uncompromising precision. Shani does not provide comfort; he provides the discipline necessary for liberation. To "withstand the impulse" is to align oneself with the energy of Shani—choosing the long-term stability of the soul over the short-term gratification of the ego.

Cosmic Context: Amavasya & Shani Jayanti

Tithi: Krishna Paksha Amavasya (New Moon) — a period of darkness and internal void.

Nakṣatra: Bharani (until 5:30 PM) → Krittika. Bharani is ruled by Yama (the god of death/transformation) and carries the energy of bearing fruit through struggle. Krittika is ruled by Agni (fire), associated with purification.

Yoga: Saubhagya (until 10:25 AM) → Sobhana (Harmony).

Weekday: Shaniwara (Saturday) — ruled by Lord Saturn.

Today is a potent convergence of energies. Amavasya provides the "blank slate" of the new moon, a time to reset the internal system and let go of old identifications. Shani Jayanti emphasizes the necessity of discipline and the endurance of hardship for the sake of growth.

The transition from Bharani to Krittika represents a shift from the struggle of transformation (Yama) to the fire of purification (Agni). This mirrors the process described in BG 5.23: the struggle to withstand the impulse (Bharani) leads to the burning away of the desire (Krittika). The Sobhana yoga prevailing throughout the day suggests that if we can maintain this discipline, the result is a state of inner harmony and auspiciousness.

Yoga Sutra Connection

YS 2.1: tapaḥ svādhyāya-īśvara-praṇidhānāni kriyā-yogaḥ

"Austerity, self-study, and surrender to the Divine constitute Kriya Yoga."

The "ability to withstand" mentioned in BG 5.23 is the practical application of tapas (austerity). Tapas is the heat generated by discipline; it is the process of deliberately placing oneself in a situation of restraint to strengthen the will. In the context of Kriya Yoga, tapas is the first tool used to dismantle the kleshas (afflictions) that fuel lust and anger.

While the Bhagavad Gītā focuses on the result (happiness and union), the Yoga Sūtras provide the technical manual. By combining tapas with svādhyāya (studying the nature of the self) and īśvara-praṇidhāna (surrendering the result to the Divine), the practitioner transforms the painful struggle of resistance into a graceful flow of detachment.

Today's Practice

On this Shaniwara and Amavasya, perform an "Impulse Audit." Throughout the day, whenever you feel a strong urge (to check your phone, to snap at someone, to eat something out of boredom, or to seek validation), do not immediately act on it.

The Protocol: 1. Detect: Notice the physical sensation of the impulse (e.g., tightness in the chest, restlessness in the hands).
2. Label: Mentally label it: "This is a Rajasic spike" or "This is a Tamasic pull."
3. Gap: Create a 10-second gap. Breathe and observe the impulse as if it were a notification on a screen—you see it, but you don't have to click it.
4. Offer: Consciously offer the energy of that impulse to Kṛṣṇa, asking for the strength of Shani to remain stable.

By doing this, you are training the buddhi to act as a filter rather than a conduit. You are moving from being the "doer" who is pushed by the wind of impulse to being the "witness" who remains still in the center of the storm.
