पवन: पवतामस्मि राम: शस्त्रभृतामहम् । झषाणां मकरश्चास्मि स्रोतसामस्मि जाह्नवी ॥ ३१ ॥

 

pavanaḥ pavatām asmi

Of purifiers I am the wind, of the wielders of weapons I am Rāma, of fishes I am the shark, and of flowing rivers I am the Ganges.

The sāṅkhya precision here is exquisite. Kṛṣṇa does not say "I am the wind" in an identificatory way; He says "of purifiers I am the wind" — establishing a hierarchy of dependence. Wind (as prāna, as movement, as the purifying force) is a manifestation (vibhūti), an expression of His opulence, not an independent source. This is the crucial sāṅkhya distinction: puruṣa (consciousness) versus prakṛti (manifestation). The winds are prakṛti in motion; Kṛṣṇa is the puruṣa whose presence makes them meaningful. Just as wind has no existence apart from the consciousness that moves through it, so all purifying forces reflect the light of consciousness. This is directly reflected in the Yoga Sūtras. YS II.40-41 (śaucāt svāṅga-saṃbhogaḥ paridūṣaṇa-parihāraḥ...) states that from purity (śauca) comes disgust for contamination and mastery over the senses — exactly what the purifying wind accomplishes. When we recognize that the wind is not self-purifying but purifies by borrowed power, we naturally turn toward the Source. YS II.41 continues: sattva-śuddhiḥ saumanasya-ekāgrya-indriya-jaya-ātma-darśana-yogyatvāni ca — "[From purity] also comes purity of sattva, cheerfulness, one-pointedness, sense-mastery, fitness for Self-vision." The wind's purifying action prepares the mind for higher states by removing the veils of rajas and tamas. For the bhakti-yogī, this is not abstract meteorology — it is lived dṛṣṭi (vision). Every time we feel a breeze, notice air movement, or experience mental clarity, we can trace it back: "This purification is not its own; it is an expression of the Divine." The practice becomes constant remembrance: wherever there is purification, there is Kṛṣṇa. This transforms ordinary perception into sacred seeing — the world becomes a field of opulences, each gust of wind a whisper of the Beloved wielding His purifying sword.

Sāṅkhya Lens

From the sāṅkhya perspective, BG 10.31 is a masterclass in vibhūti recognition — seeing the divine as the source behind all manifest energies. The verse does not say "I am the wind" in an absolutist way; it says "of purifiers I am the wind" — establishing a clear dependence. Wind (as prāna, as movement, as the purifying force) is prakṛti in motion; Kṛṣṇa is the puruṣa whose consciousness illuminates it. Just as wind has no existence apart from the consciousness that moves through it, so all purifying forces reflect the light of consciousness.

Similarly, when Kṛṣṇa says rāmaḥ śastra-bhṛtām aham ("of the wielders of weapons I am Rāma"), He establishes that righteous action and discriminative force are expressions of His opulence. The warrior's sword, like the purifying wind, is prakṛti in motion; Kṛṣṇa is the puruṣa whose presence makes it meaningful. The sāṅkhya yogi sees that even the most forceful action is but a manifestation of the Divine — the sword is not self-empowered but empowered by the consciousness that wields it.

This is directly reflected in the Yoga Sūtras. YS II.40-41 describes how purity (śauca) leads to mastery over the senses and fitness for Self-vision — exactly what the purifying wind and discriminative sword accomplish together. The wind clears the external obstacles (like prāna cleansing the body); the sword cuts the internal obstacles (like discernment cutting through mental modifications). Both are vibhūtīs — divine opulences pointing back to their Source.

Yoga Sūtra Connection

BG 10.31's declaration "pavanaḥ pavatām asmi" ("Of purifiers I am the wind") connects directly to YS II.40-41 on śauca (purity) and its fruits.

YS II.40: śaucāt svāṅga-saṃbhogaḥ paridūṣaṇa-parihāraḥ — "From purity, disgust for one's own body and avoidance of contact with others." The purifying wind develops paridūṣaṇa (disgust/revulsion) for impurities — just as wind clears away fog and pollution, purity produces natural aversion to contamination.

YS II.41: sattva-śuddhiḥ saumanasya-ekāgrya-indriya-jaya-ātma-darśana-yogyatvāni ca — "[From purity] also comes purity of sattva, cheerfulness, one-pointedness, sense-mastery, fitness for Self-vision." The wind's purifying action produces sattva-śuddhi (sattva-purity), ekāgrya (one-pointed focus), and indriya-jaya (mastery over senses) — all qualities needed to wield the sword of discernment effectively.

The warrior aspect "rāmaḥ śastra-bhṛtām aham" ("of the wielders of weapons I am Rāma") connects to YS II.41's indriya-jaya — sense-mastery. Just as Rāma wields his bow with perfect control, the yogi who has mastered the senses through purity can wield discriminative wisdom with precision. Both wind (external purification) and sword (internal discernment) are expressions of the same divine opulence: the power to discriminate between purity and impurity, reality and illusion.

For the bhakti-yogī, this is practiced as: when feeling mental fog or confusion, invoke the purifying wind (pavanaḥ pavatām asmi) to clarity; when needing to make a clear decision or cut through illusion, invoke the warrior's discernment (rāmaḥ śastra-bhṛtām aham) to act with righteous precision. Both are reminders: the wind does not purify by its own power; the sword does not discriminate by its own strength. Their power comes from Kṛṣṇa, the puruṣa behind all purifying winds and discriminative swords.
