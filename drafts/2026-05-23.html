श्रीभगवानुवाच

असंशयं महाबाहो मनो दुर्निग्रहं चलम् ।

अभ्यासेन तु कौन्तेय वैराग्येण च गृह्यते ॥ ३५ ॥

असंयतात्मना योगो दुष्प्राप इति मे मति: ।

वश्यात्मना तु यतता शक्योऽवाप्‍तुमुपायत: ॥ ३६ ॥

 

śrī-bhagavān uvāca
asaṁśayaṁ mahā-bāho
mano durnigrahaṁ calam
abhyāsena tu kaunteya
vairāgyeṇa ca gṛhyate

asaṁyatātmanā yogo
duṣprāpa iti me matiḥ
vaśyātmanā tu yatatā
śakyo 'vāptum upāyataḥ

 

Lord Śrī Kṛṣṇa said: O mighty-armed son of Kuntī, it is undoubtedly very difficult to curb the restless mind, but it is possible by suitable practice and by detachment.

For one whose mind is unbridled, self-realization is difficult work. But he whose mind is controlled and who strives by appropriate means is assured of success. That is My opinion.

 

Arjuna has just confessed his defeat. Six verses ago, he asked Kṛṣṇa to describe the yogī who is united in meditation (6.10), and Kṛṣṇa laid out the entire architecture — seat, posture, focus, mind directed toward the Supreme. Then Arjuna's nerve broke: "The mind is restless, turbulent, obstinate, very strong — controlling it seems more difficult than controlling the wind" (6.34). Kṛṣṇa doesn't disagree. He doesn't say "it's not that bad" or "you're overthinking it." He says: asaṁśayam — without a doubt. The mind is durnigraham — difficult to restrain. It is calam — flickering. This is the honest diagnosis. But the diagnosis is not the prognosis. The next word transforms everything: tu — but. The mind is uncontrollable but — abhyāsena vairāgyeṇa ca gṛhyate — by practice and detachment, it can be seized. Gṛhyate is not "it improves" or "it gets a little better." It means "it is grasped, caught, subjugated." The untameable is tamed — not through force, but through the twin methodology that every yoga tradition agrees upon.

 

Verse 36 then draws the consequence: the unbridled mind makes yoga impossible (duṣprāpa), but the controlled mind that strives by appropriate means (upāyataḥ) can achieve it. Notice Kṛṣṇa doesn't say the controlled mind achieves it automatically. He says the one who strives — yatatā — with a controlled mind, using appropriate means, is assured of success. Effort remains. The mind is harnessed, not replaced. The yogī still works — but now the work has traction. Previously, every exertion slipped on the ice of a restless mind. Now, with abhyāsa and vairāgya, the same effort finds ground. Prabhupāda's purport drives this home: trying to practice yoga while the mind chases material enjoyment is like trying to ignite a fire while pouring water on it. The method must match the diagnosis. The mind is flickering? Then the cure is what stabilizes flickering — sustained, devoted practice that builds momentum, and detachment that removes the fuel the flickering feeds on.

 

🔥 YOGA SŪTRA CONNECTION: YS I.12 — abhyāsā-vairāgyābhyāṁ tan-nirodhaḥ
"The vṛtti states of the mind are stilled by practice and dispassion." Patañjali opens his entire practical method with the same two-word formula Kṛṣṇa gives Arjuna. This is not coincidence — it is structural identity. Both systems identify the same disease (restless mind / citta-vṛtti) and the same cure (abhyāsa + vairāgya). But Patañjali then does something Kṛṣṇa doesn't in this verse — he unpacks each component into its own sūtra. YS I.13 defines abhyāsa: tatra sthitau yatno 'bhyāsaḥ — "Practice is the effort to be fixed in concentrating the mind." It's not repetition; it's the sustained effort to hold the mind still. YS I.14 gives the conditions: practice must be uninterrupted (nairantarya), sustained over a long time (dīrgha-kāla), and performed with devotion (satkārāsevita). YS I.15-16 define vairāgya at increasing depth: first, indifference to sense objects whether present or absent; then, the highest vairāgya — indifference to the guṇas themselves, born from perception of puruṣa. Together, these sūtras form the complete specification sheet for the two tools Kṛṣṇa names in a single compound. BG 6.35 gives the command; YS I.12-16 gives the manual. Vyāsa's commentary on YS I.12 makes the bridge explicit: the stream of citta flows in two directions — toward upliftment or toward downfall. Toward discrimination, it leads to liberation. Toward sensuality, it produces karma and perpetuates the cycle. The two methods — abhyāsa and vairāgya — are the two hands that redirect the stream.

 

TERM DEFINITIONS:

Abhyāsa (masculine noun) — practice, sustained effort toward stability; from abhi (toward) + ās (to sit, to be established); not casual repetition but the deliberate, continuous effort to hold the mind in concentration; Patañjali defines it as tatra sthitau yatnaḥ — the effort to be fixed in stillness; becomes firm only when uninterrupted, long-term, and performed with devotion (YS I.14)

Vairāgya (neuter noun) — dispassion, detachment, renunciation; from virāga (discoloration, fading of rāga) + ya (state of); not suppression of desire but the natural fading of attraction toward sense objects that arises from discriminative seeing; two levels: indifference to sense objects (YS I.15) and the supreme indifference to the guṇas themselves born from puruṣa-khyāti (YS I.16)

Durnigraham (adjective) — difficult to restrain, hard to curb; from dur (difficult) + nigraha (restraint, control); Kṛṣṇa's honest assessment of the mind before offering the cure; the prefix dur acknowledges the real difficulty — this is not a motivational speech, it's a diagnosis

Calam (adjective) — flickering, restless, unsteady; from cal (to move, to shake); the mind's default state — not occasionally unstable but constantly in motion; Arjuna compared it to the wind (6.34); Kṛṣṇa doesn't soften the comparison, He confirms it

Gṛhyate (verb, passive) — is seized, caught, subjugated; from grah (to seize, to grasp); the mind is not "improved" or "managed" — it is captured; the passive form indicates this seizing happens through the methods, not through the ego's willpower; abhyāsa and vairāgya do the work

Upāyataḥ (adverb, ablative) — by appropriate means, through the right method; from upāya (means, method, approach); success in yoga is not guaranteed by effort alone — the effort must be directed by correct method; the controlled mind plus appropriate means equals assured success
