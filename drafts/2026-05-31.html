BG 6.7 — The Conquered Mind: Inner Ruler, Outer Equanimity

जितात्मन: प्रशान्तस्य परमात्मा समाहित: ।

शीतोष्णसुखदु:खेषु तथा मानापमानयो: ॥ ७ ॥

jitātmanaḥ praśāntasya paramātmā samāhitaḥ
śītoṣṇa-sukha-duḥkheṣu tathā mānāpamānayoḥ

For one who has conquered the mind, the Supersoul is already reached, for he has attained tranquillity. To such a man happiness and distress, heat and cold, honor and dishonor are all the same.

Commentary

Kṛṣṇa Pratipadā — the first breath of the waning fortnight. Yesterday's Pūrṇimā flooded everything with light; today the contraction begins. And it is precisely in this contraction that BG 6.7 reveals its power. The verse doesn't describe an achievement — it describes a recognition. Jitātmanaḥ — one who has conquered the mind. Paramātmā samāhitaḥ — the Supersoul is already reached. Already. Not "will be reached after more practice." Not "might be reached if you're fortunate." Already. The Paramātmā was never absent — the mind's turbulence simply rendered Him invisible. Conquer the mind, and the inner ruler was there all along, waiting beneath the noise like the sun behind clouds.

The three pairs of opposites Kṛṣṇa names — cold and heat, happiness and distress, honor and dishonor — are not random. They are the complete spectrum of material experience. Physical sensation (śīta-uṣṇa), emotional state (sukha-duḥkha), social positioning (māna-apamāna). Body, heart, reputation. Every portal through which prakṛti's guṇas pull consciousness outward is sealed. Not by suppression — by the simple fact that when Paramātmā is reached, the dvandvas lose their grip. They still arise. They simply don't reach you. The weather still changes; you are no longer the weather.

Today's nakṣatra transition sharpens this teaching. Anurādhā — ruled by Mitra, the divine friend — governs the early morning hours until 6:41 AM. Then Jyeṣṭhā — ruled by Indra, the cosmic sovereign — takes over. The shift is from companionship to command. The conquered mind doesn't lose friendship with the Supreme; it discovers that the friend was always the king. Mitra and Indra are not two different deities — they are two faces of the same Paramātmā who meets you as companion when you seek and reveals Himself as ruler when you arrive.

Yoga Sūtra Bridge: YS II.48

ततो द्वन्द्वानभिघातः

tato dvandva-anabhighātaḥ

From that [āsana perfection] comes non-disturbance by the pairs of opposites.

Patañjali calls it anabhighātaḥ — non-disturbance, non-attack. The dvandvas exist; they simply cannot reach you. Kṛṣṇa says the same thing in BG 6.7: the pairs are "all the same" (tathā) to the one who has conquered the mind. But the two systems enter through different doors. Patañjali enters through the body — āsana perfected, effort relaxed, consciousness absorbed in the infinite. From that bodily stabilization, equanimity naturally follows. Kṛṣṇa enters through the mind — jitātman, the mind conquered, tranquillity established, and from there the Supersoul is recognized. The body path and the mind path both arrive at the same destination: the dvandvas no longer attack. The Sūtras build from the outside in; the Gītā works from the inside out. Both confirm what every practitioner eventually discovers — that equanimity is not a technique. It is the inevitable consequence of establishing the right relationship between consciousness and its conditions.

Cosmic Interpretation: Kṛṣṇa Pratipadā, Jyeṣṭhā's Sovereign Dawn

The waning fortnight begins with Pratipadā — the first step after fullness. This is not loss; this is distillation. The Pūrṇimā expanded everything — now the Kṛṣṇa paksha concentrates the essence. BG 6.7 is the perfect Pratipadā verse: it strips away everything extraneous and reveals the core. The heat, the cold, the praise, the blame — all the fluctuations that filled the Śukla paksha's expansion — now fall away. What remains is paramātmā samāhitaḥ. The distillate of spiritual practice is not more experience. It is the recognition of what was always present.

Jyeṣṭhā nakṣatra — "the eldest, the most venerable" — carries the energy of seniority, authority, and earned wisdom. Its deity is Indra, the king of the demigods, the wielder of the thunderbolt. Indra is not gentle. He is sovereignty itself — the power that commands because it has earned the right to command. When Jyeṣṭhā activates at 6:41 AM, the cosmic energy shifts from Anurādhā's devotional friendship to Jyeṣṭhā's sovereign authority. The mind that was conquered through Mitra's gentle companionship now stands before Indra's fierce clarity. Both are Paramātmā. The devotee who has wept in prayer and the warrior who has slain internal demons are the same person at different stages of recognition.

The moon in Vṛścika (Scorpio) refuses superficiality. Scorpio demands the real thing — not spiritual performance, not comforting platitudes, but the raw truth of where your equanimity actually stands. On this Pratipadā, Scorpio asks: "When the heat actually comes — not metaphorically, but the real fire of consequence and conflict — are you still undisturbed?" The honest answer for most practitioners is: not yet. And that honesty is itself the beginning of jitātman. The conquered mind is not the mind that pretends. It is the mind that sees clearly — including its own remaining disturbances — and places them before Paramātmā without flinching.

Siddha yoga (the perfected state) holds through the day, transitioning to Sadhya (the attainable) at 8:48 PM. This is the cosmic confirmation: what has been perfected in theory must now become attainable in practice. The Kṛṣṇa paksha is where theory meets reality. The full moon showed you the ideal. The dark fortnight will test whether you've actually absorbed it.

Sanskrit Terms

through saṃsāra now serves as instrument. The daemon process has been terminated and replaced with your code.

praśānta — Attained tranquillity. Not temporary calm — established peace. The system isn't just quiet; it's stable at rest. No background processes consuming resources.

paramātmā — The Supersoul, the Supreme Soul dwelling in the heart. The inner ruler who was always present — the mind's chaos simply obscured Him. When the noise clears, He was already there.

samāhitaḥ — Approached completely, fully attained. Not partially, not occasionally. The connection is established and stable.

dvandva — Pairs of opposites — hot/cold, pleasure/pain, honor/dishonor. The binary oscillations of prakṛti that keep consciousness bouncing between attachment and aversion.

anabhighāta — Non-disturbance, non-attack. The opposites still exist — they just don't reach you. The firewall is active; the traffic is filtered.

Practice for Kṛṣṇa Pratipadā

Morning: The Kṛṣṇa paksha begins today — the first day of turning inward after Pūrṇimā's expansion. During Brahma Muhūrta (3:56–4:44 AM), before the nakṣatra shifts at 6:41 AM, sit with the Anurādhā-Mitra energy still active. Offer your practice as friendship to the Supreme. After 6:41 AM, Jyeṣṭhā-Indra energy takes over — the warrior-king who demands sovereignty over the mind. Chant BG 6.7 and feel the shift: from "I seek You as friend" to "I recognize You as ruler." Both are true. The conquered mind holds both simultaneously.

Evening: At moonrise (9:11 PM), sit quietly and review the day's dvandvas — where were you pulled? Where did heat or cold, praise or blame disturb your equilibrium? Write down three instances. Then write: "Paramātmā samāhitaḥ — the Supersoul is already reached." This is not aspiration. It is the declaration of what becomes available when the mind is conquered.
