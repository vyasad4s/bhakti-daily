🏹 VERSE CONTENT

Source: Bhagavad-gītā 2.23

Date: March 6, 2026

Sanskrit:

नैनं छिन्दन्ति शस्त्राणि

नैनं दहति पावकः ।

न चैनं क्लेदयन्त्यापो

न शोषयति मारुतः ॥ २३ ॥

Transliteration:

nainaṃ chindanti śastrāṇi
nainaṃ dahati pāvakaḥ
na cainaṃ kledayanty āpo
na śoṣayati mārutaḥ

Translation:

The soul can never be cut to pieces by any weapon, nor burned by fire, nor moistened by water, nor withered by the wind.

Commentary:

Here Kṛṣṇa establishes the absolute indestructibility of the ātman—the soul as pure consciousness remains untouched by the five material elements. Weapons (earth element) cannot sever it, fire cannot burn it, water cannot dissolve it, wind cannot desiccate it. This is the essence of Sāṅkhya dualism: puruṣa (consciousness) is eternally distinct from prakṛti (matter) and its modifications.

From the hacker perspective: the soul is like immutable source code that cannot be modified by runtime operations. The elements are the execution environment—weapons, fire, water, wind are all processes running on prakṛti's hardware. They can destroy the container (the body) but not the consciousness running within it. The ātman has root access that persists through system crashes. This is why BG 2.22 (yesterday's verse) spoke of the body as a garment—clothes can be torn and replaced, but the wearer remains.

Yoga Sutra Connection:

🔥 YS 1.2 — योगश्‍ चित्त-वृत्ति-निरोधः (yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ)

"Yoga is the stilling of the changing states of the mind." — Edwin Bryant translation

The connection: Just as the soul (puruṣa) is immune to the physical elements, it is also immune to the mental fluctuations (vṛttis) of citta. The citta undergoes constant changes—right knowledge, error, imagination, sleep, memory (YS 1.6)—but the seer (draṣṭṛ) abides in its own true nature when these fluctuations cease (YS 1.3: tadā draṣṭuḥ svarūpe 'vasthānam). The mind is prakṛtic; the soul is not. When we stop identifying with the mind's modifications, we realize the ātman that cannot be cut, burned, moistened, or dried. This is liberation: not changing the soul but ceasing to confuse it with that which changes.

Sanskrit Terms:

  • śastrāṇi (noun, plural) — weapons, literally "that which cuts or kills"; from √śas (to cut, injure, kill). Hacker metaphor: destructive processes targeting containers, not the runtime.
  • pāvakaḥ (noun) — fire, the purifier; from √pū (to purify). The element of transformation.
  • kledayanti (verb, 3rd person plural, causal) — they moisten, wet, soften; from √klid (be wet, damp). The water element's dissolving power.
  • śoṣayati (verb, 3rd person singular, causal) — dries up, withers; from √śuṣ (dry up). Wind's dessicating power.
  • mārutaḥ (noun) — wind, air, Vāyu; from √mṛ (to die, fade). Prāṇa's mobile aspect.
