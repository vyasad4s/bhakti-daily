Dissolution and Rebirth

Bhagavad-gītā 2.28

अव्यक्तादीनि भूतानि व्यक्तमध्यानि भारत । अव्यक्तनिधनान्येव तत्र का परिदेवना ॥ २८ ॥
avyaktādīni bhūtāni vyakta-madhyāni bhārata avyakta-nidhanāny eva tatra kā paridevanā

All created beings are unmanifest in their beginning, manifest in their interim state, and unmanifest again when annihilated. So what need is there for lamentation?

avyakta — unmanifest, invisible, latent; the subtle state before material manifestation emerges from prakṛti; like source code before compilation. vyakta — manifest, visible, expressed; the material form that appears in the interim; the compiled program running in time. bhūta — created being, elemental manifestation; from √bhū, "to become, to come into being." paridevanā — lamentation, excessive grieving, crying out; the emotional reaction fired by ahaṅkāra's identification with temporary forms.

The moon waxes thirteenth-day full—tonight the tithi turns from Trayodaśī to Caturdaśī as the swollen lunar disk climbs toward tomorrow's Pūrṇimā. This is Soma Pradoṣa: the sacred hour at sunset when Śiva drank the poison of the churning ocean to save the worlds. What the Vedas call dissolution, physics calls entropy. What metaphysics calls pralaya, Gödel called incompleteness. The building stands; then rubble; then the materials warehouse. The unmanifest source is never lost.

The nakṣatra shifts from Pūrva to Uttara Phālgunī—Venus's pleasure-mansion yielding to the duty-mansion of Aryaman. The enjoyment matures into responsibility. The Ganda Yoga ("the Knot") binds the day in tangles of attachment, and Pradoṣa at sunset is precisely the moment Śiva's energy cuts through. avyakta-nidhanāny eva—the dissolution is also unmanifest. The things we mourn are changing form, not ending.

The instruction is surgical. Whether you believe in the eternal soul (the Gītā's position) or hold atheistic materialism (the hypothetical opponent), the logic holds: in the beginning, unmanifest elements. In the middle, manifested skyscrapers. In the end, unmanifest atoms. The energy never disappears. The paridevanā—the excessive grief, the crying—is fired by ahaṅkāra's identification: "this form was mine; now it's gone." But the form was never yours. It was vyakta (manifest) in your perception for a time, and now returns to avyakta (unmanifest). What changes?

Yoga Sūtra Connection

YS II.1 — तपः-स्वाध्यायेश्वर-प्रणिधानानि क्रिया-योगः
tapas-svādhyāya-īśvara-praṇidhānāni kriyā-yogaḥ

Kriyā Yoga—the yoga of action—consists of three elements: tapas (discipline, the burning away of impurities), svādhyāya (self-study, the study of scripture and the inquiry into one's own nature), and īśvara-praṇidhāna (devotion to God, surrender to the supreme consciousness).

Edwin Bryant notes this threefold practice "indicates the paths of karma, jñāna, and bhakti." This is precisely the architecture of BG 2.28: the materialist sees only karma—action and reaction in the cycle of manifestation. The jñānī sees the cycle itself as māyā—the dream of forms appearing and disappearing. The bhakta sees īśvara behind the cycle—Lord Śiva drinking the poison, Lord Kṛṣṇa fielding Arjuna's grief. All three are contained here.

The avyakta (unmanifest) is prakṛti in her seed-state, before the guṇas churn her into manifestation. When vyakta emerges, we mistake it for reality. Kriyā Yoga is the purification that lets us perceive the avyakta beneath—so that when dissolution comes, we do not lament. Śrīdhara Svāmī comments: "The body changes but the enjoyer remains the same." Tapas burns away the identification that causes grief. Svādhyāya reveals the scripture's testimony. Īśvara-praṇidhāna hands the grief to the Lord.

Practice

Sit in Brahma Muhūrta before sunrise (5:09–5:57 AM). Witness the transition from night to day—avyakta (unmanifest darkness) yielding to vyakta (manifest light). Do not interfere. Observe how forms emerge from no-form, and will dissolve back into no-form at sunset. This is Pradoṣa meditation: Śiva's hour is the reminder that all manifestation is temporary gift, not permanent possession.

When the Ganda Yoga tightens its knot in your chest today—some attachment, some fear of loss—recite avyaktādīni bhūtāni. Trace the thing backward: what was it before it appeared to you? Where will it be after it disappears? The anxiety is not in the thing; it's in the paridevanā—the crying, the lamentation, the refusal to accept the terms.

At Soma Pradoṣa this evening (sunset, ~7:13 PM), offer the dissolution of your own forms to Śiva. What aspects of yourself are you clutching that must unmanifest? What identities are you building that will become rubble? Grief is optional. Discernment is not.
