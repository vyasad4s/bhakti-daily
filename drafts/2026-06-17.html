Bhagavad Gita Chapter 8, Verse 9

कविं पुराणमनुशासितार- मणोरणीयांसमनुस्मरेद्य: । सर्वस्य धातरमचिन्त्यरूप- मादित्यवर्णं तमस: परस्तात् ॥ ९ ॥
kavim purāṇam anuśāsitāram
aṇor aṇīyāṁsam anusmared yaḥ
sarvasya dhātāram acintya-rūpam
āditya-varṇaṁ tamasaḥ parastāt
One should meditate upon the Supreme Person as the one who knows everything, as He who is the oldest, who is the controller, who is smaller than the smallest, who is the maintainer of everything, who is beyond all material conception, who is inconceivable, and who is always a person. He is luminous like the sun, and He is transcendental, beyond this material nature.
The process of thinking of the Supreme is mentioned in this verse. The foremost point is that He is not impersonal or void. One cannot meditate on something impersonal or void. That is very difficult. The process of thinking of Kṛṣṇa, however, is very easy and is factually stated herein. First of all, the Lord is puruṣa, a person – we think of the person Rāma and the person Kṛṣṇa. And whether one thinks of Rāma or of Kṛṣṇa, what He is like is described in this verse of Bhagavad-gītā. The Lord is kavi; that is, He knows past, present and future and therefore knows everything. He is the oldest personality because He is the origin of everything; everything is born out of Him. He is also the supreme controller of the universe, and He is the maintainer and instructor of humanity. He is smaller than the smallest. The living entity is one ten-thousandth part of the tip of a hair, but the Lord is so inconceivably small that He enters into the heart of this particle. Therefore He is called smaller than the smallest. As the Supreme, He can enter into the atom and into the heart of the smallest and control him as the Supersoul. Although so small, He is still all-pervading and is maintaining everything. By Him all these planetary systems are sustained. We often wonder how these big planets are floating in the air. It is stated here that the Supreme Lord, by His inconceivable energy, is sustaining all these big planets and systems of galaxies. The word acintya (“inconceivable”) is very significant in this connection. God’s energy is beyond our conception, beyond our thinking jurisdiction, and is therefore called inconceivable (acintya). Who can argue this point? He pervades this material world and yet is beyond it. We cannot comprehend even this material world, which is insignificant compared to the spiritual world – so how can we comprehend what is beyond? Acintya means that which is beyond this material world, that which our argument, logic and philosophical speculation cannot touch, that which is inconceivable. Therefore intelligent persons, avoiding useless argument and speculation, should accept what is stated in scriptures like the Vedas, Bhagavad-gītā and Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam and follow the principles they set down. This will lead one to understanding.

Cosmic Interpretation

Tritiya, the third tithi of the waxing moon, symbolizes growth and expansion, the third stage of creation where the initial impulse begins to take form. As Tritiya yields to Chaturthi at 12:08 PM, the energy shifts to the fourth tithi, associated with stability and foundation, and observed as Chaturthi Vrat—a day dedicated to Lord Ganesha, the remover of obstacles (Vighnaharta). The nakṣatra Pushya, ruled by Brihaspati (the guru of the gods), governs the early morning hours, representing nourishment, strengthening, and the auspicious energy of growth through wisdom. The yoga Dhruva (the fixed, steady) prevails until 11:20 AM, signifying stability, endurance, and the unchanging point of reference, after which Vyaghata (obstacle, injury) brings the energy of overcoming impediments.

BG 8.9 beautifully aligns with this cosmic rhythm: “One should meditate upon the Supreme Person as the one who knows everything, as He who is the oldest, who is the controller, who is smaller than the smallest, who is the maintainer of everything, who is beyond all material conception, who is inconceivable, and who is always a person. He is luminous like the sun, and He is transcendental, beyond this material nature.” Just as Tritiya follows the initial impulse with growth, Pushya nourishes, and Dhruva provides steadiness—so too does the devotee progress by meditating on the Supreme as the eternal controller and unchanging reality. Vyaghata’s obstacle-removing energy mirrors the verse’s declaration that the Supreme is beyond all material conception (acintya)—the inconceivable power that transcends and removes all obstacles. Chaturthi Vrat’s focus on Ganesha, the lord of obstacles, further emphasizes that the ultimate remover of obstacles is the Supreme Himself, whose nature is described in this verse.

The verse invites us to “meditate upon” (anusmared) the Supreme Person with specific attributes: the omniscient (kavi), the oldest (purāṇam), the controller (anuśāsitāram), smaller than the smallest (aṇor aṇīyāṁsam), the maintainer of everything (sarvasya dhātāram), beyond material conception (acintya-rūpam), luminous like the sun (āditya-varṇam), and transcendental beyond darkness (tamasaḥ parastāt). In the language of the simulation, He is the kernel that runs beneath all processes, the debugger that reveals the true state, and the garbage collector that reclaims memory—yet beyond all metaphors, He is the source and sustainer of everything.

Connections & Key Concepts

Yoga Sutra Connection

YS I.23: Īśvara-praṇidhānāt vā
Or [samādhi is attained] by devotion to the Lord

Connection: BG 8.9 describes the Supreme Person as the object of meditation—His attributes as the oldest, controller, smallest, maintainer, inconceivable, luminous, and transcendental. Yoga Sutra I.23 states that samādhi can be attained through devotion to Īśvara (the Lord). Both point to the same goal: focusing the mind on the Supreme Lord leads to the highest state of yoga. The verse’s injunction to meditate on the Supreme Person’s qualities is a form of Īśvara-praṇidhāna, where contemplation of the Lord’s nature leads to samādhi.
