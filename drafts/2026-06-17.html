Bhagavad Gita Chapter 8, Verse 9

कविं पुराणमनुशासितार- मणोरणीयांसमनुस्मरेद्य: । सर्वस्य धातरमचिन्त्यरूप- मादित्यवर्णं तमस: परस्तात् ॥ ९ ॥
kavim purāṇam anuśāsitāram
aṇor aṇīyāṁsam anusmared yaḥ
sarvasya dhātāram acintya-rūpam
āditya-varṇaṁ tamasaḥ parastāt
One should meditate upon the Supreme Person as the one who knows everything, as He who is the oldest, who is the controller, who is smaller than the smallest, who is the maintainer of everything, who is beyond all material conception, who is inconceivable, and who is always a person. He is luminous like the sun, and He is transcendental, beyond this material nature.
From the Sankhya perspective, puruṣa is pure consciousness, attributeless and inactive, yet the scriptures ascribe attributes to aid meditation (upāsana). This verse enumerates such attributes: omniscience (kavi), primal origin (purāṇam), supreme controller (anuśāsitāram), subtler than the subtlest (aṇor aṇīyāṁsam), sustainer of all (sarvasya dhātāram), beyond material conception (acintya), luminous like the sun (āditya-varṇam), and transcendental person (tamasaḥ parastāt). Meditating on these qualities (anusmara) turns the mind inward, loosening identification with the body-mind complex (prakṛti). As the Yoga Sutra affirms, devotion to Īśvara is a direct path to samādhi (YS I.23). Thus the verse serves as both object of meditation and reminder of the practitioner’s true nature as puruṣa, distinct yet dependent on the conscious principle.

Cosmic Interpretation

Tritiya, the third tithi of the waxing moon, symbolizes growth and expansion, the third stage of creation where the initial impulse begins to take form. As Tritiya yields to Chaturthi at 12:08 PM, the energy shifts to the fourth tithi, associated with stability and foundation, and observed as Chaturthi Vrat—a day dedicated to Lord Ganesha, the remover of obstacles (Vighnaharta). The nakṣatra Pushya, ruled by Brihaspati (the guru of the gods), governs the early morning hours, representing nourishment, strengthening, and the auspicious energy of growth through wisdom. BG 8.9 beautifully aligns with this cosmic rhythm: “One should meditate upon the Supreme Person as the one who knows everything, as He who is the oldest, who is the controller, who is smaller than the smallest, who is the maintainer of everything, who is beyond all material conception, who is inconceivable, and who is always a person. He is luminous like the sun, and He is transcendental, beyond this material nature.” Just as Tritiya follows the initial impulse with growth, Pushya nourishes, and Dhruva provides steadiness—so too does the devotee progress by meditating on the Supreme as the eternal controller and unchanging reality. Vyaghata’s obstacle-removing energy mirrors the verse’s declaration that the Supreme is beyond all material conception (acintya)—the inconceivable power that transcends and removes all obstacles. Chaturthi Vrat’s focus on Ganesha, the lord of obstacles, further emphasizes that the ultimate remover of obstacles is the Supreme Himself, whose nature is described in this verse.

The verse invites us to “meditate upon” (anusmared) the Supreme Person with specific attributes: the omniscient (kavi), the oldest (purāṇam), the controller (anuśāsitāram), smaller than the smallest (aṇor aṇīyāṁsam), the maintainer of everything (sarvasya dhātāram), beyond material conception (acintya-rūpam), luminous like the sun (āditya-varṇam), and transcendental beyond darkness (tamasaḥ parastāt). In the language of the simulation, He is the kernel that runs beneath all processes, the debugger that reveals the true state, and the garbage collector that reclaims memory—yet beyond all metaphors, He is the source and sustainer of everything.

Connections & Key Concepts

Yoga Sutra Connection

YS I.23: Īśvara-praṇidhānāt vā
Or [samādhi is attained] by devotion to the Lord

Connection: BG 8.9 describes the Supreme Person as the object of meditation—His attributes as the oldest, controller, smallest, maintainer, inconceivable, luminous, and transcendental. Yoga Sutra I.23 states that samādhi can be attained through devotion to Īśvara (the Lord). Both point to the same goal: focusing the mind on the Supreme Lord leads to the highest state of yoga. The verse’s injunction to meditate on the Supreme Person’s qualities is a form of Īśvara-praṇidhāna, where contemplation of the Lord’s nature leads to samādhi.
