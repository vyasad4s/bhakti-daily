BG 4.5 — Eternal Memory, Forgotten Past

śrī-bhagavān uvāca
bahūni me vyatītāni janmāni tava cārjuna
tāny ahaṁ veda sarvāṇi na tvaṁ vettha paraṁ-tapa

Bhagavad-gītā 4.5

Translation

The Personality of Godhead said: Many, many births both you and I have passed. I can remember all of them, but you cannot, O subduer of the enemy!

Synonyms

  • śrī-bhagavān uvāca — the Personality of Godhead said
  • bahūni — many
  • me vyatītāni — of Mine having passed
  • janmāni — births
  • tava ca — and yours
  • arjuna — O Arjuna
  • tāni — those
  • ahaṁ veda — I know
  • sarvāṇi — all
  • na tvam vettha — not you know
  • param-tapa — O subduer of the enemy

Listen. The storm of Ārdrā breaks at midday, and as the rain clears, something ancient stirs. This is the moment Kṛṣṇa chooses to remind Arjuna of what he has forgotten — not to shame him, but to wake him. "Many births you and I have passed." The words land like thunder after lightning: we have done this before. The cycles are not theoretical. They are personal. They are ours.

The difference between Kṛṣṇa and Arjuna is the difference between the fire that knows itself and the spark that forgot it is flame. Both are eternal. Both have passed through countless forms. But one remembers — every birth, every teaching, every promise made to the sun-god Vivasvān millions of years ago — while the other stands on the battlefield, drowning in temporary grief, unable to recall why he chose this body, this moment, this duty. The Lord's memory is perfect because His consciousness is never dimmed by His contact with matter. He is acyuta — infallible, never forgetting, never falling from His nature. Arjuna forgets because he is the part forgetting it belongs to the Whole.

This is the blow Ārdrā delivers: you think this is your first time here? You think this confusion, this fear, this standing at the edge of action paralyzed by doubt — you think this is new? The soul has ridden these cycles before. The storm has broken over your head a thousand times, and a thousand times you have emerged into Punarvasu, the return of light, only to forget again. The body changes like shirts discarded. The memory wipes with each transition. But the witness remains — and today, the witness speaks.

avyaya — "imperishable, inexhaustible." The soul's fundamental quality: it cannot be diminished by birth, confounded by death, or fragmented across time. Like root access to the system — credentials that persist across reboots.

param-tapa — "subduer of the enemy." Arjuna's epithet carries weight here. The enemy to be subdued is not the Kurus on the battlefield — it is the ignorance that makes him forget his nature, his past, his purpose. The warrior who can defeat external armies cannot defeat internal darkness without divine knowledge.

janma — "birth." In classical Sāṅkhya: not the origin of the soul (which is eternal), but the assumption of a new instrument — the body-mind complex — through which puruṣa falsely identifies as agent and enjoyer. The phala (fruit) of prakṛti's cycle, binding until knowledge dissolves the bond.

Yoga Sutra Connection

YS I.3: tadā draṣṭuḥ svarūpe 'vasthānam — "Then the seer abides in its own nature."

Patañjali describes the goal: the seer (draṣṭṛ, puruṣa) resting in its true nature, free from the misidentification that makes it think it is the body, the mind, the doer, the enjoyer — or in Arjuna's case, the forgetter. The Lord remembers all births because He never confuses Himself with the vehicles He manifests through. Arjuna forgets because he has confused the witness with the witnessed, the seer with the seen. The yoga of transcendental knowledge is precisely this: dissolving the ignorance (avidyā) that screens memory, restoring the seer to awareness of its own eternal status — not as a passive watcher, but as the living participant who knows, chooses, and acts from that knowing.

Practice

↓ DO THIS NOW ↓

Stop. Close your eyes. Ask yourself: What have I forgotten? Not in a mystical, cosmic-past-lives way — practically, honestly. What did you know before the storm of this week, this month, this year, that you have let slip into Ārdrā's shadow? What resolve, what clarity, what recognition of your nature? The Ātman does not require excavation. It requires remembrance. Chant Hare Kṛṣṇa as an act of mnemosyne — each name a bell calling the soul back to what it already is.

The time is now. The body will change. The memory will wipe. But this moment — this recognition of the Lord's knowing beside your forgetting — this can cut the cycle's grip. Let the storm pass. Step into Punarvasu. Remember, and act from that remembrance.

Ārdrā → Punarvasu Portal

The midday transition from Ārdrā (Rudra's storm, the weeping star) to Punarvasu (Aditi's abundance, "return of the light") is the cosmic dramaturgy of this verse. The storm of forgetting breaks. The light of memory returns. This is not abstract astronomy — it is the lived experience of every soul who has wept in confusion and then, suddenly, remembered why they came. Punarvasu means "becoming light again" — the cyclic return that is not repetition but renewal.

Ṣaṣṭhī — Skanda's Day

The sixth day of the waxing moon belongs to Skanda-Murugan, the divine warrior-commander who conquers not through force but through awakened intelligence. His spear (śakti) is discrimination (viveka) that pierces the demon of ignorance. Today is for the warfare of remembering — cutting through the saṃsāric fog with the sharp weapon of knowledge.

Wednesday — Budha's Domain

Mercury governs communication, intellect, discrimination. On this day, the distinction between divine knowing and human unknowing becomes clear. The intellect that forgot itself remembers its source. The buddhi that was clouded by storm sheds its confusion and reflects — however dimly at first — the eternal light.

Why This Verse Today

BG 4.5 is the verse of recovered memory emerging from the storm. Ārdrā→Punarvasu portal at midday is the cosmic window when the soul shaken by storm emerges into clarity. This verse was spoken by Kṛṣṇa to remind Arjuna that the soul's immortality is not speculative philosophy but lived reality — etched into the memory of the Divine even as individual consciousness forgets. Today — Ṣaṣṭhī, Skanda's day of spiritual warfare through knowledge — is the precise moment to hear this reminder.

The Śukla Pakṣa Saptamī into which the nakṣatra shifts at 1:19 PM carries forward the 7th-day energy of completion and forward movement. The saptamī tithi is associated with Indra, the king who remembers his divine status even after temporary forgetfulness. This completes the theme: the king who forgot (Arjuna) shall recall his nature and resume his duty.
