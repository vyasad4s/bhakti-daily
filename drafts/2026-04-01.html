🏹 BG 2.15 | The Equanimous Yogi

April 1, 2026 | Śukla Pūrṇimā | Hanumān Jayantī | Hasta Nakṣatra → Dhruva Yoga

यं हि न व्यथयन्त्येते पुरुषं पुरुषर्षभ ।
समदुःखसुखं धीरं सोऽमृतत्वाय कल्पते ॥ १५ ॥

yaṁ hi na vyathayanty ete puruṣaṁ puruṣarṣabha
sama-duḥkha-sukhaṁ dhīraṁ so 'mṛtatvāya kalpate

yam — one to whom; hi — certainly; na — never; vyathayanti — are distressing; ete — all these; puruṣam — to a person; puruṣa-ṛṣabha — O best among men; sama — equal; duḥkha — in distress; sukham — and happiness; dhīram — steady; saḥ — he; amṛtatvāya — for liberation; kalpate — is considered eligible.

Translation: O best among men [Arjuna], the person who is not disturbed by happiness and distress and is steady in both is certainly eligible for liberation.

Purport: Anyone who is steady in his determination for the advanced stage of spiritual realization and can equally tolerate the onslaughts of distress and happiness is certainly a person eligible for liberation. In the varṇāśrama institution, the fourth stage of life, namely the renounced order (sannyāsa), is a painstaking situation. But one who is serious about making his life perfect surely adopts the sannyāsa order of life in spite of all difficulties. The difficulties usually arise from having to sever family relationships, to give up the connection of wife and children. But if anyone is able to tolerate such difficulties, surely his path to spiritual realization is complete. Similarly, in Arjuna's discharge of duties as a kṣatriya, he is advised to persevere, even if it is difficult to fight with his family members or similarly beloved persons. Lord Caitanya took sannyāsa at the age of twenty-four, and His dependents, young wife as well as old mother, had no one else to look after them. Yet for a higher cause He took sannyāsa and was steady in the discharge of higher duties. That is the way of achieving liberation from material bondage.

— Śrīla Prabhupāda, Bhagavad-gītā As It Is

🔗 Yoga Sūtra Connection

Patañjali's framework illuminates how this equanimity is cultivated:

YS II.48: tataḥ dvandvānabhighātaḥ — "From this [practice], there is non-disturbance from the pairs of opposites."

The "pairs of opposites" (dvandva) — sukha/duḥkha (happiness/distress), success/failure, heat/cold, honor/dishonor — these are the oscillations of prakṛti. The yogi, established in practice with dispassion (vairāgya), no longer registers these fluctuations as spiritual data.

Edwin Bryant notes that this is not mere stoicism. It's the result of puruṣa-khyāti — direct perception of the soul's separateness from the guṇa's dance. When you know, experientially, that you are not the mind experiencing pleasure or pain, but the witness (sākṣī) to its activity, the disturbance stops registering as "yours." The mind still feels — this isn't anesthesia. But the appropriation of feeling, the "I am suffering / I am enjoying" — that's the break in identification.

BG 2.15 states the result: such a person — dhīra, steady, patient — is fit for amṛtatva, for liberation. Patañjali describes the mechanism (disciplined practice leading to freedom from opposites). Kṛṣṇa states the qualification (equal vision in pleasure and pain). Together they map the path and the destination.

📝 Commentary: The Hardware Doesn't Care About Output

Here is a precise teaching. Not sentimentality about "staying positive" through hardship. Not toxic positivity that denies suffering exists. Something far more radical: the recognition that experience itself is prakṛtic data, and you are not the processor.

The dhīra — patient, steady — is one who has internalized the Sāṅkhya insight that puruṣa (conscious subject) and prakṛti (material matrix) are categorically distinct. Pleasure and pain are modifications of prakṛti. They arise in the field (kṣetra) of body-mind, not in the knower of the field (kṣetra-jña). The yogi witnesses them as weather patterns: real, impactful to the body, but not compromising the sky-like awareness in which they appear and dissolve.

Prabhupāda emphasizes this requires determination — a word choice that strips the teaching of any passive interpretation. This isn't waiting for equanimity to descend as a blessing. It's the active cultivation of discrimination (viveka) and dispassion (vairāgya) through sustained practice. Lord Caitanya's example at age twenty-four — abandoning home, young wife, elderly mother for sannyāsa — isn't offered as a universal prescription but as proof-of-concept: when the goal is seen clearly, prakṛtic attachments lose their binding power.

The Pūrṇimā moon tonight is fullest illumination, but note: it will wane. The yogi who understands BG 2.15 witnesses both waxing and waning with the same steady gaze. The fullness does not intoxicate; the diminishment does not depress. Both are ete — "these things" — phenomena in the field, not touching the witness.

📚 Terms

yam — The relative pronoun "one to whom" — establishes the conditional nature of the teaching. This equanimity is not theoretical; it accrues to the one who...

vyathayanti — From √vyath (to tremble, waver, be agitated). The distress that shakes the mind from its center — like a bad signal interrupting transmission.

sama — Equal, same, balanced, equanimous — the central technical term. In Sāṅkhya-Yoga, sama is the quality of buddhi when sattva predominates and rajas/tamas are subdued.

duḥkha — Pain, distress, suffering — specifically the existential dissatisfaction of material existence (from dus- difficult + kha- space/ground — "hard ground," unhappiness).

sukha — Happiness, pleasure, ease — from su- (good) + kha (space) — "good ground," the agreeable feeling-state.

dhīra — Steady, patient, firm — from √dhṛ (to hold, carry, support). One who holds steady under pressure — the carrier of spiritual weight without collapse.

amṛtatva — Liberation, immortality, nectar-state — a-mṛta (without death). Not merely longevity but freedom from the cycle of birth and death.

kalpate — Is considered eligible, fit, suitable — from √kḷp (to be able, to be suitable). The technical term for qualified practitioner.

dvandva — Pairs of opposites — hot-cold, pleasure-pain, honor-dishonor, success-failure. Prakṛti's oscillating expressions that disturb the unsteady mind.

puruṣa-khyāti — Direct perception of the soul — the discriminative knowledge that transforms theoretical Sāṅkhya into realized truth.

Today's Sādhana: Dhruva-Vṛtti — The Steady Gaze

Given today's Dhruva Yoga (unwavering) and Hasta nakṣatra (skillful action), the practice is dhruva-dṛṣṭi — the steady gaze of the witness.

  1. Recognition Exercise (5 minutes): Throughout the day, when experience arises — pleasant or unpleasant — mentally note: "This is ete — one of 'these things' — prakṛtic data, not self."
  2. Point of View Shift: Instead of "I am happy" or "I am suffering," try: "Happiness is arising" or "Suffering is present in the field." The pronoun change is subtle but significant — it begins to separate puruṣa from prakṛti.
  3. Witness Anchoring: When caught in either extreme — intoxication with pleasure or reactivity to pain — pause. Take three breaths. Ask: "Who witnesses this?" Not to answer conceptually, but to shift the seat of identity from the experiencing mind to the awareness within which it appears.
