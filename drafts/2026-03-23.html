Bhagavad-gita 2.11


अशोच्यानन्वशोचस्त्वं प्रज्ञावादांश्च भाषसे ।
गतासूनगतासूंश्च नानुशोचन्ति पण्डिताः ॥११॥

aśocyān anvaśocas tvaṁ prajñā-vādāṁś ca bhāṣase
gatāsūn agatāsūṁś ca nānuśocanti paṇḍitāḥ
Translation: The Supreme Personality of Godhead said: While speaking learned words, you are mourning for what is not worthy of grief. Those who are wise lament neither for the living nor for the dead.

aśocyān — not worthy of lamentation; anvaśocaḥ — you are lamenting; prajñā-vādān — learned talks; bhāṣase — speaking; gatāsūn — those whose lives have ended; agatāsūn — those who have not ended their lives; paṇḍitāḥ — the learned. The term paṇḍita derives from the root paṇḍ — to accumulate wisdom, one who has gathered discrimination between the eternal and the temporal, between the soul and the body.

The Teacher Awakens the Student

At last, Kṛṣṇa speaks as teacher. For ten verses, Arjuna has poured forth his anguish — arguments clothed in dharma, appeals to compassion, fears of sin. And now the Lord cuts through it all with one surgical statement: you are crying for what is not worth crying over.

This is not cruelty. This is the beginning of Sāṅkhya — the analytical discrimination between what changes and what never does. Arjuna speaks like a man of knowledge, yet his heart is gripped by ignorance of the self. He knows the scriptures but not their essence. He can debate ethics but cannot see past the body's temporary condition.

The paṇḍita — the truly learned — does not lament for the living or the dead because the paṇḍita knows: the ātmā was never born and never dies. It simply changes vehicles, as one changes clothes. The body that Arjuna so desperately wants to preserve will perish today or tomorrow regardless. His tears cannot stop this. His refusal to fight cannot save anyone from death.

This is the first lesson of the Gītā: knowledge must precede action. We cannot perform our duty while identifying with what changes. Arjuna must become the dhīra — the sober one who is not bewildered by bodily transformation. The battlefield is the perfect classroom because it forces the question: what is worth protecting? What endures?

Yoga Sutra Connection: The Seer and the Seen

Patañjali Yoga Sūtra I.2: yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ — "Yoga is the stilling of the changing states of the mind."

I.4: vṛtti-sārūpyam itaratra — "Otherwise, at other times, the seer is absorbed in the changing states of the mind."

Here is the Sāṅkhya-Yoga dialogue made explicit. Patañjali describes the problem: the seer misidentifies with the mind's fluctuations, becoming absorbed in the drama of thought and emotion rather than abiding in its true nature as pure consciousness. This is precisely Arjuna's condition — overwhelmed by vṛttis of grief and confusion, he has lost discrimination between himself as the eternal witness and the temporary circumstances he witnesses.

Kṛṣṇa's instruction in BG 2.11 is the practical application of Patañjali's metaphysics. Arjuna must cease identifying with the vṛttis of lamentation and re-establish himself as the paṇḍita — one who has discriminating wisdom between puruṣa and prakṛti. The battlefield is the ultimate test: can one act decisively while remaining unstirred by the mind's reactive patterns?

Both traditions agree: bondage comes from sārūpya — taking on the form of the mind's modifications. Liberation comes from nirodha — stilling these modifications through discriminative knowledge. Kṛṣṇa will teach Arjuna how to fight as a yogī — not by suppressing emotion but by transcending identification with it.

Cosmic Context — Śukla Pañcamī, Kṛttikā to Rohiṇī

Tithi: Śukla Pañcamī (until 8:21 PM) — The fifth day of the waxing moon, governed by Budha (Mercury). Pañcamī is the tithi of learning, intellectual pursuits, and the acquisition of sacred knowledge. It is auspicious for beginning studies, for it carries the energy of the student approaching the teacher.

Nakṣatra: Kṛttikā until 11:19 AM, then Rohiṇī. Kṛttikā — the Pleiades — is Agni's asterism, the fire of transformation that purifies. Rohiṇī — the Red One — is the star of growth and nurturing. The transition mirrors this verse's energy: morning's fire of discrimination gives way to afternoon's abundance of wisdom.

Yoga: Sarvārtha Siddhi begins at 11:19 AM — "success in all endeavors." This highly auspicious combination of Rohiṇī nakṣatra on Monday creates a portal where whatever is begun meets with completion.

Vara: Somavār (Monday) — Moon's day, the day of mind and reflection. On Monday, Śiva drank the poison of the world to save it.

Pakṣa: Śukla Pakṣa — The bright fortnight, the time of building and increase. As the moon waxes toward fullness, so does understanding grow toward illumination.

Today's Application

On this Śukla Pañcamī, as the energy of learning intensifies, we receive the Gītā's opening teaching. The question is not whether we will face loss — we will. The question is whether we will meet it as paṇḍitas or as those who lament unnecessarily.

Today's Sarvārtha Siddhi yoga supports this work. As the nakṣatra shifts from Kṛttikā's purifying fire to Rohiṇī's nurturing abundance, we are reminded that wisdom is not harsh. The fire of discrimination burns away illusion not to destroy us but to reveal what cannot be burned.

Practice today: When grief or attachment arises, observe it without becoming it. Note the vṛtti as the paṇḍita would — a passing modification, not the self. This is the beginning of yoga.
