Bhagavad Gita Chapter 8, Verse 8

अभ्यासयोगयुक्तेन चेतसा नान्यगामिना । परमं पुरुषं दिव्यं याति पार्थानुचिन्तयन् ॥ ८ ॥
abhyāsa-yoga-yuktena
cetasā nānya-gāminā
paramaṁ puruṣaṁ divyaṁ
yāti pārthānucintayan
He who meditates on Me as the Supreme Personality of Godhead, his mind constantly engaged in remembering Me, undeviated from the path, he, O Pārtha, is sure to reach Me.
In this verse Lord Kṛṣṇa stresses the importance of remembering Him. One’s memory of Kṛṣṇa is revived by chanting the mahā-mantra, Hare Kṛṣṇa. By this practice of chanting and hearing the sound vibration of the Supreme Lord, one’s ear, tongue and mind are engaged. This mystic meditation is very easy to practice, and it helps one attain the Supreme Lord. Puruṣam means enjoyer. Although living entities belong to the marginal energy of the Supreme Lord, they are in material contamination. They think themselves enjoyers, but they are not the supreme enjoyer. Here it is clearly stated that the supreme enjoyer is the Supreme Personality of Godhead in His different manifestations and plenary expansions as Nārāyaṇa, Vāsudeva, etc. The devotee can constantly think of the object of worship, the Supreme Lord, in any of His features – Nārāyaṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Rāma, etc. – by chanting Hare Kṛṣṇa. This practice will purify him, and at the end of his life, due to his constant chanting, he will be transferred to the kingdom of God. Yoga practice is meditation on the Supersoul within; similarly, by chanting Hare Kṛṣṇa one fixes his mind always on the Supreme Lord. The mind is fickle, and therefore it is necessary to engage the mind by force to think of Kṛṣṇa. One example often given is that of the caterpillar that thinks of becoming a butterfly and so is transformed into a butterfly in the same life. Similarly, if we constantly think of Kṛṣṇa, it is certain that at the end of our lives we shall have the same bodily constitution as Kṛṣṇa.

Cosmic Interpretation

The tithi begins as Sukla Paksha Dashami (until 08:42 AM), the tenth tithi of the waxing moon associated with righteousness and the completion of virtuous acts. As Dashami yields to Ekadashi at 08:42 AM, the energy shifts to the eleventh tithi, traditionally devoted to fasting, purification, and heightened devotion to Lord Vishnu. The nakṣatra Swati, ruled by Vāyu (the wind god), governs the day, symbolizing independence, self‑motivation, and the ability to move freely like the wind—qualities essential for sustained, undeviating remembrance. The yoga Siva (meaning “auspicious” or “benign”) prevails until 01:23 AM, representing Lord Shiva’s benevolent grace that removes obstacles and bestows prosperity; it then transitions into Siddha (perfection, accomplishment), signifying the fulfillment of spiritual practice. Karana Garija (until 08:42 AM) supports stable, productive activity, while Vanija (until 09:38 PM) favors trade and commerce, and Vishti (after 09:38 PM) is considered inauspicious for new endeavors.

BG 8.8 resonates beautifully with this celestial tapestry: “He who meditates on Me as the Supreme Personality of Godhead, his mind constantly engaged in remembering Me, undeviated from the path, he, O Pārtha, is sure to reach Me.” Just as Dashami’s energy of righteous completion (through virtuous action) gives way to Ekadashi’s devotion and purification, and as Swati’s independent, wind‑like movement carries the practitioner forward—so too does the devotee, by employing constant, undeviating remembrance, navigate the auspicious grace of Siva and enter the perfection of Siddha. The declaration that the steady meditator “is sure to reach Me” mirrors the ease with which a well‑aligned system achieves its goal when guided by unwavering focus and unobstructed flow. The “constant engagement in remembering” echoes the unbroken flow of a kernel module polling a device without missing an interrupt, while the “undeviated from the path” aspect reflects Swati’s single‑minded direction and Dashami’s adherence to righteousness. Finally, Siddha’s energy of perfection affirms that such sustained, unhindered remembrance culminates in the attainment of the Supreme—just as a process that runs without error reaches its intended state.

In the language of the mind’s inner workings, this verse instructs us to maintain an unbroken flow of attention toward the divine object, much like a real‑time scheduler that prioritizes a critical task without yielding. The Nakshatra Swati’s airy independence reminds us that remembrance must be self‑motivated, not dependent on external stimulation. The Yoga Siva’s recognition of auspicious grace urges us to align our will with the divine, allowing the lord of benevolence to dissolve the karmic blockages that bind us to the cycle of birth and death. The subsequent Siddha yoga assures us that when we maintain this undeviating focus, the result is spiritual perfection and the ultimate reward: union with the Supreme.

Connections & Key Concepts

Yoga Sutra Connection

YS I.12: abhyāsair vairāgyābhyāṁ tan‑nirodhaḥ
The mental modifications are restrained by practice and detachment.

YS I.23: īśvara praṇidhānād vā
Or, [the state of samādhi] is attainable through devotion to the Lord.

Connection: BG 8.8’s call for constant remembrance (abhyāsa) and an undeviated mind (anya‑gāminā) finds direct support in Yoga Sutra I.12, which teaches that the vṛttis (mind‑fluctuations) are stilled through abhyāsa (consistent practice) and vairāgya (detachment from sense objects). The “undeviated” aspect is the abhyāsā—returning again and again to the point of focus like a process that never leaves its polling loop. The “constant engagement in remembering” reflects the bhakti‑like surrender of personal desire to the divine, akin to setting the process’s niceness to lowest priority so that only the divine service runs with full CPU affinity. Furthermore, Yoga Sutra I.23 affirms that devotion to Īśvara is a direct route to the samādhi state, echoing the verse’s promise that such remembrance makes the Supreme “sure to reach.” Together, these sutras outline the same two‑pronged approach: steady, disciplined focus on the divine object, coupled with the offering of one’s actions to that object.
