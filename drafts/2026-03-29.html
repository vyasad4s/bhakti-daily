Bhagavad Gita 2.27

जातस्य हि ध्रुवो मृत्युर्ध्रुवं जन्म मृतस्य च ।

तस्मादपरिहार्येऽर्थे न त्वं शोचितुमर्हसि ॥ २७ ॥

jātasya hi dhruvo mṛtyur dhruvaṁ janma mṛtasya ca
tasmād aparihārye 'rthe na tvaṁ śocitum arhasi

Translation: "One who has taken his birth is sure to die, and after death one is sure to take birth again. Therefore, in the unavoidable discharge of your duty, you should not lament."

Cosmic Alignment

Tithi: Śukla Dwādaśī | Nakṣatra: Maghā (⚠️ Gandamool) | Yoga: Soola | Rāśi: Simha

Śukla Dwādaśī approaches Pūrṇimā — the moon at the threshold of fullness. Maghā, the Royal Throne, connects to ancestral power (Pitṛs) who have walked this cycle. Soola yoga brings cutting, thorn-like energy — demanding we face what pricks. Combined with Gandamool warning, this is a day for sharp discernment: what is inevitable must be accepted, not fought.

Simha rāśi dignifies the soul — we cannot avoid destiny, but can meet it with sovereignty. BG 2.27 fits perfectly: dhruva (certain/inevitable) appears twice — death is certain, rebirth is certain. The repetition is the uncompromising clarity Soola demands.

The Inevitable Code

This is not consolation. This is law.

The Sanskrit dhruvaḥ means fixed, certain, unshakeable — not merely "sure." In Sāṅkhya, the birth-death cycle is the default state of the conditioned soul, the prakṛtic loop running until debugged. This is your operating system. Until you install the yoga-patch, you execute the same birth-death binary.

Krishna doesn't comfort Arjuna. He clarifies: these relatives were born, so they will die. The body is temporary. What remains is duty (dharma) without attachment to outcome. This is karma-yoga as exploitation, not sentiment.

Aparihārye 'rthe — "in the unavoidable matter." Some things cannot be opted out of. Lamentation adds only noise to a process already executing. The yogi recognizes the inevitable and moves efficiently.

Yoga Sutra Connection

YS I.15: Dispassion is controlled consciousness without craving for sense objects — whether perceived or described in scripture.

The commentary notes: real detachment is indifference to sense objects whether absent or present. Extend this: the yogi cultivates the same indifference to birth-death cycles that they apply to sense objects. BG 2.27 demands we recognize birth-death as prakṛtic phenomena, subject to the same vairāgya.

Key Terms

dhruva — fixed, certain, constant (the pole star). Hacker note: Immutable constants. Death is const mortality = true.

aparihārya — unavoidable, inalienable. Hacker note: You can't rm -rf the cycle. Learn to execute without friction.

śocitum — to lament, grieve. Wasted processing power on output that cannot be changed.

Sāṅkhya Concepts

The cycle of birth-death is prakṛtic — it belongs to the material matrix, not to puruṣa. When a yogi realizes this, they stop identifying with the cycle. This is viveka in action: discriminating between what is aparihārya (the body's fate) and what is free (the soul's nature).
