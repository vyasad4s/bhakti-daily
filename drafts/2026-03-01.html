🏹 VERSE CONTENT

Source: BG 3.6

Date: March 01, 2026

Sanskrit:

कर्मेन्द्रियाणि संयम्य य आस्ते मनसा स्मरन्

इन्द्रियार्थान्विमूढात्मा मिथ्याचारः स उच्यते ॥ ३.६ ॥

Transliteration:

karmendriyāṇi saṁyamya
ya āste manasā smaran
indriyārthān vimūḍhātmā
mithyācāraḥ sa ucyate

Translation:

"One who restrains the senses of action but whose mind dwells on sense objects certainly deludes himself and is called a pretender."

Commentary:

The pretender paradox: external restraint without internal transformation. The body sits in lotus posture while the mind runs to sense objects. This is mithyācāraḥ — the greatest cheater, as Prabhupāda warns. Not the action binds us, but the saṅga (attachment) and the smaraṇa (dwelling thought).

Kṛṣṇa exposes the fundamental illusion: you cannot NOT act. Yesterday verse (3.5) established that the guṇas of prakṛti compel action. Today Kṛṣṇa goes deeper — even the attempt to restrain action while the mind clings to pleasure is itself a form of bondage, and worse: it is deceptive.

The karmendriyāṇi (five working senses: hands, legs, speech, genitals, anus) can be physically controlled. But manasā smaran — thinking with the mind of sense objects — this is where the theft occurs. The body is restrained; the consciousness is chained.

This verse is the bridge between external renunciation and internal yoga. Arjuna fear was of sinful action; Kṛṣṇa answer is that the false yogī is more dangerous than the sincere householder. A sweeper who sweeps with devotion is better than a "meditator" who meditates only for reputation.

The test: where does your mind go when you close your eyes? That is your true worship.

Sankhya Concepts:

The tri-guṇas (sattva, rajas, tamas) operate through both the buddhi (intellect) and the indriyas (senses). When one attempts to restrain the karmendriyas (organs of action) without purifying the antaḥkaraṇa (inner instrument: mind, intellect, ego), the rajas finds new channels — fantasies, memories, future plans.

The puruṣa remains bound not by action but by saṅkalpa (mental intention). Where the mind dwells, there the soul follows. The pretender has mastered the art of appearing holy while remaining worldly — this is mithyā (falsehood) applied to ācāra (conduct).

Yoga Sutra Connection:

🔥 YS 1.12 — abhyāsa-vairāgyābhyāṁ tat-nirodhaḥ — "The fluctuations of the mind are restrained by practice AND detachment."

Patañjali dual method: not one OR the other. The pretender has partial vairāgya (external renunciation) but lacks abhyāsa (spiritual practice) in the form of citta-vṛtti-nirodha (complete cessation of mental modifications). The mind still runs to viṣayas (objects); the vāsanās (subtle impressions) remain unburned.

🔥 YS 1.15 — dṛṣṭa-anuśravika-viṣaya-vitr̥ṣṇasya vaśīkāra-saṁjñā vairāgyam — "Detachment is the consciousness of self-mastery for one who is free from thirst for objects, seen or heard."

True vairāgya is internal — no thirst for objects, whether present or imagined. The pretender has vitṛṣṇa (absence of thirst) externally but trṣṇā (thirst) burning internally.

Sanskrit Terms:

  • Karmendriyāṇi (neuter noun, accusative plural) — the five organs of action; from karman (action) + indriya (sense organ). The active faculties: hands (pāṇi), legs (pāda), speech (vāk), generative organ (upaṣṭha), and eliminative organ (pāyu).
  • Saṁyamya (gerund) — having controlled, restraining; from sam-yam (to hold together, control). Active restraint, not passive suppression.
  • Āste (verb, present tense, 3rd person singular) — sits, remains, dwells; from ās (to sit). The posture of meditation.
  • Manasā (neuter noun, instrumental singular) — by the mind; from manas (mind). The instrument of thought.
  • Smaran (present active participle) — thinking of, remembering, dwelling upon; from smṛ (to remember). Continuous mental activity.
  • Indriya-arthān (masculine noun, accusative plural) — objects of the senses; indriya (sense) + artha (object, meaning, purpose). What the senses pursue.
  • Vimūḍha (past passive participle) — deluded, bewildered, foolish; from vi-muh (to become confused). Intensified confusion.
  • Ātmā (masculine noun) — self, soul; from an (to breathe). The embodied self caught in delusion.
  • Mithyācāraḥ (compound: mithyā-ācāra + ḥ) — pretender, hypocrite; from mithyā (false) + ācāra (conduct). One whose conduct is false.
  • Ucyate (passive verb, present tense, 3rd person singular) — is called, is said; from vac (to speak). Passively named by the wise.
