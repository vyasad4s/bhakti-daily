Bhagavad Gita Chapter 8, Verse 15

मामुपेत्य पुनर्जन्म दु:खालयमशाश्वतम् । नाप्नुवन्ति महात्मान: संसिद्धिं परमां गता: ॥ १५ ॥
mām upetya punar janma
duḥkhālayam aśāśvatam
nāpnuvanti mahātmānaḥ
saṁsiddhiṁ paramāṁ gatāḥ
After attaining Me, the great souls, who are yogīs in devotion, never return to this temporary world, which is full of miseries, because they have attained the highest perfection.
Since this temporary material world is full of the miseries of birth, old age, disease and death, naturally he who achieves the highest perfection and attains the supreme planet, Kṛṣṇaloka, Goloka Vṛndāvana, does not wish to return. The supreme planet is described in Vedic literature as avyakta and akṣara and paramā gati; in other words, that planet is beyond our material vision, and it is inexplicable, but it is the highest goal, the destination for the mahātmās (great souls). The mahātmās receive transcendental messages from the realized devotees and thus gradually develop devotional service in Kṛṣṇa consciousness and become so absorbed in transcendental service that they no longer desire elevation to any of the material planets, nor do they even want to be transferred to any spiritual planet. They only want Kṛṣṇa and Kṛṣṇa’s association, and nothing else. That is the highest perfection of life. This verse specifically mentions the personalist devotees of the Supreme Lord, Kṛṣṇa. These devotees in Kṛṣṇa consciousness achieve the highest perfection of life. In other words, they are the supreme souls.

Cosmic Interpretation

The tithi has transitioned to Sukla Paksha Navami (after 06:10 AM), the ninth tithi of the waxing moon associated with completion, fulfillment, and the fruition of efforts. Navami’s energy of bringing things to culmination combines with the nakṣatra Hasta, ruled by Savitar (the sun god), which governs from 12:52 AM on June 22, representing skill, ability, and healing through enlightened action. As Hasta yields to Chitra (ruled by Tvashtar, the divine architect) at 02:23 AM on June 23, the energy continues the theme of craftsmanship, precision, and divine arrangement. The yoga Variyan (auspicious, favorable) prevails until 12:43 AM on June 22, signifying smooth progress and positive outcomes, after which Parigha (obstacle, barrier) brings challenges that test resolve and determination.

BG 8.15 beautifully aligns with this cosmic rhythm: “After attaining Me, the great souls, who are yogīs in devotion, never return to this temporary world, which is full of miseries, because they have attained the highest perfection.” Just as Navami’s energy of completion and fulfillment gives way to the skill and precision of Hasta and the divine craftsmanship of Chitra—so too does the devotee achieve the highest perfection through attaining the Supreme, never to return to the cycle of birth and death. Variyan’s auspicious, favorable energy supports steady progress toward the goal, while Parigha’s obstacle energy represents the temporary challenges of the material world that the great souls transcend through their devotion.

The verse invites us to understand that true perfection lies in attaining Krishna’s abode, where the miseries of birth, old age, disease and death are forever left behind. In the language of the energy centers, this is the realization that completion (Navami) flows from divine union, skill (Hasta) is perfected in devotion to the Divine Architect (Chitra), and favorable conditions (Variyan) support the journey while obstacles (Parigha) are overcome by fixed determination to reach the eternal goal. Thus the devotee transforms the temporary nature of the material world into a stepping stone toward the eternal, where Krishna’s association is the only desire worth having.

Connections & Key Concepts

Yoga Sutra Connection

YS 1.23: īśvara-praṇidhānāt va

Connection: BG 8.15 emphasizes that after attaining Krishna, the great souls never return to the temporary world full of miseries. Yoga Sutra 1.23 states that samādhi (perfect absorption) can be attained through special devotion to the Lord (īśvara-praṇidhāna). Both point to the same essential practice: exclusive focus on the Divine leads to liberation from the cycle of birth and death. The verse’s “after attaining Me” (mām upetya) is the practical result of īśvara-praṇidhāna, where the mind’s ceaseless flow is directed toward Krishna, resulting in the attainment of the highest perfection (saṁsiddhiṁ paramāṁ gatāḥ). Thus the sutra provides the methodological foundation for the verse’s promise of permanent liberation.
