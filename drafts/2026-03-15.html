🏹 VERSE CONTENT

Source: BG 2.65

Date: March 15, 2026

Sanskrit:

प्रसादे सर्वदुःखानां हानिरस्योपजायते ।

प्रसन्नचेतसो ह्याशु बुद्धिः पर्यवतिष्ठते ॥ ६५ ॥

Transliteration:

prasāde sarva-duḥkhānāṁ hānir asyopajāyate
prasanna-cetaso hy āśu buddhiḥ paryavatiṣṭhate

Translation:

For one thus satisfied [in Kṛṣṇa consciousness], the threefold miseries of material existence no longer exist. In such a satisfied consciousness, one's intelligence is soon fully established.

Commentary:

This is the fruit. BG 2.62-63 traced the inverted tree of saṁsāra; BG 2.64 gave the counter-sequence through rāga-dveṣa-vimukta; and now 2.65 reveals what grows when that counter-sequence bears fruit. Not mere absence of suffering, but prasāda—settled grace, divine satisfaction, the consciousness that has found its object and therefore rests.

The word prasāda is critical here, and it is not the vague "inner peace" of modern spirituality. In Sāṅkhya metaphysics, prasāda is the clarity that comes when buddhi, purified of rajas and tamas, reflects puruṣa's consciousness without distortion. It is the settled state of intelligence that has discriminated itself from prakṛti's gunas and therefore rests in its own nature. This is not something achieved by effort; it is what remains when effort-as-grasping ceases.

Sarva-duḥkhānāṁ hāniḥ—the destruction of all sufferings. This is the threefold misery: ādhyātmika (from one's own mind and body), ādhibhautika (from other beings), and ādhidaivika (from the gods/natural forces). These are not philosophical abstractions; they are the actual conditions of material existence. But the devotee established in prasāda finds these miseries uprooted—not by changing circumstances but by changing the perceiver. The root of suffering is not the world but the misidentification with it.

Prasanna-cetasaḥ—one whose consciousness is satisfied, pleased, clear as a still lake. From this clarity, buddhiḥ paryavatiṣṭhate—intelligence is fully established, stabilized, stands firm. This is the same buddhi that was destroyed (nāśa) in BG 2.63; now it is established (paryavatiṣṭhate) in its true function. The Dwādaśī energy supports this: the completion of the Ekādaśī fast brings the body-field into alignment, making prasāda possible.

Yoga Sutra Connection:

🔥 YS I.20 — श्रद्धावीर्यस्मृतिसमाधिप्रज्ञापूर्वक इतरेषाम्
"For others [who do not have direct vision of the divine], [samādhi is preceded by] śraddhā (faith), vīrya (vigor), smṛti (memory of the goal), and prajñā (wisdom)."

The devotee established in prasāda manifests the qualities Patañjali describes as prerequisites for samādhi. Śraddhā—faith that is not blind but based on evidence and experience; vīrya—vigor that is effortless because aligned with truth; smṛti—constant remembrance of the goal; and prajñā—the discriminative wisdom that buddhi was designed to deliver. BG 2.65 describes the result; YS I.20 describes the path that leads there. The settled buddhi of prasāda is the platform from which samādhi becomes possible.

Sanskrit Terms:

  • prasāda (m., locative) — settled grace, divine satisfaction, clarity of consciousness; from pra (forth) + sād (to accomplish); the state of being thoroughly accomplished, established in one's true nature
  • hāniḥ (f., nominative) — destruction, loss, cessation; the uprooting of suffering at its root
  • prasanna-cetasaḥ (genitive sing.) — of one whose consciousness is satisfied/clear; prasanna (pleased, satisfied) + cetas (consciousness); the clear lake of mind reflecting reality accurately
  • āśu (adv.) — soon, quickly, immediately; speed of establishment when conditions are right
  • buddhiḥ (f., nominative) — intelligence, discriminative capacity; the faculty that distinguishes puruṣa from prakṛti
  • paryavatiṣṭhate (3rd sing., present middle) — stands firm, is established, becomes fixed; pari (around/fully) + ava-sthā (to stand); complete establishment in true function
