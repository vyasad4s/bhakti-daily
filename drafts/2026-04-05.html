यज्ञार्थात्कर्मणोऽन्यत्र लोकोऽयं कर्मबन्धनः ।
तदर्थं कर्म कौन्तेय मुक्तसङ्गः समाचर ॥ ९ ॥

yajñārthāt karmaṇo 'nyatra loko 'yaṁ karma-bandhanaḥ
tat-arthaṁ karma kaunteya mukta-saṅgaḥ samācara

yajña-arthāt — done only for the sake of sacrifice/Viṣṇu; from the root meaning to give, to share, to unite; the merger of action with divine purpose

karma-bandhanaḥ — bondage by work; the state where action becomes chains rather than wings; when output traps rather than liberates

mukta-saṅgaḥ — liberated from association/attachment; the prerequisite for skillful action; clean hands, clear mind

samācara — perform perfectly, execute completely; from sam- (complete) + ācar (to proceed, to practice); total immersion in the act

kaunteya — O son of Kuntī; Arjuna's matronym, invoking his lineage of duty and resolve

"Work done as a sacrifice for Viṣṇu has to be performed; otherwise work causes bondage in this material world. Therefore, O son of Kuntī, perform your prescribed duties for His satisfaction, and in that way you will always remain free from bondage."

Today's Cosmic Context

Sankashti Chaturthi — The fourth day of the waning moon dedicated to Lord Gaṇeśa, remover of obstacles. This tithi asks: what blocks your action? What internal resistance prevents the work that must be done?

Viśākhā → Anurādhā Transition — The moon moves from the "forked branch" (duality, scattered energy) into "subsequent success" (devotion, aligned will). This is the cosmic signature for today's teaching: move from conflicted action to consecrated action.

Siddhi Yoga — The yoga of accomplishment, success, attainment. Not the ego's achievement, but the fruition that comes when action is yoked to purpose beyond the personal.

Kṛṣṇa does not mince words. This is the third chapter, the chapter of Karma Yoga, and here is its beating heart: Work binds. Except when it doesn't. The pivot point is not the work itself but the arthāt — the motive force, the "for-the-sake-of." When action is performed for Viṣṇu — for the whole, for the sacrifice, for something greater than the ego's hunger — it becomes liberation instead of bondage.

This is not a loophole. This is not spiritual bypassing dressed in Sanskrit. The purport is explicit: "Any other work done in this material world will be a cause of bondage, for both good and evil work have their reactions." Both. Good work and evil work. The charitable donation and the embezzlement. The selfless volunteer and the cutthroat executive. If done for the ego's expansion — for recognition, for status, for the subtle hit of "I am good" — both produce bondage. The chains look different, but they bind the same.

So what does it mean to work for Viṣṇu? The root of yajña is the same as the root of yoga — yuj, to unite. To yoke. To bring disparate elements into harmony. When work becomes yajña-arthāt, it is no longer the isolated individual scratching at the world for survival and validation. It becomes participation in a larger process. The sacrificer, the sacrifice, and the receiver of sacrifice become one continuous flow. The doer, the doing, and the done-for merge.

Today's nakṣatra transition embodies this teaching. Viśākhā — the forked branch, the two paths, the divided mind that wants this and that, that oscillates between options without commitment. This is the state of karma-bandhana: bound by work because bound by desire, pulled in conflicting directions, generating action that entangles rather than liberates. Then the transition: Anurādhā — the "subsequent success," the star of devotion, of friendship, of achieving through alignment rather than force. This is mukta-saṅgaḥ samācara: perform perfectly, but perform free.

Yoga Sūtra Connection

YS 2.1 — tapah svādhyāyeśvarapraṇidhānāni kriyā-yogaḥ

Patañjali defines kriyā-yoga — the yoga of action — as threefold: tapas (discipline, the burning heat of practice), svādhyāya (self-study, the recollection of teachings), and īśvara-praṇidhāna (surrender to the Divine). This is the method for mukta-saṅgaḥ samācara. You cannot simply decide to be free from attachment. You cultivate freedom through disciplined practice, through constant remembrance of the teaching, and through the willingness to offer the fruit of action to something larger than the self.

The sequence matters. Tapas burns away the dross of distraction. Svādhyāya keeps the goal in view. And īśvara-praṇidhāna — surrender to the Lord — is what transforms work from bondage to liberation. This is not passivity. This is samācara: perfect performance, complete engagement, total effort — but effort offered rather than hoarded.

Practice

Before your next action — the next email, the next meeting, the next meal — pause. Ask: If this is a sacrifice, what is being offered? To what? Not to perform like a pious fraud, but to discover the thread of yajña running through the mundane. The sankashti energy of today's tithi supports this introspection: remove the obstacle of habitual, ego-driven action. Enter the anurādhā flow of consecrated doing.

This is the beginning of Karma Yoga proper. Not abandoning action, but redeeming it. Not fleeing the world, but transforming it from within. The work that must be done becomes the path itself.

Why This Verse Today

BG 3.9 inaugurates the Karma Yoga chapter with its essential principle: the transformation of action through redirected motive. Selected for Viśākhā → Anurādhā transition (duality to devotion) and Siddhi Yoga (accomplishment through alignment). The Sankashti Chaturthi energy of obstacle-removal supports the teaching: remove the binding quality from your work through conscious offering.
