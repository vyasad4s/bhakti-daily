>>> COSMIC INTERPRETATION <<<

Caturdaśī is the tithi of maximum charge before completion — the fourteenth day of the waxing fortnight, the eve of Pūrṇimā. The moon is nearly full, its light almost unobscured, but the last sliver of shadow remains. This is the cosmic energy of the threshold itself — not the safety of the shore, not the security of the far bank, but the moment of being almost there. BG 6.37-40 lives in this exact space. Arjuna's doubt is a Caturdaśī doubt: "I started, I was almost there, but the mind wandered — am I now lost?" The near-full moon mirrors the near-complete yogī. The shadow that remains is the fear of having fallen short.

Viśākhā, "the forked one," is the nakṣatra of the crossroads — two branches diverging, the choice point between paths. Its deity is Indrāgnī, the combined fire of Indra (king of the devas) and Agni (sacred fire itself). This is the nakṣatra of purpose divided, of energy that could go either way. Arjuna's ubhaya-vibhraṣṭa — "fallen from both" — is the Viśākhā fear: that by choosing the spiritual path and failing, you lose access to the material one as well. But the nakṣatra transitions to Anurādhā, ruled by Mitra — the divine friend, the ally, the one who upholds contracts and guarantees. Kṛṣṇa's answer — tāta — is Mitra's answer: "I am your friend, and I guarantee your effort is preserved." The Viśākhā doubt is resolved by Anurādhā's assurance.

Parigha yoga means "crossbar" or "door-bar" — the instrument that either blocks or secures. The failed yogī feels the parigha has fallen: the door to spiritual success is shut, and the door to material life is also shut. But the yoga transitions to Śiva — "auspiciousness" — in the evening. The parigha lifts. The bar that seemed to trap becomes the bar that protects what you've already built. Śiva yoga says: your effort is auspicious. It cannot be destroyed.

The Moon in Tula (Libra) seeks balance, equilibrium, the midpoint — the exact state the failed yogī fears they've lost. After 9:08 PM, the Moon enters Vṛścika (Scorpio), the sign of hidden reserves, transformation, and the power that emerges from apparent destruction. The riven cloud doesn't vanish — its moisture enters the deep. The failed yogī's effort enters Vṛścika's reservoir.

>>> SĀṄKHYA LENS <<<

Buddhi as the preservation mechanism: In Sāṅkhya, buddhi is the highest evolute of prakṛti — the discriminative faculty that can reflect puruṣa's light or distort it through rāga. When buddhi is purified by practice, it records the impression of puruṣa with such depth that the impression cannot be erased by death. This is what the Gītā calls buddhi-saṁyoga — the "linking of intelligence" from one life to the next. In Sāṅkhya terms, the sanskāra (subliminal impression) created by sincere practice persists across the dissolution of the physical body because it is encoded in the subtle body (liṅga-śarīra), not the gross. The failed yogī's buddhi carries the signature of its own purification — and that signature survives reincarnation.

Īśvara as the transcendent preserver: YS I.24 defines Īśvara as puruṣa-viśeṣa — a special puruṣa, untouched by kleśa, karma, or subconscious predisposition. This is the metaphysical basis for Kṛṣṇa's guarantee. The failed yogī's effort doesn't vanish because it was never stored in the yogī alone — it was registered in Īśvara's awareness. Īśvara, being unlimited by time (YS I.26), holds the record across all timelines. The kalyāṇa-kṛt has an eternal witness.

The guṇas never destroy sattvic investment: In Sāṅkhya, the three guṇas continuously transform — but transformation is not annihilation. Sattvic impressions, once formed, become the foundation for future sattvic states. Rajas may scatter the mind temporarily, tamas may obscure the vision — but neither can delete the sanskāra of spiritual practice. The data persists. The checksum validates on reboot.
