 >>> COSMIC INTERPRETATION <<<

Kṛṣṇa Dashami transitions to Ekādaśī at 3:28 PM — the day splits in two: the first half under Dashami's energy of completion and fulfillment, the second half under Ekādaśī's call to fasting, discipline, and spiritual focus. This split mirrors BG 7.13 itself: the world operates under the three modes (Dashami — ordinary activity, completion of material business), while those who are awake recognize the transcendental Lord beyond the modes (Ekādaśī — the shift from material engagement to spiritual observance).

Revati nakshatra, ruled by Pushan (the nourisher, the guide of souls), holds through most of the day — a fitting energy for a verse about delusion, because Pushan is the deity who guides the departed soul along the path to the realm of light. Revati is the last nakshatra, the end of the zodiacal journey — and BG 7.13 speaks of the one who is beyond the end, beyond the modes, param avyayam (the inexhaustible Supreme). The transition to Ashwini at 10:46 PM returns to the beginning of the zodiac, completing a cosmic cycle in a single day: from the last (Revati) to the first (Ashwini), from delusion to awakening, from the end of the journey to its fresh start.

The Moon in Meena (Pisces) until 10:46 PM then shifting to Mesha (Aries) reinforces this: Meena is the ocean of dissolution where all boundaries dissolve into moha (delusion), while Mesha is the fire of new beginnings, the spark of individuality that burns through illusion. The double Gandamool period (Revati + Ashwini) across the day signals a need for careful navigation — both nakshatras are Gandamool, traditionally requiring remedial measures. This aligns with BG 7.13's warning: the three modes entangle the entire world in delusion, and only by going beyond them can one know the Supreme. Rāhu Kāla (12:55–2:46 PM), Yamaganda (7:21–9:12 AM), and Gulika (11:04 AM–12:55 PM) create three overlapping inauspicious windows during the daylight hours, supporting the verse's theme: the material world is thick with obstacles and delusive forces. The Amrit Kaal (8:28–10:00 PM) in the evening offers the auspicious window for spiritual practice after Ekādaśī has begun. No Abhijit Muhurta today — the universe offers no midday reprieve, reinforcing that transcendence of the modes cannot be achieved through auspicious timing alone but only through knowledge of the Lord who is beyond the modes.

 >>> SĀṄKHYA LENS <<<

The Three Guṇas and the Transcendental Lord: The three modes (sattva, rajas, tamas) are modifications of prakṛti that bind the jīva to the cycle of birth and death. The Lord, as pure puruṣa, is beyond these modes. When the jīva mistakes itself for the modes, it is deluded (mohitam). Liberation arises when the jīva recognizes its true nature as the witness of the modes, not the modes themselves.

Inner mechanism: The modes are prakṛti's operating system — sattva (luminosity/prakāśa), rajas (activity/kriyā), and tamas (inertia/sthiti). The jīva, conditioned by these modes, perceives only the surface of reality. The Lord is the kernel beneath the operating system, the changeless witness of all change. When the jīva turns its attention inward, beyond the modes, it experiences the Lord as param avyayam — beyond and inexhaustible.

Optional terminal metaphor: The three modes are like the three permission bits (read, write, execute) that govern every file in the cosmic filesystem. The Lord is the root user who owns the filesystem and is not subject to any permission bits. Just as a user confined to an application layer cannot see the kernel, the jīva confined to the modes cannot see the Lord.
