 >>> COSMIC INTERPRETATION <<<

Amāvāsyā — the dark moon. The lunar disk has vanished, consumed by the same dissolving force that BG 9.7 describes at cosmic scale. This is Bhaumvati Amavasya, the new moon falling on Maṅgalavāra (Tuesday), amplifying the fire of transformation. Maṅgala (Mars) rules confrontation, courage, and the willingness to face what is uncomfortable — and on Amāvāsyā, this fire turns inward, burning away the false identifications that cling to form and continuity.

The nakṣatra Punarvasu — "returning light" — dominates until mid-afternoon. Its deity is Aditi, the cosmic mother, the unbounded. Its symbol is a quiver of arrows: potential energy stored, waiting to be released. This is the precise energetics of dissolution-as-potential. Then Pushya arrives — the most auspicious of nakṣatras, ruled by Bṛhaspati, the guru of the gods. Its symbol is the udder of a cow — nourishment, sustenance, the divine providing for what has been newly created. The cycle is written in the stars: dissolution (Punarvasu) → re-manifestation (Pushya).

The yoga Harshana ("joy-giving") reinforces the message: after dissolution comes the subtle joy of renewal, not grief for what was lost. The late-evening transition to Vajra yoga adds thunderbolt clarity — the flash of understanding that shatters all illusion of permanence. Auspicious windows include Brahma Muhūrta (04:07–04:55 AM) for pre-dawn sadhana and Abhijit Muhūrta (12:32–01:31 PM) for midday practice.

 >>> VERSE SELECTION RATIONALE <<<

[BG 9.7] Alignment: This verse directly describes the cosmic cycle of dissolution and re-creation — all beings return to prakṛti at kalpa-kṣaya, and the Lord re-projects them at kalpa-ādau. Amāvāsyā is the lunar mirror of this cosmic truth: the moon vanishes, but it does not cease to exist. It returns. Punarvasu nakṣatra ("returning light") encapsulates the verse's core teaching — dissolution is not annihilation but the storage of potential before re-manifestation. Pushya (the nourisher) arriving in the afternoon mirrors the Lord's promise to re-create: the divine providing sustenance for what has been newly manifested.

Maṅgalavāra's fire (Bhaumvati Amavasya) provides the intensity needed to confront the impermanence of all material forms — the very teaching at the heart of BG 9.7. The YS 3.9-3.12 connection (nirodha-pariṇāma) extends this from cosmic to personal practice: the yogi who masters the dissolution of outgoing saṁskāras participates consciously in the same rhythm.

 >>> SĀṄKHYA LENS <<<

[Prakṛti's cyclical rest and activation]: In Sāṅkhya metaphysics, prakṛti exists in two states: pralaya (dissolution/rest) and sṛṣṭi (creation/activation). During pralaya, the three guṇas (sattva, rajas, tamas) are in perfect equilibrium — no manifestation occurs. When the equilibrium is disturbed by the proximity of puruṣa, the guṇas begin to interact, and mahat (cosmic intelligence) emerges, followed by ahaṅkāra (ego), then the subtle and gross elements. BG 9.7 describes this at the Lord's will — prakṛti is His energy (māmikām), and He initiates the cycle.

[Inner mechanism]: The Amāvāsyā field mirrors pralaya in miniature. Just as prakṛti rests in equilibrium before re-manifestation, the practitioner's mind can find its own state of rest — not empty, but potent. The outgoing saṁskāras (vyutthāna) dissolve, and if one remains alert rather than dull, the nirodha-saṁskāra arises — the seed of cessation. Tuesday's fire provides the tapas (discipline) to burn through tamasic dullness and remain conscious in the void.

[Terminal metaphor]: Think of kalpa-kṣaya as a system shutdown — all processes return to disk, uncompiled, latent. The Divine runs system boot at kalpa-ādau. Your saṁskāras are the commit history — they persist across reboots. The question is not whether the system restarts (it will), but what you've committed.
