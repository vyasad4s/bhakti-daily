 >>> COSMIC INTERPRETATION <<<

Shashthi Tithi (the sixth lunar day) is dedicated to Kartikeya, the commander of the divine forces, symbolizing the victory of discrimination over ignorance. This aligns with BG 9.2’s declaration of bhakti knowledge as the “king of education” (rāja-vidyā) — the sovereign intellect that defeats the armies of illusion. The nakshatra Shatabhisha (“the hundred healers”) ruled by Varuna governs healing, concealment, and revelation — fitting for the “most secret of all secrets” (rāja-guhyam) that heals the soul’s forgetfulness. The transition to Purva Bhadrapada (the “former auspicious feet”) brings the energy of preparation and purification, like clearing the ground for the planting of everlasting knowledge.

Yoga Ayushman (“life-giving”) until mid-morning supports vitality for study and practice, while Saubhagya (“good fortune”) later brings auspiciousness for devotional service. Sunday (Raviwar), ruled by the Sun, illuminates the intellect — the perfect day to realize that the self is not the body but the eternal servant of Krishna, the source of all light. The Moon’s transit from Kumbha to Meena moves from collective awareness to individual intuition, mirroring the shift from intellectual understanding to direct perception (pratyakṣāvagamaṁ).

 >>> VERSE SELECTION RATIONALE <<<

[BG 9.2] Alignment: The Shashthi Tithi’s warrior energy (Kartikeya) matches the verse’s claim that this knowledge is the “king of education” — sovereign over all other disciplines. Shatabhisha’s healing and concealment reveal why this knowledge is the “most secret”: it is not revealed to the proud but healed in the humble heart. The Sun-ruled Sunday illuminates the intellect, making it a fitting day to grasp that bhakti knowledge gives direct perception of the self — just as the Sun reveals what darkness hides. The transition from Shatabhisha to Purva Bhadrapada reflects the journey from hidden truth to its manifestation in action: one first realizes the self as servant, then engages in joyful service (su‑sukhaṁ).

The everlasting nature (avyayam) of this knowledge is mirrored in the Sun’s daily return — though it sets, it rises again, just as the self-realized soul never loses its identity. The joy (su‑sukha) of performing this devotion resonates with Saubhagya yoga, which brings good fortune to righteous actions.

 >>> SĀṄKHYA LENS <<<

[Buddhi as the sovereign]: In Sāṅkhya, buddhi (intellect) is the discriminating faculty that distinguishes puruṣa (consciousness) from prakṛti (matter). BG 9.2’s “rāja-vidyā” is the buddhi functioning at its highest — not entangled in prakṛti’s modifications but reflecting the puruṣa directly. The purity (pavitram) of this knowledge means it is unsullied by the three guṇas; it is translucent, allowing the puruṣa to shine through. The everlasting nature (avyayam) means that once buddhi rests in its true nature, it never again identifies with the temporary modifications of prakṛti.

[Inner mechanism]: The direct perception (pratyakṣāvagamaṁ) arises when buddhi, purified by bhakti, ceases to identify with the buddhi‑modes (thoughts, concepts) and rests as the witness. This is not an intellectual conclusion but an experiential shift — like the sun emerging from clouds. The joy (su‑sukha) comes from the cessation of internal struggle; the self no longer seeks validation from external achievements because it is established in its inherent nature as Krishna’s servant.
