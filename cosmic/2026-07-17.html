 >>> SĀṄKHYA LENS <<<

[Puruṣa-Prakṛti Non-Duality in Relationship]: Explain the day’s field through puruṣa, prakṛti, guṇas, buddhi, ahaṅkāra, manas, indriyas, karma, or liberation.

Krishna's declaration in BG 9.17 — "I am the father, mother, supporter, and grandfather" — reveals the non-dual nature of puruṣa and prakṛti in the field of relationship. From the Sāṅkhya perspective, puruṣa is pure consciousness, the unchanging witness, while prakṛti is the dynamic field of manifestation — the source of all names, forms, and relationships. What the verse reveals is that the puruṣa (Krishna as Īśvara-puruṣa) does not stand apart from prakṛti's relational play but is the very consciousness that animates and gives meaning to every role prakṛti assumes. When we interact with our father, mother, teacher, or friend, we are experiencing prakṛti in the form of a specific relationship — but the consciousness recognizing and engaging in that relationship is puruṣa. The verse collapses the subject-object divide: the seeker (puruṣa) and the sought (the divine role in prakṛti) are ultimately one. This is not a merger of identities but a recognition that the consciousness behind the role of "father" (whether in oneself or another) is the same consciousness behind the role of "seeker" — both are illuminated by the same puruṣa-light. The father who disciplines, the mother who nourishes, the friend who stands by — these are all prakṛti's expressions, but the awareness that perceives, appreciates, and responds to them is puruṣa. In this light, every relationship becomes a mirror: when we honor the father in another, we honor the puruṣa within ourselves; when we feel nourished by the mother's care, we recognize the sustaining puruṣa that is our own essence.

[Inner mechanism]: Show the actual metaphysical process at work. Avoid generic spirituality.

The mechanism described in BG 9.17 operates through the spontaneous recognition of Īśvara-puruṣa as the substratum of all vṛtti (mental modifications). Normally, the mind identifies with specific roles: "I am the son," "I am the worker," "I am the friend." This identification creates ahaṅkāra (ego-sense) tied to prakṛti's roles. The verse invites a shift: instead of "I am the son who has a father," the buddhi (intellect) recognizes, "The consciousness that is my sonhood is the same consciousness that is fatherhood in another." This is not intellectual speculation but a direct perceptual shift — like realizing that the dreamer and the dream-character are both made of the same dream-stuff. When we see Krishna as the father in another, we are not projecting an idea onto a person; we are recognizing that the consciousness animating their role as father is the same consciousness animating our role as child — both are modifications of the same puruṣa. The practice is to rest in this recognition: the vṛtti of "father" arises in prakṛti, but the light that knows it is puruṣa. As this recognition stabilizes, the binding of ahaṅkāra to specific roles loosens. We begin to move through relationships not as fixed identities ("I am only the son") but as fluid expressions of the same consciousness playing different roles. The father who corrects us is not "other" but a manifestation of the same puruṣa that is our own buddhi; the mother who feeds us is not "other" but the same puruṣa that is our own ananda (bliss). Even inanimate relationships reveal this truth: the earth that feeds us is prakṛti's nourishing aspect, but the gratitude we feel is puruṣa recognizing its own sustaining nature. This is the inner mechanism of BG 9.17: the dissolution of role-based identity into the recognition of a single consciousness wearing countless faces.
