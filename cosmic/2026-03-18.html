>>> COSMIC INTERPRETATION <<<

Amāvāsyā—the New Moon—is the moment of absolute dissolution before re-emergence. The moon is invisible, withdrawn into the sun's embrace. This is the void of prakṛti at maximum contraction, the space between exhalation and inhalation where complete cessation is possible. On this night, the boundaries between the subtle and gross worlds thin. The rivers of prakṛti flow into their source.

Pūrva Bhādrapadā gives way to Uttara Bhādrapadā—the "former" and "latter" blessed feet. The transition is subtle but crucial: from preparation to actualization, from penance's fire to the water that follows. Uttara Bhādrapadā is ruled by Ahirbudhnya, the serpent of the deep waters—the source that never runs dry. This is the cosmic source from which all rivers flow, the amṛta beneath the ocean.

Śubha yoga (auspicious) yields to Śukla yoga (bright/pure)—the movement from general auspiciousness to specific luminosity. Amāvāsyā is paradoxically the brightest dark night, the pivot where the waning becomes waxing without yet showing light.

>>> VERSE SELECTION RATIONALE <<<

āpūryamāṇam acala-pratiṣṭhaṁ samudram āpaḥ praviśanti yadvat

BG 2.70 — The ocean verse. Amāvāsyā is the night when the moon is fully absorbed into the sun—complete fullness achieved through withdrawal. The ocean metaphor of BG 2.70 captures this: the ocean is full not by rejecting rivers but by receiving them without being changed. The transition from Pūrva to Uttara Bhādrapadā mirrors the shift from scarcity-mind to abundance-mind. The verse teaches that śānti (peace) comes from fullness, not from fulfillment of desires.
