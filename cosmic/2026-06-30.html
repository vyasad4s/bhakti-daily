 >>> VERSE SELECTION RATIONALE <<<

[BG 8.27] Alignment: The verse speaks of the two paths (śukla and kṛṣṇa) of departure from the material world—one leading to no return, the other to rebirth—and declares that a devotee engaged in Kṛṣṇa consciousness (yoga‑yukta) is never bewildered by these alternatives. Today’s panchāṅga presents a clear duality: Pratipada (new beginning) vs. Dwitiya (sustained effort), Purva Ashadha (invincible victory) vs. Uttara Ashadha (universal perseverance), Brahma (creation, knowledge) vs. Indra (power, triumph). The verse’s teaching—that unwavering devotion transcends such dualities—perfectly matches the day’s energetic flow, reminding us that steadiness in yoga‑yukta arises not from the absence of opposites but from a mind fixed on the Supreme.

The tithi Pratipada inspires the initial resolve to return to Kṛṣṇa; the nakṣatra Purva Ashadha empowers that resolve with invincibility; the yoga Indra sustains it with triumphant strength. Together, they form a cosmic call to maintain single‑pointed devotion (ananya bhakti) regardless of external fluctuations, for the Supreme is attainable only through such steady yoga.

The devotee’s steadiness (yoga‑yukta) is viveka—the discriminative awareness that puruṣa is distinct from prakṛti’s fluctuations. As the moon wanes (Krishna Paksha) and nakṣatras shift, prakṛti’s energies oscillate, but the puruṣa, fixed in Kṛṣṇa consciousness, remains unshaken.

Inner mechanism: Through abhyāsa (consistent practice) and vairāgya (detachment from results), the mind’s modifications (citta‑vṛtti) cease, allowing the puruṣa to shine through prakṛti’s veil. This is the essence of Yoga Sutra I.12: “abhyāsa‑vairāgyābhyāṁ tannirodhaḥ.” The devotee’s mind, like a process with CPU affinity locked to the divine core, never wavers, never is bewildered, and attains the supreme abode.
