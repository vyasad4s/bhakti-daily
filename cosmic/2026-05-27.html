>>> COSMIC INTERPRETATION <<<

Dwādaśī is the tithi of fulfillment — the day after Ekādaśī's austerity when the vow is complete and its fruit manifests. The cosmic field today is one of arrival: the discipline was observed, the fast was held, and now the energy turns toward reception. Chitrā, ruled by Vishvakarma the cosmic architect, radiates creative brilliance — the radiant mind of the settled yogī reflected in the nakṣatra's luminosity. As the day progresses, Svātī arrives — Vāyu's domain of independence and freedom, the wind that carries the yogī beyond rajas entirely.

The Vyatipāta yoga is the most paradoxical of all yogas — literally "the calamity" — yet classical jyotiṣa considers it among the most powerful for spiritual pursuits. Vyatipāta reverses: what appears as destruction becomes the greatest opening. This is precisely the mechanism of BG 6.27 — the "calamity" of rajas being pacified is actually the gateway to sukham uttamam. When the ordinary is destroyed, the extraordinary reveals itself.

>>> VERSE SELECTION RATIONALE <<<

BG 6.27 Alignment: Three cosmic vectors converge on this verse:

1. Dwādaśī fulfillment: The post-austerity tithi perfectly mirrors the verse's sequence — practice (Ekādaśī) → pacification (Dwādaśī) → supreme happiness (sukham uttamam). The verse describes what happens after the mind settles. The tithi enacts it.

2. Chitrā→Svātī portal: Chitrā is Vishvakarma's radiance — the creative brilliance of a mind that has been shaped by discipline into something luminous. Svātī is Vāyu's freedom — the independence that comes when rajas no longer drives. BG 6.27 moves from praśānta-manas (Chitrā's settled brilliance) through śānta-rajasaḥ (Svātī's freedom from passion) to brahma-bhūtam (liberation itself).

3. Vyatipāta reversal: The "calamity yoga" that inverts destruction into opportunity maps directly onto the verse's central paradox — the pacification of rajas looks like loss from the rajas-perspective but is the gateway to uttama-sukha from the sattva-perspective.

>>> SĀṄKHYA LENS <<<

Sattva's self-revelation: In Sāṅkhya, the three guṇas are perpetually in motion — rajas drives, tamas obscures, sattva illuminates. But sattva's illumination is only visible when rajas and tamas subside. BG 6.27 describes the precise moment when sattva's light becomes uttama — not merely bright, but supreme — because rajas has been pacified (śānta-rajasaḥ) rather than merely suppressed.

Buddhi as reflector: The pacified mind (praśānta-manas) is buddhi in its purest operational state — reflecting puruṣa without distortion. When rajas agitates buddhi, the reflection fractures. When rajas stills, the reflection becomes complete. Brahma-bhūtam is not a new acquisition — it is what was always there, now visible because the distortions have ceased.

The karmic exhaust mechanism: Akalmaṣam — freed from past reactions — is the Sāṅkhya account of how saṃskāras exhaust themselves. When buddhi no longer generates new desires through rajas, old karmic seeds sprout and complete their cycle without replanting. The fire doesn't go out. It burns so cleanly that no residue accumulates.
