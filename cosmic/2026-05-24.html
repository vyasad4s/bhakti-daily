 >>> COSMIC INTERPRETATION <<<

Pūrva Phalgunī → Uttara Phalgunī at 5:20 PM is today's cosmic signature, and it maps precisely onto the two-verse sequence. Pūrva Phalgunī is the nakṣatra of creative fulfillment — the earlier of the two "reddish" stars associated with Bhaga, the deity of pleasure, share, and marital bliss. This is the energy of the mind that has almost arrived — it tastes the fruit, it glimpses the destination, but it hasn't consolidated. Uttara Phalgunī, the "later" star, is ruled by Aryaman, deity of patronage and enduring partnership. Where Pūrva Phalgunī is the rush of fulfillment, Uttara Phalgunī is the consolidation — the state that holds what was glimpsed. Verse 25 is the Pūrva Phalgunī instruction: śanaiḥ śanair uparamed — gradually withdraw, place the mind in the Self. This is the approach, the tasting of samādhi. Verse 26 is the Uttara Phalgunī reality: the mind flickers back, and from there, from the very place it wandered to, bring it home. The consolidation is not the first arrival — it is the thousandth return. And it is Sarvartha Siddhi Yoga that seals the connection. From 5:20 PM tonight — exactly when Pūrva Phalgunī yields to Uttara Phalgunī — the Sarvartha Siddhi window opens: "all purposes fulfilled." The yogī who has spent the day in the yataḥ yataḥ / tataḥ tataḥ loop of returning the mind finds, at this transition, that the accumulated returns have produced something stable. The wandering doesn't stop. But the return becomes effortless. This is Sarvartha Siddhi — not that the mind never wanders, but that the return has become its nature.

Harṣaṇa → Vajra yoga deepens the arc. Harṣaṇa — "joy, exhilaration, delight" — dominates until 6:14 PM. This is the yoga of the fulfilled practitioner, the one who has tasted the fruit of practice and feels the exhilaration of progress. But Harṣaṇa is followed by Vajra — "diamond, thunderbolt" — the yoga of indestructibility under pressure. The sequence mirrors the teaching: the joy of the mind settling (Harṣaṇa) is tested by the diamond-hard reality that it will flicker again (Vajra). The diamond is formed under pressure. The gosvāmī is formed through the pressure of repeated returns. Harṣaṇa is the sweetness; Vajra is the proof that the sweetness survives the next wandering.

Navamī tithi — the ninth day of the waxing moon — is one step from Daśamī and six from Pūrṇimā. The moon is now more than 90% illuminated, approaching fullness but not yet complete. This is the phase of near-perfection that still requires the final push. Navamī belongs to the Nava-Durgā tradition — the nine forms of the fierce feminine, each one a stage of the practitioner's own internal battle. On Navamī, the mind is nearly settled, nearly fixed in the Self — but "nearly" is not ātma-saṁstha. The gradual process (śanaiḥ śanaiḥ) has brought the practitioner this far. Now, on the ninth day, the teaching is: don't stop. The mind flickers? Return it. Again. The Navamī energy demands the final discipline that transforms "almost there" into "established."

Ravivāra (Sunday, Sūrya) illuminates the path. The Sun is the eye of the cosmos — the one witness that never turns away. On Sūrya's day, the practitioner invokes the inner eye that sees the mind wander and sees it return. Sūrya does not judge the wandering. Sūrya illuminates it. The yataḥ yataḥ is made visible by Sūrya's light. The tatas tato niyamya is made possible by Sūrya's clarity. Sunday is the day of witnessing — and these two verses are the witness's operating manual.

 >>> SĀṄKHYA LENS <<<

Puruṣa-Driven Disengagement: In Sāṅkhya, the mind (manas) is an evolute of prakṛti — it is matter, not consciousness. It flickers because it is constituted by the guṇas, which are always in motion. Cañcalam is not a personal failing; it is the mechanical consequence of being a prakṛti-product. But puruṣa — pure witness consciousness — is unaffected by the flickering. The method of BG 6.25-26 exploits this ontological distinction: the one who returns the mind is not the mind itself. The mind cannot fix itself — that would be prakṛti correcting prakṛti, which is circular. The return is initiated from the puruṣa side — the witness detects the wander and re-engages the buddhi (which is the highest prakṛti-evolute, closest to puruṣa) to redirect the manas. Verse 25's buddhyā dhṛti-gṛhītayā is precise: it is buddhi — not manas — that executes the withdrawal, and buddhi must be held firm by dhṛti. Buddhi is the prakṛti-component that reflects puruṣa most clearly; when it is sattvic and conviction-steadied, it becomes the instrument of liberation.

The Pratyāhāra Mechanism: When buddhi withdraws from viṣaya (sense objects), the entire downstream chain — ahaṅkāra, manas, indriyas — follows. This is not willpower; it is structural hierarchy. The senses don't need to be individually suppressed because they are downstream of the mind, which is downstream of buddhi. Withdraw buddhi, and the chain collapses inward. This is what Patañjali means by cittasya svarūpānukāra ivendriyāṇāṁ — the senses imitate the mind's state. When the mind goes silent, the senses have nothing to imitate. They power down. The peripherals depend on the command channel; cut the channel, the peripherals cease.

Nine is the number of completion in the Vedic framework — the nine doors of the body, the nine forms of Durgā, the nine modes of bhakti. On Navamī, the practitioner stands at the threshold. The mind has been trained, the returns have accumulated, but the fullness (Pūrṇimā) is still ahead. Today's practice: don't coast. The śanaiḥ śanaiḥ continues until the very end. The gradual one does not accelerate near the finish line. The gradual one remains gradual — and that is why they arrive.

If you have been in the return-loop all day, this window grants the consolidation — not that the mind stops wandering, but that the return has become reflexive. The purpose of practice is fulfilled not in the absence of distraction but in the automaticity of the return.

[COSMIC VECTOR] Approach → Consolidation. The day's energy moves from tasting (Pūrva Phalgunī) to holding (Uttara Phalgunī), from exhilaration (Harṣaṇa) to diamond-hardness (Vajra).
