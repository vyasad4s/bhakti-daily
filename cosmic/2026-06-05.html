>>> COSMIC INTERPRETATION <<<

Today carries Sarvartha Siddhi Yoga — the alignment of Śravaṇa nakṣatra with Friday (Śukravāsara) — from 5:30 AM until 8:33 PM. This is one of the most auspicious configurations in the lunar calendar: "all purposes accomplished." The question is: which purposes? Kṛṣṇa's verse answers precisely — the purpose that matters is knowing the distinction between aparā and parā, between the dead infrastructure and the living presence that animates it. Sarvartha Siddhi does not grant worldly success by default. It grants the capacity for the right purpose to be fulfilled — if you align with it.

Śravaṇa — "the ear," "hearing" — is governed by Viṣṇu, the All-Pervading One. This is the nakṣatra of reception, of listening, of receiving the teaching as it is given. Yesterday Śravaṇa activated at 6:11 PM, and today it holds until 8:33 PM — the entire day is saturated with the energy of sacred hearing. BG 7.5 is a verse that demands reception, not analysis. Kṛṣṇa does not argue for the existence of the jīva — He declares it. Viddhi — know. Not "consider" or "contemplate." Know. The imperative is absolute. Śravaṇa's energy supports this: the knowledge that arrives through hearing, through submission to the teacher's voice, not through the mind's dialectics.

At 8:33 PM, Śravaṇa yields to Dhaniṣṭhā — "the wealthiest," governed by the Vasus, the eight deities of material prosperity and cosmic light. The transition from the nakṣatra of hearing to the nakṣatra of wealth is not random. Śravaṇa receives the knowledge; Dhaniṣṭhā puts it to work. The Vasus are eight — the same number as Kṛṣṇa's aparā energies. The jīva that has heard the teaching now enters the field of the eight and must navigate it without succumbing to it. Dhaniṣṭhā's energy is prosperity, but in the spiritual sense — the wealth of the parā prakṛti, the living entity's true capital: consciousness itself.

Indra yoga — named after the king of the devas, the sovereign of the celestial realm — holds through the day. Indra is the wielder of the thunderbolt, the one who sees from the summit. This is the yoga of sovereignty, and BG 7.5 is a verse about sovereignty — but not the jīva's. The living entity is superior to matter, yes. It sustains the world, yes. But its superiority is derivative — it is Kṛṣṇa's superior energy, not an independent sovereign. Indra yoga reminds us that even the king of the gods is a servant of Viṣṇu. Real sovereignty is knowing whose energy you are.

The moon remains in Makara (Capricorn) — the sign of long discipline, the slow climb, the summit that demands everything. Friday (Śukravāsara) adds Venus: beauty, relationship, the longing for connection. The jīva's relationship to Kṛṣṇa is precisely this — the spark's longing for the fire, the beloved's desire for the Beloved. Venus does not oppose the discipline of Capricorn; it gives it a reason. >>> SĀṄKHYA LENS <<<

The Tripartite Architecture: BG 7.4–7.5 together reveal the complete Sāṅkhya architecture of the Gītā. Classical Sāṅkhya knows two categories: puruṣa (consciousness) and prakṛti (matter). The Gītā adds a third term: the Supreme Person (puruṣottama), who stands above and is the source of both. The eight aparā energies are prakṛti in the traditional sense — material, inert, evolving. The jīva is also prakṛti — but parā, superior, conscious. And above both is Kṛṣṇa, the puruṣottama, who is neither the aparā nor the parā but the Energetic from whom both emanate. This tripartite structure — Energetic, inferior energy, superior energy — is the Gītā's unique contribution to Sāṅkhya metaphysics.

Asmitā as the Border Error: YS II.6 identifies asmitā as the "seeming identity" of seer and instrument. BG 7.5 locates this error precisely: the jīva (parā prakṛti, the conscious principle) enters the aparā (material field) and begins to dhāryate — to sustain, exploit, drive the machinery. In the process, it risks fusing with its instrument. The seer (dṛg) and the seeing-apparatus (darśana) become seemingly one. This is not the jīva becoming the aparā — that is impossible, the categories are eternal. It is the jīva identifying with the aparā — a cognitive error, not an ontological transformation. The categories remain distinct. The confusion is in the buddhi, not in reality itself.

The Monism Error: Prabhupāda's purport strikes directly at the Advaitin conclusion that jīva = Īśvara. From the Gītā's Sāṅkhya framework, this is the ultimate asmitā — not merely the individual ego claiming "I am the body," but the cosmic ego claiming "I am the Supreme." Both errors share the same structure: confusing prakṛti (energy) with puruṣa (the Supreme Person). The individual asmitā says "I am matter." The monistic asmitā says "I am God." Both are wrong for the same reason: the energy is not the Energetic. The spark is real. It is conscious. It is superior to the firewood. But it is not the fire.

Terminal metaphor: cat /proc/krypton/energy | grep -E "(apara|para)" — two energy streams, both from the same source process. The aparā stream is the hardware interrupt table — eight channels, all material, all inert without input. The parā stream is the user-space process — conscious, interactive, driving the hardware through system calls. The kernel (Kṛṣṇa) spawned both. Neither is the kernel. Confusing the user process with the kernel is the root exploit of māyā.
