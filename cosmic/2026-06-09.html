 >>> COSMIC INTERPRETATION <<<

Kṛṣṇa Navami — the ninth day of the waning fortnight. Navami is traditionally associated with the power of the divine feminine, the remover of obstacles, and the fierce aspect of the goddess (Durga). Today carries the energy of Uttara Bhadrapada nakshatra, ruled by Saturn and associated with the fire dragon (Ajaikapad), symbolizing purification through transformative fire. The yoga Ayushman (long life, health) holds through the morning, indicating vitality and the pursuit of longevity. The Moon has transitioned to Meena (Pisces, compassionate, spiritual, devotional), shifting the consciousness from humanitarian innovation to devotional surrender — a transition that supports taking refuge in the Divine.

Rāhu Kāla (4:37–6:29 PM) and Yamaganda (9:12–11:04 AM) create inauspicious windows for material endeavors, supporting inward reflection and spiritual practice. Abhijit Muhurat (12:25–1:25 PM) and Amrit Kaal (07:05–08:40 PM) offer auspicious windows for spiritual practice and surrender. Sarvartha Siddhi Yoga (05:30 AM – 11:51 PM) makes the entire day until late night favorable for auspicious endeavors — a rare alignment that amplifies the power of any spiritual practice undertaken today.

The transition to Revati (Pushan, the nourisher) at 11:51 PM signals that the path of refuge leads to nourishment and protection, not deprivation.

 >>> SĀṄKHYA LENS <<<

[jarā-maraṇa as Prakṛti's Inevitable Code]: From the Sāṅkhya perspective, old age and death (jarā-maraṇa) are the inevitable consequences of puruṣa's false identification with prakṛti. The body belongs to prakṛti — it decays, it dies, it recycles. Puruṣa is eternal, untouched by time. Liberation (mokṣa) is the restoration of this distinction — not through intellectual analysis alone, but through āśritya, taking refuge in the puruṣa-viśeṣa, the Supreme Person who is the ultimate enjoyer and witness.

[The Chapter's Arc]: BG 7.29 completes the chapter's progression: from knowing the two prakṛtis (7.4–5), to recognizing Kṛṣṇa as their source (7.6–7), to understanding the four types of seekers (7.16–18), to the final declaration — the intelligent ones (ye) who seek freedom from time itself take refuge in the One beyond time, and thereby know the complete picture of transcendental activity.

[YS II.45 Confirmation]: īśvara-praṇidhāna → samādhi-siddhi. Surrender to the special puruṣa brings the perfection of absorption — the direct realization of the puruṣa-prakṛti distinction that Sāṅkhya describes and yoga practices. The "liberation from old age and death" in the Gītā corresponds to the transcendence of the fluctuations of citta that yoga seeks to still.

[Terminal Metaphor]: Think of jarā-maraṇa as the kernel's garbage collector — it reclaims all processes (bodies) that have exhausted their heap allocation. The only process that survives is the one linked to the Root (puruṣa-viśeṣa). Āśritya is the system call that escalates your process to root privilege, making it immune to the garbage collector. Once at root, you have full visibility — kṛtsnam adhyātmaṁ karma cākhilam — complete access to all transcendental system calls.
