Cosmic Interpretation

The full moon (pūrṇimā) represents the zenith of the waxing cycle, where the lunar disc is fully illuminated, symbolizing the peak of reflective capacity and the illumination of inner truths. In Vedic tradition, pūrṇimā is a time when the mind, like a calm lake, can most clearly reflect the light of the soul, making it highly conducive to meditation on the eternal, unchanging reality described in verse 8.20. The concurrent presence of Brahma yoga during the early morning hours adds a layer of divine harmony, as this yoga is known for fostering unity, wisdom, and the dissolution of dualistic perception—qualities that align perfectly with the realization of a transcendental, non‑dual consciousness that is nonetheless personal in its relationship with the Divine.

The nakṣatra Mūla, which dominates the early part of the day, is intrinsically tied to the theme of this verse. Mūla means “root,” and its presiding deity, Nirṛti, is associated with dissolution, destruction, and the laying bare of foundations. This energy supports the process of cutting away superficial identifications with the body‑mind complex to reveal the underlying, eternal essence of the self—exactly what verse 8.20 points to: the eternal nature that remains when all superficial layers are removed.
