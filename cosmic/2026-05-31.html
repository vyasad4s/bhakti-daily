 >>> SĀṄKHYA LENS <<<

Jitātman as buddhi-sāttvika: In Sāṅkhya, the "conquered mind" is the buddhi that has become fully sāttvika — dominated by sattva-guṇa to such a degree that rajas and tamas are functionally inert. When the buddhi is pure, it ceases to generate the vṛttis (mental fluctuations) that obscure puruṣa. The Paramātmā is not "reached" in the sense of traveling to a distant location — He is recognized when the noise of the lower mind falls away. The sun was always behind the clouds. The clouds were always prakṛti's movement. When the movement settles, the light is simply there.

Dvandvas as guṇa-oscillation: The pairs of opposites — heat/cold, pleasure/pain, honor/dishonor — are the manifest oscillations of the three guṇas. Sattva produces the "pleasant" pole (honor, happiness, comfort); tamas produces the "unpleasant" pole (dishonor, distress, discomfort); rajas drives the oscillation between them. When the buddhi is conquered — when it rests in sattva without being pulled by rajas into the oscillation — the dvandvas lose their grip. They still arise in prakṛti. They simply cease to reach puruṣa. This is anabhighātaḥ — the firewall of discernment.

The Mitra-Indra mechanism: Anurādhā (Mitra) represents the buddhi in its devotional aspect — reaching toward the Supreme through love and friendship. Jyeṣṭhā (Indra) represents the buddhi in its sovereign aspect — recognizing the Supreme as the inner authority. In Sāṅkhya terms, both are the same buddhi operating at different levels of clarity. Mitra is the buddhi that still seeks; Indra is the buddhi that has found. The nakṣatra transition at 6:41 AM encodes this very movement.
