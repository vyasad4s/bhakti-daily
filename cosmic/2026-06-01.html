 >>> COSMIC INTERPRETATION <<<

Kṛṣṇa Dwitiyā — the second day of the waning fortnight. The contraction deepens. Jyeṣṭhā (Indra, sovereign command) yields to Mūla (Nirṛti, the goddess of dissolution and the hidden roots) at 9:38 AM. This is one of the most potent nakṣatra transitions in the entire cycle. Mūla means "root" — the very foundation upon which the tree of saṃsāra grows. Its deity is Nirṛti, who governs destruction, dissolution, and the uprooting of what is false. Mūla doesn't trim branches. It goes to the root.

The moon simultaneously shifts from Vṛścika (Scorpio — depth, transformation, intensity) into Dhanu (Sagittarius — expansion through truth, the archer's aim). The combined energy: destroy the false root, then expand from the true one. This is precisely the movement of samādhi — first the dissolution of material identification, then the expansion into transcendental awareness.

Sādhya yoga — "the attainable" — holds through the day, transitioning to Śubha (auspicious) at 9:45 PM. The name itself is the message: samādhi is not theoretical. It is attainable. The roots must be touched first. Then everything changes.

 >>> VERSE SELECTION RATIONALE <<<

BG 6.20-23 Alignment: Four verses. Four stages of samādhi. Selected for the final day of Dhyāna Yoga because Mūla nakṣatra demands the root — and these verses are the root of the entire chapter. Every preceding verse in BG 6 builds toward this concentrated description of yoga's fruit.

The nakṣatra match is exact: Jyeṣṭhā-Indra's sovereign command corresponds to the mind restrained (cittaṁ niruddham) — the kingdom of the mind conquered. Mūla-Nirṛti's dissolution corresponds to the severance of suffering's root (duḥkha-saṁyoga-viyogam) — the kingdom of suffering destroyed. The transition from Indra to Nirṛti mirrors the movement from establishing order to dismantling the false. Both are necessary. Both are yoga.

 >>> SĀṄKHYA LENS <<<

The Root Mechanism: In Sāṅkhya, suffering is not an accident — it is the structural consequence of puruṣa's misidentification with prakṛti's modifications. The saṁyoga (connection) between consciousness and matter is the root disease. BG 6.23 names the cure: viyoga — disconnection. Not the destruction of prakṛti (which continues to operate), but the severance of the binding that causes puruṣa to experience prakṛti's transformations as its own.

The Guṇa Return: Patañjali's YS IV.34 describes the same event from the guṇa side: when the guṇas have no further purpose for puruṣa (puruṣārtha-śūnya), they return to their source (pratiprasava). The gunas don't disappear — they simply stop engaging puruṣa. The code continues running; consciousness is no longer logged in as root on the material server.

Citi-śakti as the Final State: What remains when the connection is severed is not void but citi-śakti — the power of pure consciousness. This is puruṣa established in its own form (svarūpa-pratiṣṭhā). In hacker terms: the OS kernel running with zero user-space processes consuming resources. Pure system, pure awareness, pure being.
